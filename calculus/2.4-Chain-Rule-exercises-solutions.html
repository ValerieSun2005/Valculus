2.4 Exercise Solutions: Chain Rule

EXERCISE 1
What is the Chain Rule?
SOLUTION The Chain Rule enables us to differentiate composite functions. If \(F(x) = f(g(x))\) where \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable, then the Chain Rule gives \[F'(x) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \pd\] We could also write \[\deriv{F}{x} = \deriv{f}{g} \deriv{g}{x} \cma\] in which we view \(f\) and \(g\) as variables such that \(f\) changes with \(g,\) which changes with \(x.\) This rule, combined with other differentiation rules, enables us to differentiate any function.
EXERCISE 2
What is the General Power Rule?
SOLUTION The General Power Rule is a special case—an application—of the Chain Rule in which the outer function is a power function. If \(g\) is a differentiable function and \(n\) is a constant, then \[ \deriv{}{x} [g(x)]^n = n \parbr{g(x)}^{n - 1} \cdot g'(x) \pd \] This equation is entirely derived using the Chain Rule; it is not a unique concept.
EXERCISE 3
Walkthrough of Using the Chain Rule Let \(y = \sqrt{2x - 7}.\)
  1. The function \(y\) is composite, meaning it can be expressed in the form \(y = f(g(x)).\) Identify \(f\) and \(g.\)
  2. Find \(f'(x)\) and \(g'(x).\)
  3. Determine \(f'(g(x)).\)
  4. Find \(\textderiv{y}{x}.\)
SOLUTION
  1. The outer function is \(f(x) = \sqrt x,\) and the inner function is \(g(x) = 2x - 7.\)
  2. We write \(f(x) = x^{1/2}\) and apply the Power Rule to get \[f'(x) = \tfrac{1}{2} x^{-1/2} = \frac{1}{2 \sqrt x} \pd\] Conversely, for \(g\) we use the Difference Rule for Derivatives (see Section 2.1): \[ \ba g'(x) &= \deriv{}{x}(2x) - \deriv{}{x}(7) \nl &= 2 - 0 = 2 \pd \ea \]
  3. From part (b), we found \(f'(x) = 1/(2 \sqrt x).\) We replace \(x\) with \(g(x)\) to get \[f'(g(x)) = \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{g(x)}} = \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{2x - 7}} \pd\]
  4. The Chain Rule gives the derivative to be \[f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \pd\] We found \(f'(g(x))\) from part (c) and \(g'(x)\) from part (b), so the derivative is \[\frac{1}{2 \sqrt{2x - 7}} \cdot 2 = \boxed{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2x - 7}}}\]

For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \sin(3x + 2)\]
evaluate the derivative.
SOLUTION The function \(\sin(3x + 2)\) is composite, so we use the Chain Rule to differentiate it. We identify the outer function to be \(f(x) = \sin x\) and the inner function to be \(g(x) = 3x + 2.\) We see \[f'(x) = \cos x \and g'(x) = 3 \pd\] The Chain Rule therefore gives the derivative to be \[f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) = \cos(3x + 2) \cdot 3 = \boxed{3 \cos(3x + 2)}\]

\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \sin^2 x\]
evaluate the derivative.
SOLUTION At first glance, the function \(\sin^2 x\) doesn't appear to be composite. EXERCISE 11
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{\theta} \sin \par{\theta^2}\]
evaluate the derivative.
SOLUTION Let's call \(y = \sin \par{\theta^2}.\) Let some variable be the inner function—say, \(u = \theta^2.\) Hence, the Chain Rule gives \[ \ba \deriv{y}{\theta} &= \deriv{y}{u} \deriv{u}{\theta} \nl &= \deriv{}{u} \par{\sin u} \cdot \deriv{}{\theta} \par{\theta^2} \nl &= (\cos u) \cdot 2 \theta \nl &= \boxed{2 \theta \cos \par{\theta^2}} \ea \] Note that we would attain the same result by choosing \(f\) and \(g\) and applying the formula \(f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x).\) This solution uses Leibniz's notation solely for diversity of methods. However, you are free to choose the method with which you feel most comfortable.

SOLUTION Do not use the Power Rule; we use the Power Rule only if the exponent is constant. Let \(y = e^{-x^2 + 3x - 4}.\) The function is composite with inner function \(u = -x^2 + 3x - 4.\) Thus, by the Chain Rule \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= \deriv{y}{u} \deriv{u}{x} \nl &= \deriv{}{u} (e^u) \cdot \deriv{}{x} \par{-x^2 + 3x - 4} \nl &= e^u \par{-2x + 3} \nl &= \boxed{e^{-x^2 + 3x - 4} (-2x + 3)} \ea \] This solution uses Leibniz's form of the Chain Rule, but we would attain the same result by selecting \(f\) and \(g\) and obtaining the derivative through the formula \(f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x).\) You can pick the method with which you feel most comfortable.

EXERCISE 22
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \frac{x}{\sqrt{4 - x^2}}\]
evaluate the derivative.
SOLUTION Because the function has terms of \(x\) in both the numerator and denominator, we first apply the Quotient Rule to attain \[\frac{1 \cdot \sqrt{4 - x^2} - x \cdot \deriv{}{x} \sqrt{4 - x^2}}{4 - x^2} \pd\] The Chain Rule is needed to differentiate \(\sqrt{4 - x^2};\) applying it gives \[\frac{1}{2 \sqrt{4 - x^2}} \cdot \deriv{}{x} \par{4 - x^2} = \frac{-2x}{2 \sqrt{4 - x^2}} = -\frac{x}{\sqrt{4 - x^2}} \pd\] Hence, the derivative of \(x/\sqrt{4 - x^2}\) becomes \[\frac{\sqrt{4 - x^2} + \frac{x^2}{\sqrt{4 - x^2}}}{4 - x^2} \pd\] Multiplying the numerator and denominator by \(\sqrt{4 - x^2}\) gives \[\frac{(4 - x^2) + x^2}{\par{4 - x^2}^{3/2}} = \boxed{\frac{4}{\par{4 - x^2}^{3/2}}}\]
EXERCISE 23
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{w} \parbr{e^{2w} \, \sqrt{w^3 + 1} \,}\]
evaluate the derivative.
SOLUTION The Product Rule shows that the derivative can be broken down into \[\sqrt{w^3 + 1} \cdot \deriv{}{w} e^{2w} + e^{2w} \cdot \deriv{}{w} \sqrt{w^3 + 1} \pd\] By the Chain Rule, \[ \baf &&\deriv{}{w} \sqrt{w^3 + 1} &= \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{w^3 + 1}} \cdot 3w^2 &\nl \laWord{and} &&\deriv{}{w} e^{2w} &= e^{2w} \cdot 2 \pd \eaf \] Hence, our derivative becomes \[\sqrt{w^3 + 1} \cdot 2e^{2w} + e^{2w} \cdot \frac{3w^2}{2 \sqrt{w^3 + 1}} = \boxed{e^{2w} \par{2 \sqrt{w^3 + 1} + \frac{3w^2}{2 \sqrt{w^3 + 1}}}} \]

EXERCISE 28
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \sin(\sin(\sin x))\]
evaluate the derivative.
SOLUTION This function is composed of three layers, so we apply the Chain Rule twice. Beginning with the outermost layer, we view \(\sin(\sin x)\) as one function composed with sine. The first application of the Chain Rule gives \[\cos(\sin(\sin x)) \cdot \deriv{}{x} \parbr{\orange{\sin(\sin x)}} \pd\] The second application gives \[\cos(\sin(\sin x)) \cdot \parbr{\orange{\cos(\sin x) \cdot \cos x}} = \boxed{\cos(\sin(\sin x)) \cos(\sin x) \cos x} \]

EXERCISE 36
Let \(f\) be a differentiable function, and let \(a\) and \(b\) be constants. Determine the derivative of the family of functions \(f(ax + b).\)
SOLUTION In the composite function \(f(ax + b),\) the outer layer is \(f\) and the inner layer is \(ax + b.\) Thus, the Chain Rule gives the derivative to be \[f'(ax + b) \cdot \deriv{}{x} \par{ax + b} = \boxed{a f'(ax + b)}\]
EXERCISE 37
Let \(f\) and \(g\) be differentiable functions, and let \(F(x) = f(g(x)).\) It is known that \(f'(3) = 6,\) \(g(2) = 3,\) and \(g'(2) = 2.\) Calculate \(F'(2).\)
SOLUTION By the Chain Rule, we have \[F'(x) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \pd\] Substituting \(x = 2\) shows \[ \ba F'(2) &= f'(g(2)) \cdot g'(2) \nl &= f'(3) \cdot 2 \nl &= 6 \cdot 2 = \boxed{12} \ea \]
EXERCISE 38
The graph of \(f\) passes through the point \((8, 1)\) with slope \(3.\) Conversely, the function \(g\) satisfies \(g(4) = 2\) and \(g'(4) = -4.\) Determine the slope of the line tangent to the graph of \(y = f(2x)/g(x)\) when \(x = 4.\)
SOLUTION We seek the value of \[\deriv{}{x} \frac{f(2x)}{g(x)} \intEval_{x = 4} \pd\] The function \(f\) satisfies \(f(8) = 1\) and \(f'(8) = 3.\) Using the Quotient Rule combined with the Chain Rule, we find the derivative to be \[ \ba \frac{2f'(2x) g(x) - f(2x) g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2} \intEval_{x = 4} &= \frac{2f'(8) g(4) - f(8) g'(4)}{[g(4)]^2} \nl &= \frac{2(3)(2) - (1)(-4)}{(2)^2} \nl &= \boxed 4 \ea \]
EXERCISE 39
The function \(f\) is differentiable at \(x = 2.\) An equation of the line tangent to the graph of \(y = f(x)\) at \(x = 2\) is \[y - 4 = -2(x - 2) \pd\] Let \(a\) be the function given by \(a(x) = x^3 \, f(2x).\) Calculate \(a'(1).\)
SOLUTION From the equation of the tangent line, one point on the curve \(f\) is \((2, 4),\) at which the slope is \(-2.\) Thus, \(f(2) = 4\) and \(f'(2) = -2.\) We differentiate \(a\) by combining the Product Rule with the Chain Rule: \[a'(x) = 3x^2 f(2x) + x^3 \cdot 2 f'(2x) \pd\] When \(x = 1,\) we see \[ \ba a'(1) &= 3(1)^2 f(2) + (1)^3 \cdot 2 f'(2) \nl &= 3(4) + 2(-2) \nl &= \boxed 8 \ea \]
EXERCISE 40
By applying the Chain Rule to each term, show that the derivatives of \(\sin^2 \alpha\) and \(\cos^2 \alpha\) sum to \(0.\)
SOLUTION We rewrite the terms as \[(\sin \alpha)^2 \and (\cos \alpha)^2\] to identify each term as a composite function whose inner function is trigonometric. Using the Chain Rule, \[ \baf && \deriv{}{\alpha} (\sin \alpha)^2 &= 2 (\sin \alpha) \cdot \cos \alpha &\nl \laWord{and} && \deriv{}{\alpha} (\cos \alpha)^2 &= 2 (\cos \alpha) \cdot - \sin \alpha \pd \eaf \] Hence, \[ \ba \deriv{}{\alpha} (\sin \alpha)^2 + \deriv{}{\alpha} (\cos \alpha)^2 &= (2 \sin \alpha \cos \alpha) + (-2 \sin \alpha \cos \alpha) \nl &= 0 \cma \ea \] as required. This result is expected because the Pythagorean identity asserts that \(\sin^2 \alpha + \cos^2 \alpha\) \(= 1,\) whose derivative is \(0\) by the Constant Rule for Derivatives.
EXERCISE 41
Show that \(\sec^2 \theta\) and \(\tan^2 \theta\) have the same derivatives.
SOLUTION We differentiate each function using the Chain Rule and expect to attain the same results. Recall that the derivative of \(\sec \theta\) is \(\sec \theta \tan \theta\) and that the derivative of \(\tan \theta\) is \(\sec^2 \theta.\) For secant, we see \[\deriv{}{\theta} \sec^2 \theta = (2 \sec \theta) \cdot \deriv{}{\theta} \sec \theta = 2 \sec^2 \theta \tan \theta \pd\] For tangent, we attain \[\deriv{}{\theta} \tan^2 \theta = (2 \tan \theta) \cdot \deriv{}{\theta} \tan \theta = 2 \tan \theta \sec^2 \theta \pd\] Since these results are the same, \(\sec^2 \theta\) and \(\tan^2 \theta\) have the same derivatives. This finding is logical because the Pythagorean identity shows \[\sec^2 \theta = \tan^2 \theta + 1 \pd\] Thus, differentiating \(\sec^2 \theta\) is actually differentiating \(\tan^2 \theta + 1,\) and the derivative of the constant \(1\) is \(0\) by the Constant Rule for Derivatives.
EXERCISE 42
Let \(p(x) = \csc^2 x\) and \(q(x) = \cot^2 x.\) Show that \(p\) and \(q\) satisfy \(p'(x) - q'(x) = 0.\)
SOLUTION For increased clarity, we write \[p(x) = \par{\csc x}^2 \and q(x) = \par{\cot x}^2 \pd\] Recall that the derivative of \(\csc x\) is \(-\csc x \cot x\) and that the derivative of \(\cot x\) is \(-\csc^2 x.\) The Chain Rule gives \[ \baf && p'(x) &= 2 (\csc x)^1 \cdot \deriv{}{x} \par{\csc x} = -2 \csc^2 x \cot x &\nl \laWord{and} && q'(x) &= 2 (\cot x)^1 \cdot \deriv{}{x} \par{\cot x} = -2 \csc^2 x \cot x \pd \eaf \] Hence, we see \[p'(x) - q'(x) = -2 \csc^2 x \cot x - (-2 \csc^2 x \cot x) = 0 \cma\] as required. In other words, the equation \(p'(x) - q'(x) = 0\) means that \(p' = q'.\) Thus, \(\csc^2 x\) and \(\cot^2 x\) have the same derivatives. This is expected because of the Pythagorean identity: \[\cot^2 x + 1 = \csc^2 x \pd\] Differentiating \(\csc^2 x\) is the same as differentiating \(\cot^2 x + 1.\)
EXERCISE 43
Find the values of \(x\) corresponding to the horizontal tangents of the graph \(y = \sqrt{x^2 + 1}.\)
SOLUTION The graph has a horizontal tangent when \(\textderiv{y}{x} = 0.\) By the Chain Rule, we see \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{x^2 + 1}} \cdot \deriv{}{x} \par{x^2 + 1} \nl &= \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{x^2 + 1}} \cdot 2x \nl &= \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2 + 1}} \cma \ea \] which is \(0\) when \(\boxed{x = 0}.\)
EXERCISE 44
Consider the function \(f(x) = \sqrt{x^2 - 4}.\)
  1. Show that \(f'(x) = x/\sqrt{x^2 - 4}.\)
  2. Find an equation of the line tangent to the graph of \(y = f(x)\) when \(x = 3.\)
  3. Does \(f\) have a horizontal tangent when \(x = 0 \ques\)
SOLUTION
  1. Using the Chain Rule, we have \[ \ba f'(x) &= \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{x^2 - 4}} \cdot \deriv{}{x} \par{x^2 - 4} \nl &= \frac{2x}{2 \sqrt{x^2 - 4}} \nl &= \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2 - 4}} \cma \ea \] as requested.
  2. When \(x = 3,\) \[ \baf &&f(3) &= \sqrt{3^2 - 4} = \sqrt 5 &\nl \laWord{and} &&f'(3) &= \frac{3}{\sqrt{3^2 - 4}} = \frac{3}{\sqrt 5} \pd \eaf \] Hence, an equation of the tangent line is \[\boxed{y - \sqrt 5 = \frac{3}{\sqrt 5} (x - 3)}\]
  3. No, \(f\) doesn't have a horizontal tangent when \(x = 0.\) The value \(x = 0\) is outside of the domain for \(f(x),\) which is \((-\infty, -2] \cup [2, \infty).\)
EXERCISE 45
An object moves along a path with time \(t\) as modeled by the position function \(s(t),\) velocity function \(v(t),\) and acceleration function \(a(t).\) Show that \[a(t) = v(t) \deriv{v}{s}\] and interpret this equation. (We use this relationship to derive fundamental equations in physics.)
SOLUTION From Section 2.1, we defined \[a(t) = \deriv{v}{t} \pd\] Using the Chain Rule, we need to express this derivative in terms of two other derivatives—namely, \[a(t) = \deriv{v}{s} \deriv{s}{t} \pd\] Since \(\textderiv{s}{t} = v(t),\) we have \[a(t) = v(t) \deriv{v}{s} \cma\] as requested. In words, the object's acceleration depends on its velocity at a certain time multiplied by how quickly its velocity is changing with position. This form is useful because it doesn't involve time.
EXERCISE 46
Prove that the derivative of an even function is an odd function.
SOLUTION
A function \(g\) is odd if and only if \[g(x) = -g(-x) \pd\]
A function \(f\) is even if and only if \[f(x) = f(-x) \pd\] We differentiate both sides: The left side becomes \(f'(x).\) For the right side we see, by the Chain Rule, \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} [f(-x)] &= f'(-x) \cdot \deriv{}{x} (-x) \nl &= -f'(-x) \pd \ea \] Thus, we have \[f'(x) = -f'(-x) \cma\] meaning that \(f'\) is an odd function.
EXERCISE 47
Prove that the derivative of an odd function is an even function.
SOLUTION
A function \(f\) is even if and only if \[f(x) = f(-x) \pd\]
A function \(g\) is odd if and only if \[g(x) = -g(-x) \pd\] We differentiate both sides: The left side becomes \(g'(x);\) the right side becomes, by the Chain Rule, \[ \ba -\deriv{}{x} [g(-x)] &= -g'(-x) \cdot \deriv{}{x} (-x) \nl &= g'(-x) \pd \ea \] Thus, we see \[g'(x) = g'(-x) \cma\] meaning that \(g'\) is an even function.
EXERCISE 48
Let \(f\) and \(g\) be differentiable functions such that \(g(x) \ne 0.\) By applying the Product Rule and Chain Rule, verify the Quotient Rule by differentiating \(f(x) \cdot [g(x)]^{-1}.\)
SOLUTION Using the Product Rule for \(f(x) \cdot [g(x)]^{-1}\) gives the derivative to be \[f'(x) [g(x)]^{-1} + f(x) \cdot \deriv{}{x} [g(x)]^{-1} \pd\] We use the Chain Rule to differentiate the last term, which becomes \[-[g(x)]^{-2} \cdot g'(x) \pd\] Thus, the derivative of \(f(x) \cdot [g(x)]^{-1}\) becomes \[ \ba f'(x) [g(x)]^{-1} - f(x) [g(x)]^{-2} \cdot g'(x) &= \frac{f'(x)}{g(x)} - \frac{f(x) g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2} \nl &= \frac{f'(x) g(x) - f(x) g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2} \cma \ea \] which is the Quotient Rule.

Given that \(\abs x = \sqrt{x^2},\) use the Chain Rule to show that \[\deriv{}{x} \abs x = \frac{x}{\abs x} \pd\]
SOLUTION By the Chain Rule, \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \sqrt{x^2} &= \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{x^2}} \cdot \deriv{}{x} x^2 \nl &= \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{x^2}} \cdot 2x \nl &= \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2}} \nl &= \frac{x}{\abs x} \cma \ea \] as requested.

EXERCISE 51
Let \(x\) represent the measure of an angle in radians, and let \(x \degree\) be the same measure in degrees. Show that \[\deriv{}{x} \sin x \degree = \frac{\pi}{180} \cos x \degree \pd\] (Note: \(x \degree\) can be treated as a function of \(x.\) Thus, the Chain Rule is needed to differentiate the composite function \(\sin x \degree.\))
SOLUTION To convert from degrees to radians, we multiply the number of degrees by the conversion factor \(\pi/180.\) Thus, \[ \baf &&x \degree &= x \cdot \frac{\pi}{180} \cma &\nl \laWord{so} &&\sin x \degree &= \sin \par{\frac{x \pi}{180}} \pd \eaf \] We see, by the Chain Rule, \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \sin x \degree &= \deriv{}{x} \sin \par{\frac{x \pi}{180}} \nl &= \cos \par{\frac{x \pi}{180}} \cdot \deriv{}{x} \par{\frac{x \pi}{180}} \nl &= \cos \par{\frac{x \pi}{180}} \cdot \frac{\pi}{180} \nl &= \frac{\pi}{180} \cos x \degree \cma \ea \] as requested. Because differentiating functions that involve degrees requires us to tack on the factor \(\pi/180,\) we typically perform all our calculations using radians for simplicity.
EXERCISE 52
Let \(g\) be a function defined by \[g(x) = (f \circ f \circ f)(x) \cma\] where \(f\) is a differentiable function. Determine an expression for \(g'(x).\)
SOLUTION The \(\circ\) symbols are an alternate notation for \[g(x) = f(f(f(x))) \pd\] Using the Chain Rule, we first treat \(f(f(x))\) as one function composed within the outermost layer \(f.\) We have \[ \ba g'(x) &= f'(f(f(x))) \cdot \deriv{}{x} [f(f(x))] \nl &= f'(f(f(x))) \cdot f'(f(x)) \cdot \deriv{}{x} f(x) \nl &= \boxed{f'(f(f(x))) \cdot f'(f(x)) \cdot f'(x)} \ea \]
EXERCISE 53
A block of mass \(m\) is attached to a spring, whose spring constant (a measure of stiffness) is measured by a number \(k.\) Suppose that the block is stretched a distance \(A\) away from its resting position, the equilibrium position. The spring then exerts a restoring force that pushes the block back toward equilibrium; then, the block compresses the spring by the same amplitude \(A\) (Figure 1). The block's position from equilibrium then oscillates as the spring repeats this periodic motion. The displacement, \(x,\) of the block from equilibrium as a function of time \(t\) is given by \[x = A \cos \par{t \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}} \pd \] Show that the block's acceleration is given by \[a = -\frac{k}{m} x \pd\] This equation asserts that acceleration, caused by a restoring spring force, varies directly with the distance between the block and its equilibrium position. This relationship is called Hooke's Law.
SOLUTION Recall that acceleration is the second derivative of position with respect to time (see Section 2.1). Thus, we differentiate \(x\) twice. Note that \(\sqrt{k/m}\) is a constant. The first derivative is, following the Chain Rule, \[ \ba \deriv{x}{t} &= A \cdot \deriv{}{t} \cos \par{t \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \,} \nl &= -A \sin \par{t \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \nl &= -A \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \sin \par{t \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \,} \pd \ea \] The second derivative is given by \[ \ba \derivOrder{x}{t}{2} &= -A \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \cdot \deriv{}{t} \sin \par{t \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \,} \nl &= -A \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \cos \par{t \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}} \sqrt{\frac{k}{m} \, } \nl &= -\frac{k}{m} \cdot \underbrace{A \cos \par{t \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}} \, }_{x \, (\textrm{as given})} \pd \ea \] Since \(a = \textderivOrder{x}{t}{2},\) the previous step becomes \[a = -\frac{k}{m} x \cma\] as requested.
EXERCISE 54
Let \(k\) be any constant and \(n\) be a positive integer. Consider the function \(f(x) = \sin kx.\) Write an expression for \(f^{(n)}(x)\) when \(n\) is even and when \(n\) is odd. (Hint: The number \(-1\) becomes positive when raised to an even power and negative when raised to an odd power.)
SOLUTION Recall that \(f^{(n)}(x)\) represents the \(n\)th derivative of \(f(x).\) To find the \(n\)th derivative of \(f,\) we write out some derivatives to identify a pattern. The first four derivatives of \(f\) are as follows: \[ \ba f'(x) &= k \cos kx \nl f''(x) &= -k^2 \sin kx \nl f'''(x) &= -k^3 \cos kx \nl f^{(4)}(x) &= k^4 \sin kx \pd \ea \]

Even \(n\) The second derivative of \(f\) has a negative sign, but its fourth derivative has a positive sign. Likewise, \(n = 6\) features a negative sign and \(n = 8\) has a positive sign. The pattern for this sign alternation is represented by \[(-1)^{n/2} \pd\] Thus, we conclude that \[f^{(n)}(x) = \boxed{(-1)^{n/2} k^n \sin kx \cma n \; \textrm{even}}\]

Odd \(n\) The first derivative of \(f\) has a positive sign, but its third derivative has a negative sign. If we kept repeating the pattern, then we would have a positive sign for \(n = 5\) and a negative sign for \(n = 7.\) The pattern for this sign alternation is represented by \[(-1)^{1 + (n + 1)/2} \pd\] Thus, we have \[f^{(n)}(x) = \boxed{(-1)^{1 + (n + 1)/2} k^n \sin kx \cma n \; \textrm{odd}}\]
