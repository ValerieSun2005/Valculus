9.6 Exercise Solutions: Additional Calculus with Parametric and Polar

EXERCISE 1
For
\[\ds x = 1 - 2t \cmaa y = 3t + 3 \scoll 0 \leq t \leq 1\]
find the length of the parameterized curve over the given interval.
SOLUTION Differentiating, we find \[\deriv{x}{t} = -2 \and \deriv{y}{t} = 3 \pd\] Then we get \[ \ba L &= \int_0^1 \sqrt{(-2)^2 + (3)^2} \di t \nl &= \int_0^1 \sqrt{13} \di t \nl &= \sqrt{13} \, t \intEval_0^1 \nl &= \boxed{\sqrt{13}} \approx 3.606 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 2
For
\[\ds x = 3 + 4t \cmaa y = 5 - 6t \scoll 0 \leq t \leq 2\]
find the length of the parameterized curve over the given interval.
SOLUTION We find \[\deriv{x}{t} = 4 \and \deriv{y}{t} = -6 \pd\] Then \[ \ba L &= \int_0^2 \sqrt{(4)^2 + (-6)^2} \di t \nl &= \int_0^2 \sqrt{52} \di t \nl &= \sqrt{52} \, t \intEval_0^2 \nl &= \boxed{2 \sqrt{52}} \approx 14.422 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 3
For
\[\ds x = \cos 2t \cmaa y = \sin 2t \scoll 0 \leq t \leq \pi\]
find the length of the parameterized curve over the given interval.
SOLUTION We differentiate to obtain \[\deriv{x}{t} = -2 \sin 2t \and \deriv{y}{t} = 2 \cos 2t \pd\] Thus, \[ \ba L &= \int_0^\pi \sqrt{(-2 \sin 2t)^2 + (2 \cos 2t)^2} \di t \nl &= \int_0^\pi \sqrt{4 \sin^2 2t + 4 \cos^2 2t} \di t \nl &= \int_0^\pi 2 \di t \nl &= 2 t \intEval_0^\pi \nl &= \boxed{2 \pi} \approx 6.283 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 4
For
\[\ds x = 2 \cos t \cmaa y = 2 \sin t \scoll -\frac{\pi}{2} \leq t \leq \pi\]
find the length of the parameterized curve over the given interval.
SOLUTION We have \[\deriv{x}{t} = -2 \sin t \and \deriv{y}{t} = 2 \cos t \pd\] Thus, \[ \ba L &= \int_{-\pi/2}^\pi \sqrt{(-2 \sin t)^2 + (2 \cos t)^2} \di t \nl &= \int_{-\pi/2}^\pi \sqrt{4 \sin^2 t + 4 \cos^2 t} \di t \nl &= \int_{-\pi/2}^\pi 2 \di t \nl &= 2 t \intEval_{-\pi/2}^\pi \nl &= \boxed{3 \pi} \approx 9.425 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 5

\[\ds x = 3 \sin 4t \cmaa y = 3 \cos 4t \scoll 0 \leq t \leq \frac{\pi}{2}\]
find the length of the parameterized curve over the given interval.
SOLUTION We find \[\deriv{x}{t} = 12 \cos 4t \and \deriv{y}{t} = -12 \sin 4t \pd\] Then \[ \ba L &= \int_0^{\pi/2} \sqrt{(12 \cos 4t)^2 + (-12 \sin 4t)^2} \di t \nl &= \int_0^{\pi/2} \sqrt{144 \cos^2 4t + 144 \sin^2 4t} \di t \nl &= \int_0^{\pi/2} 12 \di t \nl &= 12 t \intEval_0^{\pi/2} \nl &= \boxed{6 \pi} \approx 18.850 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 6

\[\ds x = 2t^{7/2} \cmaa y = 4t^{7/2} - 5 \scoll 0 \leq t \leq 1\]

EXERCISE 11
For
\[\ds r = 4 \cos \theta \cmaa 0 \leq \theta \leq \frac{\pi}{3}\]
calculate the exact arc length of the polar curve, which does not cross itself on the given interval.
SOLUTION We get \(\textderiv{r}{\theta} = -4 \sin \theta\) and \[r^2 = 16 \cos^2 \theta \and \par{\deriv{r}{\theta}}^2 = 16 \sin^2 \theta \pd\] The curve's arc length is therefore \[ \ba L &= \int_0^{\pi/3} \sqrt{16 \cos^2 \theta + 16 \sin^2 \theta} \di \theta \nl &= \int_0^{\pi/3} \sqrt{16 (\cos^2 \theta + \sin^2 \theta)} \di \theta \nl &= \int_0^{\pi/3} 4 \di \theta \nl &= 4 \theta \intEval_0^{\pi/3} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{4 \pi}{3}} \approx 4.189 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 12
For
\[r = e^{4 \theta} \cmaa -1 \leq \theta \leq 3\]
calculate the exact arc length of the polar curve, which does not cross itself on the given interval.
SOLUTION Note that \(\textderiv{r}{\theta} = 4 e^{4 \theta},\) which is continuous, and \[r^2 = e^{8 \theta} \and \par{\deriv{r}{\theta}}^2 = 16 e^{8 \theta} \pd\] The spiral does not cross itself on \([-1, 3],\) so the arc length is \[ \ba L &= \int_{-1}^3 \sqrt{e^{8 \theta} + 16 e^{8 \theta}} \di \theta \nl &= \int_{-1}^3 \sqrt{17 e^{8 \theta}} \di \theta \nl &= \sqrt{17} \int_{-1}^3 e^{4 \theta} \di \theta \nl &= \sqrt{17} \par{\tfrac{1}{4} e^{4 \theta}} \intEval_{-1}^3 \nl &= \boxed{\frac{\sqrt{17}}{4} \par{e^{12} - e^{-4}}} \approx 167763.780 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 13
For
\[\ds r = \cos^2 \frac{\theta}{2} \cmaa 0 \leq \theta \leq 2 \pi\]
calculate the exact arc length of the polar curve, which does not cross itself on the given interval.
SOLUTION We have \(\textderiv{r}{\theta} = -\cos(\theta/2) \sin(\theta/2),\) which is continuous, and \[r^2 = \cos^4 \frac{\theta}{2} \and \par{\deriv{r}{\theta}}^2 = \cos^2 \frac{\theta}{2} \sin^2 \frac{\theta}{2} \pd\] Since the graph does not cross itself on \([0, 2 \pi],\) the arc length on this interval is \[ \ba L &= \int_0^{2 \pi} \sqrt{\cos^4 \frac{\theta}{2} + \cos^2 \frac{\theta}{2} \sin^2 \frac{\theta}{2}} \di \theta \nl &= \int_0^{2 \pi} \sqrt{\cos^2 \frac{\theta}{2} \par{\cos^2 \frac{\theta}{2} + \sin^2 \frac{\theta}{2}}} \di \theta \nl &= \int_0^{2 \pi} \sqrt{\cos^2 \frac{\theta}{2}} \di \theta \nl &= \int_0^{2 \pi} \abs{\cos \frac{\theta}{2}} \di \theta \nl &= \int_0^{\pi} \cos \frac{\theta}{2} \di \theta - \int_{\pi}^{2 \pi} \cos \frac{\theta}{2} \di \theta \nl &= 2 \sin \frac{\theta}{2} \intEval_0^\pi - 2 \sin \frac{\theta}{2} \intEval_\pi^{2 \pi} \nl &= (2 - 0) - (0 - 2) = \boxed 4 \ea \]
EXERCISE 14
For
\[\ds r = \sin^4 \frac{\theta}{4} \cmaa 0 \leq \theta \leq 4 \pi\]
calculate the exact arc length of the polar curve, which does not cross itself on the given interval.
SOLUTION We have \(\textderiv{r}{\theta} = \sin^3 (\theta/4) \cos(\theta/4),\) which is continuous, and \[r^2 = \sin^8 \frac{\theta}{4} \and \par{\deriv{r}{\theta}}^2 = \sin^6 \frac{\theta}{4} \cos^2 \frac{\theta}{4} \pd\] Since the graph does not cross itself on \([0, 4 \pi],\) the arc length on this interval is \[ \ba L &= \int_0^{4 \pi} \sqrt{\sin^8 \frac{\theta}{4} + \sin^6 \frac{\theta}{4} \cos^2 \frac{\theta}{4}} \di \theta \nl &= \int_0^{4 \pi} \sqrt{\sin^6 \frac{\theta}{4} \par{\sin^2 \frac{\theta}{2} + \cos^2 \frac{\theta}{2}}} \di \theta \nl &= \int_0^{4 \pi} \sqrt{\sin^6 \frac{\theta}{4}} \di \theta \nl &= \int_0^{4 \pi} \abs{\sin^3 \frac{\theta}{4}} \di \theta \nl &= \int_0^{4 \pi} \sin^3 \frac{\theta}{4} \di \theta \nl &= \int_0^{4 \pi} \sin \frac{\theta}{4} \par{1 - \cos^2 \frac{\theta}{4}} \di \theta \pd \ea \] Substituting \(u = \cos(\theta/4),\) we have \(\dd u = -\tfrac{1}{4} \sin(\theta/4) \di \theta.\) When \(\theta = 0,\) \(u = 1;\) when \(\theta = 4 \pi,\) \(u = -1.\) We therefore have \[ \ba L &= -4 \int_1^{-1} \par{1 - u^2} \di u \nl &= 4 \int_{-1}^1 \par{1 - u^2} \di u \nl &= 8 \int_0^1 \par{1 - u^2} \di u \nl &= 8 \par{u - \tfrac{1}{3} u^3} \intEval_0^1 = \boxed{\tfrac{16}{3}} \ea \] [We simplified the calculation by observing that \((1 - u^2)\) is an even function.]
EXERCISE 15
For
\[\ds r = \theta \cmaa 0 \leq \theta \leq \frac{\pi}{2}\]
calculate the exact arc length of the polar curve, which does not cross itself on the given interval.
SOLUTION We have \(\textderiv{r}{\theta} = 1,\) which is continuous, and \[ r^2 = \theta^2 \and \par{\deriv{r}{\theta}}^2 = 1 \pd \] Because the spiral does not cross itself on \([0, \pi/2],\) its arc length is \[ \ba L &= \int_0^{\pi/2} \sqrt{\theta^2 + 1} \di \theta \pd \ea \] Performing the trigonometric substitution \(\theta = \tan u,\) we have \(\dd \theta = \sec^2 u \di u.\) When \(\theta = 0,\) \(u = 0;\) when \(\theta = \pi/2,\) \(u = \atan (\pi/2).\) Let \(\beta = \atan(\pi/2)\) for readability. Also, \(\sqrt{\theta^2 + 1}\) becomes \[\sqrt{(\tan u)^2 + 1} = \sqrt{\sec^2 u} = \abs{\sec u} = \sec u \cma\] where the absolute value bars are safe to drop because \(\sec u \gt 0\) on \([0, \beta].\) We therefore have \[ \ba L &= \int_0^\beta (\sec u) (\sec^2 u) \di u \nl &= \int_0^\beta \sec^3 u \di u \nl &= \par{\tfrac{1}{2} \sec u \tan u + \tfrac{1}{2} \ln \abs{\sec u + \tan u}} \intEval_0^{\beta} \nl &= \tfrac{1}{2} \sec \beta \tan \beta + \tfrac{1}{2} \ln(\sec \beta + \tan \beta) \pd \ea \] (See Example 6.2-12 to understand the antidifferentiation of \(\sec^3 u.\)) Because \(\beta = \atan(\pi/2),\) we have \(\tan \beta\) \(= \pi/2.\) Since \(\tan^2 \beta + 1 = \sec^2 \beta,\) we see \[\sec \beta = \sqrt{1 + \tan^2 \beta} = \sqrt{1 + \par{\frac{\pi}{2}}^2} = \sqrt{1 + \frac{\pi^2}{4}} \pd\] We therefore have \[ \ba L &= \boxed{\tfrac{1}{2} \sqrt{1 + \frac{\pi^2}{4}} \par{\frac{\pi}{2}} + \tfrac{1}{2} \ln \par{\sqrt{1 + \frac{\pi^2}{4}} + \frac{\pi}{2}}} \nl &\approx 2.079 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 16
For
\(\ds x = 8 \sin 2t \cmaa y = 8 \cos 2t \cmaa \frac{\pi}{8} \leq t \leq \frac{\pi}{4} \scoll \abtAxis{x}\)
find the exact surface area of the solid generated by rotating the curve about the specified axis.
SOLUTION The curve does not cross itself on \([\pi/8, \pi/4].\) Differentiating gives \[\deriv{x}{t} = 16 \cos 2t \and \deriv{y}{t} = -16 \sin 2t \cma\] which are both continuous. The differential arc length segment is then \[ \ba \dd s &= \sqrt{(16 \cos 2t)^2 + (-16 \sin 2t)^2} \di t \nl &= \sqrt{16^2 (\cos^2 2t + \sin^2 2t)} \di t \nl &= 16 \di t \pd \ea \] The surface area is then \(S = \int_\alpha^\beta 2 \pi y \di s,\) or \[ \ba S &= \int_{\pi/8}^{\pi/4} 2 \pi (8 \cos 2t) (16) \di t \nl &= 256 \pi \int_{\pi/8}^{\pi/4} \cos 2t \di t \nl &= 128 \pi \sin 2t \intEval_{\pi/8}^{\pi/4} \nl &= 128 \pi \par{1 - \frac{\sqrt 2}{2}} \nl &= \boxed{64 \pi \par{2 - \sqrt 2}} \approx 117.779 \pd \ea \]

\(\ds x = \tfrac{1}{2} e^{4t} + 2 \cmaa y = e^{4t} + 8 \cmaa 0 \leq t \leq 3 \scoll \abtAxis y\)

EXERCISE 20
For
\(\ds x = \cos^2 \theta \cmaa y = \sin^2 \theta \cmaa 0 \leq \theta \leq \frac{\pi}{2} \scoll \abtAxis{y}\)
find the exact surface area of the solid generated by rotating the curve about the specified axis.
SOLUTION The curve does not cross itself on \([0, \pi/2].\) Differentiating gives \[\deriv{x}{\theta} = -2 \cos \theta \sin \theta \and \deriv{y}{\theta} = 2 \sin \theta \cos \theta \cma\] which are both continuous. The differential arc length segment is then \[ \ba \dd s &= \sqrt{(-2 \cos \theta \sin \theta)^2 + (2 \sin \theta \cos \theta)^2} \di \theta \nl &= \sqrt{4 \sin^2 \theta \cos^2 \theta + 4 \sin^2 \theta \cos^2 \theta} \di \theta \nl &= \sqrt{8} \abs{\sin \theta \cos \theta} \di \theta \nl &= \sqrt{8} \sin \theta \cos \theta \di \theta \cma \ea \] where the absolute value bars are dropped because both sine and cosine are positive on \((0, \pi/2).\) The surface area is then \(S = \int_\alpha^\beta 2 \pi y \di s,\) or \[ \ba S &= \int_0^{\pi/2} 2 \pi (\sin^2 \theta) (\sqrt 8 \sin \theta \cos \theta) \di \theta \nl &= 2 \pi \sqrt 8 \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^3 \theta \cos \theta \di \theta \pd \ea \] Substituting \(u = \sin \theta,\) we have \(\dd u = \cos \theta \di \theta.\) When \(\theta = 0,\) \(u = 0;\) when \(\theta = \pi/2,\) \(u = 1.\) The integral therefore becomes \[ \ba S &= 2 \pi \sqrt 8 \int_0^1 u^3 \di u \nl &= \frac{\pi \sqrt 8}{2} u^4 \intEval_0^1 \nl &= \boxed{\frac{\pi \sqrt 8}{2}} \approx 4.443 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 21

\(\ds x = \cos^3 \theta \cmaa y = \sin^3 \theta \cmaa 0 \leq \theta \leq \frac{\pi}{2} \scoll \abtAxis{y}\)
find the exact surface area of the solid generated by rotating the curve about the specified axis.
SOLUTION In Example 4 we found the differential arc length segment \[\dd s = 3 \cos \theta \sin \theta \di \theta \pd\] But when we revolve around the \(y\)-axis, the surface area is \[ \ba S &= \int_0^{\pi/2} 2 \pi (\cos^3 \theta) (3 \cos \theta \sin \theta) \di \theta \nl &= 6 \pi \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin \theta \cos^4 \theta \di \theta \pd \ea \] Substituting \(u = \cos \theta\) gives \(\dd u = -\sin \theta \di \theta.\) When \(\theta = 0,\) \(u = 1;\) when \(\theta = \pi/2,\) \(u = 0.\) So we have \[ \ba S &= -6 \pi \int_{1}^0 u^4 \di u \nl &= 6 \pi \int_0^1 u^4 \di u \nl &= \frac{6 \pi}{5} u^5 \intEval_0^1 \nl &= \boxed{\frac{6 \pi}{5}} \approx 4.443 \pd \ea \] Due to symmetry, the surface area is the same if we revolve around the \(x\)-axis, as in Example 4.
EXERCISE 22
For
\(\ds r = \cos \theta \scoll \abtAxis{y}\)
find the exact surface area of the solid generated by rotating the polar curve about the specified axis.
SOLUTION The entire circle \(r = \cos \theta\) is traced out over \(0 \leq \theta \leq \pi.\) Observe that \(\textderiv{r}{\theta} = -\sin \theta,\) which is continuous, and \[r^2 = \cos^2 \theta \and \par{\deriv{r}{\theta}}^2 = \sin^2 \theta \pd\] Hence, the differential arc length segment is \[\dd s = \sqrt{\cos^2 \theta + \sin^2 \theta} \di \theta = \dd \theta \pd\] The surface area of revolution is therefore \(S = \int_\alpha^\beta 2 \pi r \cos \theta \di s,\) that is, \[ \ba S &= \int_0^{\pi} 2 \pi (\cos \theta) (\cos \theta) \di \theta \nl &= 2 \pi \int_0^{\pi} \cos^2 \theta \di \theta \pd \ea \] Using the power-reduction formula for cosine, \(\cos^2 \theta = \tfrac{1}{2} (1 + \cos 2 \theta),\) gives \[ \ba S &= 2 \pi \int_0^{\pi} \tfrac{1}{2} (1 + \cos 2 \theta) \di \theta \nl &= \pi \par{\theta + \tfrac{1}{2} \sin 2 \theta} \intEval_0^{\pi} \nl &= \boxed{\pi^2} \approx 9.870 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 23
For
\(\ds r = 1 + \cos \theta \cmaa 0 \leq \theta \leq \pi \scoll \abtAxis{x}\)
find the exact surface area of the solid generated by rotating the polar curve about the specified axis.
SOLUTION This curve is the top half of a horizontal cardioid. Observe that \(\textderiv{r}{\theta} = -\sin \theta,\) which is continuous, and \[r^2 = 1 + 2 \cos \theta + \cos^2 \theta \and \par{\deriv{r}{\theta}}^2 = \sin^2 \theta \pd\] Hence, the differential arc length segment is \[ \ba \dd s &= \sqrt{1 + 2 \cos \theta + \cos^2 \theta + \sin^2 \theta} \di \theta \nl &= \sqrt{2 + 2 \cos \theta} \di \theta \pd \ea \] The surface area of revolution is therefore \(S = \int_\alpha^\beta 2 \pi r \sin \theta \di s,\) that is, \[ S = 2 \pi \int_0^{\pi} (1 + \cos \theta) (\sin \theta) \sqrt{2 + 2 \cos \theta} \di \theta \pd \] Substituting \(u = 2 + 2 \cos \theta,\) we get \(1 + \cos \theta\) \(= u/2\) and \(\dd u = -2 \sin \theta \di \theta.\) When \(\theta = 0,\) \(u = 4;\) when \(\theta = \pi,\) \(u = 0.\) Then we get \[ \ba S &= - \pi \int_4^0 \par{\frac{u}{2}} \sqrt u \di u \nl &= \frac{\pi}{2} \int_0^4 u^{3/2} \di u \nl &= \frac{\pi}{5} u^{5/2} \intEval_0^4 \nl &= \boxed{\frac{32 \pi}{5}} \approx 20.106 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 24
For
\(\ds r = 3 - 3 \sin \theta \cmaa -\frac{\pi}{2} \leq \theta \leq \frac{\pi}{2} \scoll \abtAxis{y}\)
