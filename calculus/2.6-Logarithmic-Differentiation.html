2.6: Logarithmic Differentiation

In Section 2.5, we introduced the method of Implicit Differentiation. We now present an application: Logarithmic Differentiation. If we wish to differentiate an expression that contains many powers, products, and quotients, then it is strenuous to use solely the differentiation rules we have learned so far. But logarithms transform products and quotients into sums and differences, and they convert powers into multiples. Thus, Logarithmic Differentiation is an alternate method that works well for differentiating combinations of powers, multiples, and quotients. The properties of logarithms are shown below.

PROPERTIES OF LOGARITHMS
For all real \(n\) and positive \(a\) and \(b,\)
  1. \(\log(ab) = \log a + \log b.\)
  2. \(\ds \log \left(\frac{a}{b}\right) = \log a - \log b.\)
  3. \(\log \left(a^n\right) = n \log a.\)

To perform Logarithmic Differentiation on \(y = f(x),\) we take the natural logarithm of both sides. Doing so transforms the products, quotients, and powers in \(f(x)\) into sums, differences, and multiples, respectivelyâ€”producing a form that is easier to differentiate.

LOGARITHMIC DIFFERENTIATION
Consider the function \(y = f(x).\) To find \(\textDeriv{y}{x}\) using Logarithmic Differentiation,
  1. Take the natural logarithm of both sides to get \begin{equation} \label{eq:step-1} \ln y = \ln[f(x)] \period \end{equation} Use logarithm properties to transform the products and quotients in \(f(x)\) into sums and differences, respectively, of logarithms.
  2. Perform Implicit Differentiation: Differentiate both sides of \(\eqRef{eq:step-1}\) with respect to \(x,\) using the Chain Rule to differentiate expressions of \(y.\) The resulting left-hand expression is \(\frac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{x}.\)
  3. Solve for \(\textDeriv{y}{x}\) by multiplying both sides by \(y = f(x).\)
EXAMPLE 1
If \(y = e^{\sin x},\) then find \(\textDeriv{y}{x}.\)
It's possible to use the Chain Rule to find \(\textDeriv{y}{x},\) but using logarithms is an alternate method. Taking the natural logarithm of both sides of \(y = e^{\sin x}\) and using Property (c), we get \[ \ba \ln y &= \ln \left(e^{\sin x}\right) \nonumber \nl &= \sin(x) \ln e \period \nonumber \nl \ea \] Because \(\ln e = 1,\) we have \begin{equation} \label{eq:ex-1-lny} \ln y = \sin x \period \end{equation} We perform Implicit Differentiation with respect to \(x\) to solve for \(\textDeriv{y}{x}.\) Differentiating both sides of \(\eqRefer{eq:ex-1-lny}\) gives \[\tfrac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{x} = \cos x \period\] We multiply both sides by \(y = e^{\sin x}\) to isolate \(\textDeriv{y}{x} \col\) \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= \boxed{e^{\sin x} \cos x} \ea \]
EXAMPLE 2
If \(y = x \sin^2 x,\) then find \(\textDeriv{y}{x}.\)
We take the natural logarithm of both sides and use logarithm properties (a) and (c) to get \[ \ba \ln y &= \ln \left[x (\sin x)^2 \right] \nl &= \ln x + 2 \ln(\sin x) \period \ea \] We differentiate both sides gives with respect to \(x.\) We treat \(y\) as a function of \(x,\) so we use the Chain Rule: \[ \ba \tfrac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{1}{x} + \frac{2 \cos x}{\sin x} \period \ea \] At this point we solve for \(\textDeriv{y}{x}.\) Multiplying both sides by \(y = x \sin^2 x\) yields \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= x \sin^2 x \left[\frac{1}{x} + \frac{2 \cos x}{\sin x}\right] \nl &= \boxed{\sin^2 x + 2 x \cos x \sin x} \ea \]
EXAMPLE 3
Find \(\textDeriv{y}{x}\) if \[y = \sqrt{\frac{x^2 \sin x}{e^x}} \pd \]
We take the natural logarithm of both sides: \begin{equation} \ln y = \ln \sqrt{\frac{x^2 \sin x}{e^x}} \pd \label{eq:sqrt} \end{equation} The square root of a value is equivalent to raising the value to the power of \(1/2.\) Thus, logarithm properties allow us to rewrite the right side of \(\eqref{eq:sqrt}\) as \[ \ba \tfrac{1}{2} \ln \frac{x^2 \sin x}{e^x} &= \tfrac{1}{2} \ln \left[x^2 \sin x\right] - \tfrac{1}{2} \ln(e^x) \nl &= \tfrac{1}{2} \ln(x^2) + \tfrac{1}{2} \ln(\sin x) - \tfrac{1}{2} x \nl &= \ln x + \tfrac{1}{2} \ln(\sin x) - \tfrac{1}{2} x \pd \ea \] Hence, \(\eqRefer{eq:sqrt}\) becomes \[\ln y = \ln x + \tfrac{1}{2} \ln(\sin x) - \tfrac{1}{2} x \pd\] We use Implicit Differentiation with respect to \(x\) to find \(\textDeriv{y}{x} \col\) \[ \ba \tfrac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{1}{x} + \frac{\cos x}{2 \sin x} - \frac{1}{2} \nl \deriv{y}{x} &= y \left[\frac{1}{x} + \frac{\cos x}{2 \sin x} - \frac{1}{2}\right] \nl &= \boxed{\sqrt{\frac{x^2 \sin x}{e^x}} \left[\frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{2} \cot x - \frac{1}{2}\right]} \ea \]

Logarithmic Differentiation is an application of Implicit Differentiation that allows for simpler differentiation of products, quotients, and powers of functions. The properties of logarithms are as follows: For all real \(n\) and positive \(a\) and \(b,\)

  1. \(\log(ab) = \log a + \log b.\)
  2. \(\ds \log \left(\frac{a}{b}\right) = \log a - \log b.\)
  3. \(\log \left(a^n\right) = n \log a.\)

If \(y = f(x),\) then to find \(\textDeriv{y}{x}\) using Logarithmic Differentiation,

  1. Take the natural logarithm of both sides to get \begin{equation*} \ln y = \ln[f(x)] \period \end{equation*} Use logarithm properties to transform the products and quotients in \(f(x)\) into sums and differences, respectively, of logarithms.
  2. Perform Implicit Differentiation: Differentiate both sides of \(\eqRefer{eq:step-1}\) with respect to \(x,\) using the Chain Rule to differentiate expressions with \(y.\) The resulting left-hand expression is \(\frac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{x}.\)
  3. Solve for \(\textDeriv{y}{x}\) by multiplying both sides by \(y = f(x).\)
