1.6 — Limits with Infinity

This section expands our abilities with limits: We see what happens to a function as we let its input go toward \(\infty\) or \(-\infty.\) We also provide discussions of asymptotes and how they relate to infinity. This section discusses the following topics:

  • Limits of Rational Functions at Infinity
  • Horizontal Asymptotes and Vertical Asymptotes
  • Limits at Vertical Asymptotes
  • Precise Definitions of Limits with Infinity

Let's immediately begin by distinguishing terminology: A limit at infinity is the behavior of a function \(f(x)\) as \(x \to \infty\) or \(x \to -\infty.\) A limit is infinite if \(f(x) \to \infty\) or \(f(x) \to -\infty\) as \(x\) approaches some value or changes without bound. When we say a limit equals infinity, it is implied that the limit does not exist; we are elaborating on how it fails to exist. Doing so is useful since it expands our toolkit for modeling the behavior of functions.

The expression \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L\) means that as \(x\) increases without bound, \(f(x)\) is made closer and closer to \(L.\) We can read the statement as "the limit of \(f(x),\) as \(x\) approaches infinity, is \(L.\)" Likewise, \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = L\) means that \(f(x)\) is made closer and closer to \(L\) as \(x\) decreases without bound. At the end of this section, we provide formal definitions for limits at infinity and infinite limits.

Limits of Rational Functions at Infinity

Consider the family of limits \begin{equation*} L = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \comma g(x) \ne 0 \pd \end{equation*} If both \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) tend to \(\pm \infty\) as \(x\) approaches \(a,\) then \(L\) is of the indeterminate form \(\indInfty.\) As an example, the limit \(\lim_{x \to \infty} x^2/x\) is of the indeterminate form \(\indInfty\) since as \(x \to \infty,\) \[x^2 \to \infty \and x \to \infty \pd\] The limit cannot be evaluated by a direct substitution; more analysis is needed to determine its value.

INDETERMINATE FORM \(\indInfty\)
Consider the limit \[L = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \comma g(x) \ne 0 \pd\] If both \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) approach \(\pm \infty\) as \(x\) approaches \(a,\) then \(L\) is in the indeterminate form \(\indInfty.\)

Limits of rational functions at infinity are very commonly in the indeterminate form \(\indInfty.\) Consider the rational function \[f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 4x + 9}{x^3 - 2} \pd \] To find \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x),\) we cannot replace \(x\) with \(\infty\) and obtain a conclusive result. Instead, let's divide the numerator and denominator by the highest-degree term of \(f(x)\)—namely, \(x^3,\) found in the denominator. We then get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2 - 4x + 9}{x^3 - 2} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2 - 4x + 9}{x^3 - 2} \cdot \frac{1/x^3}{1/x^3} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1/x - 4/x^2 + 9/x^3}{1 - 2/x^3} \pd \ea \] As \(x\) grows without bound, each term in the numerator approaches \(0.\) In the denominator, the term \(2/x^3\) also approaches \(0,\) but the \(1\) remains constant. Hence, \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = 0/1\) \(= 0.\) Consequently, as \(x\) approaches infinity, \(f(x)\) approaches \(0.\)

It is important to note that \[f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 4x + 9}{x^3 - 2} \ne \frac{x^2}{x^3} \pd \] Instead, the behavior of \(f(x)\) is equivalent to the behavior of \(x^2/x^3\) \(= 1/x\) as \(x \to \infty\) or as \(x \to -\infty.\)

But we have a shortcut: As \(x\) grows infinitely large, only the highest-degree terms in the numerator and denominator are significant. In this case, as \(x\) grows to infinity, the behavior of \(f(x)\) matches the behavior of \(x^2/x^3 = 1/x,\) which decays to \(0.\) Therefore, \[\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2 - 4x + 9}{x^3 - 2} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2}{x^3} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{x} = 0 \pd\] Likewise, as \(x\) approaches negative infinity we also take the limit of the highest-degree terms: \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{x^2 - 4x + 9}{x^3 - 2} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{x^2}{x^3} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{1}{x} = 0 \pd\]

The end behavior of a function \(f\) describes what happens to \(f(x)\) as \(x \to \infty\) or \(x \to -\infty.\) For example, to describe the end behavior of the preceding rational function, we say \((x^2 - 4x + 9)/(x^3 - 2) \to 0\) as \(x \to \infty\) and as \(x \to -\infty.\) (See Figure 1.)

FINDING LIMITS OF RATIONAL EXPRESSIONS
To determine the limit of a rational expression, take the limit of only the highest-degree terms in the numerator and denominator. Alternatively, dividing each term by the highest-degree term of the rational expression and taking the limit yield the same result.

We now proceed with the following generalization. Suppose \(P\) and \(Q\) are polynomials, and let \(L = \lim_{x \to \infty} P(x)/Q(x).\) The value of \(L\) depends on the degrees of \(P\) and \(Q.\) This ratio represents a battle between \(P\) and \(Q \col\) If \(P\) grows faster than \(Q,\) then the value of the ratio is pulled to infinity, so \(L = \pm \infty.\) But if \(Q\) grows faster than \(P,\) then \(L = 0\) since the denominator successfully contracts the fraction to \(0.\) Lastly, the ratio \(P(x)/Q(x)\) could settle to a constant \(C,\) equal to the coefficient of the highest-degree term of \(P\) divided by the coefficient of the highest-degree term of \(Q.\)

RATIONAL FUNCTIONS AT INFINITY
Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be polynomials. Consider the limit \[L = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} \pd\] Let \(\deg(P)\) denote the degree of \(P\) and \(\deg(Q)\) denote the degree of \(Q.\)
  1. If \(\deg(P) \gt \deg(Q),\) then \(L = \pm \infty.\)
  2. If \(\deg(P) = \deg(Q),\) then \(L = C,\) where \(C\) is the coefficient of the highest-degree term of \(P\) divided by the coefficient of the highest-degree term of \(Q.\)
  3. If \(\deg(P) \lt \deg(Q),\) then \(L = 0.\)
EXAMPLE 1
Evaluate each limit.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x^3 - 4x^2 + 16}{5x^3 + 8x - 2} \)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{-8x^2 + \frac{3}{4}x + 16}{5x - 2}\)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{8 + 3x^2 - x^9}{10x^9} \)
  1. We consider only the highest-degree terms on the numerator and denominator. We then find \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x^3 - 4x^2 + 16}{5x^3 + 8x - 2} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x^3}{5x^3} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3}{5} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{3}{5}} \ea \]
  2. The highest-degree term on the numerator is \(-8x^2,\) while the highest-degree term on the denominator is \(5x.\) Therefore, \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{-8x^2 + \frac{3}{4}x + 16}{5x - 2} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{-8x^2}{5x} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{-8x}{5} \nl &= \boxed{- \infty} \ea \] Since the limit is unbounded, it's also correct to say that the limit does not exist. This result makes sense: The numerator's degree is greater than the denominator's degree. Thus, as \(x \to \infty\) the numerator overpowers the denominator, successfully forcing the ratio toward negative infinity.
  3. In taking the limit, we ignore the lower-degree terms in the numerator and denominator: \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{8 + 3x^2 - x^9}{10x^9} &= \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{-x^9}{10x^9} \nl &= \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{-1}{10} \nl &= \boxed{-\frac{1}{10}} \ea \]
EXAMPLE 2
Let \[g(x) = \frac{\sqrt{x^6 + 3x^3 + x}}{5x^3 + x^2 + 7} \pd\] Find \(\lim_{x \to \infty} g(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} g(x).\)
When you take the square root of a term, remember to include absolute value bars; for example, \(\sqrt{x^6}\) \(= \abs{x^3}.\)

To find limits at infinity of \(g(x),\) we consider only the highest-degree terms of \(g \col\) \[\frac{\sqrt{x^6}}{5x^3} = \frac{\abs{x^3}}{5x^3} \pd \] In other words, as \(x\) approaches \(\pm \infty,\) the behavior of \(g(x)\) matches the behavior of \(\abs{x^3}/5x^3. \) Then \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} g(x) &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\abs{x^3}}{5x^3} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^3}{5x^3} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{5} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{1}{5}} \ea \]

Similarly, \[ \lim_{x \to -\infty} g(x) = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{\abs{x^3}}{5x^3} \pd \] Because \(x \to -\infty,\) we assume \(x \lt 0.\) Accordingly, we rewrite \(\abs{x^3}\) as \(-x^3\) (because \(-x^3 \gt 0\) if \(x\) is negative). The limit becomes \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{-x^3}{5x^3} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{-1}{5} = \boxed{-\frac{1}{5}}\]

Horizontal Asymptotes and Vertical Asymptotes

From a precalculus calculus, you likely are familiar with horizontal asymptotes. A horizontal asymptote of a function \(f\) is the line that the curve \(y = f(x)\) approaches as \(x \to \infty\) or \(x \to -\infty.\) We now formally define horizontal asymptotes using limits: The line \(y = L\) is called a horizontal asymptote of the graph of \(y = f(x)\) if either \[\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L \or \lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = L \pd\] A function may have zero, one, or two horizontal asymptotes. The function \(f\) has no horizontal asymptotes if \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x)\) both fail to exist (for example, both limits equal infinity). Figure 2 shows the graph of a function \(f\) with one horizontal asymptote, \(y = L.\) Lastly, \(f\) has two horizontal asymptotes if \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x)\) equal different values. An example is \(f(x) = \tan^{-1} x,\) whose graph has horizontal asymptotes of \(y = -\pi/2\) and \(y = \pi/2.\)

HORIZONTAL ASYMPTOTES
The line \(y = L\) is a horizontal asymptote of the graph of \(y = f(x)\) if either \[\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L \or \lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = L \pd\] A function may have zero, one, or two horizontal asymptotes.

A vertical asymptote of \(f\) is a vertical line along which the curve \(y = f(x)\) becomes unbounded. Specifically, \(f\) has a vertical asymptote at \(p\) if either \[\lim_{x \to p^-} f(x) = \pm \infty \or \lim_{x \to p^+} f(x) = \pm \infty \pd\] See Figure 3: the graph of \(y = 1/(x - p)\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = p\) because \(\lim_{x \to p^-} f(x) = -\infty\) and \(\lim_{x \to p^+} f(x) = \infty.\) Unlike horizontal asymptotes, there is no limit to how many vertical asymptotes a function can have.

VERTICAL ASYMPTOTES
The line \(x = p\) is a vertical asymptote of \(f\) if either \[\lim_{x \to p^-} f(x) = \pm \infty \or \lim_{x \to p^+} f(x) = \pm \infty \pd\]
EXAMPLE 3
Let \(f(x) = (2x + 3)/(8 - 3x).\) Find all horizontal asymptotes and vertical asymptotes to the curve \(y = f(x).\)

To find any horizontal asymptotes to \(f,\) we evaluate \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x).\) In taking these limits, we consider only the highest-degree terms of \(f(x) \col\) \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{2x}{-3x} = -\frac{2}{3} \nl \lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) &= \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{2x}{-3x} = - \frac{2}{3} \pd \ea \] Both limits are the same, so the graph of \(f\) has only one horizontal asymptote: \(\boxed{y = -2/3}.\)

A rational function has a vertical asymptote when its denominator is \(0\) but its numerator isn't \(0.\) Equating the denominator of \(f(x)\) to \(0\) gives \(x = 8/3.\) At this point the numerator is nonzero. Thus, \(\boxed{x = 8/3}\) is the only vertical asymptote of \(f.\) (See Figure 4.)

Limits at Vertical Asymptotes

If a function \(f(x)\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = p,\) then \(f\) approaches either \(\infty\) or \(-\infty\) on either side of the asymptote. Our goal is to establish whether a one-sided limit at a vertical asymptote equals \(\infty\) or \(-\infty.\)

As an example, the function \[f(x) = \frac{1}{x - p} \pd\] The graph of \(y = f(x)\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = p\) (because at \(x = p,\) the denominator equals \(0\) but the numerator is nonzero). Thus, the curve approaches \(-\infty\) or \(\infty\) on either side of the asymptote. We compute one-sided limits of \(f\) at \(x = p\)—specifically, \(\lim_{x \to p^-} 1/(x - p)\) and \(\lim_{x \to p^+} 1/(x - p).\) We perform substitutions to convert limits at asymptotes to simpler limits. Letting \(t = x - p,\) we see \(t \to 0^-\) as \(x \to p^-.\) Hence, \[\lim_{x \to p^-} \frac{1}{x - p} = \lim_{t \to 0^-} \frac{1}{t} = -\infty \pd\] Likewise, using the same substitution \(t = x - p\) shows \[\lim_{x \to p^+} \frac{1}{x - p} = \lim_{t \to 0^+} \frac{1}{t} = \infty \pd\] Therefore, \(f\) runs to \(-\infty\) from the left of the asymptote \(x = p\) and approaches \(\infty\) from the right, as we previously saw in Figure 3.

An easier method is as follows. To find the behavior of a function \(f(x)\) at a vertical asymptote \(x = p,\) we analyze the sign of \(f\) on both sides of the asymptote: If \(f \gt 0\) on one side of the asymptote, then \(f\) approaches \(\infty\) on that side. But if \(f \lt 0\) on one side of the asymptote, then on that side \(f\) tends to \(-\infty.\)

Consider the behavior of \(f(x) = 1/(x - p)\) from the left of the asymptote \(x = p.\) For \(x \lt p,\) the numerator is positive but the denominator \((x - p)\) is negative. So the sign of \(f\) as \(x \to p^-\) is \[\frac{(+)}{(-)} = (-) \pd\] Thus, \(\lim_{x \to p^-} f(x)\) must be \(-\infty.\) Conversely, from the right of \(x = p\) the sign of \(f\) is \[\frac{(+)}{(+)} = (+) \pd\] It follows that \(\lim_{x \to p^+} f(x) = \infty.\)

LIMITS AT VERTICAL ASYMPTOTES
Consider a function \(f(x)\) whose graph has a vertical asymptote at \(x = p.\)
  1. If \(f(x) \lt 0\) as \(x \to p^-,\) then \(\lim_{x \to p^-} f(x) = -\infty.\)
  2. If \(f(x) \gt 0\) as \(x \to p^+,\) then \(\lim_{x \to p^+} f(x) = \infty.\)
EXAMPLE 4
Let \[g(x) = \frac{1}{x(x - 4)} \pd\] The graph of \(y = g(x)\) has vertical asymptotes at \(x = 0\) and \(x = 4.\) Describe the behavior of \(g\) at
  1. \(x = 0\)
  2. \(x = 4\)
  1. For \(x \lt 0,\) both factors in the denominator of \(g(x)\) are negative. Therefore, \(g(x)\) is positive as \(x \to 0^-\) and so \(\lim_{x \to 0^-} g(x) = \infty.\) Conversely, when \(0 \lt x \lt 4,\) \[x \gt 0 \and (x - 4) \lt 0 \pd\] Thus, \(g(x)\) is negative as \(x \to 0^+\) and so \(\lim_{x \to 0^+} g(x) = - \infty.\) From these limits, we conclude that \(g(x)\) approaches \(\infty\) from the left and \(-\infty\) from the right of the vertical asymptote \(x = 0.\)
  2. For \(0 \lt x \lt 4,\) \[x \gt 0 \and (x - 4) \lt 0 \pd\] Thus, the sign of \(g(x)\) as \(x \to 4^-\) is negative and so \(\lim_{x \to 4^-} g(x) = -\infty.\) But when \(x \gt 4,\) \[x \gt 0 \and (x - 4) \gt 0 \pd\] The sign of \(g(x)\) as \(x \to 4^+\) is therefore positive, so \(\lim_{x \to 4^+} g(x) = \infty.\) Therefore, \(g(x)\) approaches \(-\infty\) from the left and \(\infty\) from the right of the vertical asymptote \(x = 4.\)
These observations give us the graph in Figure 5.

Precise Definitions of Limits with Infinity

Finite Limits at Infinity Let \(f(x)\) be a function defined over some interval \(x \geq a.\) The statement \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L\) means that \(f(x)\) can be made as close to \(L\) as we want by making \(x\) sufficiently large. The precise definition is as follows: For any positive number \(\varepsilon,\) a number \(N\) exists that if \(x \gt N,\) then the distance between \(f(x)\) and \(L\) is less than \(\varepsilon.\) In other words, \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L\) if and only if \[\abs{f(x) - L} \lt \varepsilon \for x \gt N \pd\] See Figure 6: The region between \(L - \varepsilon\) and \(L + \varepsilon\) represents the values of \(f(x)\) within \(\varepsilon\) of \(L.\) If we wish to make the bound \(\varepsilon\) smaller, then \(N\) must be made bigger. Likewise, \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = L\) means that for \(\varepsilon \gt 0,\) \[\abs{f(x) - L} \lt \varepsilon \for x \lt N \pd\]

When we construct proofs of limits at infinity, our objective is to find the existence of \(N\) such that \(\abs{f(x) - L} \lt \varepsilon.\) Typically, we express \(N\) in terms of \(\varepsilon.\) Doing so gives us the freedom to choose any positive bound \(\varepsilon\)—making the function \(f\) arbitrarily close to the limit \(L\) for \(x \gt N.\)

Infinite Limits at Infinity Let \(f(x)\) be a function defined over an interval \(x \geq a.\) Suppose \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = \infty;\) in words, this statement says that \(f\) increases without bound as \(x\) is made arbitrarily large. Thus, we say that \(f\) is always greater than a given value \(M\) when \(x\) is large—say, for \(x \gt N.\) You can think of \(N\) as a lower bound for the values of \(x\) that ensure \(f(x) \gt M.\) (See Figure 7.) So \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = \infty\) if and only if \[f(x) \gt M \for x \gt N \pd\] In this statement, if we wish to increase \(M,\) then \(N\) must increase accordingly. Similarly, \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = \infty\) if and only if \(f\) is always less than some number \(M \col\) \[f(x) \gt M \for x \lt N \pd\] The number \(N\) is an upper bound for \(x\) such that \(f(x) \gt M.\)

PRECISE DEFINITIONS OF FINITE LIMITS AT INFINITY
Let \(\varepsilon\) be any positive number.
  1. \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L\) if and only if there exists a number \(N\) such that \[\abs{f(x) - L} \lt \varepsilon \for x \gt N \pd\]
  2. \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = L\) if and only if there exists a number \(N\) such that \[\abs{f(x) - L} \lt \varepsilon \for x \lt N \pd\]
PRECISE DEFINITIONS OF INFINITE LIMITS AT INFINITY
Let \(M\) be any number.
  1. \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = \infty\) if and only if there exists a number \(N\) such that \[f(x) \gt M \for x \gt N \pd\]
  2. \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = \infty\) if and only if there exists a number \(N\) such that \[f(x) \gt M \for x \lt N \pd\]
EXAMPLE 5
Prove that \(\lim_{x \to \infty} (1/x) = 0.\)
For \(\varepsilon \gt 0,\) we want to find a number \(N\) such that \[\abs{\frac{1}{x} - 0} \lt \varepsilon \for x \gt N \pd\] Our goal is to determine an expression for \(N\) in terms of \(\varepsilon\) such that, for \(x \gt N,\) \(1/x\) differs from \(0\) by less than \(\varepsilon.\) We may assume that \(x \gt 0\) because \(x \to \infty.\) Rewriting the first inequality gives \(1/x \lt \varepsilon,\) or \(x \gt 1/\varepsilon.\) (We flip the inequality sign whenever we take the reciprocal of both sides.) This form is parallel to \(x \gt N,\) so we see \(N = 1/\varepsilon.\) This choice of \(N\) ensures that \[\frac{1}{x} \lt \varepsilon \for x \gt N = \frac{1}{\varepsilon} \pd\] Our proof is complete; \(1/x\) differs from \(0\) by less than \(\varepsilon\) for \(x \gt 1/\varepsilon.\) Since \(\varepsilon\) is some positive value, we have the freedom to pick any positive value of \(\varepsilon\)—thus making \(1/x\) arbitrarily close to \(0\) for \(x \gt 1/\varepsilon.\)
EXAMPLE 6
Prove that \(\lim_{x \to \infty} x^2 = \infty.\)
We want to find a number \(N\) such that \[x^2 \gt M \for x \gt N \pd\] We determine an expression for \(N\) in terms of \(M\) such that, for \(x \gt N,\) \(x^2\) is always greater than \(M.\) Taking the square root of the first inequality gives \(x \gt \sqrt M.\) (Since \(x \to \infty,\) we assume \(x\) is positive.) The form \(x \gt \sqrt M\) is parallel to \(x \gt N,\) so we see \(N = \sqrt M.\) Accordingly, this choice of \(N\) guarantees that \[x^2 \gt M \for x \gt \sqrt M \pd\] Our proof is complete; \(x^2\) is constantly greater than any lower bound \(M\) when \(x \gt \sqrt M.\)

Limits of Rational Functions at Infinity Consider the limit \[L = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \comma g(x) \ne 0 \pd\] If both \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) approach \(\pm \infty\) as \(x\) approaches \(a,\) then \(L\) is in the indeterminate form \(\indInfty,\) meaning direct substitution yields an inconclusive result. Limits of rational functions as \(x \to \infty\) are very commonly in the indeterminate form \(\indInfty.\) To evaluate these limits, take the limit of only the highest-degree terms in the numerator and denominator; the lower-degree terms are negligible. Alternatively, you may divide each term by the highest-degree term in the rational expression and then take the limit; doing so yields the same result. We can generalize limits of rational functions depending on the degrees of the numerator and denominator: Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be polynomials. Consider the limit \(L = \lim_{x \to \infty} P(x)/Q(x).\)

  1. If \(\deg(P) \gt \deg(Q),\) then \(L = \pm \infty.\)
  2. If \(\deg(P) = \deg(Q),\) then \(L = C,\) where \(C\) is the coefficient of the highest-degree term of \(P\) divided by the coefficient of the highest-degree term of \(Q.\)
  3. If \(\deg(P) \lt \deg(Q),\) then \(L = 0.\)

Horizontal Asymptotes and Vertical Asymptotes A horizontal asymptote of a function \(f\) is the line that \(f(x)\) approaches as \(x\) becomes unbounded. The graph of \(y = f(x)\) has a horizontal asymptote of \(y = L\) if either \[\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L \or \lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = L \pd\] A function may have zero, one, or two horizontal asymptotes. On the other hand, a vertical asymptote to \(f\) at \(p\) is a vertical line along which \(y = f(x)\) becomes unbounded—namely, if \[\lim_{x \to p^-} f(x) = \pm \infty \or \lim_{x \to p^+} f(x) = \pm \infty \pd\]

Limits at Vertical Asymptotes To find the behavior of a function \(f(x)\) at a vertical asymptote \(x = p,\) we analyze the sign of \(f\) on both sides of the asymptote: If \(f \gt 0\) on one side of the asymptote, then \(f\) approaches \(\infty\) on that side. But if \(f \lt 0\) on one side of the asymptote, then on that side \(f\) tends to \(-\infty.\) Consider a function \(f(x)\) whose graph has a vertical asymptote at \(x = p.\)

  1. If \(f(x) \lt 0\) as \(x \to p^-,\) then \(\lim_{x \to p^-} f(x) = -\infty.\)
  2. If \(f(x) \gt 0\) as \(x \to p^+,\) then \(\lim_{x \to p^+} f(x) = \infty.\)

Precise Definitions of Limits with Infinity When we write proofs for finite limits at infinity, we assert that a function \(f(x)\) is confined to a value \(L\) as \(x \to \infty\) or \(x \to -\infty.\) Let \(\varepsilon\) be any positive number.

  1. \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L\) if and only if there exists a number \(N\) such that \[\abs{f(x) - L} \lt \varepsilon \for x \gt N \pd\]
  2. \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = L\) if and only if there exists a number \(N\) such that \[\abs{f(x) - L} \lt \varepsilon \for x \lt N \pd\]

For infinite limits at infinity, we show that a function \(f(x)\) changes without bound as \(x \to \infty\) or \(x \to -\infty.\) Let \(M\) be any number.

  1. \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = \infty\) if and only if there exists a number \(N\) such that \[f(x) \gt M \for x \gt N \pd\]
  2. \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = \infty\) if and only if there exists a number \(N\) such that \[f(x) \gt M \for x \lt N \pd\]
