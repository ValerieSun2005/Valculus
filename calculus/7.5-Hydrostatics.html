7.5: Hydrostatics

If you dive deeper into a body of water, then you feel more pressure on your body due to the increased weight of the water above you. Hydrostatics is the study of water's behavior on a stationary, submerged object. It enables us to test whether a submarine will be crushed in the deep water, or whether a shark can survive at a certain depth. Let's now generalize these ideas. Suppose that a thin, stationary object with area \(A\) is submerged in a body of fluid of density \(\rho\) at a distance of \(h\) below the surface (Figure 1). The hydrostatic force that acts on this body equals the weight of the water above it. The volume of the water above the object is \(Ah,\) and its mass equals density multiplied by volumeâ€”namely, \(\rho Ah.\) Because weight is the product of mass and the acceleration due to gravity, \(g = 9.8 \undiv{m}{sec}^2\) \(= 32 \undiv{ft}{sec}^2,\) the hydrostatic force acting on the object is \begin{equation} F = \rho A g h \pd \label{eq:h-force} \end{equation} Pressure is defined by the force exerted on an object divided by its area. (A sharp knife exerts high pressure because its edge is so thin.) So the hydrostatic pressure that acts upon the submerged object is \(P = F/A,\) or \begin{equation} P = \rho g h \pd \label{eq:h-pressure} \end{equation} We use \(\eqref{eq:h-force}\) and \(\eqref{eq:h-pressure}\) for a thin object whose depth is uniform. But what if we submerge a bigger body whose depth can't be taken as uniform?

Let's generalize how to calculate the hydrostatic force of a vertical body, as in Figure 2. The first step is to define an axis system. We concern ourselves primarily with the vertical direction, so we perform integration with respect to \(y.\) Suppose that the body is bounded between the lines \(y = a\) and \(y = b.\) We cut the body into \(n\) horizontal rectangles of endpoints \(y_1, y_2,\) \(\dots, y_{n - 1},\) \(y_n\) and equal height \(\Delta y.\) In the general subinterval \(\parbr{y_{i - 1}, y_i},\) we pick \(y_i^*\) to be a sample point. If \(w(y)\) is the approximating rectangle's width at \(y,\) then its area is \[A_i = w(y_i^*) \Delta y \pd\] Let \(h(y)\) be the rectangle's depth beneath the water's surface at some \(y.\) If each of the \(n\) rectangles is thin, then the hydrostatic pressure acting on an approximating rectangle is, by \(\eqref{eq:h-pressure},\) roughly \[P_i \approx \rho g h(y_i^*) \pd\] (We assume that the rectangle's depth is uniform.) And by \(\eqref{eq:h-force},\) the hydrostatic force acting on the rectangle is approximated by \[F_i = P_i A_i \approx \rho g h(y_i^*) w(y_i^*) \Delta y \pd\] Summing the hydrostatic forces of all \(n\) rectangles gives a total hydrostatic force of approximately \[F \approx \sum_{i = 1}^n F_i = \sum_{i = 1}^n \rho g h(y_i^*) w(y_i^*) \Delta y \pd\] If we make \(n\) larger and larger, then the rectangles become thinner and so better model the vertical body. Thus, by increasing \(n\) our calculation of the total hydrostatic force increases in accuracy. So \(F\) is the limiting value of the sum as \(n \to \infty \col\) \[F = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i = 1}^n \rho g h(y_i^*) w(y_i^*) \Delta y \pd\] This is a Riemann sum for the function \(\rho g h(y) w(y)\) from \(y = a\) to \(y = b,\) so we attain \begin{equation} F = \rho g \int_a^b h(y) w(y) \di y \pd \label{eq:force-h-body-constants} \end{equation}

Units The International System of Units (SI) is the international standard for measurement. The SI unit for force is the newton, whose symbol is \(\un N.\) One newton is equivalent to a kilogram-meter per second squared; that is, \(1 \un N\) \(= 1 \undiv{kg m}{sec}^2.\) Water has a density of \(\rho =\) \(1000 \undiv{kg}{m}^3\) \(= 62.5 \undiv{lb}{ft}^3.\) The SI unit for pressure is the pascal, whose symbol is \(\un{Pa}.\) Then \(1 \un{Pa} =\) \(1 \undiv{N}{m}^2.\) To simplify \(\eqref{eq:force-h-body-constants},\) let's write \(\rho g\) as one number \(\delta\) (delta). In the SI system, \(\delta = 9.8 \times 1000\) \(= 98000.\) Yet in the imperial system, force is measured in pounds; we therefore neglect the acceleration due to gravity, \(g.\) So if we perform calculations in the imperial system, then we use \(\delta = 62.5.\) Hence, we present only a single formula for the hydrostatic force: \begin{equation} F = \delta \int_a^b h(y) w(y) \di y \cma \label{eq:force-h-body} \end{equation} where \(\delta = 98000\) in SI units and \(\delta = 62.5\) in imperial units.

HYDROSTATIC FORCE ON BODY
Suppose that a stationary body lies in water between the horizontal lines \(y = a\) and \(y = b.\) At some \(y,\) if \(w(y)\) is the body's width and \(h(y)\) is its depth beneath the surface, then the hydrostatic force acting on the body is \begin{equation} F = \delta \int_a^b h(y) w(y) \di y \cma \eqlabel{eq:force-h-body} \end{equation} where \(\delta = 98000\) in SI units and \(\delta = 62.5\) in imperial units.
EXAMPLE 1
A reservoir of water is the shape of an isosceles triangle whose diagonal lengths are \(5\) feet and horizontal length is \(8\) feet (Figure 3). Calculate the hydrostatic force at the bottom of the reservoir when the water level is \(2\) feet.
Taking the downward direction to be the positive \(y\)-axis, let the water height to be \(y = 0.\) The reservoir's bottom is therefore \(y = 2.\) At some level \(y\) a thin, horizontal approximating rectangle of height \(\Delta y\) is a depth of \(h(y) = y\) beneath the water's surface. Its area is \(A = w(y) \Delta y.\) (See Figure 4.) The reservoir's height is \(\sqrt{5^2 - (8/2)^2}\) \(= 3 \un{ft}.\) By similar triangles, we see \[\frac{3}{8} = \frac{2 - y}{w \par{y}} \implies w(y) = \frac{8}{3} \par{2 - y} = \frac{16}{3} - \frac{8}{3} y \pd\] We therefore find the hydrostatic force on the reservoir to be \[ \ba F &= 62.5 \int_0^2 y \par{\frac{16}{3} - \frac{8}{3} y} \di y \nl &= 62.5 \par{\frac{8}{3} y^2 - \frac{8}{9} y^3} \intEval_0^2 \nl &= \boxed{\frac{2000}{9} \un{lb}} \approx 222.222 \un{lb} \pd \ea \]
EXAMPLE 2
A dam is constructed in a shape of a trapezoid that is \(10\) meters tall, \(20\) meters long on the bottom, and \(30\) meters long on the top, as shown by Figure 5. Calculate the force on the dam due to the water when the water height is \(8\) meters.
Let's consider horizontal strips of water (whose heights are uniform) and sum their hydrostatic forces. If we take the downward direction to be the positive \(y\)-axis, then it's most convenient to let the surface level be \(y = 0\) and the dam's bottom be \(y = 8.\) See Figure 6.] A thin, horizontal approximating rectangle has width \(w(y) = 2(10 + a)\) \(= 20 + 2a.\) Its depth below the surface is \(h(y) = y.\) By similar triangles, we see \[\frac{a}{8 - y} = \frac{5}{10} \or a = \frac{5}{10} \par{8 - y} = 4 - \frac{1}{2} y \pd\] Thus, the approximating rectangle has area \[A_i = w(y) \Delta y = \parbr{20 + 2 \par{4 - \frac{1}{2} y}} \Delta y = (28 - y) \Delta y \pd\] Hence, the force on the dam is, following \(\eqref{eq:force-h-body}\) with \(\delta = 98000,\) \[ \ba F &= 98000 \int_0^8 y (28 - y) \di y \nl &= 98000 \par{14y^2 - \frac{y^3}{3}} \intEval_0^8 \approx \boxed{7.108 \times 10^7 \un N} \ea \]
EXAMPLE 3
A right triangle has a horizontal length of \(2\) feet and a vertical length of \(3\) feet. Calculate the hydrostatic force on the triangle if its top vertex is \(1\) foot beneath the water's surface (Figure 7).
At any \(y,\) an approximating rectangle of height \(\Delta y\) is a depth of \(h(y) = y\) below the water's surface. (See Figure 8.) By similar triangles, we see \[\frac{2}{3} = \frac{w(y)}{y - 1} \implies w(y) = \frac{2}{3} (y - 1) \pd\] The triangle is between the horizontal lines \(y = 1\) and \(y = 4.\) We use \(\eqref{eq:force-h-body}\) with \(\delta = 62.5\) to find the hydrostatic force to be \[ \ba F &= 62.5 \int_1^4 y \cdot \frac{2}{3} (y - 1) \di y \nl &= \frac{2}{3} (62.5) \par{\frac{y^3}{3} - \frac{y^2}{2}} \intEval_1^4 \nl &= \boxed{562.5 \un{lb}} \ea \]
EXAMPLE 4
A circular disk of radius \(4\) meters is submerged in a body of water such that the disk's top is located \(5\) meters below the surface (Figure 9). Calculate the hydrostatic force on the disk.
The water's surface is the line \(y = 9.\) Let's consider some approximating rectangle to this disk. Since the rectangle is a distance of \(y\) above the \(x\)-axis, its depth is \(h(y) = 9 - y\) below the water's surface (Figure 10). An equation for the circle is \(x^2 + y^2 = 16,\) so the rectangle's width is \[w(y) = 2x = 2 \sqrt{16 - y^2} \pd\] Because the body is bounded between \(y = -4\) and \(y = 4,\) using \(\eqref{eq:force-h-body}\) with \(\delta = 98000\) gives the hydrostatic force to be \[ \ba F &= 98000 \int_{-4}^4 2 \sqrt{16 - y^2} \par{9 - y} \di y \nl &= 196000 \cdot 9 \int_{-4}^4 \sqrt{16 - y^2} \di y - 196000 \int_{-4}^4 y \sqrt{16 - y^2} \di y \pd \ea \] Since \(y \sqrt{16 - y^2}\) is an odd function, the second integral is \(0.\) The first integral represents the area of a semicircle of radius \(4.\) (We could also use a trigonometric substitution, but possessing geometric intuition is far easier.) We therefore have \[ \ba F &= 196000 \cdot 9 \par{\frac{\pi}{2} \cdot 4^2} + 0 \approx \boxed{4.433 \times 10^7 \un{N}} \ea \]
