5.3: Solids of Revolution

In Section 5.2 we calculated volumes of solids whose cross sections were known shapes. In this section we apply such methods to calculate the volume of a solid generated by rotating a region about an axis. We discuss the Disk Method and Washer Method.

Disk Method

Let \(f\) be a continuous, positive function over \([a, b],\) and let \(R\) be the region under the curve \(y = f(x)\) from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\) (Figure 1A). Suppose that we revolve \(R\) around the \(x\)-axis to produce a three-dimensional solid—called a solid of revolution (Figure 1B). Our objective is to calculate this solid's volume \(V.\)

Volume Calculation We cut the region \(R\) into \(n\) equal-width rectangles and rotate them about the \(x\)-axis. The \(n\) subintervals in \([a, b]\) have endpoints \(x_1, x_2, \dots,\) \(x_{n - 1}, x_n.\) Consider the point \(x_i^*\) in some subinterval \([x_{i - 1}, x_i].\) An approximating rectangle in this subinterval therefore has base \(\Delta x = (b - a)/n\) and height \(f(x_i^*)\) (Figure 2). If we rotate this rectangle about the \(x\)-axis, then we obtain a cylinder—resembling a disk—of depth \(\Delta x\) and radius \(f(x_i^*).\) At \(x = x_i^*\) the cylinder's cross section perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is a circle of radius \(f(x_i^*);\) its area is therefore \(A(x) = \pi [f(x_i^*)]^2.\) So the approximating cylinder's volume is \[\Delta V = A(x) \Delta x = \pi [f(x_i^*)]^2 \Delta x \pd\] (See Figure 3.) Therefore, to approximate the entire volume \(V\) of the solid of revolution, we rotate all \(n\) rectangles about the \(x\)-axis and compute the sum of their volumes: \[V \approx \sum_{i = 1}^n \pi [f(x_i^*)]^2 \Delta x \pd\] If we let \(n \to \infty,\) then \(\Delta x \to 0\) and so we inscribe infinitely many thin rectangles in region \(R.\) Accordingly, the depth \(\Delta x\) of the disks shrinks to \(0.\) We therefore assert that \(V\) is the limiting value of the sum as \(n \to \infty \col\) \[V = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i = 1}^n \pi [f(x_i^*)]^2 \Delta x \pd\] (A visual representation of this limit is shown by Animation 1.) This form is a Riemann sum for the function \(\pi [f(x)]^2,\) so we see \begin{equation} V = \pi \int_a^b [f(x)]^2 \di x \pd \label{eq:disk-x} \end{equation} Because each approximating cylinder is a disk shape, the use of \(\eqref{eq:disk-x}\) is called the Disk Method.

DISK METHOD, ROTATING AROUND \(x\)-AXIS
Let \(f\) be a positive, continuous function on \([a, b].\) The volume of the solid obtained by rotating the region bounded by \(y = f(x)\) from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\) about the \(x\)-axis is given by \begin{equation} V = \pi \int_a^b [f(x)]^2 \di x \pd \eqlabel{eq:disk-x} \end{equation}
EXAMPLE 1
Calculate the volume of the solid generated when the region bounded by the curve \(y = \sqrt x,\) the \(x\)-axis, and the line \(x = 4\) is rotated about the \(x\)-axis.
Figure 4 shows an approximating rectangle of width \(\Delta x\) in the region bounded by \(y = \sqrt x,\) the \(x\)-axis, and the line \(x = 4.\) When we rotate this rectangle about the \(x\)-axis, we attain a disk whose cross-sectional area is \[A(x) = \pi \par{\sqrt x \, }^2 = \pi x \pd\] (See Figure 5.) Its volume is therefore \[\Delta V = A(x) \Delta x = \pi x \Delta x \pd\] Thus, the volume of the solid of revolution is, following \(\eqref{eq:disk-x},\) \[ \ba V &= \pi \int_0^4 \par{\sqrt x \, }^2 \di x = \pi \int_0^4 x \di x \nl &= \pi \par{\tfrac{1}{2} x^2} \intEval_0^4 \nl &= \boxed{8 \pi} \approx 25.133 \pd \ea \]
For the specific example in Figure 6A, the distance between \(y = f(x)\) and the line \(y = L\) is \[r(x) = f(x) - L \pd\] We use the letter \(r\) because it suggests the radius of an approximating disk.

Disk Method with Other Axes Many solids of revolution are created by other axes of rotation—for example, by revolution of a region around some horizontal line \(y = L.\) Consider a region bounded by the curve \(y = f(x)\) and the line \(y = L\) from \(x = a\) to \(x = b.\) Let \(r(x)\) be the distance from the function \(y = f(x)\) to the line \(y = L,\) as shown by an approximating rectangle of width \(\Delta x\) in Figure 6A. Then at some \(x,\) an approximating disk's radius is \(r(x)\) and so its volume is \[\Delta V = \pi [r(x)]^2 \Delta x \pd\] (See Figure 6B.) So the total volume \(V\) of the generated solid of revolution is \begin{equation} V = \pi \int_a^b [r(x)]^2 \di x \pd \label{eq:disk-r} \end{equation} We can also rotate regions about a vertical axis of rotation. In Figure 7A, a region is enclosed between the curve \(x = g(y),\) the vertical line \(x = L,\) and the lines \(y = c\) and \(y = d.\) At any \(y,\) an approximating rectangle in this region has base \(r(y)\) and height \(\Delta y.\) Thus, if we rotate the enclosed region about the line \(x = L,\) then an approximating disk has volume \[\Delta V = \pi [r(y)]^2 \Delta y \pd\] The total volume \(V\) of the solid is therefore \begin{equation} V = \pi \int_c^d [r(y)]^2 \di y \pd \label{eq:disk-r-y} \end{equation} (See Figure 7B.) Using \(\eqref{eq:disk-r}\) and \(\eqref{eq:disk-r-y},\) we're able to calculate the volumes of solids of revolution generated by revolving any region around a line that bounds the enclosed region.

When using \(\eqref{eq:disk-r}\) and \(\eqref{eq:disk-r-y},\) don't forget to multiply by \(\pi.\)
DISK METHOD, GENERALIZED
Let function \(r\) be the distance from the axis of rotation to a point on the graph. We use the Disk Method through the following formulas: \begin{alignat}{2} V &= \pi \int_a^b [r(x)]^2 \di x \cma \lspace &&[\textrm{Horizontal Axis of Rotation}] \eqlabel{eq:disk-r} \nl V &= \pi \int_c^d [r(y)]^2 \di y \pd \lspace &&[\textrm{Vertical Axis of Rotation}] \eqlabel{eq:disk-r-y} \nl \end{alignat}
EXAMPLE 2
Let \(R\) be the region bounded by the parabola \(y = 5 - x^2\) and the line \(y = 1.\) Calculate the volume of the solid generated when \(R\) is rotated about the line \(y = 1.\)
The parabola \(y = 5 - x^2\) intersects the line \(y = 1\) when \[5 - x^2 = 1 \iffArrow x^2 = 4 \iffArrow x = -2 \cma 2 \pd\] Thus, the region \(R\) is in the first and second quadrants between \(x = -2\) and \(x = 2.\) At any \(x,\) an approximating rectangle of width \(\Delta x\) has height \[r(x) = \par{5 - x^2} - 1 = 4 - x^2 \pd\] (See Figure 8.) Consequently, \(r(x)\) is the radius of a disk at \(x\) when we revolve \(R\) around the line \(y = 1\) (Figure 9). Thus, an approximating disk has volume \[\Delta V = \pi [r(x)]^2 \Delta x = \pi \par{4 - x^2}^2 \Delta x \pd\] So, following \(\eqref{eq:disk-r},\) the solid's total volume is \[V = \pi \int_{-2}^2 \par{4 - x^2}^2 \di x \pd\] We exploit symmetry about the \(y\)-axis to simplify the calculation, as follows: \[ \ba V &= 2 \pi \int_0^2 \par{4 - x^2}^2 \di x = 2 \pi \int_0^2 \par{16 - 8x^2 + x^4} \di x \nl &= 2 \pi \par{16x - \tfrac{8}{3} x^3 + \tfrac{1}{5} x^5} \intEval_0^2 \nl &= \boxed{\frac{512 \pi}{15}} \approx 107.233 \pd \ea \]
EXAMPLE 3
Region \(R\) is bounded by the curve \(y = x^3,\) the \(y\)-axis, and the lines \(y = 1\) and \(y = 8.\) Calculate the volume of the solid formed by revolving \(R\) around the \(y\)-axis.
When we rotate a region about a vertical line (here, the \(y\)-axis), we want to express all quantities in terms of \(y.\) Thus, we rewrite \(y = x^3\) as \(x = \sqrt[3] y.\) We see that for \(1 \leq y \leq 8,\) the distance from the curve to the \(y\)-axis (the axis of rotation) is \(r(y) = \sqrt[3] y.\) In Figure 10, an approximating rectangle has height \(\Delta y\) and length \(r(y).\) Accordingly, an approximating disk shown in Figure 11 has volume \[\Delta V = \pi [r(y)]^2 \Delta y = \pi y^{2/3} \Delta y \pd\] Following \(\eqref{eq:disk-r-y},\) the volume of the solid of revolution is \[ \ba V &= \pi \int_1^8 y^{2/3} \di y = \pi \par{\tfrac{3}{5} y^{5/3}} \intEval_1^8 \nl &= \boxed{\frac{93 \pi}{5}} \approx 58.434 \pd \ea \]

Washer Method

We use the Disk Method when there is no gap between a region and the axis of rotation. But when the axis of rotation doesn't lie adjacent to the region, we use the Washer Method. In Figure 12, consider the region enclosed by the curves \(y = f(x)\) and \(y = g(x)\) from \(x = a\) to \(x = b.\) At \(x\) we have drawn an approximating rectangle of width \(\Delta x.\) From the line \(y = L,\) the distance to the top of this rectangle is \(\out r(x)\) and the distance to the bottom of the rectangle is \(\inn r(x).\) Upon rotating this rectangle about the line \(y = L,\) we attain a washer—a disk with a hollow center. A washer's cross-sectional area is therefore given by subtracting the area of the inner circle from the area of the outer circle. The outer circle has radius \(\out r(x),\) and the inner circle has radius \(\inn r(x).\) So its area is \[A(x) = \pi \parbr{\out r(x)}^2 - \pi \parbr{\inn r(x)}^2 \pd\] An approximating washer shown in Figure 13 therefore has volume \[\Delta V = \pi \par{\parbr{\out r(x)}^2 - \parbr{\inn r(x)}^2} \Delta x \pd\] The entire volume \(V\) of the solid is therefore given by integrating \(A(x)\) from \(x = a\) to \(x = b,\) from which we attain \begin{equation} V = \pi \int_a^b \par{\parbr{\out r(x)}^2 - \parbr{\inn r(x)}^2} \di x \pd \label{eq:washer-x} \end{equation} Note that the integral \(\pi \int_a^b \parbr{\inn r(x)}^2 \di x\) is the volume of the hole in the solid, which we subtracted from the volume involving the outer radius.

For the specific example in Figure 12, a washer's outer radius is \(\out r(x) = f(x) - L\) and its inner radius is \(\inn r(x) = g(x) - L.\)

Using the same logic, if we can determine expressions for \(\out r(y)\) and \(\inn r(y),\) then we can compute the volume of a solid formed by rotating a region bounded between \(y = c\) and \(y = d\) about a vertical line. We employ a nearly identical formula to \(\eqref{eq:washer-x}\)—namely, \begin{equation} V = \pi \int_c^d \par{\parbr{\out r(y)}^2 - \parbr{\inn r(y)}^2} \di y \pd \label{eq:washer-y} \end{equation}

WASHER METHOD
If \(\out r\) is the outer distance from the axis of rotation and \(\inn r\) is the inner distance from the axis of rotation, then we use the Washer Method through the following formulas: \begin{alignat}{2} V &= \pi \int_a^b \par{\parbr{\out r(x)}^2 - \parbr{\inn r(x)}^2} \di x \cma \lspace &&[\textrm{Horizontal Axis of Rotation}] \eqlabel{eq:washer-x} \nl V &= \pi \int_c^d \par{\parbr{\out r(y)}^2 - \parbr{\inn r(y)}^2} \di y \pd \lspace &&[\textrm{Vertical Axis of Rotation}] \eqlabel{eq:washer-y} \nl \end{alignat}

CAUTION The volume of a solid of revolution can never be negative. Hence, \(\eqref{eq:washer-x}\) and \(\eqref{eq:washer-y}\) should never return negative values, regardless of the quadrant in which the region or solid lies. Note that the integrand is \(\out r^2 - \inn r^2,\) not \(\par{\out r - \inn r}^2.\) In other words, we integrate a difference of squares, not the square of a difference.

We now develop a list of steps that enable us to calculate the volume of a solid formed by rotating a region \(R\) about a horizontal line or about a vertical line. Generally, in the former case we integrate with respect to \(x,\) whereas in the latter case we integrate with respect to \(y.\)

ROTATING A REGION ABOUT A HORIZONTAL LINE
If region \(R\) is bounded between two functions \(y = f(x)\) and \(y = g(x),\) then the following steps enable you to calculate the volume of the solid of revolution generated by rotating \(R\) about a horizontal line \(y = L \col\)
  1. Sketch the bounded region \(R\) and calculate the points of intersection \(x = a\) and \(x = b.\) Draw a vertical approximating rectangle.
  2. At any \(x,\) determine the distance \(\inn r(x)\) from \(y = L\) to the rectangle's closest horizontal side. Then find the distance \(\out r(x)\) from \(y = L\) to the rectangle's farther horizontal side.
  3. Use \(\eqref{eq:washer-x}\) with the bounds \(x = a\) and \(x = b.\)
ROTATING A REGION ABOUT A VERTICAL LINE
If region \(R\) is bounded between two functions \(x = f(y)\) and \(x = g(y),\) then the following steps enable you to calculate the volume of the solid of revolution generated by rotating \(R\) about a vertical line \(x = L \col\)
  1. Sketch the bounded region \(R\) and calculate the points of intersection \(y = c\) and \(y = d.\) Draw a horizontal approximating rectangle.
  2. At any \(y,\) determine the distance \(\inn r(y)\) from \(x = L\) to the rectangle's closest vertical side. Then find the distance \(\out r(x)\) from \(y = L\) to the rectangle's farther vertical side.
  3. Use \(\eqref{eq:washer-y}\) with the bounds \(y = c\) and \(y = d.\)
EXAMPLE 4
The region enclosed by the graphs of \(y = x\) and \(y = x^2\) in the first quadrant is rotated about the \(x\)-axis to produce a solid. Calculate this solid's volume.
The two graphs intersect when \(x = x^2,\) which occurs when \(x = 0\) and \(x = 1.\) In the enclosed region, we draw a vertical approximating rectangle of width \(\Delta x\) (Figure 14). From the \(x\)-axis, the distance to the rectangle's bottom side is \(x^2\) and the distance to its top side is \(x.\) Hence, when this rectangle is rotated around the \(x\)-axis to generate a washer, its outer radius is \(\out r(x) = x\) and its inner radius is \(\inn r(x) = x^2.\) An approximating washer therefore has cross-sectional area \[A(x) = \pi \parbr{(x)^2 - \par{x^2}^2} = \pi \par{x^2 - x^4} \pd\] (See Figure 15.) Thus, the volume \(V\) of the solid of revolution is given by integrating \(A(x)\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 1,\) as given by \(\eqref{eq:washer-x}.\) We see \[ \ba V &= \pi \int_0^1 \par{x^2 - x^4} \di x \nl &= \pi \par{\tfrac{1}{3} x^3 - \tfrac{1}{5} x^5} \intEval_0^1 \nl &= \boxed{\frac{2 \pi}{15}} \approx 0.419 \pd \ea \]
EXAMPLE 5
The region enclosed by the graphs of \(x = y + 2\) and \(x = y^2\) in the first quadrant is rotated about the \(y\)-axis to produce a solid. Calculate this solid's volume.
Because we rotate the enclosed region about a vertical line, we want to express all quantities in terms of \(y.\) The two graphs intersect when \(y + 2 = y^2,\) which we solve as follows: \[y^2 - y - 2 = 0 \iffArrow (y - 2)(y + 1) = 0 \iffArrow y = -1 \cma 2 \pd\] Thus, the region is bounded between \(y = -1\) and \(y = 2.\) In the region we sketch a horizontal approximating rectangle whose height is \(\Delta y\) (Figure 16). At any \(-1 \leq y \leq 2\) the distance from the line \(x = -1\) to the left side of the approximating rectangle is \(y^2 + 1,\) and the distance from the line \(x = -1\) to the right side of the approximating rectangle is \(y + 3.\) Therefore, an approximating washer has outer radius \(\out r(y) = y + 3\) and inner radius \(\inn r(y) = y^2 + 1.\) (See Figure 17.) Its volume is therefore \[\Delta V = \pi \parbr{\par{y + 3}^2 - \par{y^2 + 1}^2} \Delta y \pd\] Hence, the volume of the entire solid of revolution is, following \(\eqref{eq:washer-y},\) \[ \ba V &= \pi \int_{-1}^2 \parbr{\par{y + 3}^2 - \par{y^2 + 1}^2} \di y \nl &= \pi \int_{-1}^2 \par{-y^4 - y^2 + 6y + 8} \di y \nl &= \pi \par{-\tfrac{1}{5} y^5 - \tfrac{1}{3} y^3 + 3y^2 + 8y} \intEval_{-1}^2 \nl &= \boxed{\frac{117 \pi}{5}} \approx 73.513 \pd \ea \]

Disk Method We use the Disk Method to compute the volume of a solid of revolution generated by rotating a region about a line. This method is only applicable if there is no gap between the region and the line about which we rotate the region. Let \(f\) be a positive, continuous function on \([a, b].\) The volume of the solid obtained by rotating the region bounded by \(y = f(x)\) from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\) about the \(x\)-axis is given by \begin{equation} V = \pi \int_a^b [f(x)]^2 \di x \pd \eqlabel{eq:disk-x} \end{equation} More generally, let function \(r\) be the distance from the axis of rotation (the line around which we revolve a region) to a point on the graph.
