2.6 Exercise Solutions: Differentiating Logarithmic Functions

EXERCISE 1
Why do we use the number \(e\) in calculus?
SOLUTION The number \(e\) enables the derivatives of exponential and logarithmic functions to have very convenient derivatives; that is, \[\deriv{}{x} \par{e^x} = e^x \and \deriv{}{x} \par{\ln x} = \frac{1}{x} \pd\]
EXERCISE 2
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{\ln ax}\]
evaluate the derivative.
SOLUTION The derivative of \(\ln x\) is \(1/x.\) The Chain Rule shows \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \par{\ln ax} &= \frac{1}{ax} \cdot a \nl &= \boxed{\frac{1}{x}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 3
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{t} \ln \par{2t^3 - e^t - \acsc t}\]
evaluate the derivative.
SOLUTION The derivative of \(\ln x\) is \(1/x;\) also observe that \[\deriv{}{t} \par{2t^3 - e^t - \acsc t} = 6t^2 - e^t + \frac{1}{\abs t \sqrt{t^2 - 1}} \pd\] Hence, the derivative of \(\ln \par{2t^3 - e^t - \acsc t}\) is, by the Chain Rule, \[\frac{1}{2t^3 - e^t - \acsc t} \cdot \par{6t^2 - e^t + \frac{1}{\abs t \sqrt{t^2 - 1}}} = \boxed{\frac{\ds 6t^2 - e^t + \frac{1}{\abs t \sqrt{t^2 - 1}}}{2t^3 - e^t - \acsc t}}\]
EXERCISE 4
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{\log_5 x}\]
evaluate the derivative.
SOLUTION We use the Change of Base Rule for Logarithms to rewrite \(\log_5 x\) using natural logarithms: \[\log_5 x = \frac{\ln x}{\ln 5} \pd\] So \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \par{\log_5 x} &= \deriv{}{x} \par{\frac{\ln x}{\ln 5}} \nl &= \frac{1}{\ln 5} \deriv{}{x} \par{\ln x} \nl &= \frac{1}{\ln 5} \cdot \frac{1}{x} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{1}{x \ln 5}} \ea \] Alternatively, you could simply remember that the derivative of a logarithm whose base isn't \(e\) (and is a constant) is \(1/x\) divided by the natural logarithm of the base.
EXERCISE 5
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \atan \par{\ln x}\]
evaluate the derivative.
SOLUTION The derivative of \(\atan x\) is \(1/(x^2 + 1),\) and the derivative of \(\ln x\) is \(1/x.\) Accordingly, the Chain Rule asserts that \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \atan \par{\ln x} &= \frac{1}{(\ln x)^2 + 1} \cdot \frac{1}{x} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{1}{x(\ln x)^2 + x}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 6
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \ln \par{4x^2 + \sin x - e^x}\]
evaluate the derivative.
SOLUTION By the Chain Rule, the derivative is \[\frac{1}{4x^2 + \sin x - e^x} \deriv{}{x} \par{4x^2 + \sin x - e^x} = \boxed{8x + \cos x - e^x}{4x^2 + \sin x - e^x}\]
EXERCISE 7
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{t} \par{\frac{\asec t}{\ln t}}\]
evaluate the derivative.
SOLUTION The derivative of \(\asec t\) is \[\frac{1}{\abs t \sqrt{t^2 - 1}} \cma\] and the derivative of \(\ln t\) is \(1/t.\) Hence, the Quotient Rule shows \[ \ba \deriv{}{t} \par{\frac{\asec t}{\ln t}} &= \frac{\ds \par{\frac{1}{\abs t \sqrt{t^2 - 1}}}\ln t - \asec t \par{\frac{1}{t}}}{(\ln t)^2} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{\ds \frac{\ln t}{\abs t \sqrt{t^2 - 1}} - \frac{\asec t}{t}}{(\ln t)^2}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 8
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \parbr{\ln \par{x^3 + 2} \asec 2x}\]
evaluate the derivative.
SOLUTION The derivative \(\ln \par{x^3 + 2}\) is \[\frac{1}{x^3 + 2} \cdot \deriv{}{x} \par{x^3 + 2} = \frac{3x^2}{x^3 + 2} \pd\] The derivative of \(\asec 2x\) is \[\frac{1}{\abs{2x} \sqrt{(2x)^2 - 1}} \cdot \deriv{}{x} \par{2x} = \frac{1}{\abs{x} \sqrt{4x^2 - 1}} \pd\] Hence, the Product Rule gives \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \parbr{\ln \par{x^3 + 2} \asec 2x} &= \frac{3x^2}{x^3 + 2} \cdot \asec 2x + \ln \par{x^3 + 2} \cdot \frac{1}{\abs{x} \sqrt{4x^2 - 1}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{3x^2 \asec 2x}{x^3 + 2} + \frac{\ln \par{x^3 + 2}}{\abs{x} \sqrt{4x^2 - 1}}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 9
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{x^2 \log_{14} x}\]
evaluate the derivative.
SOLUTION To rewrite \(\log_{14} x\) using natural logarithms, we use the Change of Base Rule for Logarithms: \[\log_{14} x = \frac{\ln x}{\ln 14} \pd\] Thus, we see \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \par{\log_{14} x} &= \deriv{}{x} \par{\frac{\ln x}{\ln 14}} \nl &= \frac{1}{\ln 14} \deriv{}{x} \par{\ln x} \nl &= \frac{1}{\ln 14} \cdot \frac{1}{x} \nl &= \frac{1}{x \ln 14} \pd \ea \] Accordingly, the Product Rule gives \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \par{x^2 \log_{14} x} &= (2x) \log_{14} x + x^2 \par{\frac{1}{x \ln 14}} \nl &= \boxed{2x \log_{14} x + \frac{x}{\ln 4}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 10
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \log_{4x^2 - 3} \par{\atan x}\]
evaluate the derivative.
SOLUTION Using the Change of Base Rule for Logarithms shows \[\log_{4x^2 - 3} \atan x = \frac{\ln \par{\atan x}}{\ln \par{4x^2 - 3}} \pd\] Observe that, by the Chain Rule, \[ \baf &&\deriv{}{x} \ln \par{\atan x} &= \frac{1}{\atan x} \cdot \frac{1}{x^2 + 1} = \frac{1}{\par{x^2 + 1} \atan x} &\nl \laWord{and} &&\deriv{}{x} \ln \par{4x^2 - 3} &= \frac{1}{4x^2 - 3} \cdot (8x) = \frac{8x}{4x^2 - 3} \pd \eaf \] Thus, the Quotient Rule shows \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \par{\log_{4x^2 - 3} \atan x} &= \deriv{}{x} \frac{\ln \par{\atan x}}{\ln \par{4x^2 - 3}} \nl &= \frac{\ds \par{\frac{1}{\par{x^2 + 1} \atan x}} \ln \par{4x^2 - 3} - \par{\frac{8x}{4x^2 - 3}} \ln \par{\atan x}}{\parbr{\ln \par{4x^2 - 3}}^2} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{\ds \frac{\ln \par{4x^2 - 3}}{\par{x^2 + 1} \atan x} - \frac{8x \ln \par{\atan x}}{4x^2 - 3}}{\parbr{\ln \par{4x^2 - 3}}^2}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 11
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{t} \par{t^t}\]
evaluate the derivative.
SOLUTION Do not use the Power Rule. The Power Rule is applicable only if the exponent is constant. Instead, the Change of Base Rule for Exponents gives \[t^t = e^{t \ln t} \cma\] a composite function of inner function \(t \ln t.\) Hence, the Chain Rule gives the derivative to be \[ \ba e^{t \ln t} \cdot \deriv{}{t} \par{t \ln t} &= e^{t \ln t} \cdot (\ln t + 1) \nl &= \boxed{t^t \par{\ln t + 1}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 12
For
\[\ds xy^3 - \ln y = \frac{2}{x^2}\]
find \(\textderiv{y}{x}\).
SOLUTION When we apply Implicit Differentiation, we treat \(y\) as a function of \(x.\) The derivative of \(\ln x\) is \(1/x.\) We use the Chain Rule to differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\) and then solve for \(\textderiv{y}{x},\) as follows: \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \par{xy^3 - \ln y} &= \deriv{}{x} \par{\frac{2}{x^2}} \nl y^3 + 3xy^2 \deriv{y}{x} - \frac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{x} &= -\frac{4}{x^3} \nl \deriv{y}{x} \par{3xy^2 - \frac{1}{y}} &= -y^3 - \frac{4}{x^3} \nl \implies \deriv{y}{x} &= \boxed{\frac{y^3 + \dfrac{4}{x^3}}{\dfrac{1}{y} - 3xy^2}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 13
For
\[\ds \ln y = x^3 - e^x\]
find \(\textderiv{y}{x}\).
SOLUTION Recall that the derivative of \(\ln x\) is \(1/x.\) To find \(\textderivOrder{y}{x}{2},\) we first attain \(\textderiv{y}{x},\) as follows: \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \par{\ln y} &= \deriv{}{x} \par{x^3 - e^x} \nl \frac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{x} &= 3x^2 - e^x \nl \implies \deriv{y}{x} &= \par{3x^2 - e^x} y \pd \ea \] We use the Product Rule to differentiate \(\textderiv{y}{x};\) as we differentiate, we apply the Chain Rule to differentiate all instances of \(y.\) Whenever we see the expression \(\textderiv{y}{x},\) we substitute the expression we attained for it. Doing so shows \[ \ba \derivOrder{y}{x}{2} &= \deriv{}{x} \deriv{y}{x} \nl &= \deriv{}{x} \parbr{\par{3x^2 - e^x} y} \nl &= \par{6x - e^x} y + \par{3x^2 - e^x} \deriv{y}{x} \nl &= \par{6x - e^x} y + \par{3x^2 - e^x} \cdot \par{3x^2 - e^x} y \nl &= \boxed{\par{6x - e^x} y + \par{3x^2 - e^x}^2 y} \ea \]
EXERCISE 14
For
\[\ds y = e^x \cos x\]
differentiate the function using Logarithmic Differentiation.
SOLUTION We take the natural logarithm of each side: \[\ln y = \ln (e^x \cos x)\pd\] Since \(\ln (e^x \cos x) = \ln(e^x) + \ln(\cos x) = x + \ln(\cos x),\) the equation becomes \[\ln y = x + \ln(\cos x) \pd\] Using Implicit Differentiation yields \[\frac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{x} = 1 - \frac{\sin x}{\cos x} = 1 - \tan x \pd\] We solve for \(\textDeriv{y}{x}\) by multiplying both sides by \(y = e^x \, \cos x \col\) \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= e^x \cos x (1 - \tan x) \nl &= \boxed{e^x(\cos x - \sin x)} \ea \]
EXERCISE 15
For
\[\ds y = e^{\sin x}\]
differentiate the function using Logarithmic Differentiation.
SOLUTION Taking the natural logarithm of both sides of \(y = e^{\sin x},\) we get \[ \ba \ln y &= \ln \par{e^{\sin x}} \nl &= \sin x \pd \ea \] We perform Implicit Differentiation by differentiating both sides with respect to \(x \col\) \[\frac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{x} = \cos x \pd\] We multiply both sides by \(y = e^{\sin x}\) to isolate \(\textDeriv{y}{x} \col\) \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= \boxed{e^{\sin x} \cos x} \ea \]
EXERCISE 16
For
\[\ds y = (\sin 3x)^{x + 7}\]
differentiate the function using Logarithmic Differentiation.
SOLUTION Taking the natural logarithm of both sides gives \[\ln y = \ln \parbr{(\sin 3x)^{x + 7}} \pd\] Using logarithm properties then gives \[\ln y = (x + 7) \ln(\sin 3x) \pd\] We then perform Implicit Differentiation to obtain \(\textDeriv{y}{x} \col\) \[ \ba \frac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{x} &= \ln(\sin 3x) + (x + 7) \frac{3 \cos 3x}{\sin 3x} \nl &= \ln(\sin 3x) + 3(x + 7) \cot 3x \nl \deriv{y}{x} &= y \par{\ln(\sin 3x) + 3(x + 7) \cot 3x} \nl &= \boxed{(\sin 3x)^{x + 7} \par{\ln(\sin 3x) + 3(x + 7) \cot 3x}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 17
For
\[\ds y = x^4 \, 9^x\]
differentiate the function using Logarithmic Differentiation.
SOLUTION We take the natural logarithm of each side: \[\ln y = \ln \par{x^4 \, 9^x } \pd\] Using logarithm properties then gives \[\ln y = 4 \ln x + x \ln 9 \pd\] Differentiating both sides then gives \[\frac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{x} = \frac{4}{x} + \ln 9 \pd\] Solving for \(\textDeriv{y}{x},\) we obtain \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= y \left(\frac{4}{x} + \ln 9\right) \nl &= \boxed{x^4 \, 9^x \left(\frac{4}{x} + \ln 9\right)} \ea \]
EXERCISE 18
For
\[\ds y = \frac{x \sin x}{\sqrt{x^3 + 1}}\]
differentiate the function using Logarithmic Differentiation.
SOLUTION We take the natural logarithm of both sides, getting \[ \ba \ln y &= \ln \par{\frac{x \sin x}{\sqrt{x^3 + 1}} } \nl &= \ln (x \sin x) - \ln \par{\sqrt{x^3 + 1} } \nl &= \ln x + \ln(\sin x) - \tfrac{1}{2} \ln (x^3 + 1) \pd \ea \] Differentiating the last expression gives \[\frac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{x} = \frac{1}{x} + \frac{\cos x}{\sin x} - \frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{3x^2}{x^3 + 1}\right) \pd\] Solving for \(\textDeriv{y}{x}\) then provides \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= y \parbr{\frac{1}{x} + \cot x - \frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{3x^2}{x^3 + 1}\right)} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{x \sin x}{\sqrt{x^3 + 1}} \par{\frac{1}{x} + \cot x - \frac{3x^2}{2x^3 + 2}}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 19
For
\[\ds y = \frac{x^2 - 6}{2^{\cos x}}\]
differentiate the function using Logarithmic Differentiation.
SOLUTION Taking the natural logarithm of both sides produces \begin{align*} \ln y &= \ln \par{\frac{x^2 - 6}{2^{\cos x}}} \nonumber \nl &= \ln(x^2 - 6) - \cos x \ln 2 \period \label{eq:ex-2-lny} \end{align*} Differentiating both sides and solving for \(\textDeriv{y}{x}\) yield \[ \ba \frac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{2x}{x^2 - 6} + \sin x \ln 2 \nl \deriv{y}{x} &= \boxed{\frac{x^2 - 6}{2^{\cos x}} \left(\frac{2x}{x^2 - 6} + \sin x \ln 2\right)} \ea \]
EXERCISE 20
For
\[\ds y = t^{2t}\]
differentiate the function using Logarithmic Differentiation.
SOLUTION Inserting the natural logarithm on both sides yields \[\ln y = \ln \par{t^{2t}} = 2t \ln t \pd\] Then differentiating gives \[\frac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{t} = 2 \ln t + 2 \pd\] Solving for \(\textDeriv{y}{t},\) we find \[ \ba \deriv{y}{t} &= y(2 \ln t + 2) \nl &= \boxed{t^{2t} \, (2 \ln t + 2)} \ea \]
EXERCISE 21
For
\[\ds y = \frac{3 - x}{5^x \tan x} \]
differentiate the function using Logarithmic Differentiation.
SOLUTION Taking the natural logarithm of both sides gives \[ \ba \ln y &= \ln \par{\frac{3 - x}{5^x \tan x}} \nl &= \ln(3 - x) - \ln \par{5^x \tan x } \nl &= \ln(3 - x) - \ln(\tan x) - x \ln 5 \pd \ea \] Differentiating, we get \[\frac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{x} = \frac{1}{x - 3} - \frac{\sec^2 x}{\tan x} - \ln 5 \pd\] Now we solve for \(\textDeriv{y}{x} \col\) \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= y \par{ \frac{1}{x - 3} - \frac{\sec^2 x}{\tan x} - \ln 5} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{3 - x}{5^x \tan x} \par{ \frac{1}{x - 3} - \frac{\sec^2 x}{\tan x} - \ln 5}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 22
For
\[\ds y = \frac{\sec^2 x}{x^3 + \ln x}\]
differentiate the function using Logarithmic Differentiation.
SOLUTION We use logarithms to rewrite the equation as \[ \ba \ln y &= \ln \par{\frac{\sec^2 x}{x^3 + \ln x}} \nl &= \ln (\sec^2 x) - \ln \par{x^3 + \ln x} \nl &= 2 \ln(\sec x) - \ln \par{x^3 + \ln x} \pd \ea \] We then take the derivative of each side: \[ \ba \frac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{x} &= 2 \par{\frac{\sec x \tan x}{\sec x}} - \frac{3x^2 + \frac{1}{x}}{x^3 + \ln x} \nl &= 2 \tan x - \frac{3x^2 + \frac{1}{x}}{x^3 + \ln x} \pd \ea \] Solving for \(\textDeriv{y}{x}\) then yields \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= y \par{2 \tan x - \frac{3x^2 + \frac{1}{x}}{x^3 + \ln x}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{\sec^2 x}{x^3 + \ln x} \par{2 \tan x - \frac{3x^2 + \frac{1}{x}}{x^3 + \ln x}}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 23
For
\[\ds y = \sqrt{\frac{x^2 \sin x}{e^x}}\]
differentiate the function using Logarithmic Differentiation.
SOLUTION We take the natural logarithm of both sides: \[ \ba \ln y &= \ln \par{\frac{x^2 \sin x}{e^x}}^{1/2} \nl &= \tfrac{1}{2} \ln \par{\frac{x^2 \sin x}{e^x}} \nl &= \tfrac{1}{2} \ln \par{x^2} + \tfrac{1}{2} \ln(\sin x) - \tfrac{1}{2} \ln \par{e^x} \nl &= \ln x + \tfrac{1}{2} \ln(\sin x) - \tfrac{1}{2} x \pd \ea \] Differentiating implicitly, we have \[ \ba \frac{1}{y} \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{1}{x} + \frac{\cos x}{2 \sin x} - \frac{1}{2} \nl \deriv{y}{x} &= y \par{\frac{1}{x} + \frac{\cos x}{2 \sin x} - \frac{1}{2}} \nl &= \boxed{\sqrt{\frac{x^2 \sin x}{e^x}} \par{\frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{2} \cot x - \frac{1}{2}}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 24

differentiate the function using Logarithmic Differentiation.

EXERCISE 32
Find the point on the graph of \(y = \ln x + 1\) to which the tangent line passes through the origin.
SOLUTION Let's call the point of tangency \((a, \ln a + 1).\) The slope of the tangent at \(a\) is \(1/a,\) so an equation of the tangent line at \(x = a\) is \[y - \par{\ln a + 1} = \tfrac{1}{a} (x - a) \pd\] This line must pass through the origin, so \((0, 0)\) must be a valid point; substituting \(x = 0\) and \(y = 0\) shows \[ \ba 0 - \par{\ln a + 1} &= \tfrac{1}{a} (0 - a) \nl \ln a &= 0 \nl \implies a &= 1 \pd \ea \] Then \(y = \ln 1 + 1\) \(= 1.\) So the point on the curve is \(\boxed{(1, 1)}.\)
EXERCISE 33
Determine the area of the triangle bounded between the \(y\)-axis, the tangent line to \(y = \ln x\) at \(x = e,\) and the normal line to \(y = \ln x\) at \(x = e.\)
SOLUTION The bounded region is a triangle of width \(e.\) To find its height, we must calculate the \(y\)-intercept of the normal line. Recall from Section 2.2 that a normal line is perpendicular to a tangent line. The derivative of \(\ln x\) is \(1/x,\) so the tangent to the curve at \(x = e\) has slope \(1/e.\) Accordingly, the normal's slope is the negative reciprocal of this value, that is, \(-e.\) An equation of the normal line is therefore \[y - \ln e = -e(x - e) \or y = 1 - e(x - e) \pd\] When \(x = 0\) we see \[y = 1 - e(0 - e) = 1 + e^2 \pd\] The \(y\)-intercept \((1 + e^2)\) is the height of the bounded triangle. Thus, its area is \[\tfrac{1}{2} (e)(1 + e^2) = \boxed{\tfrac{1}{2} (e + e^3)}\]
