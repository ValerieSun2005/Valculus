9.4 Exercise Solutions: Differentiating Polar Functions

EXERCISE 1
For
\[\ds r = 2 + \cos \theta \cma \deriv{r}{\theta} \intEval_{\theta = \pi/2}\]
calculate and interpret the derivative.
SOLUTION Differentiating gives \[\deriv{r}{\theta} = -\sin \theta \pd\] So \[\deriv{r}{\theta} \intEval_{\theta = \pi/2} = -\sin \tfrac{\pi}{2} = \boxed{-1}\] Also, \(r(\pi/2) = 2.\) Since \(r\) and \(\textderiv{r}{\theta}\) have opposite signs, as \(\theta\) increases the graph is moving toward the pole at \(\theta = \pi/2.\)
EXERCISE 2
For
\[\ds r = 4\theta + \sin \theta \cma \deriv{r}{\theta} \intEval_{\theta = \pi}\]
calculate and interpret the derivative.
SOLUTION We differentiate to obtain \[\deriv{r}{\theta} = 4 + \cos \theta \pd\] Thus, \[\deriv{r}{\theta} \intEval_{\theta = \pi} = 4 + \cos \pi = \boxed{3}\] We find \(r(\pi) = 4 \pi.\) Since \(r\) and \(\textderiv{r}{\theta}\) have the same sign, the graph is moving away from the pole at \(\theta = \pi.\)
EXERCISE 3
For
\[\ds r = \sin \theta \cma \deriv{y}{\theta} \intEval_{\theta = -\pi/6}\]
calculate and interpret the derivative.
SOLUTION We find \(y\) in terms of \(\theta\) to be \[y(\theta) = \sin^2 \theta \pd\] Then we differentiate, obtaining \[\deriv{y}{\theta} = 2 \sin \theta \cos \theta = \sin 2 \theta \pd\] So \[\deriv{y}{\theta} \intEval_{\theta = -\pi/6} = \sin \par{-\tfrac{\pi}{3}} = \boxed{-\tfrac{\sqrt 3}{2}}\] Also, \(y(-\pi/6) \gt 0.\) The signs of \(y\) and \(\textderiv{y}{\theta}\) are opposite, so the graph is moving toward the \(\bf x\)-axis as \(\theta\) increases from \(-\pi/6.\)
EXERCISE 4
For
\[\ds r = 3 \sin 2 \theta \cma \deriv{r}{\theta} \intEval_{\theta = \pi/3}\]
calculate and interpret the derivative.
SOLUTION Differentiating gives \[\deriv{r}{\theta} = 6 \cos 2 \theta \pd\] We have \[\deriv{r}{\theta} \intEval_{\theta = \pi/3} = 6 \cos \tfrac{2 \pi}{3} = \boxed{-3}\] In addition, \(r(\pi/3) \gt 0.\) Since \(r\) and \(\textderiv{r}{\theta}\) have different signs, the graph is moving toward the pole at \(\theta = \pi/3.\)
EXERCISE 5
For
\[\ds r = 4 \sin \theta \cma \deriv{x}{\theta} \intEval_{\theta = 7\pi/6}\]
calculate and interpret the derivative.
SOLUTION We find \(x\) in terms of \(\theta \col\) \[x(\theta) = 4 \sin \theta \cos \theta \pd\] Since \(2 \sin \theta \cos \theta = \sin 2 \theta,\) we rewrite the function as \[x(\theta) = 2 \sin 2 \theta \pd\] Then we differentiate, finding \[\deriv{x}{\theta} = 4 \cos 2 \theta \pd\] We have \[\deriv{x}{\theta} \intEval_{\theta = 7 \pi/6} = 4 \cos \tfrac{7 \pi}{3} = \boxed 2\] Also, \(x(7\pi/6) = \sqrt 3 \gt 0.\) Since \(r\) and \(\textderiv{r}{\theta}\) have the same sign, the graph is moving away from the \(\bf y\)-axis at \(\theta = 7 \pi/6.\)
EXERCISE 6
For
\[\ds r = \sec \theta \cma \deriv{x}{\theta} \intEval_{\theta = 3 \pi/4}\]
calculate and interpret the derivative.
SOLUTION The \(x\) as a function of \(\theta\) is \[x = (\sec \theta) \cos \theta = 1 \pd\] [Note that writing \(x\) conveys the same meaning as writing \(x(\theta).\)] So \(\textderiv{x}{\theta} = 0\) for all \(\theta,\) meaning the graph's \(x\)-coordinate is not changing. In fact, the graph of \(r = \sec \theta\) is the vertical line \(x = 1.\) (See Section 9.3 to review polar equations for lines.)
EXERCISE 7
For
\[\ds r = \cos 5 \theta \cma \deriv{y}{\theta} \intEval_{\theta = \pi/2}\]
calculate and interpret the derivative.
SOLUTION We find \(y\) in terms of \(\theta \col\) \[y(\theta) = \cos 5\theta \sin \theta \pd\] Then we differentiate, obtaining \[\deriv{y}{\theta} = \cos \theta \cos 5 \theta - 5 \sin 5 \theta \sin \theta \pd\] We have \[\deriv{y}{\theta} \intEval_{\theta = \pi/2} = 0 -5 \sin \tfrac{5 \pi}{2} = \boxed{-5}\] At \(\theta = \pi/2,\) \[y = \cos \tfrac{5 \pi}{2} \sin \tfrac{\pi}{2} = 0 \pd\] Consequently, at \(\theta = \pi/2\) the graph is already on the \(x\)-axis, and \(y\) is decreasing. So at \(\theta = \pi/2,\) the graph touches the \(x\)-axis and is moving away from the \(\bf x\)-axis.
EXERCISE 8
For
\[\ds r = \theta \cma \deriv{y}{\theta} \intEval_{\theta = 3\pi/2}\]
calculate and interpret the derivative.
SOLUTION First, we find \(y\) in terms of \(\theta\) as \[y(\theta) = \theta \sin \theta \pd\] Then we differentiate, obtaining \[\deriv{y}{\theta} = \sin \theta + \theta \cos \theta \pd\] So \[\deriv{y}{\theta} \intEval_{\theta = 3\pi/2} = \sin \tfrac{3 \pi}{2} + \tfrac{3 \pi}{2} \cos \tfrac{3 \pi}{2} = \boxed{-1}\] We also attain \[y \par{\tfrac{3 \pi}{2}} = \tfrac{3 \pi}{2} \sin \tfrac{3 \pi}{2} = -\tfrac{3 \pi}{2} \pd\] Since \(y\) and \(\textderiv{y}{\theta}\) are both negative, the graph is moving away from the \(\bf x\)-axis.
EXERCISE 9
For
\[\ds r = 2 + \cos \theta \cma \theta = \frac{\pi}{4}\]
calculate the slope of the line tangent to the polar graph at the given angle.
SOLUTION The graph's slope at \(\theta = \pi/4\) is \(\textderiv{y}{x}|_{\theta = \pi/4}.\) We express this quantity in terms of \(\theta\) using \[\deriv{y}{x} = \frac{\textderiv{y}{\theta}}{\textderiv{x}{\theta}} \pd\] We want \(\textderiv{y}{x} |_{\theta = \pi/4}.\) Expressing \(x\) and \(y\) as functions of \(\theta\) gives \[x = (2 + \cos \theta) \cos \theta \lspace y = (2 + \cos \theta) \sin \theta \pd\] Hence, \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{2 \cos \theta + \cos^2 \theta - \sin^2 \theta}{-2 \sin \theta - 2 \cos \theta \sin \theta} \nl &= \frac{2 \cos \theta + \cos 2 \theta}{-2 \sin \theta - \sin 2 \theta} \pd \ea \] The slope at \(\theta = \pi/4\) is therefore \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} \intEval_{\theta = \pi/4} &= \frac{2 \cos \tfrac{\pi}{4} + \cos \tfrac{\pi}{2}}{-2 \sin \tfrac{\pi}{4} - \sin \tfrac{\pi}{2}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{\sqrt 2}{-1 - \sqrt 2}} \approx -0.586 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 10
For
\[\ds r = \cos 3 \theta \cma \theta = 0\]
calculate the slope of the line tangent to the polar graph at the given angle.
SOLUTION The graph's slope at any value \(\theta\) is \(\textderiv{y}{x}.\) We express this quantity in terms of \(\theta\) using \[\deriv{y}{x} = \frac{\textderiv{y}{\theta}}{\textderiv{x}{\theta}} \pd\] We first write functions for \(x\) and \(y\) in terms of \(\theta \col\) \[x = \cos 3 \theta \cos \theta \and y = \cos 3 \theta \sin \theta \pd\] Taking the ratio of derivatives gives \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{\cos 3 \theta \, \cos \theta - 3 \sin 3 \theta \, \sin \theta}{-3 \sin 3 \theta \, \cos \theta - \cos 3 \theta \, \sin \theta} \pd \ea \] When \(\theta = 0,\) the numerator is \(1\) and the denominator is \(0.\) Because \(\textderiv{x}{\theta}|_{\theta = \pi} = 0\) and \(\textderiv{y}{\theta}|_{\theta = \pi} \ne 0,\) the graph of \(r = 3 \cos \theta\) has a vertical tangent when \(\theta = 0.\)
EXERCISE 11
For
\[\ds r = \theta^2 \cma \theta = \pi\]
calculate the slope of the line tangent to the polar graph at the given angle.
SOLUTION The slope of the differentiable polar graph at \(\theta = \pi\) is \(\textderiv{y}{x}|_{\theta = \pi}.\) In terms of \(\theta,\) we have \[\deriv{y}{x} = \frac{\textderiv{y}{\theta}}{\textderiv{x}{\theta}} \pd\] First, we express \(x\) and \(y\) as functions of \(\theta \col\) \[x = \theta^2 \cos \theta \lspace y = \theta^2 \sin \theta \pd\] We have \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{2 \theta \, \sin \theta + \theta^2 \, \cos \theta}{2 \theta \, \cos \theta - \theta^2 \, \sin \theta} \nl &= \frac{2 \sin \theta + \theta \, \cos \theta}{2 \cos \theta - \theta \, \sin \theta} \pd \ea \] So the slope is \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} \intEval_{\theta = \pi} &= \frac{2 \sin \pi + \pi \, \cos \pi}{2 \cos \pi - \pi \, \sin \pi} \nl &= \boxed{\tfrac{\pi}{2}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 12
For
\[\ds r = 2 \sin \theta \cma \theta = \frac{3 \pi}{2}\]
calculate the slope of the line tangent to the polar graph at the given angle.
SOLUTION At any value of \(\theta,\) the graph's slope is \(\textderiv{y}{x}.\) We express this quantity in terms of \(\theta\) using \[\deriv{y}{x} = \frac{\textderiv{y}{\theta}}{\textderiv{x}{\theta}} \pd\] We first write functions for \(x\) and \(y\) in terms of \(\theta \col\) \[x = 2 \sin \theta \cos \theta \and y = 2 \sin^2 \theta \pd\] Taking the ratio of derivatives gives \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{4 \sin \theta \cos \theta}{2 \cos^2 \theta - 2 \sin^2 \theta} \nl &= \frac{\sin 2 \theta}{\cos 2 \theta} \pd \ea \] When \(\theta = 3 \pi/2,\) the numerator is \(0\) and the denominator is \(-1.\) Because \(\textderiv{y}{\theta}|_{\theta = \pi} = 0\) and \(\textderiv{x}{\theta}|_{\theta = \pi} \ne 0,\) the graph of \(r = 2 \sin \theta\) has a horizontal tangent when \(\theta = 3 \pi/2.\)
EXERCISE 13
For
\[\ds r = \cos 3 \theta \cma \theta = \frac{\pi}{3}\]
calculate the slope of the line tangent to the polar graph at the given angle.
SOLUTION Because \(r\) is differentiable, the graph's slope at \(\theta = \pi/3\) is \(\textderiv{y}{x}|_{\theta = \pi/3}.\) In terms of \(\theta,\) we have \[\deriv{y}{x} = \frac{\textderiv{y}{\theta}}{\textderiv{x}{\theta}} \pd\] We express \(x\) and \(y\) as functions of \(\theta \col\) \[x = (\cos 3 \theta) \cos \theta \lspace y = (\cos 3 \theta) \sin \theta \pd\] Thus, the slope is \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} \intEval_{\theta = \pi/3} &= \frac{\cos \theta \, \cos 3 \theta - 3 \sin \theta \, \sin 3 \theta}{- \sin \theta \, \cos 3 \theta - 3 \sin 3 \theta \, \cos \theta} \intEval_{\theta = \pi/3} \nl &= \frac{\cos \tfrac{\pi}{3} \cos \pi - 3 \sin \tfrac{\pi}{3} \, \sin \pi}{- \sin \tfrac{\pi}{3} \, \cos \pi - 3 \sin \pi \, \cos \tfrac{\pi}{3}} \nl &= \boxed{-\tfrac{\sqrt 3}{3}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 14
For
\[\ds r = 1 - 2 \sin \theta \cma \theta = \frac{\pi}{2}\]
calculate the slope of the line tangent to the polar graph at the given angle.
SOLUTION Since \(r\) is differentiable, the graph's slope at any value \(\theta\) is \(\textderiv{y}{x}.\) We express this quantity in terms of \(\theta\) using \[\deriv{y}{x} = \frac{\textderiv{y}{\theta}}{\textderiv{x}{\theta}} \pd\] Our first objective is to write functions for \(x\) and \(y\) in terms of \(\theta \col\) \[x = (1 - 2 \sin \theta) \cos \theta \and y = (1 - 2 \sin \theta) \sin \theta \pd\] So \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{\cos \theta - 4 \sin \theta \cos \theta}{-\sin \theta - 2 \cos^2 \theta + 2 \sin^2 \theta} \nl &= \frac{\cos \theta - 2 \sin 2 \theta}{-\sin \theta - 2 \cos 2 \theta} \pd \ea \] We therefore obtain \[\deriv{y}{x} \intEval_{\theta = \pi/2} = \frac{\cos \tfrac{\pi}{2} - 2 \sin \pi}{-\sin \tfrac{\pi}{2} - 2 \cos \pi} = 0 \pd\] Since \(\textderiv{y}{x} = 0\) at \(\theta = \pi/2,\) the graph has a horizontal tangent at that point. Alternatively, we see that \(\textderiv{y}{\theta}|_{\theta = \pi/2} = 0\) and \(\textderiv{x}{\theta}|_{\theta = \pi/2} \ne 0,\) giving us the same conclusion.
EXERCISE 15
For
\[\ds r = \sqrt \theta \cma \theta = \pi\]
calculate the slope of the line tangent to the polar graph at the given angle.
SOLUTION The slope of the differentiable polar graph at \(\theta = \pi\) is \(\textderiv{y}{x}|_{\theta = \pi}.\) We use \[\deriv{y}{x} = \frac{\textderiv{y}{\theta}}{\textderiv{x}{\theta}} \pd\] To begin, we express \(x\) and \(y\) as functions of \(\theta \col\) \[x = \sqrt \theta \cos \theta \lspace y = \sqrt \theta \sin \theta \pd\] We have \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{\sqrt \theta \cos \theta + (\sin \theta)/(2 \sqrt \theta)}{- \sqrt \theta \sin \theta + (\cos \theta)/(2 \sqrt \theta)} \scol \nl \deriv{y}{x} \intEval_{\theta = \pi} &= \frac{\sqrt \pi \cos \pi + (\sin \pi)/(2 \sqrt \pi)}{- \sqrt \pi \sin \pi + (\cos \pi)/(2 \sqrt \pi)} \nl &= \boxed{2 \pi} \ea \]
EXERCISE 16
For
\[\ds r = \sin 2 \theta \cma \theta = \frac{\pi}{2}\]
calculate the slope of the line tangent to the polar graph at the given angle.
SOLUTION The function \(r\) is differentiable, so the graph's slope at \(\theta = \pi/2\) is \(\textderiv{y}{x}|_{\theta = \pi/2}.\) Using \(\theta,\) we have \[\deriv{y}{x} = \frac{\textderiv{y}{\theta}}{\textderiv{x}{\theta}} \pd\] We want \(\textderiv{y}{x} |_{\theta = \pi/2}.\) We express \(x\) and \(y\) as functions of \(\theta \col\) \[x = (\sin 2 \theta) \cos \theta \lspace y = (\sin 2 \theta) \sin \theta \pd\] Thus, the slope is \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} \intEval_{\theta = \pi/2} &= \frac{\cos \theta \, \sin 2 \theta + 2 \sin \theta \, \cos 2 \theta}{-\sin \theta \, \sin 2 \theta + 2 \cos \theta \, \cos 2 \theta} \intEval_{\theta = \pi/2} \nl &= \frac{\cos \tfrac{\pi}{2} \, \sin \pi + 2 \sin \tfrac{\pi}{2} \, \cos \pi}{-\sin \tfrac{\pi}{2} \, \sin \pi + 2 \cos \tfrac{\pi}{2} \, \cos \pi} \pd \ea \] The denominator is \(0\) and the numerator is \(-2.\) Thus, the graph has a vertical tangent at \(\theta = \pi/2.\)
EXERCISE 17
Determine an equation of the line tangent to the graph of \(r = 2\) when \(\theta = -\pi/4.\)
SOLUTION For the equation of the tangent line, we need the slope of the graph at \(\theta = - \pi/4\) as well as its \(x\)- and \(y\)-coordinates. We have \[x = 2 \cos \theta \and y = 2 \sin \theta \] We obtain \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{\textderiv{y}{\theta}}{\textderiv{x}{\theta}} \nl &= \frac{2 \cos \theta}{-2 \sin \theta} \nl &= -\cot \theta \pd \ea \] Thus, the slope at \(\theta = -\pi/4\) is \(-\cot(-\pi/4) = 1.\) When \(\theta = 5 \pi/6,\) \[ \ba x &= 2 \cos \par{-\tfrac{\pi}{4}} = \sqrt 2 \nl y &= 2 \sin \par{-\tfrac{\pi}{4}} = - \sqrt 2 \pd \ea \] Thus, an equation of the tangent line is \[y - (-\sqrt 2) = 1 (x - \sqrt 2) \or \boxed{y = x - 2\sqrt 2}\]
EXERCISE 18
Find an equation of the line tangent to the graph of \(r = 4 \cos \theta\) when \(\theta = 5 \pi/6.\)
SOLUTION To construct an equation, we need the slope of the graph at \(\theta = 5 \pi/6\) as well as the \(x\)- and \(y\)-coordinates. We have \[x = 4 \cos^2 \theta \and y = 4 \sin \theta \cos \theta\] We obtain \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{\textderiv{y}{\theta}}{\textderiv{x}{\theta}} \nl &= \frac{4 \cos^2 \theta - 4 \sin^2 \theta}{-8 \sin \theta \cos \theta} \nl &= -\frac{\cos 2 \theta}{\sin 2 \theta} \pd \ea \] So \[\deriv{y}{x} \intEval_{\theta = 5 \pi/6} = -\frac{\cos \tfrac{5 \pi}{3}}{\sin \tfrac{5 \pi}{3}} = \tfrac{\sqrt 3}{3} \pd\] At \(\theta = 5 \pi/6\) \[ \ba x &= 4 \cos^2 \tfrac{5 \pi}{6} = 3 \nl y &= 4 \sin \tfrac{5 \pi}{6} \, \cos \tfrac{5 \pi}{6} = -\sqrt 3 \pd \ea \] Thus, an equation of the tangent line is \[y - (-\sqrt 3) = \tfrac{\sqrt 3}{3} (x - 3) \or \boxed{y = -\sqrt 3 + \tfrac{\sqrt 3}{3} (x - 3)}\]
EXERCISE 19
The polar graph of \(r = k\) is a circle of radius \(\abs k\) centered at the origin. Prove that the slope of the line tangent to the circle at any angle \(\theta\) is \(-\cot \theta.\)
SOLUTION In terms of \(\theta,\) we write \(x\) and \(y\) as \[x = k \cos \theta \and y = k \sin \theta \pd\] The slope of the tangent to the circle is \(\textderiv{y}{x};\) we find \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{\textderiv{y}{\theta}}{\textderiv{x}{\theta}} \nl &= \frac{k \cos \theta}{-k \sin \theta} \nl &= -\cot \theta \pd \ea \] Hence, at any \(\theta\) the slope of the circle is \(-\cot \theta,\) as given.
EXERCISE 20
Consider the circle \(r = k \sin \theta.\) Prove that the slope of the line tangent to the circle at any angle \(\theta\) is \(\tan 2\theta.\)
SOLUTION We write \(x\) and \(y\) as \[x = k \sin \theta \cos \theta \and y = k \sin^2 \theta \pd\] We find the slope at any \(\theta\) to be \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{\textderiv{y}{\theta}}{\textderiv{x}{\theta}} \nl &= \frac{2k \sin \theta \cos \theta}{k \cos^2 \theta - k \sin^2 \theta} \nl &= \frac{\sin 2 \theta}{\cos 2 \theta} \nl &= \tan 2 \theta \cma \ea \] as requested. Thus, at any \(\theta\) the slope of the tangent to the circle is \(\tan 2\theta.\)
EXERCISE 21
Prove that the slope of the line tangent to the circle \(r = k \cos \theta\) at any angle \(\theta\) is \(-\cot 2\theta.\)
SOLUTION We write \(x\) and \(y\) as \[x = k \cos^2 \theta \and y = k \sin \theta \cos \theta \pd\] The slope of the tangent to the circle is \(\textderiv{y}{x};\) we find \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{\textderiv{y}{\theta}}{\textderiv{x}{\theta}} \nl &= \frac{k \cos^2 \theta - k \sin^2 \theta}{-2 k \cos \theta \sin \theta} \nl &= \frac{\cos 2 \theta}{- \sin 2 \theta} \nl &= -\cot 2 \theta \cma \ea \] as requested. So at any \(\theta,\) the slope of the tangent to the circle is \(-\cot 2\theta.\)
EXERCISE 22
A particle travels along the polar graph of \(r = 1 + 3 \sin \theta\) with time \(t\) such that \(\textderiv{\theta}{t} = 2\) radians per second. Find and interpret \(\textderiv{r}{t}|_{\theta = \pi/3}.\)
SOLUTION We must relate \(\textderiv{r}{t}\) to the quantities we can find: \(\textderiv{r}{\theta}\) and \(\textderiv{\theta}{t} = 2.\) (See Section 3.1 to review the procedure of working with related rates.) By the Chain Rule, \[ \ba \deriv{r}{t} &= \deriv{r}{\theta} \deriv{\theta}{t} \nl &= 2 \deriv{r}{\theta} \pd \ea \] Note that \[\deriv{r}{\theta} = 3 \cos \theta \cma\] so \(\textderiv{r}{\theta} |_{\theta = \pi/3}\) \(= 3/2.\) Hence, \[\deriv{r}{t} \intEval_{\theta = \pi/3} = 2 \par{\tfrac{3}{2}} = \boxed 3\] Also, \(r(\pi/3) \gt 0.\) Since \(r\) and \(\textderiv{r}{\theta}\) have the same sign, the particle is getting farther from the pole. Therefore, the particle moves away from the pole at a rate of \(3\) units per second.
EXERCISE 23
The polar graph of \(r = \sin 3 \theta\) is a rose of three petals. Find the vertex of the petal in the first quadrant.
SOLUTION The graph has a vertex in the first quadrant when \(r\) is maximized. We notice that \(r = 0\) when \(\theta = 0\) and \(\theta = \pi/3,\) meaning the full petal is traced over this interval. Thus, we find the absolute maximum of \(r = 3 \sin \theta\) over \(0 \leq \theta \leq \pi/3.\) (See Section 3.2 to review the procedure for locating absolute extrema.) Differentiating \(r\) and equating it to \(0\) give \[r' = 3 \cos 3 \theta = 0 \implies \theta = \tfrac{\pi}{6} \pd\] Hence, the absolute maximum of \(r\) in the first quadrant occurs either at the critical number \(\theta = \pi/6,\) or at either endpoint \(\theta = 0\) and \(\theta = \pi/3.\) We check each: \[ \ba r(0) &= \sin 0 = 0 \nl r \par{\tfrac{\pi}{6}} &= \sin \tfrac{\pi}{2} = 1 \nl r \par{\tfrac{\pi}{3}} &= \sin \pi = 0 \pd \ea \] Thus, \(r\) is maximized when \(\theta = \pi/6.\) So the vertex is \((r, \theta)\) \(= \boxed{(1, \pi/6)}.\)
EXERCISE 24
Identify the point on the polar graph \(r = 2 + \sin \theta\) that is farthest from the origin.
SOLUTION Note that \(r \gt 0\) for all \(\theta.\) Hence, we seek the absolute maximum of \(r\) for \(0 \leq \theta \leq 2 \pi.\) (See Section 3.2 to review locating absolute extrema.) Differentiating \(r\) and equating it to \(0\) give \[r' = \cos \theta = 0 \implies \theta = \tfrac{\pi}{2} \cma \tfrac{3 \pi}{2} \pd\] Hence, the absolute maximum of \(r\) occurs either at either critical number \(\theta = \pi/2\) or \(\theta = 3 \pi/2,\) or at either endpoint \(\theta = 0\) or \(\theta = 2 \pi.\) We check each: \[ \ba r(0) &= 2 + \sin 0 = 2 \nl r \par{\tfrac{\pi}{2}} &= 2 + \sin \tfrac{\pi}{2} = 3 \nl r \par{\tfrac{3\pi}{2}} &= 2 + \sin \tfrac{3\pi}{2} = 1 \nl r(2 \pi) &= 2 + \sin 2 \pi = 2 \pd \ea \] Thus, \(r\) is maximized when \(\theta = \pi/2.\) So the farthest point is \((r, \theta)\) \(= \boxed{(3, \pi/2)}.\)
EXERCISE 25
The shape of a horse racetrack is given by the polar graph \(r = 2 + 2 \cos \theta,\) where \(r\) is measured in meters. An bell tower is located at the pole. A horse runs counterclockwise along the track at a constant angular speed of \(8\) radians per minute. When \(\theta = 3 \pi/2,\) is the horse moving toward or away from the bell tower?
SOLUTION The horse's angular speed is \(\textderiv{\theta}{t} =\) \(8 \undiv{rad}{sec}.\) Our objective is connect \(\textderiv{r}{t}\) to the quantities we can find: \(\textderiv{r}{\theta}\) and \(\textderiv{\theta}{t} = 8.\) (See Section 3.1 to review the procedure of working with related rates.) By the Chain Rule, \[ \ba \deriv{r}{t} &= \deriv{r}{\theta} \deriv{\theta}{t} \nl &= 8 \deriv{r}{\theta} \pd \ea \] We obtain \[\deriv{r}{\theta} = - 2 \sin \theta \cma\] so \(\textderiv{r}{\theta} |_{\theta = 3 \pi/2}\) \(= 2.\) Hence, \[\deriv{r}{t} \intEval_{\theta = 3\pi/2} = 8(2) = 16 \pd\] Also, \(r(3\pi/2) = 2.\) Since \(r\) and \(\textderiv{r}{t}\) have the same sign, the horse is moving away from the bell tower.
EXERCISE 26
The family of polar functions \(r = a + b \sin \theta\) produces a limacon for nonzero \(a\) and \(b\) such that \(a \ne b.\) Show that the limacon has a horizontal tangent when \(\theta = \pi/2.\)
SOLUTION The quantity \(\textderiv{y}{x}\) in terms of \(\theta\) is \[\deriv{y}{x} = \frac{\textderiv{y}{\theta}}{\textderiv{x}{\theta}} \pd\] The graph of \(r = a + b \sin \theta\) has a horizontal tangent if \(\textderiv{y}{\theta} = 0\) and \(\textderiv{x}{\theta} \ne 0.\) In other words, we show that the numerator of \(\textderiv{y}{x}\) is \(0\) but the denominator is nonzero. We have \[x = (a + b \sin \theta) \cos \theta \and y = (a + b \sin \theta) \sin \theta \pd\] The ratio of derivatives is therefore \[\deriv{y}{x} = \frac{(a + b \sin \theta) \cos \theta + 2b \sin \theta \cos \theta}{-(a + b \sin \theta) \sin \theta + b \cos^2 \theta} \pd\] When \(\theta = \pi/2,\) the numerator is \(0\) and the denominator is \(b - a \ne 0.\) Thus, the family of graphs \(r = a + b \sin \theta\) has a horizontal tangent when \(\theta = 0.\)
EXERCISE 27
For nonzero \(a,\) show that the family of cardioids \(r = a + a \cos \theta\) has a vertical tangent when \(\theta = 0.\)
SOLUTION The quantity \(\textderiv{y}{x}\) in terms of \(\theta\) is \[\deriv{y}{x} = \frac{\textderiv{y}{\theta}}{\textderiv{x}{\theta}} \pd\] The graph of \(r = a + a \cos \theta\) has a vertical tangent if \(\textderiv{x}{\theta} = 0\) and \(\textderiv{y}{\theta} \ne 0.\) In other words, we show that the denominator of \(\textderiv{y}{x}\) is \(0\) but the numerator is nonzero. We have \[x = (a + a \cos \theta) \cos \theta \and y = (a + a \cos \theta) \sin \theta \pd\] The ratio of derivatives is therefore \[\deriv{y}{x} = \frac{(a + a \cos \theta) \cos \theta - a \sin^2 \theta}{-(a + a \cos \theta) \sin \theta - a \cos \theta \sin \theta} \pd\] When \(\theta = 0,\) the numerator is \(2a \ne 0\) and the denominator is \(0.\) Hence, the family of graphs \(r = a + a \cos \theta\) has a vertical tangent when \(\theta = 0.\)
