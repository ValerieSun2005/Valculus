9.3: Polar Coordinates and Functions

In this section we introduce a new coordinate system—polar coordinates. We cover the following:

  • Introducing Polar Coordinates and converting between Cartesian equations and polar equations
  • Properties of Symmetry in polar functions
  • The polar graphs of Circles, Lines, Spirals, Limacons and Cardioids, Roses, and Lemniscates

Polar Coordinates

So far, we have used Cartesian coordinates (or rectangular coordinates), which represent a point in the \(xy\)-plane as \((x, y).\) We now use polar coordinates, which represent a point in a plane in terms of an angle \(\theta\) and distance \(r\) from the origin. In Figure 1, a point in the plane is represented in polar coordinates as \((r, \theta).\) The angle \(\theta\) is formed between the horizontal—called the polar axis—and the line segment that connects the pole (denoted \(O\)) to point \((r, \theta).\) The angle \(\theta\) is positive if it extends in the counterclockwise direction; otherwise, \(\theta\) is negative if it extends in the clockwise direction.

From Figure 1, we see \begin{equation} x = r \cos \theta \lspace y = r \sin \theta \pd \label{eq:polar-x-y} \end{equation} Moreover, \(\theta = \tan^{-1} (y/x)\) and \(r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}.\) These formulas allow us to convert from polar coordinates to Cartesian coordinates, and vice versa.

If \(r\) is replaced by \(-r,\) then the line segment that connects \(O\) to \((r, \theta)\) is rotated \(\pi\) radians about the origin, as in Figure 2. We write this new point either as \((-r, \theta)\) or \((r, \theta + \pi).\)

EXAMPLE 1
Plot the following polar coordinates.
  1. \((4, \, \pi/4)\)
  2. \((7, \, 5\pi/6)\)
  3. \((-7, \, 5\pi/6)\)
  1. We draw an angle that begins at the positive \(x\)-axis—the polar axis—and extends \(\pi/4\) radian in the counterclockwise direction. Then we draw a line segment that begins at the pole \((O)\) and extends \(4\) units. This point is labeled \(A\) in Figure 3.
  2. From the positive \(x\)-axis, we draw an angle that extends \(5 \pi/6\) radians in the counterclockwise direction. The line segment needs to extend \(7\) units from the pole. This point is labeled \(B\) in Figure 3.
  3. We follow the same procedure in (ii) but rotate the line segment \(\pi\) radians about the pole because \(r \lt 0.\) Hence, we obtain point \(C.\)

A polar graph is a graph defined by a polar function, \(r = f(\theta).\) For an input \(\theta,\) the distance from the pole is \(r = f(\theta).\) Many curves are best defined using polar functions. In addition, \(\eqref{eq:polar-x-y}\) enables us to define a polar curve using parametric functions, in which the central angle \(\theta\) is the parameter. To convert a polar function to a Cartesian equation, we want to express \(r\) and \(\theta\) solely in terms of \(x\) and \(y.\) The steps of conversion are as follows.

CONVERTING FROM POLAR TO CARTESIAN
  1. Apply \(\eqref{eq:polar-x-y}\) [\(x = r \cos \theta\) and \(y = r \sin \theta\)] to express trigonometric functions in terms of \(x\) and \(y.\)
  2. Use \(r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\) to rewrite \(r\) in terms of \(x\) and \(y.\)
  3. Use \(\theta = \tan^{-1}(y/x)\) to express any remaining terms of \(\theta\) in terms of \(x\) and \(y.\)

Conversely, to convert a Cartesian equation to polar, we employ a similar strategy—expressing all \(x\) and \(y\) in terms of \(r\) and \(\theta.\) The steps are as follows.

CONVERTING FROM CARTESIAN TO POLAR
  1. Apply \(\eqref{eq:polar-x-y}\) [\(x = r \cos \theta\) and \(y = r \sin \theta\)] to express \(x\) and \(y\) in terms of \(r\) and \(\theta.\)
  2. Convert any remaining terms of \(r\) to \(x\) and \(y\) by using \(r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\) and \(\tan \theta = y/x.\)
  3. Isolate \(r\) if possible.
EXAMPLE 2
Find a Cartesian equation for the graph of the polar function \(r = \sec \theta \tan \theta.\)
We want to eliminate \(r\) and \(\theta\) to write the polar function entirely in terms of \(x\) and \(y.\) It is a good idea to rewrite the trigonometric expression in terms of sine and cosine. We get \[r = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos^2 \theta} \pd\] Then we use \(\eqref{eq:polar-x-y}\) [\(x = r \cos \theta\) and \(y = r \sin \theta\)] to write \(\sin \theta\) as \(y/r\) and \(\cos^2 \theta\) as \(x^2/r^2 \col\) \[ \ba r &= \frac{y/r}{x^2/r^2} \nl r &= \frac{ry}{x^2} \pd \ea \] We attain \[1 = \frac{y}{x^2} \or \boxed{y = x^2}\] Thus, the graph of the polar function \(r = \sec \theta \tan \theta\) is the same as the graph of \(y = x^2\) (Figure 4).
EXAMPLE 3
Represent the graph of \(x - 1 = (y/x)^2\) as a polar function.
Our goal is to eliminate \(x\) and \(y\) and solve for \(r\) in terms of \(\theta.\) We write \(y/x\) as \(\tan \theta\) and \(x\) as \(r \cos \theta,\) obtaining \[ \ba r \cos \theta - 1 &= \tan^2 \theta \nl r \cos \theta &= \tan^2 \theta + 1 \pd \ea \] Since \(\tan^2 \theta + 1 = \sec^2 \theta,\) we have \[r \cos \theta = \sec^2 \theta \or \boxed{r = \sec^3 \theta}\] The graph of this function is in Figure 5.

Symmetry

Understanding symmetry helps us sketch graphs of polar functions. We must first discuss how to transform polar coordinates. Recall the following properties of symmetry:

  • The points \((x, y)\) and \((x, -y)\) are symmetrical about the \(x\)-axis.
  • The points \((x, y)\) and \((-x, y)\) are symmetrical about the \(y\)-axis.
  • The points \((x, y)\) and \((-x, -y)\) are symmetrical about the origin.
In polar coordinates we may refer to the \(x\)-axis as the polar axis, the \(y\)-axis as the line \(\theta = \pi/2,\) and the origin as the pole.

From a trigonometry class, you may remember that the angle \(-\theta\) is a mirror of angle \(\theta\) across the \(x\)-axis. Thus, the point \((r, -\theta)\) is a reflection of \((r, \theta)\) across the \(x\)-axis. If a polar function \(f\) satisfies \(f(\theta) = f(-\theta),\) then the graph of \(r = f(\theta)\) is said to be symmetric about the \(x\)-axis. (See Figure 6.)

On the other hand, the point \((r, \pi - \theta)\) is a reflection of \((r, \theta)\) across the \(y\)-axis. If a polar function \(f\) satisfies \(f(\theta) = f(\pi - \theta),\) then the graph of \(r = f(\theta)\) is said to be symmetric about the \(y\)-axis (or about the line \(\theta = \pi/2\)), as in Figure 7.

The point \((-r, \theta)\) is a rotation of \((r, \theta)\) by \(180 \degree\) about the origin. These points are said to be symmetrical about the origin. Hence, if the polar equation \(r = f(\theta)\) is unchanged if we replace \(r\) with \(-r,\) then we conclude that the graph is symmetrical about the origin. (See Figure 8.)

SYMMETRY OF POLAR GRAPHS
Let \(f\) be a polar function.
  • Property 1 If \(f(\theta) = f(-\theta),\) then the graph of \(r = f(\theta)\) is symmetric about the \(x\)-axis.
  • Property 2 If \(f(\theta) = f(\pi -\theta),\) then the graph of \(r = f(\theta)\) is symmetric about the \(y\)-axis.
  • Property 3 If \(f(\theta) = -f(\theta),\) then the graph of \(r = f(\theta)\) is symmetric about the origin.

Circles in Polar Form

A simple polar graph is a circle of radius \(|k|\) centered at the origin. In Cartesian coordinates, this circle's equation is \(x^2 + y^2 = k^2.\) But we can convert this form to polar by using \(\eqref{eq:polar-x-y}\) [\(x = r \cos \theta\) and \(y = r \sin \theta\)]: Noting that \(\cos^2 \theta + \sin^2 \theta = 1,\) we obtain \begin{align} [r\cos \theta]^2 + [r \sin \theta]^2 &= k^2 \nonumber \nl r^2 (\cos^2 \theta + \sin^2 \theta) &= k^2 \nonumber \nl r &= |k| \pd \label{eq:polar-circle} \end{align} This equation is intuitive: at any value of \(\theta,\) the length of the line segment connecting the pole to a point on the circle is \(|k|.\) (See Figure 9.) Representing this circle is easier in polar form than in Cartesian form.

EXAMPLE 4
Plot the graph of the polar function \(r = 2 \cos \theta.\) Find a Cartesian equation for this circle in standard form.

The key idea is to obtain values of \(r = 2 \cos \theta\) for some values of \(\theta,\) and plot and connect the corresponding points \((r, \theta).\) We assemble a table with convenient values of \(\theta,\) as follows.

\(\theta\) \(0\) \(\ds \frac{\pi}{6}\) \(\ds \frac{\pi}{4}\) \(\ds \frac{\pi}{3}\) \(\ds \frac{\pi}{2}\) \(\ds \frac{2 \pi}{3}\) \(\ds \frac{3 \pi}{4}\) \(\ds \frac{5 \pi}{6}\) \(\pi\)

The graph makes a full loop in \(\pi\) radians. For \(\theta \gt \pi,\) the graph of \(r = 2 \cos \theta\) stacks on top of itself. We therefore plot the graph in Figure 10.

The standard form of a circle in Cartesian form is \(\par{x - x_0}^2 + \par{y - y_0}^2 = k^2,\) where the circle is centered at \(\par{x_0, y_0}\) with a radius of \(k.\) We use the conversion formulas in \(\eqref{eq:polar-x-y}\) [\(x = r \cos \theta\) and \(y = r \sin \theta\)] and \(r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\) to convert \(r = 2 \cos \theta\) into \begin{align} r &= 2 \, \frac{x}{r} \nonumber \nl r^2 &= 2x \nonumber \nl x^2 + y^2 &= 2x \nonumber \pd \end{align} We then complete the square to get \[x^2 - 2x + \orange{1} + y^2 = \orange 1 \implies \boxed{(x - 1)^2 + y^2 = 1} \] This circle is centered at \((x, y) =\) \((1, 0)\) with a radius of \(1.\)

We now generalize Example 4 by considering the graph of the family of polar functions \(r = k \cos \theta.\) We employ the identical strategy to convert \(r = k \cos \theta\) to a Cartesian equation: Replacing \(\cos \theta\) with \(x/r\) and \(r^2\) with \(x^2 + y^2\) gives \begin{align*} r &= k \, \frac{x}{r} \nl r^2 &= kx \nl x^2 + y^2 &= kx \pd \end{align*} We then complete the square to find \begin{align} x^2 - kx + \orange{\frac{k^2}{4}} + y^2 &= \orange{\frac{k^2}{4}} \nonumber \nl \par{x - \frac{k}{2}}^2 + y^2 &= \frac{k^2}{4} \pd \label{eq:polar-kcostheta} \end{align} Hence, the graph of the polar function \(r = k \cos \theta\) is a circle centered at \((x, y) = \par{k/2, \, 0}\) with a radius of \(|k/2|.\) By \(\eqrefer{eq:polar-kcostheta},\) if \(k \lt 0,\) then the circle is centered on the negative \(x\)-axis; if \(k \gt 0,\) then the circle is centered on the positive \(x\)-axis. Figure 11 shows a pair of circles: the left circle is the graph of \(r = k \cos \theta\) for \(k \lt 0,\) and the right circle is the graph of \(r = k \cos \theta\) for \(k \gt 0.\)

Likewise, we can convert \(r = k \sin \theta\) to a Cartesian equation using a similar approach. We replace \(\sin \theta\) with \(y/r\) and complete the square, attaining \begin{align} r &= k \, \frac{y}{r} \nonumber \nl r^2 &= ky \nonumber \nl x^2 + y^2 &= ky \nonumber \nl x^2 + y^2 - ky + \orange{\frac{k^2}{4}} &= \orange{\frac{k^2}{4}} \nonumber \nl x^2 + \par{y - \frac{k}{2}}^2 &= \frac{k^2}{4} \pd \label{eq:polar-ksintheta} \end{align} Thus, graphing \(r = k \sin \theta\) produces a circle centered at \((x, y) = \par{0, k/2}\) with a radius of \(|k/2|.\) Similar to the case of \(\eqref{eq:polar-kcostheta},\) in \(\eqref{eq:polar-ksintheta}\) the circle \(r = k \sin \theta\) is centered on the positive \(y\)-axis if \(k \gt 0\) and on the negative \(y\)-axis if \(k \lt 0.\) In Figure 12 the top circle represents the graph of \(r = k \sin \theta\) if \(k \gt 0,\) and the bottom circle represents the same graph for \(k \lt 0.\)

Lines in Polar Form

Suppose a line passes through the origin with slope \(m.\) Then the angle between the positive \(x\)-axis and the line is constant; hence, in polar form \(\theta\) is fixed and \(r\) doesn't change with \(\theta.\) In Cartesian form, the equation of this line is \(y = mx.\) Dividing both sides by \(x\) gives \(m = y/x.\) Since \(\tan \theta = y/x,\) we have \(m = \tan \theta,\) or \begin{equation} \theta = \tan^{-1} m \pd \label{eq:polar-line} \end{equation} In words, this form asserts that the angle between the positive \(x\)-axis and a line that crosses the origin is the inverse tangent of the line's slope. (See Figure 13.) If the line is steep with a positive slope, then \(m\) is large and so \(\theta\) is also large. Conversely, if the line has a negative slope, then \(m\) and \(\theta\) are both negative.

In Cartesian coordinates, a vertical line is of the form \(x = c.\) We convert this form to polar by replacing \(x\) with \(r \cos \theta\) and solving for \(r \col\) \begin{align} r \cos \theta &= c \nonumber \nl r &= c \sec \theta \pd \label{eq:polar-vertical-line} \end{align} In Figure 14, if you trace the vertical line upward—that is, as \(\theta\) approaches \(\pi/2\)—then \(r\) increases. In fact, \(\eqrefer{eq:polar-vertical-line}\) proves this observation because \(\lim_{\theta \to (\pi/2)^-} (c \sec \theta) = \infty.\) Similarly, \(\lim_{\theta \to (-\pi/2)^+} (c \sec \theta)\) \(= \infty,\) so the distance between the pole and the vertical line is increasing infinitely if you trace the vertical line downward. And when \(\theta = 0,\) the line segment connecting the pole to \((r, \theta)\) is a straight, horizontal line of length \(r = c.\)

Through a similar procedure, we represent the horizontal line \(y = c\) in polar form by replacing \(y\) with \(r \sin \theta \col\) \begin{align} r \sin \theta &= c \nonumber \nl r &= c \csc \theta \pd \label{eq:polar-horizontal-line} \end{align} (See Figure 15.) As you trace the horizontal line in the direction of positive \(x,\) the angle \(\theta\) is shrinking to \(0.\) During this process the distance between the horizontal line and the point \(O\) is increasing to infinity, which we confirm by noting that \(\eqrefer{eq:polar-horizontal-line}\) satisfies \(\lim_{\theta \to 0^+} r = \infty.\) Likewise, \(\lim_{\theta \to \pi^-} r = \infty,\) so if we trace the horizontal line in the direction of negative \(x,\) then the distance between \(O\) and the horizontal line is also increasing to infinity. When \(\theta = \pi/2,\) the line segment connecting \(O\) to the point \((r, \theta)\) is vertical and has length \(r = c.\)

In \(\eqref{eq:polar-vertical-line}\) if \(c \lt 0,\) then the polar graph of \(r = c \sec \theta\) represents the line \(x = -c.\) Likewise, in \(\eqref{eq:polar-horizontal-line}\) if \(c \lt 0,\) then the polar graph of \(r = c \csc \theta\) is equivalent to the line \(y = -c.\)

Spirals

Many graphs aren't expressed more easily in Cartesian coordinates. One example of a graph that is best expressed in polar form is a spiral (also called an Archimedean spiral).

EXAMPLE 7
Sketch the graph of the polar function \(r = \theta.\)
We note that \(r\) is increasing directly with \(\theta.\) Hence, plotting some values of \((r, \theta)\) shows that the graph is a spiral that passes through the pole (Figure 16).

We now generalize this example. An Archimedean spiral is represented by the polar function \begin{equation} r = a + b \theta \pd \label{eq:polar-spiral} \end{equation} This form asserts that a particle's position from its starting point is proportional to the angle \(\theta.\) Positive \(a\) shifts the spiral toward the positive \(x\)-axis, and negative \(a\) shifts the spiral toward the negative \(x\)-axis. The spiral in \(\eqref{eq:polar-spiral}\) curls in the counterclockwise direction if \(b \gt 0,\) and in the clockwise direction if \(b \lt 0.\) In Example 7, \(r\) increases as \(\theta\) increases—meaning each point on the graph of \(r = \theta\) grows farther away from the pole as we trace the angle \(\theta\) in the counterclockwise direction. So we say the graph of \(r = \theta\) curls in the counterclockwise direction.

If you wrap a strand of rope around your finger and spin your finger, then the uncoiling motion of the string forms a shape that resembles an Archimedean spiral. Attempting to write \(\eqrefer{eq:polar-spiral}\) as a Cartesian equation yields \[\sqrt{x^2 + y^2} = a + b \tan^{-1} \par{\frac{y}{x}} \cma \] a more complicated form. This form is very difficult to visualize, whereas \(\eqref{eq:polar-spiral}\) is much easier to understand.

THE ARCHIMEDEAN SPIRAL
The graph of \(r = a + b \theta\) forms an Archimedean spiral.
  • If \(a \gt 0,\) then the spiral begins at the positive \(x\)-axis. If \(a \lt 0,\) then the spiral begins at the negative \(x\)-axis.
  • If \(b \gt 0,\) then the spiral curls in the counterclockwise direction. If \(b \lt 0,\) then the spiral curls in the clockwise direction.

Limacons and Cardioids

EXAMPLE 8
Sketch the graph of the polar function \(r = 1 + \cos \theta.\)

We need to determine how \(r\) varies with \(\theta\) to sketch the polar curve. It is easiest to first graph \(r = 1 + \cos \theta\) in Cartesian coordinates (Figure 17). We note four key stages:

  • During stage I, \(r\) decreases from \(2\) to \(1\) as \(\theta\) increases from \(0\) to \(\pi/2.\) Hence, two points on the polar curve are \((r, \theta) = (2, 0)\) and \((r, \theta) = (1, \pi/2).\) Note the decreasing distance between \((r, \theta)\) and the pole. We connect these points using an arc that extends over these points.
  • In II \(r\) is decreasing, and \(r = 0\) when \(\theta = \pi.\) Thus, the graph of the polar curve hits the origin when \(\theta = \pi.\) We therefore draw an arc that connects \((r, \theta)\) \(= \par{1, \pi/2}\) to \((r, \theta)\) \(= \par{0, \pi}.\)
  • In III, \(r\) increases from \(0\) to \(1\) as \(\theta\) changes from \(\pi\) to \(3 \pi/2.\) Accordingly, on the polar curve we draw an arc connecting \((r, \theta)\) \(= \par{0, \pi}\) to \((r, \theta)\) \(= (1, 3\pi/2).\)
  • Lastly, in IV \(r = 1 + \cos \theta\) finishes a complete revolution. So we close the loop on the polar graph.
These remarks allow us to sketch the graph in Figure 18. If we wished to graph \(r = 1 + \cos \theta\) for \(\theta \gt 2 \pi,\) then we would retrace our path.

Alternatively, we could have noticed that, because cosine is an even function, \[1 + \cos \theta = 1 + \cos(-\theta) \pd\] Hence, the polar graph of \(r = 1 + \cos \theta\) is symmetrical about the \(x\)-axis by Property 1. So we could have traced the upper half of the graph \((0 \leq \theta \leq \pi)\) and mirrored it across the \(x\)-axis to obtain the bottom half of the graph \((\pi \leq \theta \leq 2\pi).\)

EXAMPLE 9
Sketch the graph of the polar function \(r = 1 + 2 \sin \theta.\)
To understand the behavior of \(r\) as \(\theta\) changes, we first plot the graph of \(r = 1 + 2 \sin \theta\) in Cartesian coordinates (Figure 19). Six phases are of interest to us:
  • In stage I, \(r\) increases from \(1\) to \(3\) as \(\theta\) increases from \(0\) to \(\pi/2.\) Thus, the polar graph of \(r = 1 + 2 \sin \theta\) passes through the points \((r, \theta) = (1, 0)\) and \((r, \theta) = (3, \pi/2).\) We draw an arc that connects these points.
  • In II, \(r\) decreases from \(3\) to \(1\) as \(\theta\) increases from \(\pi/2\) to \(\pi.\) So the polar curve passes through points \((r, \theta) = (3, \pi/2)\) and \((r, \theta) = (1, \pi).\)
  • In III, the Cartesian graph of \(r = 1 + 2 \sin \theta\) has a zero when \(\theta = 7 \pi/6.\) The polar graph therefore crosses the pole when \(\theta = 7 \pi/6.\) So we connect the point \((r, \theta) = (1, \pi)\) to \((r, \theta) = (0, 7 \pi/6).\)
  • In IV, \(r\) decreases from \(0\) to \(-1\) as \(\theta\) increases from \(7 \pi/6\) to \(3 \pi/2.\) Accordingly, the polar curve passes through \((r, \theta) = (-1, 3\pi/2).\) Since \(r \lt 0,\) this point is a reflection of the point \((1, 3 \pi/2)\) by \(\pi\) radians—giving us a point on the positive \(y\)-axis.
  • In V, \(r\) has another zero when \(\theta = 11 \pi/6.\) So we draw an arc connecting the polar curve from \((r, \theta) = (-1, 3\pi/2)\) to the pole at \(\theta = 11 \pi/6.\) Steps IV and V trace an inner loop of the graph.
  • Because \((r, \theta) = (1, 2 \pi)\) represents the same point as \((r, \theta) = (1, 0),\) we close the graph of the polar curve in step VI.
We therefore plot the polar curve in Figure 20.

We now generalize these examples. Polar graphs of the forms \[r = a \pm b \cos \theta \and r = a \pm b \sin \theta\] are called limacons. The graph of \(r = a \pm b \cos \theta\) is symmetric about the \(x\)-axis and is called a horizontal limacon. In contrast, a vertical limacon is symmetric about the \(y\)-axis and is represented by the function \(r = a \pm b \sin \theta.\) The maximum value of \(r\) is \(a + b,\) and the minimum value of \(r\) is \(a - b.\) We have two types of limacons:

  • If \(a \geq b,\) then the limacon has only one loop. If \(a = b,\) then the graph is called a cardioid, a special kind of limacon shaped like a heart—as in Example 8, where \(a = b = 1.\)
  • If \(a \lt b,\) then the limacon has an inner loop. For example, the polar function in Example 9 has \(a = 1\) and \(b = 2;\) its graph therefore has an inner loop.

Roses

We now graph polar curves whose loops resemble a rose's petals. Roses exhibit one or more types of symmetry.

EXAMPLE 10
Sketch the graph of the polar function \(r = \cos 2 \theta.\)

We first plot the graph of \(r = \cos 2 \theta\) in Cartesian coordinates (Figure 21). We analyze eight stages:

  • In stage I, \(r\) decreases from \(1\) to \(0\) as \(\theta\) increases from \(0\) to \(\pi/4.\) Hence, on the polar graph of \(r = \cos 2 \theta\) we plot two points: \((r, \theta) = (1, 0)\) and \((r, \theta) = (0, \pi/4).\) The arc connecting these points forms half a petal.
  • In II \(r\) is negative for \(\pi/4 \lt \theta \leq \pi/2,\) dropping to \(-1\) when \(\theta = \pi/2.\) The points on the polar graph therefore lie on the opposite side of the first quadrant—in the third quadrant, forming a half-petal that begins at the origin and terminates at the line \(\theta = 3 \pi/2.\)
  • During III \(r\) increases from \(-1\) to \(0\) as \(\theta\) increases from \(\pi/2\) to \(3 \pi/4.\) Since \(r\) is negative in this interval, instead of the points on the polar graph \(r = \cos 2 \theta\) residing in the second quadrant, they are on the opposite side, in the fourth quadrant. These points trace a half-petal that starts at the line \(\theta = 3\pi/2\) and ends at the origin. Stages II and III form a full petal of the graph on the negative \(y\)-axis.
  • In IV \(r\) increases from \(0\) to \(1\) as \(\theta\) increases from \(3 \pi/4\) to \(\pi.\) Thus, points on the polar graph of \(r = \cos 2 \theta\) trace a half-petal in the second quadrant that begins at the origin and terminates at the line \(\theta = \pi.\)
  • In V \(r\) drops from \(1\) to \(0\) as \(\theta\) increases from \(\pi\) to \(5 \pi/4.\) Accordingly, in the third quadrant the polar graph of \(r = \cos 2 \theta\) traces a half-petal that begins at the line \(\theta = \pi\) and ends at the origin. Stages IV and V therefore form a full petal that resides on the negative \(x\)-axis.
  • In VI the polar graph runs through points \((r, \theta) = (0, 5 \pi/4)\) and \((r, \theta) = (-1, 3\pi/2).\) Since \(r \lt 0,\) instead of the points tracing an arc in the third quadrant, they form an arc in the first quadrant that begins at the origin and ends at the positive \(y\)-axis.
  • During VII \(r\) increases from \(-1\) to \(0\) as \(\theta\) increases from \(3\pi/2\) to \(7 \pi/4.\) Because \(r \lt 0,\) the arc of the polar graph—instead of residing in the fourth quadrant—traces out a half-petal in the second quadrant. Stages VI and VII trace a full petal that lies on the positive \(y\)-axis.
  • In VIII the polar graph passes through the points \((r, \theta) = (0, 7\pi/4)\) and \((r, \theta) = (1, 2\pi).\) Hence, this last arc culminates the petal that lies on the positive \(x\)-axis.
We therefore sketch the graph in Figure 22.

Alternatively, a simpler option is to exploit the symmetry of the graph: The function \(r = \cos 2 \theta\) satisfies Property 1 because \[\cos 2 \theta \equalsCheck \cos(-2 \theta) \cma\] so the polar graph of \(r = \cos 2 \theta\) is symmetric about the \(x\)-axis. Moreover, the function satisfies Property 2: \[\cos 2 \theta \equalsCheck \cos[2(\pi - \theta)] \pd\] Thus, the graph is symmetric about the \(y\)-axis. From these remarks, we can sketch the half-petal in stage I, reflect a copy across the \(x\)-axis (obtaining VIII), and then reflect both halves across the \(y\)-axis (to get IV and V). Doing so gives us the two petals that lie on the \(x\)-axis. To obtain the vertical petals, we can sketch stage II of the polar graph. Reflecting a copy of this half-petal across the \(y\)-axis gives III, and reflecting both halves across the \(x\)-axis yields portions VII and VI. This entire procedure covers all values of \(\theta\) and therefore produces the full graph of \(r = \cos 2 \theta.\) When we apply symmetry, we must ensure that our strategy covers all values of \(\theta;\) otherwise, we may miss portions of the graph.

First graphing \(r = \sin 3 \theta\) in Cartesian coordinates (Figure 23), we remark the following properties.

We now generalize these examples. The polar graph of \begin{equation} r = \cos n \theta \or r = \sin n \theta \label{eq:polar-rose} \end{equation} forms a rose whose center is at the origin. We consider \(n\) to be a positive integer. Then we have two cases: when \(n\) is odd and when \(n\) is even. If \(n\) is odd, then the graph of either function in \(\eqref{eq:polar-rose}\) has \(n\) petals. Conversely, if \(n\) is even, then either graph in \(\eqref{eq:polar-rose}\) has \(2 n\) petals. For example, in Example 10 we see \(n = 2,\) so the graph has \(4\) petals. The function in Example 11 has \(n = 3,\) so its graph has \(3\) petals. In Figure 25, two graphs of \(r = \sin n \theta\) are shown: The first graph has even \(n,\) so its graph has \(2n\) petals. Because the rose has eight petals, \(n = 8/2 = 4.\) Conversely, in the second graph \(n\) is odd, so the graph has \(n\) petals. The rose has \(5\) petals, so \(n = 5.\) Note that since the graphs take the form \(r = \sin n \theta,\) they cross through \((r, \theta) = (0, 0).\)

POLAR ROSES
If \(n\) is a positive integer, then the graphs of \[r = \cos n \theta \and r = \sin n \theta\] are roses.
  1. If \(n\) is odd, then the graph has \(n\) petals.
  2. If \(n\) is even, then the graph has \(2n\) petals.

Lemniscates

A lemniscate is an \(\infty\)-shaped curve. It is formed by either equation \[r^2 = a^2 \cos 2 \theta \or r^2 = a^2 \sin 2 \theta \pd \] We graph a lemniscate by splitting \(r^2\) into two functions of \(r\)—one positive and one negative. Graphing both functions yields the full lemniscate.

Sketch the graph of the polar function \(r^2 = 2 \cos 2 \theta.\)

Both graphs pass through the points \((r, \theta) = (\sqrt 2, 0)\) and \((r, \theta) = (\sqrt 2, \pi).\) At \(\theta = \pi/4\) and \(\theta = 3 \pi/4,\) both graphs hit the pole. The graph of \(r = \sqrt 2 \sqrt{\cos 2 \theta}\) forms the upper half of the lemniscate, and the graph of \(r = -\sqrt 2 \sqrt{\cos 2 \theta}\) forms the bottom half. (See Figure 26.)

Polar Coordinates Polar coordinates represent a point in a plane as \((r, \theta).\) Here, \(r\) is the length of the line that connects the pole (the origin) to point \((r, \theta),\) and \(\theta\) is the angle between the polar axis—the positive \(x\)-axis—and \((r, \theta).\) We convert between polar and Cartesian forms by using \(x = r \cos \theta,\) \(y = r \sin \theta,\) \(r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2},\) and \(\theta = \tan^{-1}(y/x).\)

Symmetry Let \(f\) be a polar function. Then the following properties of symmetry apply:

  • Property 1 If \(f(\theta) = f(-\theta),\) then the graph of \(r = f(\theta)\) is symmetric about the \(x\)-axis.
  • Property 2 If \(f(\theta) = f(\pi -\theta),\) then the graph of \(r = f(\theta)\) is symmetric about the \(y\)-axis.
  • Property 3 If \(f(\theta) = -f(\theta),\) then the graph of \(r = f(\theta)\) is symmetric about the origin.

Spirals A spiral (or Archimedean spiral) is the graph of \(r = a + b \theta,\) where \(a\) and \(b\) are constants.

  • If \(a \gt 0,\) then the spiral begins at the positive \(x\)-axis. If \(a \lt 0,\) then the spiral begins at the negative \(x\)-axis.
  • If \(b \gt 0,\) then the spiral curls in the counterclockwise direction. If \(b \lt 0,\) then the spiral curls in the clockwise direction.

Limacons and Cardioids The graphs of \(r = a \pm b \cos \theta\) and \(r = a \pm b \sin \theta\) are limacons. The limacon only has one loop if \(a \geq b.\) If \(a = b,\) then the graph is called a cardioid. If \(a \lt b,\) then the limacon has an inner loop.
