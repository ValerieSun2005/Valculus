EXERCISE 1
A triangle has sides of lengths \(a\) and \(b,\) and the angle between them is \(\theta\) (Figure 13). The area of the triangle is \[A = \tfrac{1}{2} ab \sin \theta \pd\] Using calculus, show that the area is maximized when \(\theta = 90 \degree.\)
SOLUTION We differentiate \(A\) with respect to \(\theta\) to obtain \[A'(\theta) = \tfrac{1}{2} a b \cos \theta \pd\] The domain of \(A\) is \(0 \degree \leq \theta \leq 180 \degree;\) we find \(A'(\theta) = 0\) when \(\theta = 90 \degree.\) For \(0 \lt \theta \lt 90 \degree,\) \(A' \gt 0;\) for \(90 \degree \lt \theta \lt 180 \degree,\) \(A' \lt 0.\) Thus, the First Derivative Test asserts that \(A\) has a relative maximum when \(\theta = 90 \degree\) because at that point \(A'\) switches sign from positive to negative. At the endpoints—\(\theta = 0 \degree\) and \(\theta = 180 \degree\)—the triangle collapses into a line, and so \(A = 0.\) We therefore conclude that \(\theta = 90 \degree\) corresponds to the absolute maximum of \(A,\) as requested. This problem shows that arranging two sides perpendicular to each other maximizes the area of the corresponding triangle.
EXERCISE 2
Find two numbers whose sum is \(30\) such that their product is maximized.
SOLUTION Let \(x\) and \(y\) be the two numbers. We want to find the maximum of \begin{equation} P = xy \pd \label{eq:sum-30-prod} \end{equation} We have \begin{equation} x + y = 30 \cma \label{eq:sum-30-sum} \end{equation} from which we solve for one variable—for example, \(y = 30 - x.\) We substitute this expression into \(\eqRefer{eq:sum-30-prod}\) to express \(P\) solely in terms of \(x \col\) \[P = x(30 - x) = 30x - x^2 \pd\] We now find the critical numbers of \(P\) by equating \(P'(x) = 0.\) We find \[ \ba P'(x) = 30 - 2x &= 0 \nl \implies x &= 15 \pd \ea \] We see \(P' \gt 0\) for \(x \lt 15\) and \(P' \lt 0\) for \(x \gt 15.\) Thus, the First Derivative Test asserts that \(x = 15\) corresponds to a relative maximum of \(P.\) Because \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} P = -\infty\) and \(\lim_{x \to \infty} P = -\infty,\) \(P\) needs to have a maximum value, which must occur when \(x = 15.\) We therefore conclude that \(x = 15\) corresponds to the absolute maximum of \(P.\) When \(x = 15,\) \(\eqRefer{eq:sum-30-sum}\) gives \(y = 15.\) The two numbers therefore are \(\boxed{15}\) and \(\boxed{15}.\)
EXERCISE 3
Find two numbers whose difference is \(20\) such that their product is minimized.
SOLUTION If \(x\) and \(y\) represent the two numbers, then we want to minimize \begin{equation} P = xy \pd \label{eq:diff-20-prod} \end{equation} We have \begin{equation} x - y = 20 \cma \label{eq:diff-20-diff} \end{equation} so \(y = x - 20.\) We plug this expression into \(\eqRefer{eq:diff-20-prod}\) to obtain \[P = x(x - 20) = x^2 - 20x \pd \] This expression only contains terms of \(x,\) so we can now differentiate: \[P'(x) = 2x - 20 \pd\] We seek the critical numbers of \(P,\) so solving \(P'(x) = 0\) yields \(x = 10.\) If we visualize the graph of \(y = P(x),\) then we imagine an upward-opening parabola with an absolute minimum at \(x = 10.\) Then \(\eqRefer{eq:diff-20-diff}\) gives \(y = -10.\) We therefore conclude that the two numbers are \(\boxed{-10}\) and \(\boxed{10}.\)
EXERCISE 4
The sum of two positive numbers is \(10.\) What is the smallest possible sum of their squares?
SOLUTION Let the numbers be \(x\) and \(y.\) We have two equations: \begin{align} x + y &= 10 \label{eq:sum-10-10} \nl S &= x^2 + y^2 \pd \label{eq:sum-10-S} \end{align} We want to minimize \(S.\) We first express \(S\) solely in terms of one variable—say, \(x.\) From \(\eqRefer{eq:sum-10-10},\) we find \(y = 10 - x.\) We plug this expression into \(\eqRefer{eq:sum-10-S}\) to obtain \[S = x^2 + (10 - x)^2 = 2x^2 - 20x + 100 \pd\] Differentiating, we find \[S'(x) = 4x - 20 \pd\] At \(x = 5,\) \(S\) has a relative minimum because \[ \ba S'(x) &\lt 0 \for x \lt 5 \nl S'(x) &\gt 0 \for x \gt 5 \pd \ea \] Also, \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} S = \infty\) and \(\lim_{x \to \infty} S = \infty,\) so \(x = 5\) must correspond to an absolute minimum of \(S.\) Then \(\eqRefer{eq:sum-10-10}\) gives \(y = 5,\) and the smallest possible sum is \[S = 5^2 + 5^2 = \boxed{50}\]
EXERCISE 5
Two positive numbers multiply to \(14.\) What is their smallest possible sum?
SOLUTION Let \(x\) and \(y\) be the numbers. We have \begin{equation} xy = 14 \cma \label{eq:14-p} \end{equation} and we want to minimize \begin{equation} S = x + y \pd \label{eq:14-s} \end{equation} To express \(S\) in terms one variable, we use \(\eqRefer{eq:14-p}\) to get \(y = 14/x.\) Then, substituting this expression into \(\eqRefer{eq:14-s},\) we have \begin{equation} S = x + \frac{14}{x} \pd \label{eq:14-s-x} \end{equation} Differentiating \(\eqRefer{eq:14-s-x}\) yields \[S'(x) = 1 - \frac{14}{x^2} \cma\] and we find a positive critical number of \(x = \sqrt{14}.\) For \(x \lt \sqrt{14},\) \(S' \lt 0;\) for \(x \gt \sqrt{14},\) \(S' \gt 0.\) Thus, \(x = \sqrt{14}\) corresponds to a relative minimum of \(S.\) The number \(x\) is given to be positive and so it must lie in \((0, \infty).\) At the ends of this interval, from \(\eqRefer{eq:14-s-x}\) we see \[\lim_{x \to 0^+} S = \infty \and \lim_{x \to \infty} S = \infty \pd\] Thus, \(x = \sqrt{14}\) must correspond to the absolute minimum of \(S.\) Then \(\eqRefer{eq:14-p}\) gives \(y = 14/\sqrt{14} = \sqrt{14}.\) The smallest possible sum is therefore \[S = \sqrt{14} + \sqrt{14} = \boxed{2\sqrt{14}}\]
EXERCISE 6
The number of Tapioca balls in a cylindrical cup of boba is modeled by the function \[b(h) = h \sqrt{81 - h^2} \cma \] where \(h\) is measured in inches from the top of the cup. At what height is the number of Tapioca balls the largest?
SOLUTION Note that the cup is \(9\) inches tall because the domain of \(b(h)\) in context is \(0 \leq h \leq 9.\) The absolute minimum of \(b\) is obtained at the endpoints because \(b(0) = b(9) = 0.\) We now locate the critical numbers of \(b\) by letting \(b'(h) = 0 \col \) \[ \ba b'(h) = \frac{81 - 2h^2}{\sqrt{81 - h^2}} &= 0 \nl \implies h &= \frac{9}{\sqrt{2}} \pd \ea \] We note the following: For \(0 \lt h \lt 9/\sqrt{2},\) \(b' \gt 0;\) for \(9/\sqrt{2} \lt h \lt 9,\) \(b' \lt 0.\) Thus, \(h = 9/\sqrt{2}\) corresponds to a relative maximum of \(b;\) in fact, it corresponds to the absolute maximum. We therefore conclude that the number of Tapioca balls is greatest at a distance of \(9/\sqrt{2} \approx 6.364\) inches from the top of the cup.
EXERCISE 7
In a rectangular pond of uniform depth, the density of salmon per cubic meter is modeled by the function \[\rho(r) = 200r^2 e^{-4r} \cma \] where \(r\) is measured in meters from the shoreline and \(\rho(r)\) is measured in salmon per cubic meter. How far from the shoreline should a fisherman fish in order to catch the most salmon?
SOLUTION The fisherman wants to fish in the area in which the density of salmon is highest. Thus, we want to maximize \(\rho(r).\) Its derivative is \[\rho'(r) = 400r e^{-4r} - 800r^2 e^{-4r} \pd\] Letting \(\rho'(r) = 0,\) we find \(r = 0\) and \(r = 0.5.\) At \(r = 0,\) \(\rho'(r)\) does not change sign, so \(\rho\) does not have an extremum there. At \(r = 0.5,\) \(\rho'(r)\) switches sign from positive to negative; thus, \(r = 0.5\) corresponds to a relative maximum of \(\rho.\) The domain of \(\rho\) is \(0 \leq r \lt \infty.\) We see \(\rho(0) = 0,\) so the fisherman should not fish at \(r = 0.\) To consider \(\rho\) as \(r \to \infty,\) we use L'Hôpital's rule to find \[ \ba \lim_{r \to \infty} \rho(r) &= \lim_{r \to \infty} \frac{200r^2}{e^{4r}} \nl &= \lim_{r \to \infty} \frac{400r}{4e^{4r}} \nl &= \lim_{r \to \infty} \frac{400}{16e^{4r}} \nl &= 0 \pd \ea \] Thus, \(\rho\) has its absolute maximum when \(r = 0.5.\) The fisherman therefore should fish at a distance of \(\boxed{0.5 \un{meter}}\) from the shoreline.
EXERCISE 8
A uniform, thin rod of length \(30\) centimeters is pivoted at a point \(x\) centimeters from its center of gravity as it oscillates (see Figure 14). This device is called a physical pendulum. The equation for its period, the time it takes to complete one full revolution, in seconds, is \[T = 2 \pi \sqrt{\frac{(0.01x)^2 + 0.3^2/12}{9.8(0.01x)}} \pd \] Find the value of \(x\) for which the period is minimized.
SOLUTION If \(f(x)\) has an extremum at \(x = a,\) then \(\sqrt{f(x)}\) also has an extremum at \(x = a.\) Thus, we choose to minimize \(T^2\) because its derivative is easier to compute. Let \(q(x) = T^2.\) Then \[q'(x) = \frac{4 \pi^2}{9.8(0.01)} \par{(0.01)^2 - \frac{(0.3)^2}{12x^2}} \pd\] We find \(q'(x) = 0\) when \(x = \sqrt{\frac{(0.3)^2}{12(0.01)^2}},\) whose value is approximately \(8.66 \un{cm}.\) We now consider extreme values of \(x \col\) We see \(\lim_{x \to 0^+} T = \infty\) and \(\lim_{x \to \infty} T = \infty.\) Thus, \(x = \boxed{8.66 \un{cm}}\) minimizes the period of the physical pendulum.
EXERCISE 9
At a party, identical speakers are on either side of a room that is \(8\) meters wide. If a dancer stands \(x\) meters from one speaker, then the sound intensity she experiences is \[I = k \par{\frac{1}{x^2} + \frac{1}{(8 - x)^2}} \cma\] where \(k\) is a constant of proportionality. Where in the room should the dancer stand to experience the loudest sound?
SOLUTION We must maximize \(I.\) Differentiating gives \[\deriv{I}{x} = k \par{\frac{-2}{x^3} + \frac{2}{(8 - x)^3}} \pd\] We solve for \(\textDeriv{I}{x} = 0,\) as follows: \[ \ba k \par{\frac{-2}{x^3} + \frac{2}{(8 - x)^3}} &= 0 \nl \frac{2}{x^3} &= \frac{2}{(8 - x)^3} \nl x^3 &= (8 - x)^3 \nl x &= 4 \pd \ea \] The sign of \(\textDeriv{I}{x}\) switches from negative to positive when \(x = 4,\) so this value corresponds to a relative minimum of \(I.\) We want to maximize \(I,\) so we have no further consideration of this extremum. The domain of \(I\) is \(0 \lt x \lt 8;\) we see \[\lim_{x \to 0^+} I = \infty \qquad \lim_{x \to 8^-} I = \infty \pd\] Thus, sound intensity is maximized at either \(x = 0\) or \(x = 8.\) The dancer therefore should stand right next to either speaker.
EXERCISE 10
Farmer Ana has \(24\) feet of fence to build a closed, rectangular pen for her animals. She uses the side of her farmhouse as one side of the pen. Find the maximum possible area of the pen.
SOLUTION Drawing a sketch, we call \(\ell\) the pen's length and \(w\) pen's width. The perimeter is \(2w + \ell\) and must equal \(24 \col\) \begin{equation} 2w + \ell = 24 \pd \label{eq:pen-p} \end{equation} Our goal is to maximize \begin{equation} A = w \ell \cma \label{eq:pen-a} \end{equation} which we express in terms of one variable. From \(\eqRefer{eq:pen-p},\) we find \(\ell = 24 - 2w.\) We substitute this expression into \(\eqRefer{eq:pen-a}\) to obtain \begin{equation} A = w(24 - 2w) = 24w - 2w^2 \pd \label{eq:pen-Aw} \end{equation} Since \(A\) is now expressed in terms of \(w,\) we differentiate to find \[A'(w) = 24 - 4w \pd\] At \(w = 6\) we find a critical point of \(A.\) For \(w \lt 6,\) \(A' \gt 0;\) for \(w \gt 6,\) \(A' \lt 0.\) Thus, \(w = 6\) corresponds to a relative maximum of \(A.\) The question is now whether \(w = 6\) corresponds to the absolute maximum of \(A.\) And it does, because the domain of \(A\) is \(0 \leq w \leq 12;\) at each endpoint \(A = 0\) by \(\eqRefer{eq:pen-Aw}.\) Then \(\eqRefer{eq:pen-p}\) gives \(\ell = 24 - 2(6) = 12,\) meaning the pen's area is maximized by instating a width of \(6 \un{feet}\) and length of \(12 \un{feet}.\) The area is then \[6 \un{ft} \cdot 12 \un{ft} = \boxed{72 \un{ft}^2}\]
EXERCISE 11
Find the point on the line \(y = 2x\) that is closest to the point \((4, 3).\)
SOLUTION Let \((x, y)\) be a point on the line. The horizontal distance from \((x, y)\) to \((4, 3)\) is \(4 - x.\) The vertical distance between \((x, y)\) and \((4, 3)\) is \(y - 3.\) Thus, the distance from the point to the line is \begin{equation} d = \sqrt{(4 - x)^2 + (y - 3)^2} \pd \label{eq:line-distance} \end{equation} We want to express \(d\) entirely in terms of one variable, \(x.\) Since \(y = 2x,\) \(\eqRefer{eq:line-distance}\) becomes \[d = \sqrt{(4 - x)^2 + (2x - 3)^2} \cma\] the expression we want to minimize. The critical numbers of \(d\) and \(d^2\) are the same, so for simplicity we find the critical numbers of \(d^2.\) Let \(q(x) = d^2;\) then \[q'(x) = -2(4 - x) + 4(2x - 3) \pd\] We solve \(q'(x) = 0,\) finding \(x = 2.\) Since \(q'\) changes sign from negative to positive at \(x = 2,\) this value corresponds to a relative minimum of \(q\) (and, therefore, of \(d\)). The domain of \(q\) is \(-\infty \lt x \lt \infty;\) as \(x\) approaches each endpoint, \(q \to \infty.\) Thus, \(x = 2\) must be the absolute minimum of \(q\) (and, hence, of \(d\)). Then \(y = 2(2) = 4,\) so the point \(\boxed{(2, 4)}\) on the line is closest to \((4, 3).\)

An open-top tank with a square base is manufactured to hold \(2000 \un{mL}\) of water. What dimensions of the tank minimize its surface area? (\(1 \un{mL}\) = \(1 \un{cm}^3.\))
SOLUTION We label each side of the tank's square base as \(x.\) Also let \(y\) be the tank's height. The tank's volume is then \begin{equation} x^2 y = 2000 \pd \label{eq:tank-V} \end{equation} The tank has an open top, so its surface area is \begin{equation} S = x^2 + 4xy \cma \label{eq:tank-S} \end{equation} the expression we wish to minimize. From \(\eqRefer{eq:tank-V},\) we obtain \(y = 2000/x^2.\) Substituting this expression into \(\eqRefer{eq:tank-S}\) then produces \[S = x^2 + 4x \par{\frac{2000}{x^2}}= x^2 + \frac{8000}{x} \pd\] Differentiating gives \[S'(x) = 2x - \frac{8000}{x^2} \pd\] We solve \(S'(x) = 0,\) as follows: \[ \ba 2x - \frac{8000}{x^2} &= 0 \nl 2x^3 &= 8000 \nl x &= \sqrt[3]{4000} \approx 15.87 \un{cm} \pd \ea \] Then \(y = 2000/(15.87)^2 \approx 7.94 \un{cm}.\) Thus, the tank should be \(15.87 \un{cm}\) by \(15.87 \un{cm}\) by \(7.94 \un{cm}.\)

EXERCISE 17
A trapezoidal trough of length \(L\) is designed. Three sides of the trapezoidal face have length \(1\) (Figure 15). What value of \(\theta\) maximizes the trough's volume?
SOLUTION We first determine the area of the trough's trapezoidal face. This trapezoid has two triangles and a square. Each triangle has area \(\tfrac{1}{2} \sin \theta \cos \theta,\) and the square has area \(1 \cdot \cos \theta.\) Thus, the area of the trapezoidal face is \[ \ba A &= 2 \par{\tfrac{1}{2} \sin \theta \cos \theta} + \cos \theta \nl &= \sin \theta \cos \theta + \cos \theta \pd \ea \] We use the identity \(\sin \theta \cos \theta = \tfrac{1}{2} \sin 2 \theta\) to obtain \[ A = \tfrac{1}{2} \sin 2 \theta + \cos \theta \pd \label{eq:trough-A} \] The trough's volume is then \begin{equation} V = (\tfrac{1}{2} \sin 2 \theta + \cos \theta) \, L \pd \label{eq:trough-V} \end{equation} Differentiating \(\eqRefer{eq:trough-V}\) yields \begin{equation} V'(\theta) = L(\cos 2 \theta - \sin \theta) \pd \label{eq:trough-V'} \end{equation} We locate the critical numbers of \(V\) by letting \(V'(\theta) = 0.\) We want to write \(V'(\theta)\) entirely in terms of one trigonometric function—the simplest being sine. Combining the double-angle identity for cosine with the Pythagorean Identity, we find \[ \ba \cos 2 \theta &= \cos^2 \theta - \sin^2 \theta \nl &= (1 - \sin^2 \theta) - \sin^2 \theta \nl &= 1 - 2 \sin^2 \theta \pd \ea \] \(\eqRefer{eq:trough-V'}\) then becomes \[V'(\theta) = L(1 - 2 \sin^2 \theta - \sin \theta) \pd\] We equate this expression to \(0,\) finding \[-2 \sin^2 \theta - \sin \theta + 1 = 0 \cma\] from which we get \[\sin \theta = \tfrac{1}{2} \qquad \sin \theta = -1 \pd\] In the first case \(\theta = \pi/6.\) The second case yields \(\theta = 3\pi/2,\) a clear extraneous solution in this context—and so we discard it. We note that \(V'\) changes sign from positive to negative when \(\theta = \pi/6,\) so this value corresponds to a relative maximum of \(V.\) The domain of \(V\) is \(0 \leq \theta \leq \pi/2,\) and at each endpoint the trough collapses into a straight line. So we conclude that \(\theta = \boxed{\pi/6}\) corresponds to the absolute maximum of \(V,\) maximizing the volume of the trough.

SOLUTION Let \((x, y)\) be one vertex of the rectangle.
