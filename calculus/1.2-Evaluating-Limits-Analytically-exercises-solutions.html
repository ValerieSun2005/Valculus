1.2 Exercise Solutions — Evaluating Limits Analytically

EXERCISE 1
Define Direct Substitution.
SOLUTION Direct Substitution allows us to evaluate the limit of a function by simply evaluating the function at the number the input is approaching. Mathematically, this property gives \[\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a) \pd\] In this section we use Direct Substitution for polynomial and rational functions \(f\) if \(a\) is within the domain of \(f.\)
EXERCISE 2
Define the indeterminate form \(\bf \indZero\). What does this form imply (or not imply) about a limit?
SOLUTION A limit \[L = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \cmaa g(x) \ne 0\] is of the indeterminate form \(\bf \indZero\) if \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = 0\) and \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = 0.\) A limit in this form cannot initially be evaluated by Direct Substitution, which would yield the undefined expression \(\indZero.\) This form simply means that further analysis is required to evaluate the limit; it does not immediately imply the limit fails to exist.
EXERCISE 3
Let \(f\) and \(g\) be functions that satisfy \[\lim_{x \to 7} f(x) = 5 \and \lim_{x \to 7} g(x) = -4 \pd\] Calculate the following limits.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 7} \parbr{f(x) + g(x)}\)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 7} \parbr{2 f(x) - 3 g(x)}\)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 7} \parbr{\tfrac{1}{2} f(x) g(x)}\)
  4. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 7} \parbr{\frac{2 f(x) - 3 g(x)}{4 f(x) + 4g(x)}}\)
SOLUTION
  1. Using the Sum Law, we get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 7} \parbr{f(x) + g(x)} &= \lim_{x \to 7} f(x) + \lim_{x \to 7} g(x) \nl &= 5 + (-4) = \boxed 1 \ea \]
  2. Combining the Difference Law with the Constant Multiple Law, we see \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 7} \parbr{2 f(x) - 3 g(x)} &= \lim_{x \to 7} \parbr{2 f(x)} - \lim_{x \to 7} \parbr{3 g(x)} \nl &= 2 \lim_{x \to 7} f(x) - 3 \lim_{x \to 7} g(x) \nl &= 2(5) - 3(-4) = \boxed{22} \ea \]
  3. Using the Constant Multiple Law in conjunction with the Product Law, we get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 7} \parbr{\tfrac{1}{2} f(x) g(x)} &= \tfrac{1}{2} \lim_{x \to 7} \parbr{f(x) g(x)} \nl &= \tfrac{1}{2} \lim_{x \to 7} f(x) \cdot \lim_{x \to 7} g(x) \nl &= \tfrac{1}{2} \par{5 \cdot -4} = \boxed{-10} \ea \]
  4. Let's analyze this limit one step at a time, as follows: \[ \baat{2} \lim_{x \to 7} \parbr{\frac{2 f(x) - 3 g(x)}{4 f(x) + 4g(x)}} &= \frac{\ds \lim_{x \to 7} \parbr{2 f(x) - 3 g(x)}}{\ds \lim_{x \to 7} \parbr{4 f(x) + 4g(x)}} \comment{\text{by Quotient Law}} \nl &= \frac{22}{\ds \lim_{x \to 7} \parbr{4 f(x) + 4g(x)}} \comment{\text{from part (b)}} \nl &= \frac{22}{\ds 4 \lim_{x \to 7} \parbr{f(x) + g(x)}} \comment{\text{by Constant Multiple Law}} \nl &= \frac{22}{4 (1)} \comment{\text{from part (a)}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{11}{2}} \eaat \]
EXERCISE 4
Suppose that functions \(f\) and \(g\) satisfy \[ \baat{4} &\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) &&= 6 &\and &\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) &&= -4 \cma \nl &\lim_{x \to a^-} g(x) &&= -2 &\and &\lim_{x \to a^+} g(x) &&= 8 \pd \eaat \]
  1. Explain why \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x)\) both do not exist.
  2. Calculate \(\lim_{x \to a} \parbr{f(x) + g(x)}\) or justify why it does not exist.
  3. Let \(h(x) = f(x)/g(x).\) Find \(\lim_{x \to a} h(x)\) or show that it does not exist.
SOLUTION
  1. First, \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) does not exist because \[\underbrace{\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x)}_{6} \ne \underbrace{\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)}_{-4} \pd\] Second, \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x)\) does not exist since \[\underbrace{\lim_{x \to a^-} g(x)}_{-2} \ne \underbrace{\lim_{x \to a^+} g(x)}_{8} \pd\]
    2. You cannot argue that a limit fails to exist simply because you cannot use a limit law. (This is a baseless guess.) Instead, try applying the limit laws to the one-sided limits.
  2. We can't use the Sum Law for \(\lim_{x \to a} \parbr{f(x) + g(x)}.\) (This does not, however, allow us to say the limit fails to exist.) But since the one-sided limits exist, we can say \[ \ba \lim_{x \to a^-} \parbr{f(x) + g(x)} &= \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) + \lim_{x \to a^-} g(x) \nl &= 6 + (-2) = 4 \pd \ea \] Likewise, we see \[ \ba \lim_{x \to a^+} \parbr{f(x) + g(x)} &= \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) + \lim_{x \to a^+} g(x) \nl &= (-4) + 8 = 4 \pd \ea \] Since these one-sided limits are equal, we conclude \[\lim_{x \to a} \parbr{f(x) + g(x)} = \boxed 4\]
  3. We can't use the Quotient Law as \[\wrongMath{\lim_{x \to a} h(x) = \frac{\ds \lim_{x \to a} f(x)}{\ds \lim_{x \to a} g(x)}}\] because neither \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) nor \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x)\) exists. But the one-sided limits exist, so we can say \[ \ba \lim_{x \to a^-} h(x) &= \frac{\ds \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x)}{\ds \lim_{x \to a^-} g(x)} \nl &= \frac{6}{-2} = -3 \pd \ea \] Similarly, we have \[ \ba \lim_{x \to a^+} h(x) &= \frac{\ds \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)}{\ds \lim_{x \to a^+} g(x)} \nl &= \frac{-4}{8} = -\frac{1}{2} \pd \ea \] Because \(-3 \ne -1/2,\) we conclude that \(\lim_{x \to a} h(x)\) does not exist.
EXERCISE 5
For the graphs of \(y = f(x)\) and \(y = g(x)\) in Figure 5, calculate the following limits if they exist.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to -2} \parbr{g(x) - f(x)}\)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 4^-} \parbr{2 g(x) - 7 f(x)}\)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} \parbr{f(x) g(x)}\)
  4. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} \parbr{f(x) + g(x)}\)
SOLUTION
  1. The graph shows \[\lim_{x \to -2} g(x) = 0 \and \lim_{x \to -2} f(x) = -2 \pd\] By the Difference Law, we therefore see \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -2} \parbr{g(x) - f(x)} &= \lim_{x \to -2} g(x) - \lim_{x \to -2} f(x) \nl &= 0 - (-2) = \boxed 2 \ea \]
  2. From the graph, we see \[\lim_{x \to 4^-} g(x) = 4 \and \lim_{x \to 4^-} f(x) = -1 \pd\] Hence, by combining the Difference and Constant Multiple Laws, we find \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 4^-} \parbr{2 g(x) - 7 f(x)} &= \lim_{x \to 4^-} \parbr{2 g(x)} - \lim_{x \to 4^-} \parbr{7 f(x)} \nl &= 2 \lim_{x \to 4^-} g(x) - 7 \lim_{x \to 4^-} f(x) \nl &= 2(4) - 7(-1) = \boxed{15} \ea \]
    3. You cannot argue that a limit fails to exist simply because you cannot use a limit law. (This is a baseless guess.) Instead, try applying the limit laws to the one-sided limits.
  3. Observe that \[\lim_{x \to 0} f(x) \and \lim_{x \to 0} g(x)\] both fail to exist. So we cannot use the Product Law for \(\lim_{x \to 0} \parbr{f(x) g(x)},\) but we also cannot immediately conclude that the limit does not exist. Let's use the Product Law for the one-sided limits: We see \[ \baat{3} \lim_{x \to 0^-} f(x) &= -3 &&\and &\lim_{x \to 0^-} g(x) &= 2 \cma \nl \lim_{x \to 0^+} f(x) &= 3 &&\and &\lim_{x \to 0^+} g(x) &= -2 \pd \eaat \] Accordingly, we get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0^-} \parbr{f(x) g(x)} &= \lim_{x \to 0^-} f(x) \cdot \lim_{x \to 0^-} g(x) \nl &= (-3)(2) = -6 \cma \nl \lim_{x \to 0^+} \parbr{f(x) g(x)} &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} f(x) \cdot \lim_{x \to 0^+} g(x) \nl &= (3)(-2) = -6 \pd \ea \] Since the one-sided limits of the product \(f(x) g(x)\) equal each other, we conclude that \[\lim_{x \to 0} \parbr{f(x) g(x)} = \boxed{-6}\]
  4. Both limits \[\lim_{x \to 0} f(x) \and \lim_{x \to 0} g(x)\] fail to exist. We therefore cannot use the Sum Law for \(\lim_{x \to 0} \parbr{f(x) + g(x)}.\) But we also cannot immediately guess that the limit does not exist. Using the appropriate one-sided limits found in part (c), we see \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0^-} \parbr{f(x) + g(x)} &= \lim_{x \to 0^-} f(x) + \lim_{x \to 0^-} g(x) \nl &= -3 + 2 = -1 \cma \nl \lim_{x \to 0^+} \parbr{f(x) + g(x)} &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} f(x) + \lim_{x \to 0^+} g(x) \nl &= 3 + (-2) = 1 \pd \ea \] Because these one-sided limits don't agree with each other, we conclude that \(\lim_{x \to 0} \parbr{f(x) + g(x)}\) does not exist.

EXERCISE 22
Find \(\lim_{x \to 5} f(x)\) if \[ f(x) = \bc \dfrac{6}{x - 4} &x \lt 5 \nl x^2 - 19 &x \gt 5 \pd \ec \]
SOLUTION The limit \(\lim_{x \to 5} f(x)\) exists if and only if \[\lim_{x \to 5^-} f(x) \and \lim_{x \to 5^+} f(x)\] exist and equal each other. [But \(f\) does not need to be defined at \(5\) in order for \(\lim_{x \to 5} f(x)\) to exist.] Because \(f(x) = 6/(x - 4)\) for \(x \lt 5,\) we take the limit of the left-hand piecewise definition as follows: \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 5^-} f(x) &= \lim_{x \to 5^-} \frac{6}{x - 4} \nl &= \frac{6}{5 - 4} = 6 \pd \ea \] Similarly, \(f(x) = x^2 - 19\) for \(x \gt 5,\) so the limit of the right-hand piecewise definition is \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 5^+} f(x) &= \lim_{x \to 5^+} \par{x^2 - 19} \nl &= 5^2 - 19 = 6 \pd \ea \] Hence, \(\lim_{x \to 5} f(x) = \boxed{6}.\)
EXERCISE 23
Let \[ f(x) = \bc x + 3 &x \lt a \nl \sqrt{x^2 + 6} &x \gt a \pd \ec \] For what value of \(a\) does \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) exist?
SOLUTION The limit \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) exists if and only if the one-sided limits \[\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \and \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)\] equal each other. Let's compute each limit. Since \(f(x) = x + 3\) for \(x \lt a,\) Direct Substitution gives \[\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = a + 3 \pd\] Similarly, because \(f(x) = \sqrt{x^2 + 6}\) for \(x \gt a,\) we use the Root Law and Direct Substitution to attain \[\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a^+} \sqrt{x^2 + 6} = \sqrt{a^2 + 6} \pd\] These expressions must equal each other, so we solve for \(a,\) as follows: \[ \ba a + 3 &= \sqrt{a^2 + 6} \nl (a + 3)^2 &= a^2 + 6 \nl a^2 + 6a + 9 &= a^2 + 6 \nl 6a &= -3 \nl a &= \boxed{-\tfrac{1}{2}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 24
Calculate \(k\) so that \(\lim_{x \to 3} f(x)\) exists, where \[ f(x) = \bc kx^2 - 2 &x \lt 3 \nl 6x - k &x \gt 3 \pd \ec \]
SOLUTION The limit \(\lim_{x \to 3} f(x)\) exists if and only if \[\lim_{x \to 3^-} f(x) \and \lim_{x \to 3^+} f(x)\] both exist and equal each other. Since \(f(x) = kx^2 - 2\) for \(x \lt 3,\) we see \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 3^-} f(x) &= \lim_{x \to 3^-} \par{kx^2 - 2} \nl &= 9k - 2 \pd \ea \] Likewise, the right-hand limit is given by \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 3^+} f(x) &= \lim_{x \to 3^+} (6x - k) \nl &= 18 - k \pd \ea \] Equating these expressions shows \[9k - 2 = 18 - k \implies \boxed{k = 2}\]
EXERCISE 25
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to -2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x + 2} \]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION
As we evaluate the limit, it is irrelevant that \(\par{x^2 - 4}/(x + 2)\) is undefined at \(x = -2.\) We only concern ourselves with this function's behavior near \(-2.\)
Trying Direct Substitution immediately gives the indeterminate form \(\indZero,\) so we instead try to find any common factors. The numerator is a difference of squares, \(x^2 - 2^2;\) observe that \[x^2 - 4 = (x + 2)(x - 2) \pd\] Reexpressing the numerator and canceling the common factor \((x + 2)\) give \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -2} \frac{\cancel{(x + 2)}(x - 2)}{\cancel{x + 2}} &= \lim_{x \to -2} (x - 2) \nl &= \boxed{-4} \ea \]
EXERCISE 26
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{x^2}{x}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION
As we evaluate the limit, it is irrelevant that \(x^2/x\) is undefined at \(x = 0.\) We only concern ourselves with this function's behavior near \(0.\)
As \(x \to 0,\) both \(x^2\) and \(x\) approach \(0.\) Direct Substitution gives the meaningless expression \(\indZero,\) so the limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) Observe that \(x^2/x = x\) for \(x \ne 0.\) So the limit becomes \[\lim_{x \to 0} x = \boxed 0\]
EXERCISE 27

\[\ds \lim_{t \to 9} \frac{t^2 - 81}{t - 9}\]

EXERCISE 31
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{\ds \frac{1}{x} - \frac{1}{4}}{x - 4}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION This limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) Let's merge the fractions in the numerator of the limit. Doing so shows \[\frac{1}{x} - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{4}{4x} - \frac{x}{4x} = \frac{4 - x}{4x} \pd\] As a result, our limit becomes \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{\dfrac{4 - x}{4x}}{x - 4} &= \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{-\cancel{(x - 4)}}{(4x)\cancel{(x - 4)}} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{-1}{4x} \nl &= \boxed{-\frac{1}{16}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 32
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\dfrac{2}{x - 2} + 1}{x}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION This limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) Condensing the numerator, we see \[ \ba \frac{2}{x - 2} + 1 &= \frac{2}{x - 2} + \frac{x - 2}{x - 2} \nl &= \frac{2 + (x - 2)}{x - 2} \nl &= \frac{x}{x - 2} \pd \ea \] Therefore, the limit becomes \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\dfrac{x}{x - 2}}{x} &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\cancel{x}}{(x - 2) \cancel x} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{x - 2} \nl &= \boxed{-\frac{1}{2}} \ea \]

\[\ds \lim_{t \to 1} \frac{t - 1}{1 - \sqrt t} \]

\[\ds \lim_{x \to 9} \frac{9x - x^2}{3 - \sqrt x}\]

EXERCISE 35
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to 7} \frac{7 - x}{2 - \sqrt{11 - x}}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION This limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero,\) yet we identify no common factors. But we have a radical in the denominator, so let's try to eliminate it. We'll rationalize the denominator by multiplying the numerator and denominator each by the denominator's conjugate, \((2 + \sqrt{11 - x}).\) Doing so gives \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 7} \frac{7 - x}{2 - \sqrt{11 - x}} \cdot \frac{2 + \sqrt{11 - x}}{2 + \sqrt{11 - x}} &= \lim_{x \to 7} \frac{(7 - x)(2 + \sqrt{11 - x} \, )}{4 - (11 - x)} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 7} \frac{-\cancel{(x - 7)}(2 + \sqrt{11 - x} \, )}{\cancel{x - 7}} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 7} \parbr{-(2 + \sqrt{11 - x} \, )} \nl &= \boxed{-4} \ea \] The purpose of rationalization is to expose a common factor—in this case, \((x - 7).\) By keeping the numerator in factored form (instead of expanding it), we identify the common factor more easily.
EXERCISE 36
For
\[\ds \lim_{u \to 3} \frac{\sqrt{u - 2} - 1}{2u - 6}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION This limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) We have no immediate simplifications of the fraction. But the numerator has a radical, so we attempt to rationalize the numerator by multiplying the numerator and denominator each by the numerator's conjugate, \((\sqrt{u - 2} + 1).\) We get \[ \ba \lim_{u \to 3} \frac{\sqrt{u - 2} - 1}{2u - 6} &= \lim_{u \to 3} \frac{\sqrt{u - 2} - 1}{2u - 6} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{u - 2} + 1}{\sqrt{u - 2} + 1} \nl &= \lim_{u \to 3} \frac{(u - 2) - 1^2}{(2u - 6)(\sqrt{u - 2} + 1)} \nl &= \lim_{u \to 3} \frac{\cancel{u - 3}}{2\cancel{(u - 3)}(\sqrt{u - 2} + 1)} \nl &= \lim_{u \to 3} \frac{1}{2(\sqrt{u - 2} + 1)} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{1}{4}} \ea \] This problem shows that rationalizing may yield a common factor to cancel—exactly what we want!
EXERCISE 37
For
\[\ds \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{4 + h} - 2}{h}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION This limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) The numerator has a radical, so we attempt to rationalize the numerator. Since the numerator's conjugate is \((\sqrt{4 + h} + 2),\) multiplying across shows \[ \ba \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{4 + h} - 2}{h} &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{4 + h} - 2}{h} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{4 + h} + 2}{\sqrt{4 + h} + 2} \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(4 + h) - 4}{h (\sqrt{4 + h} + 2)} \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\cancel{h}}{\cancel{h} (\sqrt{4 + h} + 2)} \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{\sqrt{4 + h} + 2} \nl &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{4 + 0} + 2} = \boxed{\frac{1}{4}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 38
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to 9/4} \frac{\sqrt{4x - 5} - 2}{16x - 36} \]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION This limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) The numerator has a radical, so we attempt to rationalize the numerator. We do so multiplying the numerator and denominator by the conjugate \((\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2),\) obtaining \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 9/4} \frac{\sqrt{4x - 5} - 2}{16x - 36} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2}{\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2} &= \lim_{x \to 9/4} \frac{(4x - 5) - 4}{(16x - 36)(\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2) } \nl &= \lim_{x \to 9/4} \frac{\cancel{4x - 9}}{4\cancel{(4x - 9)}(\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2)} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 9/4} \frac{1}{4(\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2)} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{1}{16}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 39
For
\[\ds \lim_{y \to a} \frac{y^4 - a^4}{y^2 - a^2}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION This limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) The numerator is a difference of squares; we reexpress it as follows: \[y^4 - a^4 = \par{y^2 + a^2} \par{y^2 - a^2} \pd\] So we see \[ \ba \lim_{y \to a} \frac{y^4 - a^4}{y^2 - a^2} &= \lim_{y \to a} \frac{\par{y^2 + a^2} \cancel{\par{y^2 - a^2}}}{\cancel{y^2 - a^2}} \nl &= \lim_{y \to a} \par{y^2 + a^2} \nl &= \boxed{2a^2} \ea \]
EXERCISE 40
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{\sqrt{13 - x} - 3}{\sqrt{x} - 2}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION This limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) The fraction has radicals on the numerator and denominator. Let's rationalize the numerator by multiplying the numerator and denominator each by \((\sqrt{13 - x} + 3).\) Doing so, we see \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{\sqrt{13 - x} - 3}{\sqrt x - 2} &= \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{\sqrt{13 - x} - 3}{\sqrt x - 2} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{13 - x} + 3}{\sqrt{13 - x} + 3} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{(13 - x) - 9}{(\sqrt x - 2)(\sqrt{13 - x} + 3)} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{4 - x}{(\sqrt x - 2)(\sqrt{13 - x} + 3)} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{(2 + \sqrt x)\cancel{(2 - \sqrt x)}}{-\cancel{(2 - \sqrt x)}(\sqrt{13 - x} + 3)} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{2 + \sqrt x}{-(\sqrt{13 - x} + 3)} \nl &= \frac{2 + \sqrt 4}{-(\sqrt{13 - 4} + 3)} \nl &= \boxed{-\frac{2}{3}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 41
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to 10} \frac{\sqrt{26 - x} - 4}{\sqrt{19 - x} - 3}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION Let's start by rationalizing the numerator, although we could also begin by rationalizing the denominator. We multiply the numerator and denominator each by \((\sqrt{26 - x} + 4),\) yielding \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 10} \frac{\sqrt{26 - x} - 4}{\sqrt{19 - x} - 3} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{26 - x} + 4}{\sqrt{26 - x} + 4} &= \lim_{x \to 10} \frac{10 - x}{(\sqrt{19 - x} - 3)(\sqrt{26 - x} + 4)} \pd \nl \ea \] We can't factor this expression any further. Now we multiply the numerator and denominator each by \((\sqrt{19 - x} + 3),\) from which the limit becomes \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 10} \frac{10 - x}{(\sqrt{19 - x} - 3)(\sqrt{26 - x} + 4)} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{19 - x} + 3}{\sqrt{19 - x} + 3} &= \lim_{x \to 10} \frac{\cancel{(10 - x)}(\sqrt{19 - x} + 3)}{\cancel{(10 - x)}(\sqrt{26 - x} + 4)} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 10} \frac{\sqrt{19 - x} + 3}{\sqrt{26 - x} + 4} \nl &= \frac{\sqrt{19 - 10} + 3}{\sqrt{26 - 10} + 4} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{3}{4}} \ea \] In general, if you have radicals on the numerator and denominator, then you should rationalize both if no other resources are available. The order of rationalization does not matter.
