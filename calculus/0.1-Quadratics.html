0.1 — Quadratics

In this section we review Forms and Features of Quadratics, Identifying the Vertex of a Quadratic, Completing the Square, and using the Quadratic Formula and Discriminant. We conclude with solving Quadratic Inequalities.

Forms and Features of Quadratics

A quadratic is a second-degree polynomial whose graph is a parabola: a symmetrical, U-shaped curve. The general form of a quadratic is \begin{equation} f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \label{eq:standard-form} \end{equation}

where \(a\) is the leading coefficient of \(f\) and \((0, c)\) is the \(y\)-intercept, the point at which the graph of \(y = f(x)\) crosses the \(y\)-axis (Figure 1).

The intercept form of a quadratic is \begin{equation} f(x) = a(x - p)(x - q) \comma \label{eq:intercept-form} \end{equation} where \(x = p\) and \(x = q\) are zeros of \(y = f(x)\) (Figure 2).

The vertex form is \begin{equation} f(x) = a(x - h)^2 + k \period \label{eq:vertex-form} \end{equation} Here \((h, k)\) is the vertex, the maximum or minimum the parabola. We call the vertical line \(x = h\) the axis of symmetry since it's the line to which the graph of \(y = f(x)\) is symmetrical. In any of these three forms, if \(a\) is negative, then the parabola opens downward and the vertex is a maximum (Figure 3). Conversely, if \(a\) is positive, then the parabola opens upward and the vertex is a minimum (Figures 1 and 2).

FORMS OF A QUADRATIC EXPRESSION
  • General Form: \[y = ax^2 + bx + c \comma \] where \((0, c)\) is the \(y\)-intercept of the parabola.
  • Vertex Form: \[y = a(x - h)^2 + k \comma \] where \((h, k)\) is the vertex of the parabola.
  • Intercept Form: \[y = a(x - p)(x - q) \comma \] where \(p\) and \(q\) are the zeros of the parabola.
EXAMPLE 1
For each of (i)–(iii), classify the form in which the quadratic is written.
  1. \(y = 4(x - 2)(x + 4)\)
  2. \(y = -2x^2 + 8x - 7\)
  3. \(y = 2(x + 8)^2 - 1\)
  1. Intercept form.
  2. General form.
  3. Vertex form.

Identifying the Vertex of a Quadratic

From \(\eqRefer{eq:standard-form},\) we may find vertex of the graph of \(y = f(x)\) by noting that the axis of symmetry, \(x = h,\) is \[h = \frac{-b}{2a} \period \] Then the \(y\)-coordinate of the vertex is \(k = f(-b/2a).\) For example, the vertex of \[g(x) = x^2 - 2x + 8\] has \(x\)-coordinate \[ \ba h = \frac{-b}{2a} &= \frac{-(-2)}{2(1)} \nl &= 1 \period \ea \] Then the \(y\)-coordinate of the vertex of \(g(x)\) is \[k = (1)^2 - 2(1) + 8 = 7 \period\] Thus, \(g(x)\) has a vertex of \((1 , 7).\)

Completing the Square

We convert general form to vertex form by completing the square. Consider the example \[y = 3x^2 - 9x + 2 \period \] We factor out the leading coefficient, \(3,\) to obtain \[y = 3 \left(x^2 - 3x + \frac{2}{3}\right) \period\] In the parentheses, we insert the term \(9/4,\) the square of half of \(-3;\) that is, \((-3/2)^2 = 9/4.\) But we also subtract \(9/4\) to avoid changing the value of the expression: \[3 \left(x^2 - 3x + \underline{\frac{9}{4}} - \underline{\frac{9}{4}} + \frac{2}{3} \right)\period \] The term \(x^2 - 3x + 9/4\) creates the perfect square \((x - 3/2)^2,\) so we have \[ \ba 3 \left[\left(x - \frac{3}{2}\right)^2 - \frac{9}{4} + \frac{2}{3} \right] &= 3 \left[\left(x - \frac{3}{2}\right)^2 - \frac{19}{12} \right] \nl &= \boxed{3 \left(x - \frac{3}{2}\right)^2 - \frac{19}{4}} \ea \] The strategic insertion of the square of \(9/4\) (half the coefficient of the \(x\)-term) forces the expression into a perfect square that corresponds to vertex form.

COMPLETING THE SQUARE
  1. Factor out the leading coefficient, \(a.\)
  2. Analyze the coefficient of the \(x\)-term, and insert the square of half the coefficient. Be sure to also subtract the quantity to maintain equality.
  3. Verify that you have a perfect square. Simplify the resulting expression to obtain vertex form.
EXAMPLE 2
For each of (i)–(iii), complete the square.
  1. \(y = x^2 - 5x + 6\)
  2. \(y = 3x^2 - 7x + 5\)
  3. \(y = -2x^2 - 8x + 11\)
  1. There is no leading coefficient to factor out, so we can immediately analyze the coefficient of the \(x\)-term, \(-5.\) We need to insert \((-5/2)^2 = 25/4\) into the expression to force the existence of the perfect square \(\left(x - \frac{5}{2} \right)^2 :\) \[ \ba x^2 - 5x + 6 &= x^2 - 5x + \underline{\frac{25}{4}} - \underline{\frac{25}{4}} + 6 \nl &= \left(x - \frac{5}{2}\right)^2 + 6 -\frac{25}{4} \nl &= \boxed{\left(x - \frac{5}{2}\right)^2 - \frac{1}{4}} \ea \]
  2. We must first factor out the leading coefficient, \(3,\) to get \[3 \left(x^2 - \frac{7}{3} x + \frac{5}{3}\right) \period\] The coefficient of the \(x\)-term is \(-7/3.\) Half of the coefficient is \(-7/6,\) whose square is \(49/36.\) Thus, we need to insert this quantity into the parentheses to create the perfect square \(\left(x - \frac{7}{6} \right)^2.\) \[ \ba 3 \left(x^2 - \frac{7}{3} x + \underline{\frac{49}{36}} - \underline{\frac{49}{36}} + \frac{5}{3} \right) &= 3 \left(x - \frac{7}{6} \right)^2 + 3 \left(\frac{5}{3} - \frac{49}{36} \right) \nl &= \boxed{3 \left(x - \frac{7}{6} \right)^2 + \frac{11}{12}} \ea \]
  3. Let us factor out the leading coefficient, \(-2,\) to obtain \[-2 \left(x^2 + 4x - \frac{11}{2} \right) \period\] The coefficient of the linear term is \(4,\) so \((4/2)^2 = 4\) is the quantity we need to insert. We get \[ \ba -2 \left(x^2 + 4x + \underline{4} - \underline{4} - \frac{11}{2}\right) &= -2(x + 2)^2 - 2 \left(-4 - \frac{11}{2}\right) \nl &= \boxed{-2(x + 2)^2 + 19} \ea \]

Quadratic Formula and Discriminant

To find the zeros of a quadratic equation, we factor if possible. But for any nonfactorable quadratic, we may use the quadratic formula: For the quadratic equation \[f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \comma \] where \(a \ne 0,\) the quadratic formula states that \[x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \period\] A quadratic may have two, one, or no zeros, depending on the sign of the discriminant: \(\Delta = b^2 - 4ac,\) the expression in the square root of the quadratic formula.

  • If \(\Delta \gt 0,\) then \(f\) has two real zeros.
  • If \(\Delta = 0,\) then \(f\) has one real zero.
  • If \(\Delta \lt 0,\) then \(f\) has no real zeros.

These facts are intuitive since if \(\Delta \lt 0,\) then the quadratic formula produces a negative square root, which is undefined. Similarly, if \(\Delta = 0,\) then the quantity to which the \(\pm\) sign applies is \(0\) and so the only solution is \(x = -b/2a.\)

Figure 4 shows three parabolas: \(A,\) \(B,\) and \(C.\) Parabola \(A\) has a positive discriminant because it has two zeros, while parabola \(B\) has a discriminant equal to \(0\) since its graph only has one \(x\)-intercept. Lastly, parabola \(C\) never touches the \(x\)-axis and so its equation has negative discriminant.

SOLUTIONS OF A QUADRATIC
The quadratic \[f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c\] has solutions given by the quadratic formula: \[x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \] for nonzero \(a.\) The discriminant is the quantity in the square root, \(\Delta = b^2 - 4ac.\)
  • If \(\Delta \gt 0,\) then the quadratic has two real zeros.
  • If \(\Delta = 0,\) then the quadratic has one real zero.
  • If \(\Delta \lt 0,\) then the quadratic has no real zeros.
EXAMPLE 3
Solve the following equations.
  1. \(x^2 = 16\)
  2. \(x^2 - 4x + 3 = 0\)
  3. \(2x^2 - 4x = 6\)
  4. \(x^2 - 2x + 2 = 0\)
  5. \(3x^2 - 5x - 7 = 0\)
  1. We take the square root of both sides to get \[ \ba x^2 &= 16 \nl |x| &= \sqrt{16} \nl &= \boxed{\pm 4} \ea \]
  2. We factor the equation by identifying two numbers that multiply to \(3\) and add to \(-4.\) They are \(-1\) and \(-3,\) so we have \[(x - 1)(x - 3) = 0 \period\] By the zero-product property of multiplication, if one of these factors equals \(0,\) then the entire product \((x - 1)(x - 3)\) equals \(0.\) Each of \(x = 1\) and \(x = 3\) causes one factor—and, thus, the entire expression—to equal zero. Therefore, \(\boxed{x = 1}\) and \(\boxed{x = 3.}\)
  3. We move all the terms to one side: \[2x^2 - 4x - 6 = 0 \period \] Dividing everything by \(2\) yields \[x^2 - 2x - 3 = 0 \comma \] which we can factor to obtain \[(x - 3)(x + 1) = 0\period \] Thus, \(\boxed{x = -1}\) and \(\boxed{x = 3.}\)
  4. The expression \(x^2 - 2x + 2\) cannot be factored, so we instead resort to either completing the square or using the quadratic formula. There is no leading coefficient, so completing the square is arguably the simplest method. The coefficient of the \(x\)-term is \(-2\) and so we insert the term \((-2/2)^2 = 1:\) \[ \ba \underbrace{x^2 - 2x + \underline{1}}_{(x - 1)^2} - \underline{1} + 2 &= 0 \nl (x - 1)^2 + 1 &= 0 \nl (x - 1)^2 &= -1. \ea \] Note that the term \((x - 1)^2\) is never negative because a term squared is always positive or zero. Likewise, if we take the square root of both sides, we get \[|x - 1| = \sqrt{-1} \comma \] where \(\sqrt{-1}\) is a nonreal quantity. Thus, this equation has no solution. The discriminant for this expression is \[\Delta = b^2 - 4ac = (-2)^2 - 4(1)(2) = -4 \comma \] which is negative. Since a negative discriminant implies that a quadratic has no zeros, this result is affirmed.
  5. For \(3x^2 - 5x - 7 = 0,\) the leading coefficient of \(3\) and uneven numbers make it inconvenient to complete the square, as we did in part (iv). Instead, we will use the quadratic formula: \[ \ba x &= \frac{5 \pm \sqrt{(-5)^2 - 4(3)(-7)}}{2(3)} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{5 \pm \sqrt{109}}{6}.} \ea \]

Quadratic inequalities

Let us now discuss quadratic inequalities. Consider, as an example, the inequality \[x^2 - 5x + 6 \gt 0\period \] Factoring, we get \[(x - 3)(x - 2) \gt 0\period \] The factors \((x - 3)\) and \((x - 2)\) must both be positive or negative. For \(x \lt 2\) both factors are negative; for \(x \gt 3,\) both factors are positive. But for \(2 \lt x \lt 3,\) \[(x - 3)(x - 2) \lt 0 \period\] Therefore, the only correct interval is \(\boxed{x \lt 2 \comma x \gt 3.}\) Alternatively, a shortcut is to consider two intervals: The interval between the zeros, \(2 \lt x \lt 3;\) and the interval outside, \(x \lt 2 \comma x \gt 3.\) For convenience, we can plug \(x = 0\) into the inequality: \[(0 - 3)(0 - 2) \gt 0 \comma \] which is a true inequality and so the interval \(x \lt 2, x \gt 3\) is correct.

SOLVING QUADRATIC INEQUALITIES
To solve a quadratic inequality, follow these steps:
  1. Find the zeros of the quadratic expression.
  2. Identity whether the solution set of the inequality is between the zeros or beyond the zeros. It is easiest to plug in \(x = 0\) to determine the correct interval.
EXAMPLE 4
Solve the following inequalities.
  1. \(x^2 - 2x - 3 \gt 0\)
  2. \(2x^2 - 5x + 3 \geq 0\)
  3. \(x^2 - 4x \leq -3\)
  4. \(2x^2 + 4 \lt 6x\)
  5. \(5x - 2 \gt -4x^2\)
  1. The factors of \[x^2 - 2x - 3 = 0\] are \(x = -1\) and \(x = 3.\) We need to consider two intervals: \[-1 \lt x \lt 3 \or x \lt -1 \comma x \gt 3 \period \] For simplicity we plug \(x = 0\) into the inequality: \[0^2 - 2(0) - 3 \gt 0 \comma \] which isn't true. So the interval \(-1 \lt x \lt 3\) is incorrect, leaving us with \(\boxed{x \lt -1, x \gt 3.}\)
  2. We factor the expression \(2x^2 - 5x + 3\) as \((2x - 3)(x - 1),\) whose its zeros are \(x = 1\) and \(x = 3/2.\) We test two intervals: \[1 \lt x \lt \frac{3}{2} \and x \lt 1 \comma x \gt 3/2 \period \] At \(x = 0\) the inequality becomes \[2(0)^2 - 5(0) + 3 \geq 0 \comma \] which is true. So the correct interval is \(\boxed{x \lt 1, x \gt 3/2.}\)
  3. We first move all the terms to one side to obtain \[x^2 - 4x + 3 \leq 0\period \] The zeros of \(x^2 - 4x + 3\) are \(x = -3\) and \(x = -1.\) We test the intervals \[-3 \lt x \lt -1 \and x \lt -3 \comma x \gt -1.\] At \(x = 0\) we have \[(0)^2 - 4(0) + 3 \leq 0\period \] This inequality is false, so the interval \(-3 \lt x \lt -1\) is invalid. Thus, the interval \(\boxed{x \lt -3, x \gt -1}\) is correct.
  4. We move each term to one side to get \[2x^2 - 6x + 4 \lt 0 \comma \] which we factor as \[2(x - 2)(x - 1) \lt 0 \period \] The zeros of \(2(x - 2)(x - 1)\) are \(x = 1\) and \(x = 2.\) Now we determine which of \[1 \lt x \lt 2 \and x \lt 1 \comma x \gt 2 \] is correct: Plugging in \(x = 0,\) we get \[2(0)^2 - 6(0) + 4 \lt 0 \comma \] a false inequality. So we're left with \(\boxed{1 \lt x \lt 2.}\)
  5. Let's rewrite the inequality as \[4x^2 + 5x - 2 \gt 0\period \] Using the quadratic formula gives \[ \frac{-5 \pm \sqrt{(5)^2 - 4(4)(-2)}}{2(4)} &= \frac{-5 \pm \sqrt{57}}{8} \period \] The zeros are then \[ x = \underbrace{\frac{-5 - \sqrt{57}}{8}}_{\lt 0} \quad &\and x = \underbrace{\frac{-5 + \sqrt{57}}{8}}_{\gt 0} \period \ea \] Plugging \(0\) into the inequality yields \[4(0)^2 + 5(0) - 2 \gt 0 \comma \] which is false. Therefore, \[\boxed{x \lt \frac{-5 - \sqrt{57}}{8} \comma x \gt \frac{-5 + \sqrt{57}}{8}}\]
