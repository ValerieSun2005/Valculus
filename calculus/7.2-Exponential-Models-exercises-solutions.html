7.2 Exercise Solutions — Exponential Models

EXERCISE 1
If \(y\) grows exponentially with \(x,\) then what differential equation does \(y\) satisfy? Let \(y_0\) be the value of \(y\) when \(x = 0.\) What is the function \(y \ques\)
SOLUTION By definition, if \(y\) undergoes exponential growth, then the rate at which \(y\) grows is directly proportional to itself. In other words, \(y\) satisfies the differential equation \begin{equation} \deriv{y}{x} = ky \cma \label{eq:exp-growth-diff-eq} \end{equation} where \(k\) is a positive constant called the growth constant. The solution to the differential equation is \begin{equation} y = y_0 e^{kx} \pd \label{eq:exp-growth} \end{equation}
EXERCISE 2
If \(y\) decays exponentially with \(x,\) then what differential equation does \(y\) satisfy? If \(y_0\) is the value of \(y\) when \(x = 0,\) then what is the function \(y \ques\)
SOLUTION If \(y\) decays exponentially, then the rate at which \(y\) decreases is directly proportional to itself; this is by definition. That is, \(y\) satisfies the differential equation \begin{equation} \deriv{y}{x} = -ky \cma \label{eq:exp-decay-diff-eq} \end{equation} where \(k\) is a positive constant called the decay constant. The solution to the differential equation is \begin{equation} y = y_0 e^{-kx} \pd \label{eq:exp-decay} \end{equation}
EXERCISE 3
What are doubling time and half-life?
SOLUTION Suppose \(y\) grows exponentially with time \(t.\) The doubling time is the time \(y\) takes to double; if \(k\) is the growth constant, then its formula is \begin{equation} \doublingTime = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \pd \label{eq:doubling-time} \end{equation} Conversely, if \(y\) exhibits exponential decay with \(t,\) then the half-life is the time \(\halfLife\) that \(y\) takes to shrink in half. The formula is identical to \(\eqref{eq:doubling-time},\) where \(k\) is the decay constant—namely, \begin{equation} \halfLife = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \pd \label{eq:half-life} \end{equation}
EXERCISE 4
What is the difference between simple interest and compound interest?
SOLUTION Simple interest is paid once per time period (for example, \(1\) year), whereas compound interest is paid several times in a time period.
EXERCISE 5
Money in a bank account grows at an interest rate \(r.\) Let \(A\) be the amount of money in the account after \(t\) time periods and \(P\) be the principal amount of money. What is \(A\) if the interest rate is compounded \(n\) times per time period? What is \(A\) if the money grows continuously?
SOLUTION If the money is compounded discretely at \(n\) times per time period, then we use \begin{equation} A = P \par{1 + \frac{r}{n}}^{nt} \pd \label{eq:compound-interest} \end{equation} But if the money grows continuously, then \begin{equation} A = Pe^{rt} \pd \label{eq:compound-interest-cont} \end{equation}
EXERCISE 6
Let chemical species \(\ce A\) be a reactant that is converted into products: \[\ce{A -> products} \pd\] If this reaction is first-order with respect to \(\ce A,\) then what are the rate law and integrated rate law of the reaction?
SOLUTION The rate law of the reaction is a differential equation that describes the change in the concentration of \(\ce A,\) which we denote \([\ce A].\) For a first-order equation, if \(t\) is time and \(k\) is a positive rate constant, then the rate law is \begin{equation} \deriv{\ce{[A]}}{t} = -k \ce{[A]} \pd \label{eq:reaction-diff-eq} \end{equation} The integrated rate law is the solution to \(\eqref{eq:reaction-diff-eq};\) namely, if \([\ce A]_0\) is the initial concentration of \(\ce A,\) then \begin{equation} [\ce A] = [\ce A]_0 e^{-kt} \pd \label{eq:reaction} \end{equation}
EXERCISE 7
What does Newton's Law of Cooling state?
SOLUTION Newton's Law of Cooling states that when a hot object cools down in a room of constant temperature, the object's temperature decays exponentially with time \(t.\) If an object's initial temperature is \(T_0,\) and it cools in a room of temperature \(T_s,\) then \begin{equation} T = T_s + (T_0 - T_s) e^{-kt} \cma \label{eq:newtons-cooling} \end{equation} where \(k\) is a positive constant that is inherent to the cooling object.
EXERCISE 8
Suppose that a charged capacitor of capacitance \(C\) holds an initial charge \(Q_0.\) The capacitor is then connected to a resistor of resistance \(R,\) and the charge on the capacitor decreases. What are the time constant \(\tau\) and the charge \(Q\) on the capacitor as a function of time?
SOLUTION The charge on a discharging capacitor, when connected to a resistor, undergoes exponential decay. This circuit is called a resistance–capacitance (RC) circuit. The time constant is \(\tau = RC.\) If \(t\) is time, then \begin{equation} Q = Q_0 e^{-t/\tau} \pd \label{eq:rc-Q} \end{equation}
EXERCISE 9
For \[\ds \deriv{y}{x} = 3x \cma y(0) = 1\] classify the differential equation as defining exponential growth, exponential decay, or neither. If the differential equation represents an exponential model, then find the solution given the initial condition.
SOLUTION In an exponential model, \(y\) changes at at a rate proportional to itself. That is, \(y\) satisfies the differential equation \(\textDeriv{y}{x} = \pm ky.\) But since the differential equation includes \(x,\) not \(y,\) it does not represent an exponential model. In fact, the general solution to the differential equation is \(y = \frac{3}{2} x^2 + C.\)
EXERCISE 10
For \[\ds \deriv{y}{x} = 2y \cma y(0) = 8\] classify the differential equation as defining exponential growth, exponential decay, or neither. If the differential equation represents an exponential model, then find the solution given the initial condition.
SOLUTION Quantity \(y\) increases at a rate proportional to itself. This differential equation is in the form of \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth-diff-eq}\) \([\textDeriv{y}{x} = ky],\) where \(k = 2.\) Hence, this differential equation represents exponential growth. We can determine the solution by performing Separation of Variables, or we can remember that the solution to \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth-diff-eq}\) is \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth}\) \([y = y_0e^{kx}].\) We have \(y_0 = 8,\) so the solution is \[\boxed{y = 8e^{2x}}\]

SOLUTION We recognize that \(g\) grows at a rate proportional to itself. Thus, this differential equation represents exponential growth. So our solution is \[\boxed{g = 4e^{9x}}\]

EXERCISE 14
For \[\ds y = e^{2t}\] find the doubling time of the exponential growth model.
SOLUTION By \(\eqref{eq:doubling-time}\) the doubling time \(\doublingTime\) equals \(\ln 2\) divided by the growth constant, \(k = 2.\) We have \[\doublingTime = \frac{\ln 2}{2} = \boxed{0.347}\] Thus, \(y\) doubles after multiples of \(t = 0.347\) have elapsed. For example, when \(t = 0,\) \(y = 1;\) when \(t = 0.347,\) \(y = 2;\) and when \(t = 0.694,\) \(y = 4.\)
EXERCISE 15
For \[\ds A = 6e^{0.04t}\] find the doubling time of the exponential growth model.
SOLUTION The doubling time \(\doublingTime\) equals \(\ln 2\) divided by the growth constant \((k = 0.04).\) We have, from \(\eqrefer{eq:doubling-time},\) \[\doublingTime = \frac{\ln 2}{0.04} = \boxed{17.329}\] So \(y\) doubles after multiples of \(t = 17.329\) have passed.
EXERCISE 16
For \[\ds A = 9e^{0.6x}\] find the doubling time of the exponential growth model.
SOLUTION The doubling time equals \(\ln 2\) divided by the growth constant; here \(k = 0.6.\) From \(\eqrefer{eq:doubling-time},\) the doubling time is \[\frac{\ln 2}{0.6} = \boxed{1.155}\] Hence, \(A\) doubles after multiples of \(x = 1.155\) have elapsed.
EXERCISE 17
For \[\ds g(t) = 2^{t}\] find the doubling time of the exponential growth model.
SOLUTION We cannot immediately apply \(\eqref{eq:doubling-time},\) because the base of the exponential model isn't \(e.\) Instead, we convert the model to have base \(e \col\) \[g(t) = e^{(\ln 2)t} \pd\] Now we see the growth constant is \(\ln 2.\) Thus, by \(\eqref{eq:doubling-time}\) the doubling time is \[\doublingTime = \frac{\ln 2}{\ln 2} = \boxed 1\] This result makes sense because \(g(0) = 1,\) \(g(1) = 2,\) \(g(2) = 4,\) and so on. Alternatively, we could have solved the equation \(2g(0) = g(\doublingTime)\)—that is, \[2 = 2^{\doublingTime} \implies \doublingTime = 1 \pd\]
EXERCISE 18
For \[\ds y = 4^{0.2t}\] find the doubling time of the exponential growth model.
SOLUTION We cannot immediately use \(\eqref{eq:doubling-time},\) since the base of the exponential model isn't \(e.\) We therefore convert the model to have base \(e \col\) \[y = e^{(\ln 4)0.2 t} \pd\] Now we see the growth constant is \(0.2 \ln 4,\) so \(\eqref{eq:doubling-time}\) gives the doubling time to be \[\doublingTime = \frac{\ln 2}{0.2 \ln 4} = \boxed{2.5}\] Or we could have solved the equation \(8 = 4^{0.2 \doublingTime}\) for \(\doublingTime;\) either method yields the same value of \(\doublingTime.\)
EXERCISE 19
For \[\ds y = e^{-0.6t}\] find the half-life of the exponential decay model.
SOLUTION The decay constant is \(k = 0.6.\) Thus, \(\eqrefer{eq:half-life}\) gives the half-life of the function to be \[\halfLife = \frac{\ln 2}{0.6} \approx \boxed{1.155}\]
EXERCISE 20
For \[\ds y = 3e^{-0.9t}\] find the half-life of the exponential decay model.
SOLUTION The decay constant is \(k = 0.9.\) Thus, \(\eqrefer{eq:half-life}\) gives the half-life of the function to be \[\halfLife = \frac{\ln 2}{0.9} \approx \boxed{0.770}\]
EXERCISE 21
For \[\ds f(t) = 2^{-t}\] find the half-life of the exponential decay model.
SOLUTION We first convert \(f\) to have base \(e \col\) \[f(t) = e^{(\ln 2)(-t)} \pd \] In this form the decay constant is \(k = \ln 2.\) Thus, \(\eqrefer{eq:half-life}\) gives the half-life of \(f\) to be \[\halfLife = \frac{\ln 2}{\ln 2} = \boxed 1\] We also could have gotten this result by solving the equation \(\frac{1}{2} f(0) = f(\halfLife)\) [here \(f(0) = 1\)]; that is, \[\tfrac{1}{2} = 2^{-\halfLife} \implies \halfLife = - \log_2 \tfrac{1}{2} = \log_2 2 = 1 \pd \] Either pathway yields the same value of \(\halfLife.\)
EXERCISE 22
For \[\ds g(t) = 3^{-2t}\] find the half-life of the exponential decay model.
SOLUTION First, we convert \(g\) to have base \(e \col\) \[g(t) = e^{(\ln 3)(-2t)} \pd \] In this form the decay constant is \(k = 2 \ln 3.\) Thus, \(\eqrefer{eq:half-life}\) gives the half-life of \(f\) to be \[\halfLife = \frac{\ln 2}{2 \ln 3} \approx \boxed{0.315}\] We also could have gotten this result by solving the equation \(\frac{1}{2} g(0) = f(\halfLife);\) both methods are valid.
EXERCISE 23
Let \(f\) be a function whose graph passes through the point \((2, 3)\) and satisfies the following property: at any \(x,\) the slope of \(f(x)\) is twice the value of \(f(x).\) Determine \(f(x).\)
SOLUTION The slope of \(f\) is twice the value of \(f,\) so so we have the differential equation \[f'(x) = 2 f(x) \pd\] For unknown \(y_0\) the solution to the differential equation is \begin{equation*} f(x) = y_0e^{2x} \pd \end{equation*} We then substitute the initial condition \(f(2) = 3\) to find \[y_0 e^{2(2)} = 3 \implies y_0 = \frac{3}{e^4} \pd\] Hence, \[f(x) = \frac{3}{e^4} e^{2x} = \boxed{3 e^{2x - 4}}\]
EXERCISE 24
The graph of function \(g\) passes through the point \((1, 5).\) At any \(x,\) the slope of \(g(x)\) is half the value of \(g(x).\) Find \(g(x).\)
SOLUTION Because the slope of \(g\) is half the value of \(g,\) we arrange the differential equation \[g'(x) = \tfrac{1}{2} g(x) \pd\] For unknown \(y_0\) the solution to the differential equation is \begin{equation*} g(x) = y_0e^{x/2} \pd \end{equation*} Then we substitute the initial condition \(g(1) = 5,\) finding \[y_0 e^{1/2} = 5 \implies y_0 = \frac{5}{e^{1/2}} \pd\] Hence, \[g(x) = \frac{5}{e^{1/2}} e^{x/2} = \boxed{5 e^{(x - 1)/2}}\]
EXERCISE 25
Function \(f\) grows at a rate proportional to itself and satisfies \(f(1) = 6\) and \(f(2) = 18.\) Find \(f(x).\)
SOLUTION The growth of \(f(x)\) is proportional to itself, so \(f\) satisfies the differential equation \[f'(x) = k f(x) \cma\] where \(k\) is the growth constant. This equation matches \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth-diff-eq},\) which defines exponential growth. Hence, for unknown \(y_0\) and \(k\) the form of \(f\) is \begin{equation} f(x) = y_0e^{kx} \pd \label{eq:exp-growth-f} \end{equation} Our goal is to solve for \(y_0\) and \(k\) that satisfy the initial conditions \(f(1) = 6\) and \(f(2) = 18.\) In \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth-f}\) we substitute the initial conditions to solve for \(y_0\) and \(k;\) we have \begin{equation} 6 = y_0e^{k} \and 18 = y_0e^{2k} \pd \label{eq:exp-growth-f-eqs} \end{equation} To solve for \(k,\) we divide the equations in \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth-f-eqs}\) since doing so cancels out the unknown factor \(y_0.\) Dividing the latter equation by the former equation shows \[ \ba \frac{18}{6} &= \frac{\cancel{y_0} e^{2k}}{\cancel{y_0} e^{k}} \nl 3 &= e^{2k - k} = e^{k} \nl k &= \ln 3 \pd \ea \] To find \(y_0,\) we substitute \(k = \ln 3\) into either equation in \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth-f-eqs}\)—for example, the former equation becomes \[ \ba 6 &= y_0e^{\ln 3} \nl 6 &= 3 y_0 \nl 2 &= y_0 \pd \ea \] Now we have the missing values of \(y_0\) and \(k\) in \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth-f},\) so \[f(x) = 2e^{(\ln 3)x} = \boxed{2(3)^x}\]
EXERCISE 26
Money in a bank account grows at a rate of \(1.5\%\) per year. If a bank account initially contains \(\$900,\) then calculate the money in the account after one year if the interest rate is
  1. compounded annually
  2. compounded biannually
  3. compounded \(10\) times per year
  4. compounded continuously
SOLUTION We use \(\eqrefer{eq:compound-interest}\) \([A = P(1 + r/n)^{nt}]\) for all discrete-growth scenarios. In these cases \(P = 900,\) \(r = 0.015,\) and \(t = 1.\)
  1. In \(\eqrefer{eq:compound-interest},\) \(n = 1.\) So the money is \[A = 900(1 + 0.015)^{1(1)} = \boxed{\$913.50}\]
  2. From \(\eqref{eq:compound-interest}\) we have \(n = 2.\) Hence, the money becomes \[A = 900 \par{1 + \frac{0.015}{2}}^{2(1)} \approx \boxed{\$913.55}\]
  3. Using \(\eqref{eq:compound-interest}\) with \(n = 10\) gives \[A = 900 \par{1 + \frac{0.015}{10}}^{10(1)} \approx \boxed{\$913.59}\]
  4. Because the money grows continuously, we use \(\eqref{eq:compound-interest-cont}\) \([A = Pe^{rt}]\) to get \[A = 900 e^{0.015(1)} \approx \boxed{\$913.60}\]

EXERCISE 30
A small town whose population is \(900\) grows continuously at a rate of \(0.7 \%\) per year. After how many years does the population reach \(1,\!300 \ques\)
SOLUTION Let \(N\) be the population and \(t\) be time in years. The differential equation is \(\textDeriv{N}{t} = 0.007N.\) If \(N_0\) is the initial population \((900),\) then \(\eqrefer{eq:exp-growth}\) gives the solution to be \[N = 900 e^{0.007t} \pd\] Then we solve for \(t\) when \(N = 1300 \col\) \[900 e^{0.007t} = 1300 \implies t = \tfrac{1}{0.007} \ln \tfrac{1300}{900} \approx \boxed{52.532 \un{years}}\]
EXERCISE 31
Bacteria in a petri dish double every \(45\) minutes. The colony initially contains \(30\) bacteria. Find \(B(t),\) the number of bacteria in the dish as a function of time \(t\) in minutes.
SOLUTION By \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth},\) \(B(t) = 30e^{kt}.\) We now seek to find the growth constant \(k.\) The doubling time is \(45\) minutes, so we have \[45 = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \implies k = \tfrac{1}{45} \ln 2 \pd\] Thus, \[B(t) = 30e^{\par{\tfrac{1}{45} \ln 2} t} = \boxed{30(2)^{t/45}}\]
EXERCISE 32
A retirement fund earns simple interest at a rate of \(2\%\) per year. Find the doubling time of the money in the account.
SOLUTION Let \(A\) be the amount of money in the retirement account, with \(P\) being the principal amount. After \(t\) years, the money in the fund becomes \[A = P(1.02)^t \pd\] We rewrite this expression as \(Pe^{(\ln 1.02)t},\) and we find the growth constant to be \(k = \ln 1.02.\) So \(\eqrefer{eq:doubling-time}\) gives the doubling time of the money to be \[\doublingTime = \frac{\ln 2}{\ln 1.02} \approx \boxed{35.003 \un{years}}\] Alternatively, we could have solved for \(\doublingTime\) in \(P(1.02)^{\doublingTime} = 2P;\) both methods produce the same value of doubling time.
EXERCISE 33
Let \(f(t) = e^t\) and \(g(t) = 2^{2 t}.\) By comparing the doubling times of \(f\) and \(g,\) show that \(g\) grows faster than does \(f.\)
SOLUTION In \(f(t),\) the growth constant is \(k = 1.\) So the doubling time of \(f\) is, by \(\eqref{eq:doubling-time},\) \[\doublingTime_f = \frac{\ln 2}{1} = \ln 2 \pd\] To find the growth constant of \(g,\) we rewrite the function as \(e^{(\ln 2) 2 t};\) then the growth constant is \(k = 2 \ln 2.\) Therefore, \(\eqref{eq:doubling-time}\) shows the doubling time of \(g\) to be \[\doublingTime_g = \frac{\ln 2}{2 \ln 2} = \tfrac{1}{2} \pd\] We know \(\ln 2 \approx 0.7 \gt 1/2.\) So \(\doublingTime_f \gt \doublingTime_g.\) Because \(f\) takes longer to double than does \(g,\) function \(g\) grows faster than does \(f.\) Note that we also could have found the doubling time of \(g\) by solving \(2 = 2^{2 \doublingTime_g};\) either method gives the same value and result.
EXERCISE 34
The price of a good decays exponentially. Initially, the price is \(\$500,\) and after \(3\) years the price is \(\$350.\) Determine the price \(P\) of the object as a function of time \(t\) in years.
SOLUTION By \(\eqref{eq:exp-decay}\) the function takes the form \(P = 500 e^{-kt},\) where \(k\) is the decay constant. To solve for \(k\) we substitute the initial condition \(P(3) = 350 \col\) \[350 = 500 e^{-3k} \implies -3k = \ln \tfrac{350}{500} \implies k = -\tfrac{1}{3} \ln \tfrac{350}{500} \pd\] So we have \[P = 500e^{-\left(-\tfrac{1}{3} \ln \tfrac{350}{500}\right)t} = \boxed{500 \par{\tfrac{350}{500}}^{t/3}}\]
EXERCISE 35
Caffeine, on average, has a half-life of \(5\) hours in the plasma of a healthy person. A healthy woman consumes \(150\) milligrams of caffeine at \(6\) . What mass of caffeine remains in the woman's body at \(10\) ?
SOLUTION Let \(y\) be the mass of caffeine remaining in the woman's body. We have \(y = 150e^{-kt},\) where \(k\) is the decay constant. To find \(k\) we consult \(\eqref{eq:half-life},\) where the half-life is \(5\) hours, finding \[5 = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \implies k = \tfrac{1}{5} \ln 2 \pd\] Thus, \[y = 150e^{-\par{\tfrac{1}{5} \ln 2} t} = 150(2)^{-t/5} = 150 \par{\tfrac{1}{2}}^{t/5}\] At \(t = 4\) we find \[y = 150 \par{\tfrac{1}{2}}^{4/5} \approx 86.152 \pd\] Hence, at \(10\) the woman has \(86.152\) grams of caffeine in her body.
EXERCISE 36
Plutonium-\(239,\) \(\ce{^{239}_94 Pu},\) is an isotope used in the production of nuclear weapons—most notably, the Fat Man, the nuclear bomb detonated over Nagasaki, Japan, in \(1945\) during World War II. The half-life of \(\ce{^{239}_94 Pu}\) is approximately \(24,\!110\) years. How long does a sample of \(20\) grams of \(\ce{^{239}_94 Pu}\) take to decay to \(5\) grams?
SOLUTION The exponential function is \(y = 20e^{-kt},\) where \(t\) is time in years. Because the half-life is \(24110\) years, \(\eqref{eq:half-life},\) we have \[24110 = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \implies k = \tfrac{1}{24110} \ln 2 \pd\] Hence, we get \[y = 20e^{-\par{\tfrac{1}{24110} \ln 2}t} = 20(2)^{-t/24110} = 20 \par{\tfrac{1}{2}}^{t/24110}\] We solve \(20 \par{\tfrac{1}{2}}^{t/24110} = 5,\) obtaining \[ \ba \par{\tfrac{1}{2}}^{t/24110} &= \tfrac{1}{4} \nl \tfrac{t}{24110} &= \log_{1/2} \tfrac{1}{4}\nl t &= 24110 \log_{1/2} \tfrac{1}{4} = \boxed{48220 \un{years}} \ea \] Alternatively, we could have noticed that, because the initial amount was halved twice, two half-lives have passed—or \(2 \times 24110 = 48220\) years. Luckily, because the Fat Man was detonated very high above the ground, the levels of radiation on the ground were low—such that the city of Nagasaki is safe today.
EXERCISE 37
Derive the tripling time of an exponential growth model.
SOLUTION The exponential function is \(y = y_0 e^{kt}.\) To obtain the tripling time we solve the equation \(y_0 e^{k \triplingTime} = 3y_0 \col\) \[ \ba y_0 e^{k \triplingTime} &= 3 y_0 \nl e^{k \triplingTime} &= 3 \nl \triplingTime &= \boxed{\frac{\ln 3}{k}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 38
Show that the exponential growth function \(y = y_0 e^{kt}\) grows by a factor of \(c\) in time \(\mathcal{t_C} = (\ln c)/k.\)
SOLUTION We solve the equation \(y_0 e^{k \mathcal{t_C}} = cy_0 \col\) \[ \ba y_0 e^{k \mathcal{t_C}} &= c y_0 \nl e^{k \mathcal{t_C}} &= c \nl \mathcal{t_C} &= \boxed{\frac{\ln c}{k}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 39
Consider the function \(y = y_0 e^{-kt},\) where \(k \gt 0.\) Show that \(y\) shrinks by a factor of \(5\) in time \(\mathcal{t_5} = (\ln 5)/k.\)
SOLUTION We solve the equation \(y_0 e^{k \mathcal{t_5}} = \frac{1}{5} y_0 \col\) \[ \ba y_0 e^{-k \mathcal{t_5}} &= \tfrac{1}{5} y_0 \nl e^{-k \mathcal{t_5}} &= \tfrac{1}{5} \nl \mathcal{t_5} &= -\frac{\ln \tfrac{1}{5}}{k} = \boxed{\frac{\ln 5}{k}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 40
Let \(\doublingTime\) be the doubling time of the exponential function \(y = y_0 e^{kt}.\) Show that the function can be rewritten in the form \(y = y_0(2)^{kt/\doublingTime}.\)
SOLUTION The expression for the doubling time in terms of the growth constant \(k\) is given by \(\eqref{eq:doubling-time}.\) Thus, we have \[\doublingTime = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \implies k = \frac{\ln 2}{\doublingTime} \pd\] Hence, the exponential function becomes \[y = y_0 e^{[(\ln 2)/\doublingTime]t} = \boxed{y_0(2)^{t/\doublingTime}}\]
EXERCISE 41
If \(\halfLife\) is the half-life of \(y,\) then show that \(y = y_0 \par{\tfrac{1}{2}}^{t/\halfLife}.\)
SOLUTION In terms of \(e,\) we write \(y = y_0 e^{-kt},\) where \(k\) is the decay constant. To solve for \(k\) we have, from \(\eqref{eq:half-life},\) \[\halfLife = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \implies k = \frac{\ln 2}{\halfLife} \pd\] Hence, the exponential model becomes \[y = y_0 e^{-[(\ln 2)/\halfLife]t} = y_0(2)^{-t/\halfLife} = \boxed{y_0 \par{\tfrac{1}{2}}^{t/\halfLife}}\]
EXERCISE 42
Dinitrogen pentoxide \((\ce{N2O5})\) is a compound used in the preparation of explosives. In its gas phase, the compound decomposes into nitrogen dioxide and oxygen gas, a first-order reaction: \[\ce{N2O5 -> 2 NO2 + \tfrac{1}{2} O2} \pd\] The initial concentration of a sample of \(\ce{N2O5}\) is \(0.1\) mole per liter, and after \(100\) seconds its concentration is \(0.04\) mole per liter. Find the rate law and integrated rate law of the reaction.
SOLUTION The rate law of the reaction describes the rate at which the reactant \(\ce{N2O5}\) is consumed. Since the reaction is first-order, the rate at which the concentration of \(\ce{N2O5}\)—which we denote \([\ce{N2O5}]\)—decreases is directly proportional to itself. That is, if \(t\) is time, then we have \[\deriv{[\ce{N2O5}]}{t} = - k [\ce{N2O5}] \cma\] where \(k\) is the rate constant. The integrated rate law of the reaction is the solution to this differential equation—namely, \[[\ce{N2O5}] = [\ce{N2O5}]_0 e^{-kt} \cma\] where \([\ce{N2O5}]_0\) is the initial concentration, \(0.1\) mole per liter. Now the question is how to find \(k.\) We substitute the initial condition \([\ce{N2O5}]= 0.04\) when \(t = 100 \col\) \[0.1 e^{-100 k} = 0.04 \implies k = -\tfrac{1}{100} \ln \tfrac{0.04}{0.1} \approx 0.009 \pd\] Thus, the rate law is \[\boxed{\deriv{[\ce{N2O5}]}{t} = - 0.009 [\ce{N2O5}]}\] and the integrated rate law is \[\boxed{[\ce{N2O5}] = 0.1 e^{-0.009t}}\]
EXERCISE 43
A bowl of noodles is taken out of the microwave at \(210 \farenheit\) and allowed to cool in a room whose temperature is \(70 \farenheit.\) After \(5\) minutes, the temperature of the noodles is measured to be \(130 \farenheit.\) Find the temperature \(T\) of the noodles as a function of time \(t.\)
SOLUTION Newton's Law of Cooling asserts the following: If a hot object cools in an environment of constant temperature \(T_s,\) then the rate at which the object's temperature \(T\) decreases is directly proportional to \(T - T_s.\) If \(k\) is a positive constant, then the differential equation for this scenario is \[\deriv{T}{t} = -k(T - T_s) = -k(T - 70) \pd\] The solution is \begin{equation} T = 70 + (210 - 70) e^{-kt} = 70 + 140 e^{-kt} \pd \label{eq:noodles-cooling} \end{equation} In \(\eqref{eq:noodles-cooling},\) substituting the initial condition \(T(5) = 130\) allows us to solve for \(k \col\) \[70 + 140 e^{-5k} = 130 \implies k = -\tfrac{1}{5} \ln \tfrac{60}{140} \pd\] So \(\eqref{eq:noodles-cooling}\) becomes \[T = 70 + 140 e^{-\left(-\tfrac{1}{5} \ln \tfrac{60}{140}\right) t} = \boxed{70 + 140\par{\tfrac{3}{7}}^{t/5}}\]
EXERCISE 44
A batch of cookies is prepared at \(250 \farenheit\) before they are cooled in a room of constant temperature \(60 \farenheit.\) After \(3\) minutes, a cookie's temperature is \(202 \farenheit.\) Calculate the cookie's temperature after \(10\) minutes.
SOLUTION By Newton's Law of Cooling, we know the following: If a hot object cools in an environment of constant temperature \(T_s,\) then the rate at which the object's temperature \(T\) decreases is directly proportional to the difference \(T - T_s.\) Let \(k\) be a positive constant; then the differential equation is \[\deriv{T}{t} = -k(T - T_s) = -k(T - 60) \pd\] The solution is \begin{equation} T = 60 + (250 - 60) e^{-kt} = 60 + 190 e^{-kt} \pd \label{eq:cookies-cooling} \end{equation} Substituting in \(\eqref{eq:cookies-cooling}\) the initial condition \(T(3) = 202\) allows us to solve for \(k \col\) \[60 + 190 e^{-3k} = 202 \implies k = -\tfrac{1}{3} \ln \tfrac{142}{190} \approx 0.097 \pd\] Thus, from \(\eqref{eq:cookies-cooling}\) we obtain \[T(10) = 60 + 190 e^{-0.097(10)} = \boxed{132.026 \farenheit}\]
EXERCISE 45
The time constant of a discharging resistance–capacitance circuit is \(30\) seconds. How much time elapses before the capacitor's stored charge decays by a factor of \(4 \ques\)
SOLUTION The equation relating the capacitor's charge \(Q\) to time is \[Q = Q_0 e^{-t/\tau} \pd\] We wish to solve \(Q_0 e^{-t/\tau} = Q_0/4 \col\) \[ \ba e^{-t/\tau} &= \tfrac{1}{4} \nl \implies t &= -\tau \ln \tfrac{1}{4} = 30 \ln 4 \approx \boxed{41.589 \un{sec}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 46
A circuit consists of a capacitor of capacitance \(6 \times 10^{-6}\) farad and a resistor of resistance \(8 \times 10^8\) ohms. The capacitor then begins to discharge. Initially, the capacitor holds a charge of \(5 \times 10^{-5}\) coulomb.
  1. Write a differential equation for the charge \(Q\) on the capacitor as a function of time \(t\) in seconds.
  2. Find the time constant \(\tau\) of this circuit.
  3. How much time elapses before \(90\%\) of the capacitor's initial charge has been dissipated?
SOLUTION
  1. The differential equation relating charge \(Q\) to resistance \(R\) and capacitance \(C\) is \begin{equation*} \deriv{Q}{t} = - \frac{1}{RC} Q = -\frac{1}{(8 \times 10^8)(6 \times 10^{-6})} Q \approx \boxed{-(2.08 \times 10^{-4}) Q} \end{equation*}
  2. The time constant is defined as \(\tau = RC.\) So \[\tau = (8 \times 10^8)(6 \times 10^{-6}) \approx \boxed{4800}\]
  3. The solution to the differential equation in (a) is \[Q = Q_0 e^{-4800t} \cma\] where \(Q_0 = 5 \times 10^{-5}\) coulomb. We want the time \(t\) at which the capacitor holds a charge of \(0.1Q_0;\) thus, we have \[ \ba Q_0 e^{-4800t} &= 0.1 Q_0 \nl e^{-4800t} &= 0.1 \nl -4800t &= \ln 0.1 \nl t &= -\tfrac{1}{4800} \ln 0.1 \approx 4.797 \times 10^{-4} \pd \ea \] Hence, after \(4.797 \times 10^{-4}\) second, the capacitor discharges by \(90 \%.\)
