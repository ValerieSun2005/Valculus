0.6 Exercise Solutions: Exponents and Logarithms

EXERCISE 1
Define the bidirectional relationship between exponents and logarithms.
SOLUTION Exponential functions and logarithmic functions are inverses of each other. If \(b\) is a positive number and \(b \ne 1,\) then \[y = b^x \iffArrow x = \log_b y \pd\] Thus, \(\log_b y\) gives the power \(x\) to which \(b\) must be raised to become \(y.\)
EXERCISE 2
If \(b \gt 0\) and \(b \ne 1,\) then state the domain and range of
  1. \(y = b^x\)
  2. \(y = \log_b x\)
SOLUTION
  1. Domain: \((-\infty, \infty);\) range: \((0, \infty).\)
  2. Domain: \((0, \infty);\) range: \((-\infty, \infty).\)
EXERCISE 3
Define the common logarithm and the natural logarithm.
SOLUTION The common logarithm is a logarithm with base \(10;\) it is denoted by \(\log\) with no base. For example, \(\log 1000 = 3\) since \(10^3 = 1000.\) In contrast, the base of the natural logarithm is Euler's number, the irrational number \(e \approx 2.718.\) The natural logarithm is denoted by \(\ln;\) for example, \(\ln \par{e^6} = 6.\)
EXERCISE 4

SOLUTION What number is produced by raising \(2\) to the power of \(5 \ques\) The answer is \[x = 2^5 = \boxed{32}\] Another perspective is to exponentiate both sides of \(\log_2 x = 5\) to cancel the logarithm: \[ \ba \cancel{2}^{\cancel{\log_2} x} &= 2^5 \nl x &= 2^5 = 32 \pd \ea \]

EXERCISE 10
For
\[\ds \par{3 a b^2 c}^3\]
use exponent laws to simplify the expression, and ensure that the final answer does not contain fractional or negative powers.
SOLUTION
The power cannot be distributed to each term if there is addition or subtraction in the parentheses; famously, \[(a + b)^n \ne a^n + b^n \pd\]
When the parentheses contain terms being multiplied or divided, the power is applied to each term. Thus, \[ \ba \par{3 a b^2 c}^3 &= (3)^3 (a)^3 \par{b^2}^3 (c)^3 \nl &= \boxed{27 a^3 \, b^6 \, c^3} \ea \]

For
\[\ds \par{6x^4 \, y^2}^{-3}\]
use exponent laws to simplify the expression, and ensure that the final answer does not contain fractional or negative powers.
SOLUTION
The power cannot be distributed to each term if there is addition or subtraction in the parentheses; famously, \[(a + b)^n \ne a^n + b^n \pd\]
Because the terms inside the parentheses are multiplying each other, the power is applied to each term: \[ \ba \par{6 x^4 \, y^2}^{-3} &= (6)^{-3} \par{x^4}^{-3} \, \par{y^2}^{-3} \nl &= 6^{-3} \, x^{-12} \, y^{-6} \nl &= \frac{1}{6^3 \, x^{12} \, y^6} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{1}{216 x^{12} \, y^6}} \ea \]

EXERCISE 14
For
\[\ds \frac{30 a^3 \, b^4 \sqrt c}{4 b^6 \, c \, \sqrt[4]{a^3}}\]

SOLUTION Note that \(\sqrt c\) \(= c^{1/2}\) and \(\sqrt[4]{a^3}\) \(= a^{3/4}.\) When exponential terms of matching bases are divided, their powers are subtracted. So we have \[ \ba \frac{30 a^3 \, b^4 \, c^{1/2}}{4 b^6 \, c \,a^{3/4}} &= \frac{15 a^{9/4}}{2 b^2 \, c^{1/2}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{15 \sqrt[4]{a^9}}{2 b^2 \sqrt c}} \ea \]

EXERCISE 17
For

SOLUTION When expanding logarithms, it is a good idea to proceed one step at a time, as follows: \[ \ba \log_7 \par{7x^3 \, y^7 \, z^{10}} &= \log_7 7 + \log_7 \par{x^3} + \log_7 \par{y^7} + \log_7 \par{z^{10}} \nl &= 1 + \log_7 \par{x^3} + \log_7 \par{y^7} + \log_7 \par{z^{10}} \nl &= \boxed{1 + 3 \log_7 x + 7 \log_7 y + 10 \log_7 z} \ea \]
EXERCISE 18

EXERCISE 25
For
\[\ds 2^x = 4^{1 - x}\]
solve the equation.
SOLUTION Let's force both sides to have the same base. Since \(4\) \(= 2^2,\) we have \[ \ba 2^x &= \par{2^2}^{1 - x} \nl 2^x &= 2^{2 - 2x} \pd \ea \] Now we set the powers equal to each other: \[ \ba x &= 2 - 2x \nl \implies x &= \boxed{\tfrac{2}{3}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 26
For
\[\ds 9^{5 - 2x} = 27^{x + 3}\]
solve the equation.
SOLUTION Let's force both sides to have the same base. We express \(9\) and \(27\) as \(3^2\) and \(3^3,\) respectively, to get \[ \ba \par{3^2}^{5 - 2x} &= \par{3^3}^{x + 3} \nl 3^{10 - 4x} &= 3^{3x + 9} \pd \ea \] Now equating the powers, we see \[ \ba 10 - 4x &= 3x + 9 \nl \implies x &= \boxed{\tfrac{1}{7}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 27
For
\[\ds 7^{2x + 3} = 9^{5x - 8}\]
solve the equation.
SOLUTION Unfortunately, there is no way to force both sides to have the same base. When this occurs, we take the logarithm of both sides. (The base is irrelevant; let's just use the common logarithm.) Doing so, we see \[ \ba \log \par{7^{2x + 3}} &= \log \par{9^{5x - 8}} \nl (2x + 3) \log 7 &= (5x - 8) \log 9 \nl 2x \log 7 + 3 \log 7 &= 5x \log 9 - 8 \log 9 \nl 2x \log 7 - 5x \log 9 &= -3 \log 7 - 8 \log 9 \nl \implies x &= \boxed{\frac{-3 \log 7 - 8 \log 9}{2 \log 7 - 5 \log 9}} \approx 3.301 \pd \ea \]

EXERCISE 30
For
\[\ds \log_6 \par{x^3 + 2x^2} = \log_6 3x\]
solve the equation.
SOLUTION Note the restriction \(x \gt 0;\) otherwise, the logarithm on the right side would have a negative argument. Because both logarithms have the same base \((6),\) we equate their inputs: \[ \ba x^3 + 2x^2 &= 3x \nl x^3 + 2x^2 - 3x &= 0 \nl x \par{x^2 + 2x - 3} &= 0 \nl x(x + 3)(x - 1) &= 0 \nl \implies x &= -3 \cma x = 0 \cma x = 1 \pd \ea \] The only solution is \(\boxed{x = 1};\) the other solutions, \(x = -3\) and \(x = 0,\) are extraneous.
EXERCISE 31
For
\[\ds 100^x - 10^x - 6 = 0\]
solve the equation.
SOLUTION Note that \(100\) \(= 10^2.\) We therefore have \[\par{10^x}^2 - 10^x - 6 = 0 \cma\] which resembles a quadratic equation for which factoring gives \[\par{10^x - 3} \par{10^x + 2} = 0 \pd\] Equating each factor to \(0,\) we get \[ \ba 10^x - 3 &= 0 \lspace &10^x + 2 &= 0 \nl 10^x &= 3 \lspace &10^x &= -2 \nl x &= \boxed{\log 3} \approx 0.477 \pd \ea \] Note that \(10^x = -2\) has no solutions because the range of \(10^x\) is \((0, \infty).\)

EXERCISE 34
For
\[\ds \log_2 (x - 1) + \log_2 (x - 3) = 3\]
solve the equation.
SOLUTION The domain is \(x \gt 3\) so that neither logarithm has a negative argument. Condensing the logarithms on the left side produces \[\log_2 \par{x^2 - 4x + 3} = 3 \pd\] Exponentiating both sides with base \(2\) yields \[ \ba 2^{\log_2 \par{x^2 - 4x + 3}} &= 2^3 \nl x^2 - 4x + 3 &= 8 \nl x^2 - 4x - 5 &= 0 \nl (x - 5)(x + 1) &= 0 \pd \ea \] Only \(\boxed{x = 5}\) is within the domain; the solution \(x = -1\) is extraneous.
EXERCISE 35
For
\[\ds \log_5 (x + 2) - \log_5 x = 1\]
solve the equation.
SOLUTION Since neither logarithm can have a negative argument, the domain is \(x \gt 0.\) Condensing the logarithms on the left side, we get \[ \log_5 \par{\frac{x + 2}{x}} = 1 \pd \] Exponentiating both sides with base \(5,\) we attain \[ \ba 5^{\log_5 [(x + 2)/x]} &= 5^1 \nl \frac{x + 2}{x} &= 5 \nl x + 2 &= 5x \nl \implies x &= \boxed{\tfrac{1}{2}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 36

\[\ds \log (x - 7) - \log x = 2\]
solve the equation.

Condensing the logarithms on the left side, we get \[\log \par{\frac{x - 7}{x}} = 2 \pd\] Exponentiating both sides with base \(10\) shows \[ \ba 10^{\log [(x - 7)/x]} &= 10^2 \nl \frac{x - 7}{x} &= 100 \nl x - 7 &= 100x \nl \implies x &= -\tfrac{7}{99} \pd \ea \] But this value is outside the domain, so this equation has no solutions.
EXERCISE 37
For
\[\ds \log_7 x + \log_7(x - 4) = 1\]
solve the equation.
SOLUTION The domain is \(x \gt 4\) so that neither logarithm has a negative argument. Condensing the logarithms on the left side, we get \[\log_7 \par{x^2 - 4x} = 1 \pd\] Exponentiating both sides with base \(7,\) we see \[ \ba 7^{\log_7 \par{x^2 - 4x}} &= 7^1 \nl x^2 - 4x &= 7 \nl x^2 - 4x - 7 &= 0 \pd \ea \] The Quadratic Formula (see Section 0.4) then gives \[ \ba x &= \frac{-(-4) \pm \sqrt{(-4)^2 - 4(1)(-7)}}{2(1)} \nl &= \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{44}}{2} \nl &= \frac{4 \pm 2 \sqrt{11}}{2} \nl &= 2 \pm \sqrt{11} \pd \ea \] Only \(\boxed{x = 2 + \sqrt{11}}\) \(\approx 5.317\) is in the domain of \(\log_7 x + \log_7(x - 4);\) the other solution is extraneous.
EXERCISE 38
Prove the logarithm laws. (Hint: Let \(x = b^m\) and \(y = b^n,\) and use exponent laws.)
SOLUTION If \(x = b^m\) and \(y = b^n,\) then \(m = \log_b x\) and \(n = \log_b y.\)

Product Law By exponent laws, \[ \ba \log_b (xy) &= \log_b \par{b^m \, b^n} \nl &= \log_b \par{b^{m + n}} \nl &= m + n \nl &= \log_b x + \log_b y \pd \ea \]

Quotient Law Likewise, \[ \ba \log_b \par{\frac{x}{y}} &= \log_b \par{\frac{b^m}{b^n}} \nl &= \log_b \par{b^{m - n}} \nl &= m - n \nl &= \log_b x - \log_b y \pd \ea \]

Power Law Let \(p\) be any number. We have \[ \ba \log_b \par{x^p} &= \log_b \parbr{\par{b^m}^p} \nl &= \log_b \par{b^{pm}} \nl &= pm \nl &= p \log_b x \pd \ea \]

EXERCISE 39
A solution's \(\pH\) is defined by the logarithmic relationship \[\pH = -\log \, [\ce{H+}] \cma\] where \([\ce{H+}]\) is the concentration of hydrogen ions in moles per liter. Solutions with \(\pH\) values less than \(7\) are acidic and \(\pH\) values greater than \(7\) are basic. Calculate the \(\pH\) of each solution, and classify it as basic or acidic:
  1. a \(2\)-liter solution containing \(0.004\) mole of hydrogen ions
  2. a \(16\)-liter solution containing \(1.5 \times 10^{-8}\) mole of hydrogen ions
SOLUTION
  1. The concentration of hydrogen ions, in moles per liter, is \[[\ce{H+}] = \frac{0.004}{2} = 0.002 \pd\] So \[\pH = -\log 0.002 \approx \boxed{2.699}\] Since \(\pH \lt 7,\) this solution is acidic.
  2. The concentration of hydrogen ions, in moles per liter, is \[[\ce{H+}] = \frac{1.5 \times 10^{-8}}{16} = 9.375 \times 10^{-10} \pd\] So \[\pH = -\log \par{9.375 \times 10^{-10}} \approx \boxed{9.028}\] Since \(\pH \gt 7,\) this solution is basic.

Calculate the concentration of hydrogen ions, in moles per liter, of a solution whose \(\pH\) is \(6.\)
SOLUTION We have \[6 = -\log \, [\ce{H+}] \iffArrow [\ce{H+}] = \boxed{10^{-6}}\]
EXERCISE 41
The population of a small town grows exponentially according to the function \[P(t) = 9000(1.04)^t \cma\] where \(t\) is the number of years after 2020.
  1. Determine the population in 2020.
  2. In what year will the population reach \(11000 \ques\)
  3. After how many years does the population double? (This number is called the doubling time.)
SOLUTION
  1. In 2020 the population is \[P(0) = 9000(1.04)^0 = 9000(1) = \boxed{9000}\]
  2. Equating \(P(t) = 11000\) and solving, we attain \[ \ba 9000(1.04)^t &= 11000 \nl (1.04)^t &= \frac{11000}{9000} \nl \implies t &= \log_{1.04} \par{\frac{11000}{9000}} \approx 5.116 \pd \ea \] Thus, the population reaches \(11000\) after \(5.116\) years, during the year 2025.
  3. The population is initially \(9000,\) so it doubles when \(P(t) = 18000.\) We solve as follows: \[ \ba 9000(1.04)^t &= 18000 \nl (1.04)^t &= 2 \nl \implies t &= \log_{1.04} 2 \approx \boxed{17.673 \un{years}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 42
An earthquake's intensity can be measured by the Richter scale, a logarithmic scale based on the amplitudes of seismic waves—mechanical waves of acoustic energy formed by the sudden movement of materials within Earth. On March 11, 2011, a devastating earthquake of magnitude \(9.0\) struck Japan's northeastern region, right off the coast of Honshu, killing thousands. On August 24, 2014, an earthquake of magnitude \(6.0\) occurred in the San Francisco Bay Area. (Luckily, only one person died from the San Francisco earthquake.) Based on the seismic waves, how many times more intense was the earthquake in Japan than the earthquake in San Francisco?
SOLUTION For the earthquake in Japan, \[9 = \log x_J \iffArrow x_J = 10^9 \pd\] For the earthquake in the San Francisco Bay Area, \[6 = \log x_B \iffArrow x_B = 10^6 \pd\] So we see \[\frac{x_J}{x_B} = \frac{10^9}{10^6} = 1000 \pd\] Therefore, the earthquake in Japan was \(1000\) times more intense than the earthquake in San Francisco!
EXERCISE 43
A belt is wrapped around the top half of a circular shaft, and the belt is pulled at the left end with a force \(T_1.\) A frictional force opposes the shaft's rotation, so the right end of the belt experiences a tensile force \(T_2.\) (See Figure 6.) The coefficient of friction between the belt and shaft is \(\mu;\) large values of \(\mu\) indicate high levels of friction. Then \(T_1,\) \(T_2,\) and \(\mu\) satisfy \[\ln \par{\frac{T_1}{T_2}} = \mu \pi \pd\] Solve for \(T_1\) in terms of \(T_2\) and \(\mu.\) Then calculate \(T_2\) if \(T_1 = 70\) pounds and \(\mu = 0.6.\)

Some data trends are best modeled by an exponential function \(y = ab^x,\) such as the model in Figure 7A. The logarithm of the \(y\)-values is plotted against the original \(x\)-values to produce a linear pattern; the equation of the best-fit line is \[\log y = -0.6482 + 0.3633 x \pd\] (See Figure 7B.) Find the values of \(a\) and \(b\) to complete the exponential model.
SOLUTION Exponentiating both sides of the best-fit equation for the line, we have \[ \ba 10^{\log y} &= 10^{-0.6482 + 0.3633 x} \nl y &= 10^{-0.6482} \, 10^{0.3633 x} \nl y &= 0.2248 \par{2.3083}^x \pd \ea \] Comparing this equation to \(y = ab^x,\) we see \(\boxed{a = 0.2248}\) and \(\boxed{b = 2.3083}.\)
