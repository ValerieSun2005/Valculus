10.4 Exercise Solutions: Comparison Tests

EXERCISE 1
State both parts of the Direct Comparison Test and justify why it works.
SOLUTION In the Direct Comparison Test, we test whether a positive sum \(\sum a_n\) converges or diverges by comparing \(\sum a_n\) to either a larger convergent sum \(\sum b_n\) or a smaller divergent sum \(\sum b_n.\)
  1. If \(0 \lt a_n \leq b_n\) and \(\sum b_n\) converges, then \(\sum a_n\) also converges. In other words, if the larger sum converges, then the smaller sum also converges.
  2. If \(a_n \geq b_n \gt 0\) and \(\sum b_n\) diverges, then \(\sum a_n\) also diverges. Informally, if the smaller sum diverges, then the larger sum also diverges.
EXERCISE 2
What is the Limit Comparison Test?
SOLUTION If we cannot establish an inequality needed to use the Direct Comparison Test, then we may use the Limit Comparison Test: Let \(a_n\) and \(b_n\) be positive functions such that \[L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} \pd\] If \(L\) is positive and finite, then the Limit Comparison Test states that \(\sum a_n\) and \(\sum b_n\) either both converge or both diverge.
EXERCISE 3
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{(8n + 5)^3}\]
test the series for convergence or divergence.
SOLUTION Observe that the given sum is positive. We want to find either a convergent series that is larger than this series or a divergent series that is smaller than this series. Because \((8n + 5)^3 \geq n^3 \gt 0\) for \(n \geq 1,\) it follows that \[ 0 \lt \frac{1}{(8n + 5)^3} \leq \frac{1}{n^3} \cmaa n \geq 1 \pd \] We therefore compare the given series to \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \par{1/n^3},\) which converges because it is a \(p\)-series with \(p = 3 \gt 1.\) Hence, the Direct Comparison Test says that, too, \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{(8n + 5)^3} \convBoxed\]

EXERCISE 6
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{4n + 2}\]
test the series for convergence or divergence.
SOLUTION It is difficult to show that this series \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) is greater than a known divergent series or less than a known convergent series. When this occurs, we use the Limit Comparison Test because the Direct Comparison Test has little use. We compare the series to the known series \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty b_n = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{n} \cma\] the divergent Harmonic series. We see \[ \ba L &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{\dfrac{1}{4n + 2}}{1/n} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n}{4n + 2} = \tfrac{1}{4} \pd \ea \] Since \(L\) is positive and finite, and \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \par{1/n}\) diverges, the Limit Comparison Test asserts that, too, \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{4n + 2} \divBoxed\]

For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{2}{8^n - 3}\]
test the series for convergence or divergence.
SOLUTION It is difficult to show that this series \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) is greater than a known divergent series or less than a known convergent series. When this occurs, we use the Limit Comparison Test because the Direct Comparison Test has little use. We compare the series to \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty b_n = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{8^n} \cma\] a geometric series that converges because its common ratio is \(r = 1/8 \lt 1.\) We see \[ \ba L &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{\dfrac{2}{8^n - 3}}{1/8^n} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{2 \cdot 8^n}{8^n - 3} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{2}{1 - 3\par{8^{-n}}} = 2 \pd \ea \] Since \(L\) is positive and finite, and \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \par{1/8^n}\) converges, the Limit Comparison Test holds that \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{2}{8^n - 3} \convBoxed\]

For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{n + 2}{7n^2 + 4}\]
test the series for convergence or divergence.
SOLUTION If the summation function \(a_n\) is a rational expression, then we compare \(\sum a_n\) to a series containing the highest-degree terms of the numerator and denominator. We therefore compare the series to \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{n}{7n^2} = \tfrac{1}{7} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{n}{n^2} = \tfrac{1}{7} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{n} \cma\] the divergent Harmonic series. We then see \[ \ba L &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{\dfrac{n + 2}{7n^2 + 4}}{1/7n} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{7n^2 + 14n}{7n^2 + 4} = 1 \pd \ea \] Because \(L\) is finite and positive, the Limit Comparison Test asserts that \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{n + 2}{7n^2 + 4} \divBoxed\] because \(\tfrac{1}{7} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \par{1/n}\) also diverges.

EXERCISE 25
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^n}\]
test the series for convergence or divergence.
SOLUTION Testing this series requires creativity. As \(n \to \infty,\) \(n^n\) grows faster than a power function does. So we can compare \(n^n\) to any power function—say, \(n^p,\) where \(p \gt 1.\) Since \(n^n \geq n^p\) for \(n \geq N\) (the value of \(N\) is irrelevant), we have \[0 \lt \frac{1}{n^n} \leq \frac{1}{n^p} \cmaa n \geq N \pd\] Because we choose \(p \gt 1,\) the series \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \par{1/n^p}\) converges by the \(p\)-series test. Accordingly, the Direct Comparison Test asserts that, too, \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^n} \convBoxed\] because it is bounded between two finite numbers.

EXERCISE 28
For positive \(a\) and \(b,\) show that \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{an + b} \conv \pd\]
SOLUTION We compare the series to the Harmonic series \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \par{1/n},\) which diverges. EXERCISE 29
Suppose that \(a_n \gt 0\) and \(\sum a_n\) diverges. Must \(\sum na_n\) also diverge?
SOLUTION Because \(a_n \gt 0,\) we have \[0 \lt a_n \leq n a_n \pd\] Since \(\sum a_n\) diverges, the Direct Comparison Test states that yes, \(\sum n a_n\) also diverges.

EXERCISE 32
Let \(p\) be a positive integer, If \(a_n \gt 0\) and \(\sum a_n\) diverges, then must \(\sum \par{a_n}^p\) also diverge?
SOLUTION No, \(\sum \par{a_n}^p\) could converge or diverge. For example, if \(a_n = 1/n\) then \(\sum a_n\) diverges because it is the Harmonic series. For \(p = 1,\) \(\sum \par{a_n}^p\) diverges too. Yet for \(p = 2,\) \[\sum \par{a_n}^p = \sum \par{\frac{1}{n}}^2 = \sum \frac{1}{n^2} \cma\] which converges.
EXERCISE 33
If \(a_n \gt 0\) and \(\sum a_n\) converges, then must \(\sum \sin \par{a_n}\) also converge?
SOLUTION We take the limit of the ratio of the two summation functions: \[L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{\sin \par{a_n}}{a_n} \pd\] Since \(\sum a_n\) converges, \(\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = 0\) by the Divergence Test. (See Section 10.2 to review the conditions of the Divergence Test.) Accordingly, \(a_n \to 0\) as \(n \to \infty.\) If we let \(u = a_n,\) then \(u \to 0\) as \(n \to \infty\) and so we have \[L = \lim_{u \to 0} \frac{\sin u}{u} = 1 \pd\] Because \(L\) is positive and finite, and \(\sum a_n\) converges, the Limit Comparison Test says yes, \(\sum \sin \par{a_n}\) must also converge.
EXERCISE 34
By using the Limit Comparison Test with \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \par{1/n^2},\) test the following series for convergence or divergence: \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \sin^2 \par{\frac{1}{n}} \pd\]
SOLUTION We take the limit of the ratio of the summation functions, as follows: \[ \ba L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{\sin^2 \par{\dfrac{1}{n}}}{1/n^2} \pd \ea \] Let \(u = 1/n;\) then \(u \to 0\) as \(n \to \infty.\) The limit therefore becomes \[ \ba L &= \lim_{u \to 0} \frac{\sin^2 u}{u^2} \nl &= \par{\lim_{u \to 0} \frac{\sin u}{u}}^2 = 1 \pd \ea \] Because \(L\) is finite and positive, and because \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \par{1/n^2}\) converges, the Limit Comparison Test states that \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \sin^2 \par{\frac{1}{n}} \convBoxed\]
EXERCISE 35
Let \(a_n\) and \(b_n\) be positive functions such that \(\sum b_n\) diverges, and let \[L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} \pd\] Prove that the Limit Comparison Test is inconclusive if \(L = 0.\)
SOLUTION To prove that the test is inconclusive, we simply need to find two examples that are contradictory. We need to find two cases, in each of which \(\sum a_n\) has a different conclusion when \(\sum b_n\) diverges. If \(a_n = 1/n^3\) and \(b_n = 1/n,\) then \(\sum a_n\) converges and \[L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = 0 \pd \] But if \(a_n = 1/n\) and \(b_n = 1/\sqrt n,\) then \(\sum a_n\) and \(\sum b_n\) both diverge, and \[L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = 0 \pd \] Thus, the Limit Comparison Test cannot provide consistent conclusions about \(\sum a_n\) if \(L = 0.\)
