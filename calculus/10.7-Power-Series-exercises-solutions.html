10.7 Exercise Solutions: Power Series

EXERCISE 1
What is a power series? What is the general form of a power series?
SOLUTION A power series is a polynomial with infinitely many terms. The general form of a power series is \[\sum_{n = 0}^\infty c_n (x - a)^n = c_0 + c_1 (x - a) + c_2 (x - a)^2 + \cdots \cma\] where the series is centered at \(x = a.\)
EXERCISE 2
Define the interval of convergence and radius of convergence.
SOLUTION The interval of convergence consists of the values of \(x\) such that a power series \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty c_n (x - a)^n\) converges. The radius of convergence is how far the endpoints in the interval of convergence extend from the center of the series, \(x = a.\)
EXERCISE 3
Suppose that function \(f(x)\) has a power series whose radius of convergence is \(R.\) What are the radii of convergence for the power series of \(f'(x)\) and \(\int f(x) \di x \ques\)
SOLUTION When we differentiate or integrate a power series, its radius of convergence is preserved (but not always its interval of convergence). Hence, the power series representations of \(f'(x)\) and \(\int f(x) \di x\) each have a radius of convergence of \(R.\)
EXERCISE 4
The power series \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty c_n (x - 2)^n\) has a radius of convergence of \(3.\) The series converges at \(x = -1\) and diverges at \(x = 5.\)
  1. Identify the center of the series.
  2. Determine the series' interval of convergence.
SOLUTION
  1. The series has the factor \((x - 2),\) so it is centered at \(x = 2.\)
  2. The series' interval of convergence extends \(3\) in either direction of the center, \(2.\) So our preliminary interval is \((-1, 5).\) Since we are given that the series converges at \(x = -1\) and diverges at \(x = 5,\) we include \(-1\) and exclude \(5.\) The interval is \[\boxed{-1 \leq x \lt 5}\]
EXERCISE 5
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(x + 1)^n}{n}\]
find the series' radius of convergence and interval of convergence.
SOLUTION Let \(a_n = (x + 1)^n/n.\) The series \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) converges if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1,\) by the Ratio Test. Hence, we write a limit that satisfies this inequality, and solve for \(x\) that meets this condition. We obtain \[ \ba \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{(x + 1)^{n + 1}}{n + 1} \cdot \frac{n}{(x + 1)^n}} &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{(x + 1) n}{n + 1}} \nl &= \abs{x + 1} \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n}{n + 1} \nl &= \abs{x + 1} \pd \ea \] The series converges if \(\abs{x + 1} \lt 1,\) so the radius of convergence is \(R = 1.\) We then get, as the preliminary interval of convergence, \[-1 \lt x + 1 \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay -2 \lt x \lt 0 \pd\] We now check the endpoints: At \(x = -2\) the series \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) becomes \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(-2 + 1)^n}{n} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{n} \cma\] which converges by the Alternating Series Test because \(\lim_{n \to \infty} 1/n = 0\) and \(1/n\) is decreasing for \(n \geq 1.\) But at \(x = 0\) the series is \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(0 + 1)^n}{n} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{n} \cma\] the divergent Harmonic series. Hence, the interval of convergence must include \(x = -2\) and exclude \(x = 0\)—that is, \[\boxed{-2 \leq x \lt 0}\]
EXERCISE 6
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n!}\]
find the series' radius of convergence and interval of convergence.
SOLUTION We let \(a_n = x^n/n! \, .\) Then \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) converges if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1\) by the Ratio Test. Recall that \((n + 1)! = (n + 1) n! \, ;\) hence, we have \[ \ba \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{x^{n + 1}}{(n + 1)!} \cdot \frac{n!}{x^n}} &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{x^n \cdot x}{(n + 1) n!} \cdot \frac{n!}{x^n}} \nl &= \abs{x} \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{1}{n + 1}} \nl &= 0 \lt 1 \pd \ea \] Any value of \(x\) satisfies the inequality \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1.\) Thus, the interval of convergence is \[\boxed{-\infty \lt x \lt \infty}\] and the radius of convergence is \(R = \infty.\)
EXERCISE 7
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(3x)^n}{n + 1}\]
find the series' radius of convergence and interval of convergence.
SOLUTION Let \(a_n = (3x)^n/(n + 1).\) The series \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n\) converges if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1,\) by the Ratio Test. Thus, we write a limit that satisfies this inequality, and solve for \(x\) that meets this condition. We obtain \[ \ba \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{(3x)^{n + 1}}{n + 2} \cdot \frac{n + 1}{(3x)^n}} &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{(3x) (n + 1)}{n + 2}} \nl &= \abs{3x} \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{n + 1}{n + 2}} \nl &= \abs{3x} \pd \ea \] So the series converges if \(\abs{3x} \lt 1.\) The inequality gives, as the preliminary interval of convergence, \[-1 \lt 3x \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay -\tfrac{1}{3} \lt x \lt \tfrac{1}{3} \pd\] The radius of convergence is \(1/3.\) We now check the endpoints: At \(x = -1/3\) the series \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n\) becomes \[\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{\par{3 \cdot -\frac{1}{3}}^n}{n + 1} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{n + 1} \cma\] which converges by the Alternating Series Test since \(\lim_{n \to \infty} 1/(n + 1) = 0\) and \(1/(n + 1)\) is decreasing for \(n \geq 0.\) Conversely, at \(x = 1/3\) the series is \[\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{\par{3 \cdot \frac{1}{3}}^n}{n + 1} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{1}{n + 1} \cma\] which diverges by the Integral Test. \(\big[\int_0^\infty \dd x/(x + 1)\) is divergent.\(\big]\) Thus, the interval of convergence must include \(x = -1/3\) and exclude \(x = 1/3 \col\) \[\boxed{-\tfrac{1}{3} \leq x \lt \tfrac{1}{3}}\]
EXERCISE 8
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(5x)^n}{n + 1}\]
find the series' radius of convergence and interval of convergence.
SOLUTION Let \(a_n = (5x)^n/(n + 1).\) The series \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n\) converges if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1,\) by the Ratio Test. Thus, we write a limit that satisfies this inequality, and solve for \(x\) that meets this condition. We have \[ \ba \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{(5x)^{n + 1}}{n + 2} \cdot \frac{n + 1}{(5x)^n}} &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{(5x) (n + 1)}{n + 2}} \nl &= \abs{5x} \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{n + 1}{n + 2}} \nl &= \abs{5x} \pd \ea \] So the series converges if \(\abs{5x} \lt 1.\) The inequality gives, as the preliminary interval of convergence, \[-1 \lt 5x \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay -\tfrac{1}{5} \lt x \lt \tfrac{1}{5} \pd\] The radius of convergence is \(1/5.\) We now check the endpoints: At \(x = -1/5\) the series \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n\) becomes \[\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{\par{5 \cdot -\frac{1}{5}}^n}{n + 1} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{n + 1} \cma\] which converges by the Alternating Series Test since \(\lim_{n \to \infty} 1/(n + 1) = 0\) and \(1/(n + 1)\) is decreasing for \(n \geq 0.\) But at \(x = 1/5\) the series is \[\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{\par{5 \cdot \frac{1}{5}}^n}{n + 1} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{1}{n + 1} \cma\] which diverges by the Integral Test. \(\big[\int_0^\infty \dd x/(x + 1)\) is divergent.\(\big]\) Hence, the interval of convergence must include \(x = -1/5\) and exclude \(x = 1/5\)—so, \[\boxed{-\tfrac{1}{5} \leq x \lt \tfrac{1}{5}}\]
EXERCISE 9
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^n}{2^n}\]
find the series' radius of convergence and interval of convergence.
SOLUTION Let \(a_n = (-1)^n x^n/2^n.\) By the Ratio Test \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n\) converges if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1.\) We can ignore the alternator \((-1)^n\) in \(\abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n}\) because the absolute value bars force the quantity to be positive. We get \[ \ba \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{x \cdot x^n}{2 \cdot 2^n} \cdot \frac{2^n}{x^n}} &= \abs{\frac{x}{2}} \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{1} \nl &= \abs{\frac{x}{2}} \lt 1 \pd \ea \] (Note that we obtain the same interval by using the Root Test.) Thus, \[\abs{\frac{x}{2}} \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay -1 \lt \frac{x}{2} \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay -2 \lt x \lt 2 \pd\] The radius of convergence is \(2.\) This is our preliminary interval of convergence. We now test the endpoints: When \(x = -2,\) \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n\) becomes \[\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n (-2)^n}{2^n} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (-1)^{2n} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty 1 \cma\] which diverges since a constant is added infinitely many times. Likewise, at \(x = 2,\) \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n\) becomes \[\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n (2)^n}{2^n} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (-1)^{n} \pd\] The Alternating Series Test does not assert that this sum converges. Instead, we note that \(\lim_{n \to \infty} (-1)^n\) doesn't exist, so \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty (-1)^n\) diverges by the Divergence Test. Thus, we exclude both endpoints; the interval of convergence is \[\boxed{-2 \lt x \lt 2}\]
EXERCISE 10
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n (x - 3)^n}{e^n}\]
find the series' radius of convergence and interval of convergence.
SOLUTION Calling \(a_n = (-1)^n (x - 3)^n/e^n,\) we understand that Ratio Test states that \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n\) converges if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1.\) We ignore the alternator \((-1)^n\) in \(\abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n}\) because the absolute value bars force the quantity to be positive. We obtain \[ \ba \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{(x - 3)^n \cdot (x - 3)}{e^n \cdot e} \cdot \frac{e^n}{(x - 3)^n}} &= \abs{\frac{x - 3}{e}} \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{1} \nl &= \abs{\frac{x - 3}{e}} \pd \ea \] The series converges if \(\abs{(x - 3)/e} \lt 1,\) or \[-1 \lt \frac{x - 3}{e} \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay -e + 3 \lt x \lt e + 3 \pd\] This is our preliminary interval of convergence. The radius of convergence is \(e,\) since the interval extends a distance \(e\) from either side of the series' center, \(x = 3.\) We now test the endpoints: When \(x = -e + 3,\) \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n\) becomes \[\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n (-e + 3 - 3)^n}{e^n} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n (-e)^n}{e^n} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (-1)^{2n} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty 1 \cma\] which diverges because a constant is added infinitely many times. Similarly, at \(x = e + 3\) the series becomes \[\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n (e + 3 - 3)^n}{e^n} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (-1)^n \cma\] which diverges by the Divergence Test because \(\lim_{n \to \infty} (-1)^n\) does not exist. Thus, we exclude both endpoints; the interval of convergence is \[\boxed{-e + 3 \lt x \lt e + 3}\]
EXERCISE 11
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{x^{2n}}{n \cdot 9^n}\]
find the series' radius of convergence and interval of convergence.
SOLUTION Let \(a_n = x^{2n}/(n \cdot 9^n).\) The series \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) converges if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1,\) by the Ratio Test. Note that \[a_{n + 1} = \frac{x^{2(n + 1)}}{(n + 1)(9)^{n + 1}} = \frac{x^{2n} \cdot x^2}{(n + 1) (9)^n \cdot 9} \pd\] We obtain \[ \ba \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{x^{2n} \cdot x^2}{(n + 1) \cdot 9^n \cdot 9} \cdot \frac{n \cdot 9^n}{x^{2n}}} &= \abs{\frac{x^2}{9}} \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{n}{n + 1}} \nl &= \abs{\frac{x^2}{9}} \lt 1 \pd \ea \] Then \[\abs{\frac{x^2}{9}} \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay \abs{\frac{x}{3}} \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay -3 \lt x \lt 3 \pd\] The radius of convergence of the series is \(3.\) This is our preliminary interval of convergence. We now test the endpoints: When \(x = -3,\) \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) becomes \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(-3)^{2n}}{n(9)^n} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(9)^{n}}{n(9)^n} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{n}\cma\] which diverges since it is the Harmonic series. And at \(x = 3\) the series becomes \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(3)^{2n}}{n(9)^n} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(9)^{n}}{n(9)^n} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{n}\cma\] the same result from substituting \(x = -3.\) Hence, we exclude both endpoints; our interval of convergence is \[\boxed{-3 \lt x \lt 3}\]
EXERCISE 12
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{x^{3n}}{8^n}\]
find the series' radius of convergence and interval of convergence.
SOLUTION Let \(a_n = x^{3n}/8^n.\) The series \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) converges if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1,\) by the Ratio Test. Note that \[a_{n + 1} = \frac{x^{3(n + 1)}}{(8)^{n + 1}} = \frac{x^{3n} \cdot x^3}{8^n \cdot 8} \pd\] We obtain \[ \ba \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{x^{3n} \cdot x^3}{8^n \cdot 8} \cdot \frac{8^n}{x^{3n}}} &= \abs{\frac{x^3}{8}} \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{1} \nl &= \abs{\frac{x^3}{8}} \lt 1 \pd \ea \] Then \[\abs{\frac{x^3}{8}} \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay -8 \lt x^3 \lt 8 \arrowTwoWay -2 \lt x \lt 2 \pd\] The radius of convergence of the series is \(2.\) This is our preliminary interval of convergence. We now test the endpoints: When \(x = -2,\) \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) becomes \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(-2)^{3n}}{8^n} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(-8)^n}{8^n} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty (-1)^n \cma\] which diverges by the Divergence Test since \(\lim_{n \to \infty} (-1)^n\) does not exist. And at \(x = 2\) the series becomes \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(2)^{3n}}{8^n} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(8)^n}{8^n} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty 1 \cma\] which also diverges by the Divergence Test because \(\lim_{n \to \infty} 1 \ne 0.\) Hence, we exclude both endpoints; our interval of convergence is \[\boxed{-2 \lt x \lt 2}\]
EXERCISE 13
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n \, x^{2n + 1}}{n + 3}\]
find the series' radius of convergence and interval of convergence.
SOLUTION Letting \(a_n = (-1)^n \, x^{2n + 1}/(n + 3),\) we note that by the Ratio Test the series \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n\) converges if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1.\) Also, \[a_{n + 1} = \frac{(-1)^{n + 1} \, x^{2(n + 1) + 1}}{(n + 1) + 3} = \frac{(-1)^{n + 1} x^{2n} \cdot x^3}{n + 4} \pd\] In the expression \(\abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n},\) we omit the alternators \((-1)^{n + 1}\) and \((-1)^n\) because they don't affect the absolute value. Therefore, we have \[ \ba \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{ \frac{x^{2n} \cdot x^3}{n + 4} \cdot \frac{n + 3}{x^{2n} \cdot x} } &= \abs{x^2} \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{ \frac{n + 3}{n + 4} } \nl &= \abs{x^2} \lt 1 \pd \ea \] Then \[\abs{x^2} \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay \abs{x} \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay -1 \lt x \lt 1 \pd\] This is our preliminary interval of convergence. The radius of convergence of the series is \(1.\) We now test the endpoints: When \(x = -1,\) \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n\) becomes \[\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n \, (-1)^{2n + 1}}{n + 3} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n \cdot (-1)^{2n} \cdot (-1)}{n + 3} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n + 1}}{n + 3} \cma\] which converges by the Alternating Series Test because \(\lim_{n \to \infty} 1/(n + 3) = 0\) and \(1/(n + 3)\) is decreasing for \(n \geq 0.\) And at \(x = 1\) the series becomes \[\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n \, (1)^{2n + 1}}{n + 3} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{n + 3} \cma\] which converges by the Alternating Series Test by similar logic. Hence, we include both endpoints; our interval of convergence is \[\boxed{-1 \leq x \leq 1}\]
EXERCISE 14
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{x^n}{(n + 1) (-2)^n}\]
find the series' radius of convergence and interval of convergence.
SOLUTION Let \[a_n = \frac{x^n}{(n + 1) (-2)^n} = \frac{x^n}{(n + 1) (2^n) (-1)^n} \pd\] The series \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n\) converges if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1,\) by the Ratio Test. We can ignore the alternator \((-1)^n\) in \(\abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n}\) because the absolute value bars force the quantity to be positive. We obtain \[ \ba \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{x^n \cdot x}{(n + 2) (2^n)(2)} \cdot \frac{(n + 1) (2^n)}{x^n}} &= \abs{\frac{x}{2}} \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{n + 1}{n + 2}} \nl &= \abs{\frac{x}{2}} \lt 1 \pd \ea \] Then \[\abs{\frac{x}{2}} \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay -1 \lt \frac{x}{2} \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay -2 \lt x \lt 2 \pd\] The radius of convergence is \(2.\) This is our preliminary interval of convergence. We now test the endpoints: When \(x = -2,\) \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n\) becomes \[\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-2)^n}{(n + 1) (-2)^n} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{1}{n + 1}\cma\] which diverges by the Integral Test. \(\big[\int_0^\infty \dd x/(x + 1)\) is divergent.\(\big]\) But at \(x = 2\) the series becomes \[\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(2)^n}{(n + 1) (-2)^n} = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{1}{(n + 1) (-1)^n} \cma\] which converges by the Alternating Series Test because \(\lim_{n \to \infty} 1/(n + 1) = 0\) and \(1/(n + 1)\) is decreasing for \(n \geq 0.\) Thus, our interval of convergence is \[\boxed{-2 \lt x \leq 2}\]
EXERCISE 15
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{5(x + 5)^{2n}}{4^n \cdot n}\]
find the series' radius of convergence and interval of convergence.
SOLUTION Letting \(a_n = 5(x + 5)^{2n}/(4^n \cdot n),\) we note that by the Ratio Test the series \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) converges if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1.\) Here, \[a_{n + 1} = \frac{5(x + 5)^{2(n + 1)}}{4^{n + 1} (n + 1)} = \frac{5(x + 5)^{2n} (x + 5)^2}{4(n + 1) \cdot 4^n} \pd\] Therefore, we have \[ \ba \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{ \frac{5(x + 5)^{2n} (x + 5)^2}{4(n + 1) \cdot 4^n} \cdot \frac{n \cdot 4^n}{5(x + 5)^{2n}} } &= \abs{\frac{(x + 5)^2}{4}} \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{ \frac{n}{n + 1} } \nl &= \abs{\frac{(x + 5)^2}{4}} \pd \ea \] Then \[\abs{\frac{(x + 5)^2}{4}} \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay \abs{\frac{x + 5}{2}} \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay -7 \lt x \lt -3 \pd\] This is our preliminary interval of convergence. The radius of convergence of the series is \(R = 2,\) since the interval extends a distance of \(R\) from either side of the center of the series, \(x = -5.\) We now test the endpoints: When \(x = -7,\) \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) becomes \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{5 \cdot (-7 + 5)^{2n}}{n \cdot 4^n} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{5 \cdot 4^{n}}{n \cdot 4^n} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{5}{n} \cma\] which diverges because it is the Harmonic series. Similarly, at \(x = -3\) the series becomes \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{5 \cdot (-3 + 5)^{2n}}{n \cdot 4^n} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{5 \cdot 4^n}{n \cdot 4^n} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{5}{n} \cma\] the same divergent series produced when \(x = -7.\) We therefore exclude both endpoints, obtaining the interval of convergence of \[\boxed{-7 \lt x \lt -3}\]
EXERCISE 16
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{x^{5n}}{(2n)!}\]
find the series' radius of convergence and interval of convergence.
SOLUTION We let \(a_n = x^{5n}/(2n)! \, .\) Then \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n\) converges if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1\) by the Ratio Test. The expansion of \((2n + 2)!\) is \((2n + 2)(2n + 1)(2n)! \, ;\) consequently, \[a_{n + 1} = \frac{x^{5n} \cdot x^5}{(2n + 2)(2n + 1)(2n)!} \pd\] Thus, our limit is \[ \ba \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{ \frac{x^{5n} \cdot x^5}{(2n + 2)(2n + 1)(2n)!} \cdot \frac{(2n)!}{x^{5n}}} &= \abs{x^5} \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{ \frac{1}{(2n + 2)(2n + 1)} } \nl &= \abs{x^5} \cdot 0 \lt 1 \pd \ea \] Any value of \(x\) satisfies this inequality. Thus, the interval of convergence is \[\boxed{-\infty \lt x \lt \infty}\] and the radius of convergence is \(R = \infty.\)
EXERCISE 17
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(5x)^n \sqrt{n + 1}}{n^2}\]
find the series' radius of convergence and interval of convergence.
SOLUTION Let \(a_n = (5x)^n \sqrt{n + 1}/n^2.\) The series \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) converges if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1.\) We then solve for \(x\) that satisfies this condition. We have \[ \ba \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{(5x)^{n + 1} \sqrt{(n + 1) + 1}}{(n + 1)^2} \cdot \frac{n^2}{(5x)^n \sqrt{n + 1}}} &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{(5x)^n (5x) \sqrt{n + 2}}{n^2 + 2n + 1} \cdot \frac{n^2}{(5x)^n \sqrt{n + 1}}} \nl &= \abs{5x} \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{ \frac{\sqrt{n + 2}}{n^2 + 2n + 1} \cdot \frac{n^2}{\sqrt{n + 1}}} \nl &= \abs{5x} \lt 1 \pd \ea \] Hence, our preliminary interval of convergence is \[-1 \lt 5x \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay -\tfrac{1}{5} \lt x \lt \tfrac{1}{5} \pd\] We therefore find the radius of convergence to be \(R = 1/5.\) Lastly, we evaluate the series at the endpoints of this interval: At \(x = -1/5\) the series becomes \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{\left(5 \cdot -\frac{1}{5}\right)^n \sqrt{n + 1}}{n^2} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n \sqrt{n + 1}}{n^2} \cma\] which converges by the Alternating Series Test because \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt{n + 1}/n^2 = 0\) and \(\sqrt{n + 1}/n^2\) is decreasing for \(n \geq 1.\) At \(x = 1/5\) the series becomes \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{\left(5 \cdot \frac{1}{5}\right)^n \sqrt{n + 1}}{n^2} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(1)^n \sqrt{n + 1}}{n^2} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{\sqrt{n + 1}}{n^2} \cma\] which converges by Limit Comparison with \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \sqrt{n}/n^2,\) a \(p\)-series with \(p = 3/2 \gt 1.\) Thus, we include both endpoints. The interval of convergence is therefore \[\boxed{-\tfrac{1}{5} \leq x \leq \tfrac{1}{5}}\]
