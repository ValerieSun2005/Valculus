1.1 â€” Limit Properties

This section covers limit properties involving the sums, differences, products, quotients, and compositions of functions. We'll also cover the laws and procedures for more complex limits.

Limit Properties with Constants

If \(c\) is a constant, then \begin{equation} \lim_{x \to a} c = c \period \label{eq:lim-c} \end{equation} and \begin{equation} \lim_{x \to a} [cf(x)] = c \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \period \label{eq:cf(x)} \end{equation} Another trivial limit property is \begin{equation} \lim_{x \to a} x = a \period \label{eq:lim-x} \end{equation}

Limit Properties with Sums, Differences, Products, and Quotients

Consider two functions \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) such that \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x)\) both exist. Then \begin{align} \lim_{x \to a} [f(x) + g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to a} f(x) + \lim_{x \to a} g(x) \label{eq:lim-sum} \nl \lim_{x \to a} [f(x) - g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to a} f(x) - \lim_{x \to a} g(x) \label{eq:lim-diff} \nl \lim_{x \to a} [f(x) \cdot g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \cdot \lim_{x \to a} g(x) \label{eq:lim-prod} \nl \lim_{x \to a} \left[\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right] &= \frac{\ds \lim_{x \to a} f(x)}{\ds \lim_{x \to a} g(x)} \quad \textrm{if} \quad \lim_{x \to a} g(x) \ne 0 \label{eq:lim-quot} \period \end{align}

LIMITS OF SUMS, DIFFERENCES, PRODUCTS, AND QUOTIENTS
If \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x)\) both exist, then \begin{align*} \lim_{x \to a} [f(x) + g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to a} f(x) + \lim_{x \to a} g(x) &&(\text{Sum Law}) \nl \lim_{x \to a} [f(x) - g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to a} f(x) - \lim_{x \to a} g(x) &&(\text{Difference Law}) \nl \lim_{x \to a} [f(x) \cdot g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \cdot \lim_{x \to a} g(x) &&(\text{Product Law}) \nl \lim_{x \to a} \left[\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right] &= \frac{\ds \lim_{x \to a} f(x)}{\ds \lim_{x \to a} g(x)} \quad \textrm{if} \quad \lim_{x \to a} g(x) \ne 0 &&(\text{Quotient Law}) \end{align*}
EXAMPLE 1
Let \(f\) and \(g\) be functions such that \(\lim_{x \to 2} f(x) = 4\) and \(\lim_{x \to 2} g(x) = -2.\) Calculate the following limits.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 2}[f(x) + g(x)]\)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 2} [f(x) - g(x)]\)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 2} [f(x) g(x)]\)
  4. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 2} [f(x)/g(x)]\)
  1. The limits \(\lim_{x \to 2} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to 2} g(x)\) both exist, so we use \(\eqRefer{eq:lim-sum}\) to obtain \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 2}[f(x) + g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to 2} f(x) + \lim_{x \to 2} g(x) \nl &= 4 - 2 \nl &= \boxed{2} \ea \]
  2. We use \(\eqRefer{eq:lim-diff}\) to find \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 2}[f(x) - g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to 2} f(x) - \lim_{x \to 2} g(x) \nl &= 4 - (-2) \nl &= \boxed{6} \ea \]
  3. Using \(\eqRefer{eq:lim-prod}\) gives \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 2}[f(x) g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to 2} f(x) \cdot \lim_{x \to 2} g(x) \nl &= 4 \cdot (-2) \nl &= \boxed{-8} \ea \]
  4. We apply \(\eqRefer{eq:lim-quot} \col\) \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 2} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} &= \frac{\lim_{x \to 2} f(x)}{\lim_{x \to 2} g(x)} \nl &= \frac{4}{-2} \nl &= \boxed{-2} \ea \]
EXAMPLE 2
The graphs of \(f\) and \(g\) are shown in Figure 1. Determine each of the following limits if it exists.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0^+} [f(x)/g(x)]\)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to -1} [2f(x) + g(x)]\)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \, 0 \;} [f(x) - g(x)]\)
  4. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \, 0 \;} [f(x) + g(x)]\)

  1. Because \(\lim_{x \to 0^+} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to 0^+} g(x)\) both exist and the latter limit doesn't equal \(0,\) we use the Quotient Law to find \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} &= \frac{\lim_{x \to 0^+} f(x)}{\lim_{x \to 0^+} g(x)} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{4}{3}} \ea \]
  2. We find \(\lim_{x \to -1} f(x) = 1\) and \(\lim_{x \to -1} g(x) = 2.\) By \(\eqRefer{eq:cf(x)},\) \[\lim_{x \to -1} [2f(x)] = 2 \lim_{x \to -1} f(x) = 2(1) = 2 \period\] Now we apply the Sum Law to find \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -1} [2f(x) + g(x)] &= 2 \lim_{x \to -1} f(x) + \lim_{x \to -1} g(x) \nl &= 2(1) + 2 \nl &= \boxed{4} \ea \]
  3. The limits \(\lim_{x \to 0} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to 0} g(x)\) both do not exist, so we cannot apply \(\eqRefer{eq:lim-diff}.\) We instead consider the one-sided limits of \(\lim_{x \to 0} [f(x) - g(x)].\) Since \(\lim_{x \to 0^-} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to 0^-} g(x)\) both exist, we can apply \(\eqRefer{eq:lim-diff}\) to \(\lim_{x \to 0^-} [f(x) - g(x)] \col\) \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0^-} [f(x) - g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to 0^-} f(x) - \lim_{x \to 0^-} g(x) \nl &= 3 - 2 \nl &= 1 \period \nl \ea \] Likewise, the right-hand limit is \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0^+} [f(x) - g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} f(x) - \lim_{x \to 0^+} g(x) \nl &= 4 - 3 \nl &= 1 \period \ea \] Since these one-sided limits are equal, we conclude \[\lim_{x \to 0} [f(x) - g(x)] = \boxed{1}\]
  4. Neither \(\lim_{x \to 0} f(x)\) nor \(\lim_{x \to 0} g(x)\) exists, so we can't apply \(\eqRefer{eq:lim-sum}\) to \(\lim_{x \to 0} [f(x) + g(x)].\) But we can apply \(\eqRefer{eq:lim-sum}\) to the one-sided limits: \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0^-} [f(x) + g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to 0^-} f(x) + \lim_{x \to 0^-} g(x) = 3 + 2 = 5 \nl \lim_{x \to 0^+} [f(x) + g(x)] &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} f(x) + \lim_{x \to 0^+} g(x) = 4 + 3 = 7 \period \ea \] These values aren't equivalent, so we conclude that \(\lim_{x \to 0^+} [f(x) + g(x)]\) does not exist.

If we use \(\eqRefer{eq:lim-prod}\) repeatedly with \(f(x) = g(x),\) we find \begin{equation} \lim_{x \to a} [f(x)]^n = \left[\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\right]^n \end{equation} if \(n\) is a positive integer. We also have the properties \begin{align} \lim_{x \to a} x^n &= a^n \label{eq:lim-x^n} \nl \lim_{x \to a} \sqrt[n]{x} &= \sqrt[n]{a} \period \label{eq:lim-sqrtx} \end{align}

EXAMPLE 3
Let \(f\) be the function defined by \[f(x) = x^2 + 4x - 6 \period\] Find \(\lim_{x \to 3} f(x)\) and justify your answer using limit properties as appropriate.
We use the Sum Law to find \[\lim_{x \to 3} f(x) = \lim_{x \to 3} (x^2) + \lim_{x \to 3} (4x) + \lim_{x \to 3}(-6) \period\]

Limits of Composite Functions

Consider the composition \(f(g(x)),\) alternatively written as \((f \circ g)(x).\) If \(f\) is continuous at the value \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x),\) then \begin{equation} \lim_{x \to a} f(g(x)) = f \left(\lim_{x \to a} g(x) \right) \period \label{eq:lim-composite} \end{equation}
