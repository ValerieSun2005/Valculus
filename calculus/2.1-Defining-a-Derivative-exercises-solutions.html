2.1 Exercise Solutions — Defining a Derivative

EXERCISE 1
What does the derivative measure?
SOLUTION A derivative gives a function's instantaneous rate of change at a point. Specifically, the derivative of function \(f\) at \(x\) is \(f'(x),\) which is the slope of \(f\) at \(x.\)
EXERCISE 2
Define a tangent line. Why do we use a tangent line to calculate a derivative?
SOLUTION A line is tangent to the graph of \(f\) at point \(P(x, f(x))\) if the line passes through \(P\) with a slope of \(f'(x).\) We use a tangent line to calculate \(f'(x)\) because the line's slope matches the slope of \(f\) at \(x.\) Transforming a secant line into a tangent line enables us to derive a limit expression for the slope, \(f'(x).\)
EXERCISE 3
Let \(f\) be a differentiable function. Selected values of \(f(x)\) are shown in the table below. Find the best approximation for \(f'(2).\)
\(x\) \(-3\) \(-1\) \(2\) \(4\) \(6\) \(8\)
\(f(x)\) \(8\) \(-3\) \(-5\) \(2\) \(4\) \(1\)
SOLUTION We approximate \(f'(2)\) using the slope of the secant line formed by \((2, -5)\) and another nearby point. The \(x\)-value closest to \(2\) is \(4,\) so the slope of the line connecting \((2, -5)\) and \((4, 2)\) is \[\frac{2 - (-5)}{4 - 2} = \frac{7}{2} \pd\] Hence, this value is the best approximation for \(f'(2).\)
EXERCISE 4
Let \(h\) be a differentiable function. Selected values of \(h(t)\) are shown in the table below. Find the best approximation for \(h'(-1).\)
\(t\) \(-5\) \(-2\) \(-1\) \(2\) \(3\) \(8\)
\(h(t)\) \(4\) \(-1\) \(2\) \(5\) \(6\) \(3\)
SOLUTION We approximate \(h'(-1)\) using the slope of the secant line formed by \((-1, 2)\) and another nearby point. The \(x\)-value closest to \(-1\) is \(-2,\) so the slope of the line connecting \((-1, 2)\) and \((-2, -1)\) is \[\frac{2 - (-1)}{-1 - (-2)} = 3 \pd\] Hence, this value is the best approximation for \(h'(-1).\)
EXERCISE 5
A population of deer is modeled by the function \(N(t),\) where \(t\) is time measured in months. What is the best interpretation of the expression \(N'(5) = 30 \ques\)
SOLUTION After \(5\) months the deer's population is growing at a rate of \(30\) deer per month.
EXERCISE 6
The amount of money in a bank is modeled by function \(M(t),\) where \(M\) is measured in dollars and \(t\) is measured in years. It is known that \(M'(2) = -40.\) What is the best interpretation of this value?
SOLUTION After \(2\) years, the amount of money in the bank account is decreasing at a rate of \(\$40\) per year.
EXERCISE 7
The temperature of a cooling batch of cookies is modeled by function \(T(t),\) where \(T(t)\) is measured in degrees Fahrenheit and \(t\) is measured in minutes since the cookies were removed from the oven. What is the best interpretation of the value \(\textDeriv{T}{t}|_{t = 3} = -14 \ques\)
SOLUTION Three minutes after the cookies are taken out of the oven, the cookies are cooling at a rate of \(14 \undiv{°F}{min}.\)
EXERCISE 8
Let \(f\) be a differentiable function. Explain the behavior of \(f(x)\) at \(x = a\) in each of the following cases.
  1. \(f'(a) \lt 0\)
  2. \(f'(a) \gt 0\)
  3. \(f'(a) = 0\)
SOLUTION It is important to note that \(f'(a)\) is the slope of \(f\) at \(a.\)
  1. If \(f'(a) \lt 0,\) then the slope of \(f\) at \(a\) is negative. Hence, \(f(x)\) is decreasing at \(x = a.\)
  2. If \(f'(a) \gt 0,\) then the slope of \(f\) at \(a\) is positive. Thus, \(f(x)\) is increasing at \(x = a.\)
  3. If \(f'(a) = 0,\) then the slope of \(f\) at \(a\) is \(0.\) Therefore, \(f(x)\) has a horizontal tangent when \(x = a.\)
EXERCISE 9
For \[\ds \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{x + h} - \sqrt x}{h}\] rewrite the limit as a derivative.
SOLUTION We compare this form to the limit definition of \(f'(x) \col\) \[f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \pd\] We have \(f(x) = \sqrt x\) and \(f(x + h) = \sqrt{x + h}.\) Thus, the limit in question represents \(\textDeriv{}{x} \left[\sqrt x\right].\)
EXERCISE 10
For \[\ds \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sin(x + h) - \sin x}{h}\] rewrite the limit as a derivative.
SOLUTION Comparing this form to the limit definition of \(f'(x),\) \[f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \cma\] we see \(f(x) = \sin x\) and \(f(x + h) = \sin(x + h).\) Hence, the given limit represents \(\textDeriv{}{x} \left[\sin x\right].\)
EXERCISE 11
For \[\ds \lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}\] rewrite the limit as a derivative.
SOLUTION This limit takes the form \[f'(a) = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a} \pd\] Here \(a = 2,\) \(f(x) = x^2,\) and \(f(2) = 4.\) Therefore, the limit represents the derivative of \(x^2\) at \(x = 2.\)
EXERCISE 12
For \[\ds \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \left(\frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{2 + h}\right)\] rewrite the limit as a derivative.
SOLUTION We rewrite the limit as \[\lim_{h \to 0} -\frac{1}{h} \left(\frac{1}{2 + h} - \frac{1}{2} \right) \pd\] We recognize this limit as the negative derivative of \(1/x\) at \(x = 2.\) Hence, the limit equals \(\textDeriv{}{x} [1/x]_{x = 2}.\)
EXERCISE 13
For \[\ds \deriv{}{x} [7x]\] use limits to calculate the derivative.
SOLUTION By the limit definition of the derivative, we find \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} [7x] &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{7(x + h) - 7x}{h} \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{7h}{h} \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} 7 \nl &= \boxed{7} \ea \]
EXERCISE 14
For \[\ds \deriv{}{x} [\pi]\] use limits to calculate the derivative.
SOLUTION The limit definition of \(f'(x)\) is \[f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \pd\] In our case \(f(x) = \pi.\) Accordingly, \(f(x + h) = \pi,\) and \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} [\pi] &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\pi - \pi}{h} \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{0}{h} \nl &= \boxed{0} \ea \] Note that \(\lim_{h \to 0} (0/h)\) is not in the indeterminate form \(\indZero,\) since the numerator is fixed at, not approaching, \(0.\) This result affirms Property 1 in the section, which states that the derivative of any constant is \(0.\)
EXERCISE 15
For \[\ds \deriv{}{x} [x + 3]\] use limits to calculate the derivative.
SOLUTION Using the limit definition of the derivative and simplifying the resulting limit give \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} [x + 3] &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x + h + 3) - (x + 3)}{h} \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{h}{h} \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} 1 \nl &= \boxed 1 \ea \]
EXERCISE 16
For \[\ds \deriv{}{x} \left[5x - 4\right]_{x = 1}\] use limits to calculate the derivative.
SOLUTION We may use either difference quotient \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{[5(1 + h) - 4] - [5(1) - 4]}{h} \or \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{[5x - 4] - [5(1) - 4]}{x - 1} \pd\] We choose the latter option, as it is simpler because no expansion of terms is necessary. But because both limits should produce the same answer, the choice is yours and is insignificant. Simplifying the latter limit gives \[\lim_{x \to 1} \frac{5x - 5}{x - 1} = \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{5(x - 1)}{x - 1} = \lim_{x \to 1} 5 = \boxed{5}\] Hence, the derivative equals \(5.\)
EXERCISE 17
For \[\ds \deriv{}{x} \left[2x^2 \right]\] use limits to calculate the derivative.
SOLUTION We write the derivative as \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2(x + h)^2 - 2x^2}{h} \pd\] We cannot immediately substitute \(h = 0,\) since the limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) Instead, we expand the terms in the numerator and search for a factor to cancel in the numerator and denominator. Doing so, we attain \[ \ba \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2(x^2 + 2xh + h^2) - 2x^2}{h} &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{4xh + 2h^2}{h} \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} (4x + 2h) \nl &= \boxed{4x} \ea \]
EXERCISE 18
For \[\ds \deriv{}{t} \left[4t^2 - 2\right]_{t = 1}\] use limits to calculate the derivative.
SOLUTION We require the derivative at a point. We can use either \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{[4(1 + h)^2 - 2] - [4(1)^2 - 2]}{h} \or \lim_{t \to 1} \frac{[4t^2 - 2] - [4(1)^2 - 2]}{t - 1} \pd\] The latter form is slightly easier to use because no expansion of terms is necessary. But since both choices should yield the same result, the decision is insignificant. Simplifying the latter difference quotient and factoring yield \[ \ba \lim_{t \to 1} \frac{4t^2 - 4}{t - 1} &= \lim_{t \to 1} \frac{4(t^2 - 1)}{t - 1} \nl &= \lim_{t \to 1} \frac{4(t + 1)(t - 1)}{t - 1} \nl &= \lim_{t \to 1} 4(t + 1) \nl &= \boxed 8 \ea \]
EXERCISE 19
For \[\ds \deriv{}{t} \left[\sqrt{t + 1}\right]_{t = 3}\] use limits to calculate the derivative.
SOLUTION We can choose to use either \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{3 + h + 1} - \sqrt{3 + 1}}{h} \or \lim_{t \to 3} \frac{\sqrt{t + 1} - \sqrt{3 + 1}}{t - 3} \pd\] We select the latter choice. (The decision is insignificant.) Simplifying the limit and rationalizing the numerator, by multiplying the numerator and denominator by the numerator's conjugate, give \[ \ba \lim_{t \to 3} \frac{\sqrt{t + 1} - 2}{t - 3} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{t + 1} + 2}{\sqrt{t + 1} + 2} &= \lim_{t \to 3} \frac{(t + 1) - 4}{(t - 3) \par{\sqrt{t + 1} + 2}} \nl &= \lim_{t \to 3} \frac{t - 3}{(t - 3) \par{\sqrt{t + 1} + 2}} \nl &= \lim_{t \to 3} \frac{1}{\sqrt{t + 1} + 2} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{1}{4}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 20
For \[\ds \deriv{}{x} \left[\frac{3}{x} \right]\] use limits to calculate the derivative.
SOLUTION We use the limit definition of the derivative to obtain \[\deriv{}{x} \left[\frac{3}{x} \right] = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \left(\frac{3}{x + h} - \frac{3}{x}\right) \pd\] We can't immediately plug in \(h = 0,\) since doing so yields the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) Instead, we find a common denominator for the fractions and merge them. Doing so gives \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \left[\frac{3}{x} \right] &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \left(\frac{3x - 3(x + h)}{x(x + h)}\right) \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \left(\frac{-3h}{x(x + h)}\right) \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{-3}{x(x + h)} \nl &= \boxed{-\frac{3}{x^2}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 21
For \[\ds \deriv{}{x} \left[\frac{1}{x + 2}\right]\] use limits to calculate the derivative.
SOLUTION Using the limit definition of the derivative gives \[\deriv{}{x} \left[\frac{1}{x + 2}\right] = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \left[\frac{1}{x + h + 2} - \frac{1}{x + 2}\right] \pd\] This limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) Finding a common denominator and merging the fractions yield \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \left[\frac{1}{x + 2}\right] &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \left[\frac{(x + 2) - (x + h + 2)}{(x + h + 2)(x + 2)}\right] \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \left[\frac{-h}{(x + h + 2)(x + 2)}\right] \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{-1}{(x + h + 2)(x + 2)} \nl &= \boxed{-\frac{1}{(x + 2)^2}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 22
For \[\ds \deriv{}{u} \left[u^3\right]\] use limits to calculate the derivative.
SOLUTION The limit that represents the derivative is \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(u + h)^3 - u^3}{h} \pd\] The expansion of \((u + h)^3\) is \[u^3 + 3u^2 h + 3uh^2 + h^3 \pd\] Hence, the limit becomes \[ \ba \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{u^3 + 3u^2 h + 3uh^2 + h^3 - u^3}{h} &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{3u^2 h + 3uh^2 + h^3}{h} \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} (3u^2 + 3uh + h^2) \nl &= \boxed{3u^2} \ea \]
EXERCISE 23
For \[\ds \deriv{}{x} \left[\frac{1}{x^2}\right]_{x = 2}\] use limits to calculate the derivative.
SOLUTION We can use either difference quotient \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \left(\frac{1}{(2 + h)^2} - \frac{1}{2^2}\right) \or \lim_{x \to 2} \frac{1}{x - 2} \left(\frac{1}{x^2} - \frac{1}{2^2}\right) \pd \] It is difficult to argue that the first quotient is easiest to use; we choose the second quotient because no expansion of terms is necessary. Simplifying the limit gives \[\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{1}{x - 2} \left(\frac{1}{x^2} - \frac{1}{4}\right) \pd\] Finding a common denominator and merging fractions, we obtain \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 2} \frac{1}{x - 2} \par{\frac{4 - x^2}{4x^2}} &= \lim_{x \to 2} \frac{1}{x - 2} \par{\frac{-(x^2 - 4)}{4x^2}} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 2} \frac{-1}{x - 2} \par{\frac{(x + 2)(x - 2)}{4x^2}} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 2} -\frac{x + 2}{4x^2} \nl &= \boxed{-\frac{1}{4}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 24
For \[\ds \deriv{}{t} \left[\frac{1}{t^2 - 1}\right]_{t = 3}\] use limits to calculate the derivative.
SOLUTION We write a difference quotient for the derivative as \[\lim_{t \to 3} \frac{1}{t - 3} \left(\frac{1}{t^2 - 1} - \frac{1}{3^2 - 1}\right) \pd\] Simplifying and finding a common denominator of the fractions give \[ \lim_{t \to 3} \frac{1}{t - 3} \left(\frac{1}{t^2 - 1} - \frac{1}{8} \right) = \lim_{t \to 3} \frac{9 - t^2}{8(t - 3)(t^2 - 1)} \pd \] The numerator is a difference of squares: \((3 + t)(3 - t).\) Hence, the limit becomes \[\lim_{t \to 3} \frac{(3 + t)(3 - t)}{8(t - 3)(t^2 - 1)} = \lim_{t \to 3} \frac{-(3 + t)}{8(t^2 - 1)} = \boxed{-\frac{3}{32}}\]
EXERCISE 25
For \[\ds \deriv{}{t} \left[\frac{1}{\sqrt t}\right]\] use limits to calculate the derivative.
SOLUTION The limit of our difference quotient is \[\deriv{}{t} \left[\frac{1}{\sqrt t}\right] = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{t + h}} - \frac{1}{\sqrt t} \right) \pd\] We find a common denominator in the fractions and merge them. Doing so yields \[ \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \left(\frac{\sqrt t - \sqrt{t + h}}{\sqrt{t(t + h)}} \right) \pd \] The limit still is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) Since the numerator is a sum or difference of radicals, we rationalize the numerator by multiplying the numerator and denominator by the numerator's conjugate: \(\sqrt t + \sqrt{t + h}.\) Doing so gives \[ \ba \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \left(\frac{\sqrt t - \sqrt{t + h}}{\sqrt{t(t + h)}}\right) \cdot \frac{\sqrt t + \sqrt{t + h}}{\sqrt t + \sqrt{t + h}} &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \left[\frac{t - (t + h)}{\left(\sqrt t + \sqrt{t + h}\right) \sqrt{t(t + h)}}\right] \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \left[\frac{-h}{\left(\sqrt t + \sqrt{t + h}\right) \sqrt{t(t + h)}}\right] \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{-1}{\left(\sqrt t + \sqrt{t + h}\right) \sqrt{t(t + h)}} \nl &= \frac{-1}{\par{2 \sqrt t} t} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{-1}{2t^{3/2}}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 26
For \[g(t) = \begin{cases} 8t + 2 & t \leq 0 \nl 2 & t \gt 0 \end{cases} \] determine whether the function is differentiable at the piecewise split.
SOLUTION Function \(g(t)\) is differentiable at \(t = 0\) if \(g\) is continuous at \(t = 0\) and if the one-sided derivatives of \(g\) at \(t = 0\) are equal. We check for continuity by checking whether \(\lim_{t \to 0} g(t) = g(0).\) The left-hand limit of \(g\) at \(t = 0\) is \[\lim_{t \to 0^-} g(t) = 8(0) + 2 = 2 \pd\] The right-hand limit of \(g\) is \[\lim_{t \to 0^+} g(t) = 2 \pd\] Since both one-sided limits are equivalent, \(\lim_{t \to 0} g(t)\) exists and equals \(2,\) which is \(g(0).\) Hence, \(g\) is continuous at \(t = 0.\) Also, the one-sided derivatives of \(g\) at \(t = 0\) must be equivalent. The left-hand derivative is \[\lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{8(0 + h) - 8(0)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{8h}{h} = 8 \pd\] The right-hand derivative is \[\lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{2 - 2}{h} = 0 \pd\] Since the one-sided derivatives aren't equal, \(g'(0)\) does not exist. Hence, \(g\) is not differentiable at \(t = 0.\)
EXERCISE 27
For \[f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 + 1 & x \lt 1 \nl 2x - 1 & x \geq 1 \end{cases} \] determine whether the function is differentiable at the piecewise split.
SOLUTION Function \(f(x)\) is differentiable at \(x = 1\) if \(f\) is continuous at \(x = 1\) and if the one-sided derivatives of \(f\) at \(x = 1\) are equal. To check for continuity we check whether \(\lim_{x \to 1} f(x) = f(1).\) The left-hand limit of \(f\) at \(x = 1\) is \[\lim_{x \to 1^-} g(x) = (1)^2 + 1 = 2 \pd\] The right-hand limit of \(g\) is \[\lim_{x \to 1^+} g(x) = 2(1) - 1 = 1 \pd\] Because these one-sided limits are not equivalent, \(\lim_{x \to 1} f(x)\) does not exist. Thus, \(f\) is not continuous at \(x = 1;\) consequently, \(f\) is not differentiable at \(x = 1.\)
EXERCISE 28
For \[g(x) = \begin{cases} 4x & x \lt 2 \nl x^2 + 4 & x \geq 2 \end{cases} \] determine whether the function is differentiable at the piecewise split.
SOLUTION Function \(g(x)\) is differentiable at \(x = 2\) if \(g\) is continuous at \(x = 2\) and if the one-sided derivatives of \(g\) at \(x = 2\) are equal. We check for continuity by determining whether \(\lim_{x \to 2} g(x) = g(2).\) The left-hand limit of \(g\) at \(x = 2\) is \[\lim_{x \to 2^-} g(x) = 4(2) = 8 \pd\] The right-hand limit of \(g\) is \[\lim_{x \to 2^+} g(x) = (2)^2 + 4 = 8 \pd\] Both one-sided limits are equivalent, so \(\lim_{x \to 2} g(x)\) exists and equals \(8,\) which is \(g(2).\) So \(g\) is continuous at \(x = 2.\) The one-sided derivatives of \(g\) at \(x = 2\) must also be equivalent. The left-hand derivative is \[\lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{4(2 + h) - 4(2)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{4h}{h} = 4 \pd\] The right-hand derivative is \[\lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{[(2 + h)^2 + 4] - [(2)^2 + 4]}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{4h}{h} = 4 \pd\] Because the one-sided derivatives are equivalent, \(g'(2)\) exists. Hence, \(g\) is differentiable at the piecewise split, \(x = 2.\)
EXERCISE 29
For \[h(t) = \begin{cases} \frac{t + 1}{2} & t \leq 1 \nl \sqrt t & t \gt 1 \end{cases} \] determine whether the function is differentiable at the piecewise split.
SOLUTION Function \(h(t)\) is differentiable at \(t = 1\) if \(h\) is continuous at \(t = 1\) and if the one-sided derivatives of \(h\) at \(t = 1\) are equal. We check for continuity by seeing whether \(\lim_{t \to 1} h(t) = h(1).\) The left-hand limit of \(h\) at \(t = 1\) is \[\lim_{t \to 1^-} h(t) = \frac{1 + 1}{2} = 1 \pd\] The right-hand limit of \(h\) is \[\lim_{t \to 1^+} h(t) = \sqrt 1 = 1 \pd\] Because both one-sided limits are equivalent, \(\lim_{t \to 1} h(t)\) exists and equals \(1,\) which is \(h(1).\) Thus, \(h\) is continuous at \(t = 1.\) Also, the one-sided derivatives of \(h\) at \(t = 1\) must be equivalent. The left-hand derivative of \(h\) at \(t = 1\) is \[ \ba \lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{(1 + h + 1) - (1 + 1)}{2h} &= \lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{1}{2} \nl &= \frac{1}{2} \pd \ea \] The right-hand derivative is \[ \ba \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{\sqrt{1 + h} - \sqrt 1}{h} &= \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{\sqrt{1 + h} - 1}{h} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{1 + h} + 1}{\sqrt{1 + h} + 1} \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{(1 + h) - 1}{h \left(\sqrt{1 + h} + 1 \right)} \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + h} + 1} \nl &= \frac{1}{2} \pd \ea \] These one-sided derivatives are equal; \(h\) is therefore differentiable at \(t = 1.\)
EXERCISE 30
For \[\ds \deriv{^2}{x^2} [8x]\] compute the given higher-order derivative.
SOLUTION The key idea is to take derivatives one at time. The first derivative is given by \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{8(x + h) - 8x}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{8h}{h} = 8 \pd\] The second derivative is \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{8 - 8}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{0}{h} = \boxed 0\] Note that the limit \(\lim_{h \to 0} (0/h)\) is not in the indeterminate form \(\indZero,\) since the numerator is fixed at \(0.\)
EXERCISE 31
For \[\ds \deriv{^2}{x^2} [x^2 - 2]\] compute the given higher-order derivative.
SOLUTION We take derivatives one at time. The first derivative is given by \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{[(x + h)^2 - 2] - [x^2 - 2]}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2xh + h^2}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} (2x + h) = 2x \pd\] Then the second derivative is the derivative of \(2x\)—that is, \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2(x + h) - 2x}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2h}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} 2 = \boxed 2\]
EXERCISE 32
For \[\ds \deriv{^2}{x^2} [3x - 4]\] compute the given higher-order derivative.
SOLUTION The first derivative is given by \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{[3(x + h) - 4] - [3x - 4]}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{3h}{h} = 3 \pd\] The second derivative is \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{3 - 3}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{0}{h} = \boxed 0\] The limit \(\lim_{h \to 0} (0/h)\) is not in the indeterminate form \(\indZero,\) because the numerator stays at \(0.\)
EXERCISE 33
For \[\ds \deriv{^3}{t^3} [3t^2 + 5]\] compute the given higher-order derivative.
SOLUTION Taking derivatives incrementally, we begin with the first derivative: \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{[3(t + h)^2 + 5] - [3t^2 + 5]}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{6th + h^2}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} (6t + h) = 6t \pd\] Then the second derivative is the derivative of \(6t\)—namely, \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{6(t + h) - 6t}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{6h}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} 6 = 6 \pd\] Lastly, the third derivative is \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{6 - 6}{0} = \boxed 0\]
EXERCISE 34
Let \(f\) and \(g\) be differentiable functions such that \(f'(2) = 3\) and \(g'(2) = -1.\) Evaluate the following derivatives.
  1. \(\ds \deriv{}{x} [f(x) + g(x)]_{x = 2}\)
  2. \(\ds \deriv{}{x} [f(x) - g(x)]_{x = 2}\)
  3. \(\ds \deriv{}{x} [2f(x)]_{x = 2}\)
  4. \(\ds \deriv{}{x} [4f(x) - 3g(x)]_{x = 2}\)
SOLUTION
  1. The derivative of a sum is a sum of derivatives. Hence, \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} [f(x) + g(x)]_{x = 2} &= f'(2) + g'(2) \nl &= 3 + (-1) = \boxed{2} \ea \]
  2. The derivative of a difference is a difference of derivatives. Thus, we have \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} [f(x) - g(x)]_{x = 2} &= f'(2) - g'(2) \nl &= 3 - (-1) = \boxed 4 \ea \]
  3. The derivative of a constant times a function is the same as the constant times a derivative. Hence, we pull out the constant, obtaining \[\deriv{}{x} [2f(x)]_{x = 2} = 2 f'(2) = 2(3) = \boxed 6\]
  4. Applying derivative properties gives \[ 4 f'(2) - 3 g'(2) = 4(3) - 3(-1) = \boxed{15} \]
EXERCISE 35
Let \(f\) and \(g\) be differentiable functions such that \(f'(-1) = 2\) and \(g'(-1) = -3.\) Evaluate the following derivatives.
  1. \(\ds \deriv{}{x} [f(x) + g(x)]_{x = -1}\)
  2. \(\ds \deriv{}{x} [g(x) - f(x)]_{x = -1}\)
  3. \(\ds \deriv{}{x} [4f(x)]_{x = -1}\)
  4. \(\ds \deriv{}{x} [2g(x) - 5f(x)]_{x = -1}\)
SOLUTION
  1. The derivative of a sum is a sum of derivatives. Hence, \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} [f(x) + g(x)]_{x = -1} &= f'(-1) + g'(-1) \nl &= 2 - 3 = \boxed{-1} \ea \]
  2. The derivative of a difference is a difference of derivatives. Thus, we have \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} [g(x) - f(x)]_{x = -1} &= g'(-1) - f'(-1) \nl &= -3 - 2 = \boxed{-5} \ea \]
  3. The constant \(4\) can be pulled out: \[\deriv{}{x} [4f(x)]_{x = -1} = 4 f'(-1) = 4(2) = \boxed 8\]
  4. Applying derivative properties gives \[ 2 g'(-1) - 5 f'(-1) = 2(-3) - 5(2) = \boxed{-16} \]
EXERCISE 36
An object's momentum \(p\) is the product of its mass \(m\) and velocity \(v.\) The force \(F\) that acts on an object is the derivative of momentum with respect to time \(t.\) If the object's mass remains constant, then show that \(F = ma,\) where \(a\) is the object's acceleration. This relationship is Newton's Second Law of Motion.
SOLUTION The object's momentum is \(p = mv.\) The force that acts on the object is the derivative of this expression with respect to time—that is, \[F = \deriv{}{t} [mv] \pd\] Since \(m\) is constant, we take it out. And acceleration is the time derivative of velocity, so we obtain \[F = m \deriv{v}{t} = ma \cma\] as requested.
EXERCISE 37
A particle moves in a straight line such that its position function with respect to time is \(s(t) = t^2 + 3t - 2,\) where \(s(t)\) is measured in inches and \(t\) is measured in seconds. After \(2\) seconds, find the particle's velocity.
SOLUTION The particle's velocity is the derivative of position. Hence, \[ \ba v(2) = s'(2) &= \lim_{t \to 2} \frac{s(t) - s(2)}{t - 2} \nl &= \lim_{t \to 2} \frac{[t^2 + 3t - 2] - 8}{t - 2} \nl &= \lim_{t \to 2} \frac{t^2 + 3t - 10}{t - 2} \nl &= \lim_{t \to 2} \frac{(t + 5)(t - 2)}{t - 2} \nl &= \lim_{t \to 2} (t + 5) \nl &= 7 \pd \ea \] The particle's velocity after \(2\) seconds is \(7 \undiv{in}{sec}.\)
EXERCISE 38
A ball falls to the ground as modeled by the height function \(h(t) = -16t^2 - 32t + 96,\) where \(h(t)\) is measured in feet and \(t\) is measured in seconds for \(t \geq 0.\) After \(1\) second, what is the ball's speed?
SOLUTION Velocity is the derivative of position with respect to time. Position in this case is the ball's height above the ground. At \(t = 1\) the ball's velocity is \[h'(1) = \lim_{t \to 1} \frac{h(t) - h(1)}{t - 1} = \lim_{t \to 1} \frac{[-16t^2 -32t + 96] - [48]}{t - 1} \pd\] Simplifying and factoring the limit give \[ \ba h'(1) &= \lim_{t \to 1} \frac{-16t^2 - 32t + 48}{t - 1} \nl &= \lim_{t \to 1} \frac{-16(t^2 + 2t - 3)}{t - 1} \nl &= \lim_{t \to 1} \frac{-16(t + 3)(t - 1)}{t - 1} \nl &= \lim_{t \to 1} [-16(t + 3)] \nl &= -64 \pd \ea \] Hence, at \(t = 1\) the ball's velocity is \(-64 \undiv{ft}{sec}.\) Velocity is speed in a certain direction, meaning the ball's speed at \(t = 1\) is \(|-64| = 64 \undiv{ft}{sec}.\)
EXERCISE 39
The quadratic \(f(x) = a(x - p)^2 + q\) has its vertex at the point \((p, q).\) Show that \(f\) has a horizontal tangent at the vertex.
SOLUTION Since \(f(x)\) has a horizontal tangent at \(x = h,\) \(f'(h) = 0.\) We aim to show this fact. A limit definition of \(f'(h)\) is \[\lim_{x \to p} \frac{f(x) - f(p)}{x - p} = \lim_{x \to p} \frac{[a(x - p)^2 + q] - [a(p - p)^2 + q]}{x - p} \pd\] Simplifying the limit, we obtain \[\lim_{x \to p} \frac{a(x - p)^2}{x - p} = \lim_{x \to p} a(x - p) = a(p - p) = 0 \pd\] Hence, \(f\) has a horizontal tangent at \(x = p.\)
