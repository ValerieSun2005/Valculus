7.2 — Surface Areas of Revolution

A three-dimensional object has two properties of interest: volume (which we explored in Chapter 5) and surface area. We define a solid's surface area to be how much area its outer surface occupies. You are likely familiar with calculating surface areas of cubes and rectangular prisms, but how do we calculate the surface area of any smooth object? In this section we discuss how to find the surface area of the solid produced upon rotating a region about an axis.

Let's first establish formulas for the surface areas of simple solids. In Figure 1 the body of a cylinder of radius \(r\) and length \(L\) is unwrapped into a rectangular sheet of dimensions \(L\) by \(2 \pi r\) (the circumference of the circular base). The cylinder's lateral surface area is therefore \(A = 2 \pi r L.\) Conversely, to find the lateral surface area of a right cone's large base (a conical frustum), we average its radii \(r_1\) and \(r_2.\) If \(L\) is the frustum's slant height, then we treat it as a cylinder of length \(L\) and average radius \(r.\) Since the average radius is \(r = \par{r_1 + r_2}/2,\) the frustum's lateral surface area is \begin{align} A &= 2 \pi \par{\frac{r_1 + r_2}{2}} L \nonum \nl &= \pi \par{r_1 + r_2} L \pd \label{eq:A-frus} \end{align} (See Figure 2.)

Surface Area of Smooth Solid Suppose that some region \(R,\) bounded by the smooth curve \(y = f(x)\) and the \(x\)-axis from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\) (Figure 3A), is rotated about the \(x\)-axis to generate a solid. We can calculate this solid's volume using the Disk and Washer Methods of Section 5.3. Yet to compute its surface area, we employ a new strategy: We cut the interval \([a, b]\) into \(n\) subintervals with endpoints \(x_0, x_1, \dots, x_{n}\) and equal width \(\Delta x.\) In each subinterval we calculate the surface area of a band of the solid. Let \(P_0, P_1, \dots, P_n\) be points that lie on the curve. If \(n\) is small, then each approximating band resembles a conical frustum. Then we sum the surface areas of all \(n\) bands and take the limit as \(n \to \infty.\) In a general subinterval \(\parbr{x_{i - 1}, x_i},\) in which \(x_i^*\) is a sample point, the band's left radius is \(y_{i - 1},\) its right radius is \(y_i,\) and its slant height is \(\length{P_{i - 1} P_i}.\) (See Figure 3B.) By \(\eqref{eq:A-frus},\) the band's surface area is roughly \[\Delta A \approx 2 \pi \par{\frac{y_{i - 1} + y_i}{2}} \length{P_{i - 1} P_i} = \pi \par{y_{i - 1} + y_i} \length{P_{i - 1} P_i} \pd\] From Section 7.1, the arc length \(\length{P_{i - 1} P_i}\) is given by \(\sqrt{1 + [f'\par{x_i^*}]^2}.\) Assuming \(\Delta x\) is small, and because \(f\) is continuous, we take \(y_{i - 1} = f \par{x_{i - 1}}\) \(\approx f \par{x_i^*}\) and \(y_i = f \par{x_i}\) \(\approx f \par{x_i^*}.\) Thus, we have \[\Delta A \approx \pi \parbr{2 f \par{x_i^*}} \sqrt{1 + [f'\par{x_i^*}]^2} \Delta x = 2 \pi f \par{x_i^*} \sqrt{1 + [f'\par{x_i^*}]^2} \Delta x \pd\] Summing the surface areas of all \(n\) bands, we obtain \[A = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i = 1}^n 2 \pi f \par{x_i^*} \sqrt{1 + [f'\par{x_i^*}]^2} \Delta x \pd\] As \(n \to \infty,\) our assumptions become more valid and the limit approaches the solid's surface area. We recognize the right-hand side as a Riemann Sum for the function \(2 \pi f(x) \sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2},\) so we get \begin{equation} A = \int_a^b 2 \pi f(x) \sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2} \di x \pd \label{eq:A-x-f} \end{equation} Or in Leibniz notation, we write \[A = \int_a^b 2 \pi y \sqrt{1 + \par{\deriv{y}{x}}^2} \di x \pd\] Similarly, if a region is bounded between the smooth curve \(x = g(y)\) and the \(y\)-axis from \(y = c\) to \(y = d,\) then the surface area of the generated solid is \begin{equation} A = \int_c^d 2 \pi g(y) \sqrt{1 + [g'(y)]^2} \di y \cma \label{eq:A-y-g} \end{equation} or in Leibniz notation, \[A = \int_c^d 2 \pi x \sqrt{1 + \par{\deriv{x}{y}}^2} \di y \pd\]

SURFACE AREAS OF REVOLUTION
If the region bounded by the smooth curve \(y = f(x)\) and the \(x\)-axis from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\) is rotated about the \(x\)-axis, then the generated solid has a surface area of \begin{equation} A = \int_a^b 2 \pi f(x) \sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2} \di x \pd \eqlabel{eq:A-x-f} \end{equation} Likewise, if the region bounded by the smooth curve \(x = g(y)\) and the \(y\)-axis from \(y = c\) to \(y = d\) is rotated about the \(y\)-axis, then the generated solid has a surface area of \begin{equation} A = \int_c^d 2 \pi g(y) \sqrt{1 + [g'(y)]^2} \di y \pd \eqlabel{eq:A-y-g} \end{equation}

General Formulas for Surface Area If we rotate a region about the \(x\)-axis, then \(\eqref{eq:A-x-f}\) can only be used if we express all quantities in terms of \(x.\) Similarly, if we rotate about the \(y\)-axis, then \(\eqref{eq:A-y-g}\) is only applicable if we work entirely in terms of \(y.\) Yet sometimes we must rotate about an axis while using the opposite variable—for example, rotating about the \(x\)-axis while integrating with \(y.\) The following formulas provide us this flexibility: We generalize \(\eqref{eq:A-x-f}\) as \begin{align} A &= \int_a^b 2 \pi y \di s \pd \label{eq:A-x} \end{align} Likewise, \(\eqref{eq:A-y-g}\) is generalized as \begin{align} A &= \int_c^d 2 \pi x \di s \pd \label{eq:A-y} \end{align} In each case, we choose the differential \(\dd s\) to be either \[\di s = \sqrt{1 + \par{\deriv{y}{x}}^2} \di x \or \di s = \sqrt{1 + \par{\deriv{x}{y}}^2} \di y \pd\]

GENERALIZED FORMULAS FOR SURFACE AREAS OF REVOLUTION
We calculate the surface area of revolution using the following formulas: \begin{alignat}{2} A &= \int_a^b 2 \pi y \di s \cma \lspace &&[\text{Rotating about } x \text{-axis}] \eqlabel{eq:A-x} \nl A &= \int_c^d 2 \pi x \di s \cma \lspace &&[\text{Rotating about } y \text{-axis}] \eqlabel{eq:A-y} \end{alignat} where \[\di s = \sqrt{1 + \par{\deriv{y}{x}}^2} \di x \or \di s = \sqrt{1 + \par{\deriv{x}{y}}^2} \di y \pd\]
EXAMPLE 1
Calculate the surface area of the solid generated when the region bounded by the graph of \(y = x^3,\) the \(x\)-axis, the \(y\)-axis, and the line \(x = 1\) is rotated about the \(x\)-axis.
The region is bounded from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 1.\) Using \(\eqref{eq:A-x-f}\) with \(f(x) = x^3,\) we find the surface area to be \[A = \int_0^1 2 \pi \par{x^3} \sqrt{1 + \par{3x^2}^2} \di x = 2 \pi \int_0^1 x^3 \sqrt{1 + 9x^4} \di x \pd\] Letting \(u = 1 + 9x^4,\) we get \(\dd u = 36x^3 \di x.\) When \(x = 0,\) \(u = 1;\) when \(x = 1,\) \(u = 10.\) We therefore get \[ \ba A &= 2 \pi \par{\tfrac{1}{36}} \int_1^{10} \sqrt u \di u = \frac{\pi}{27} u^{3/2} \intEval_1^{10} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{\pi}{27} \par{\sqrt{1000} - 1}} \approx 3.563 \pd \ea \] (See Figure 4.)
EXAMPLE 2
The region bounded by the parabola \(y = x^2,\) the \(y\)-axis, and the lines \(y = 1\) and \(y = 4\) is rotated about the \(y\)-axis. Calculate the surface area of the generated solid.

Method 1 (Integrating with \(x\)) If we choose to integrate with \(x,\) then we express all quantities in terms of \(x.\) The region is bounded between \(x = 1\) and \(x = 2.\) We have \(\textderiv{y}{x} = 2x,\) so we use \[\dd s = \sqrt{1 + (2x)^2} \di x = \sqrt{1 + 4x^2} \di x \pd\] So by \(\eqref{eq:A-y},\) the surface area is given by \[A = \int_1^2 2 \pi x \sqrt{1 + 4x^2} \di x \pd\] Letting \(u = 1 + 4x^2,\) we have \(\dd u = 8x \di x.\) When \(x = 1,\) \(u = 5;\) when \(x = 2,\) \(u = 17.\) The integral therefore becomes \[ \ba A &= 2 \pi \par{\tfrac{1}{8}} \int_5^{17} \sqrt u \di u = \frac{\pi}{6} u^{3/2} \intEval_5^{17} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{\pi}{6} \par{17 \sqrt{17} - 5 \sqrt 5}} \approx 30.846 \pd \ea \]

Method 2 (Integrating with \(y\)) In integrating with \(y,\) we need all quantities to be in terms of \(y.\) We therefore rewrite \(y = x^2\) as \(x = \sqrt y.\) The region is bounded between \(y = 1\) and \(y = 4.\) Since \(\textderiv{x}{y} = \tfrac{1}{2} y^{-1/2},\) we attain \[\dd s = \sqrt{1 + \par{\tfrac{1}{2} y^{-1/2}}^2} = \sqrt{1 + \frac{1}{4y}} \pd\] Then \(\eqref{eq:A-y}\) gives the surface area to be \[ \ba A &= \int_1^4 2 \pi \sqrt y \sqrt{1 + \frac{1}{4y}} \di y \nl &= \int_1^4 2 \pi \sqrt{y + \tfrac{1}{4}} \di y \nl &= \pi \int_1^4 \sqrt{4y + 1} \di y \pd \ea \] Substituting \(u = 4y + 1,\) we find \(\dd u = 4 \di y.\) When \(y = 1,\) \(u = 5;\) when \(y = 4,\) \(u = 17.\) Thus, we have \[ \ba A &= \frac{\pi}{4} \int_5^{17} \sqrt u \di u = \frac{\pi}{6} u^{3/2} \intEval_5^{17} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{\pi}{6} \par{17 \sqrt{17} - 5 \sqrt 5}} \approx 30.846 \pd \ea \] Both methods provide the same surface area \(A.\) (See Figure 5.)

EXAMPLE 3
Calculate the surface area of the solid generated by rotating the region bounded by the graph of \(y = \cos x,\) the \(x\)-axis, the \(y\)-axis, and the line \(x = \pi/2\) about the \(x\)-axis.
Because \(\textderiv{y}{x} = -\sin x,\) we have \[\dd s = \sqrt{1 + (-\sin x)^2} = \sqrt{1 + \sin^2 x} \pd\] So using \(\eqref{eq:A-x}\) from \(a = 0\) to \(b = \pi/2,\) we find the surface area to be \[A = \int_0^{\pi/2} 2 \pi (\cos x) \sqrt{1 + \sin^2 x} \di x \pd\] Substituting \(u = \sin x,\) we attain \(\dd u = \cos x \di x.\) When \(x = 0,\) \(u = 0;\) when \(x = \pi/2,\) \(u = 1.\) The integral therefore becomes \[A = 2 \pi \int_0^1 \sqrt{1 + u^2} \di u \pd\] Now we perform the trigonometric substitution \(u = \tan \theta,\) from which \(\dd u = \sec^2 \theta \di \theta.\) (See Section 6.3 to review how to select trigonometric substitutions.) Observe that \(\theta = 0\) when \(u = 0,\) and \(\theta = \pi/4\) when \(u = 1.\) We then obtain \[A = 2 \pi \int_0^{\pi/4} \sqrt{\sec^2 \theta} \sec^2 \theta \di \theta = 2 \pi \int_0^{\pi/4} \sec^3 \theta \di \theta \pd\] In Section 6.2 we used Integration by Parts to antidifferentiate \(\sec^3 \theta.\) Using this result, we see \[ \ba A &= 2 \pi \par{\tfrac{1}{2} \sec \theta \tan \theta + \tfrac{1}{2} \ln \abs{\sec \theta + \tan \theta} } \intEval_0^{\pi/4} \nl &= 2 \pi \parbr{\tfrac{1}{2} \par{\sqrt 2} (1) + \tfrac{1}{2} \ln \par{\sqrt 2 + 1}} - 0 \nl &= \boxed{\pi \parbr{\sqrt 2 + \ln \par{\sqrt 2 + 1}}} \approx 7.212 \pd \ea \] (See Figure 6.)

If the region bounded by the smooth curve \(y = f(x)\) and the \(x\)-axis from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\) is rotated about the \(x\)-axis, then the generated solid has a surface area of \begin{equation} A = \int_a^b 2 \pi f(x) \sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2} \di x \pd \eqlabel{eq:A-x-f} \end{equation} Likewise, if the region bounded by the smooth curve \(x = g(y)\) and the \(y\)-axis from \(y = c\) to \(y = d\) is rotated about the \(y\)-axis, then the generated solid has a surface area of \begin{equation} A = \int_c^d 2 \pi g(y) \sqrt{1 + [g'(y)]^2} \di y \pd \eqlabel{eq:A-y-g} \end{equation} More generally, we use the following formulas: \begin{alignat}{2} A &= \int_a^b 2 \pi y \di s \cma \lspace &&[\text{Rotating about } x \text{-axis}] \eqlabel{eq:A-x} \nl A &= \int_c^d 2 \pi x \di s \cma \lspace &&[\text{Rotating about } y \text{-axis}] \eqlabel{eq:A-y} \end{alignat} where \[\di s = \sqrt{1 + \par{\deriv{y}{x}}^2} \di x \or \di s = \sqrt{1 + \par{\deriv{x}{y}}^2} \di y \pd\]
