10.6 Exercise Solutions: Absolute Convergence and the Ratio and Root Tests

EXERCISE 1
Define absolute convergence and conditional convergence.
SOLUTION The series \(\sum a_n\) is absolutely convergent if and only if \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) converges. Conversely, \(\sum a_n\) is conditionally convergent if and only if \(\sum a_n\) converges but \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) diverges.
EXERCISE 2
If \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) is convergent, then what is true about \(\sum a_n \ques\)
SOLUTION If \(\sum a_n\) is absolutely convergent, then it is convergent. In other words, if \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) converges, then \(\sum a_n\) converges.
EXERCISE 3
How do you use the Ratio Test to test \(\sum a_n\) for convergence or divergence?
SOLUTION To test whether \(\sum a_n\) converges or diverges, we consider the ratio \(\abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n}\) as \(n\) grows large. That is, we let \[L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}} \pd\] The Ratio Test then states the following:
  • If \(L \lt 1,\) then \(\sum a_n\) converges absolutely.
  • If \(L \gt 1,\) then \(\sum a_n\) diverges.
  • If \(L = 1,\) then the test gives no information. We must use another test if this is the case.
EXERCISE 4
Using the Root Test, how do you test \(\sum a_n\) for convergence or divergence?
SOLUTION To test whether \(\sum a_n\) converges or diverges, we consider the \(n\)th root of \(\abs{a_n}\) as \(n\) grows large. We let \[L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{\abs{a_n}} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_n}^{1/n} \pd\] The Root Test then states the following:
  • If \(L \lt 1,\) then \(\sum a_n\) converges absolutely.
  • If \(L \gt 1,\) then \(\sum a_n\) diverges.
  • If \(L = 1,\) then we have no information and must use another test.
EXERCISE 5
What does the Ratio Test conclude about \(\sum a_n\) in each of the following cases?
  1. \(\ds \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}} = 0.2\)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}} = 1.2\)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}} = 1\)
SOLUTION
  1. Since \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1,\) the Ratio Test gives \(\sum a_n\) to be convergent.
  2. Because \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \gt 1,\) the Ratio Test states that \(\sum a_n\) is divergent.
  3. Since \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} = 1,\) the Ratio Test is inconclusive in determining whether \(\sum a_n\) converges or diverges.
EXERCISE 6
By the Root Test, what do we know about \(\sum a_n\) in each of the following cases?
  1. \(\ds \lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{\abs{a_n}} = 0.5\)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{\abs{a_n}} = 3.5\)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{\abs{a_n}} = 1\)
SOLUTION
  1. Since \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{\abs{a_n}} \lt 1,\) the Root Test gives \(\sum a_n\) to be convergent.
  2. Because \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{\abs{a_n}} \gt 1,\) by the Root Test \(\sum a_n\) is divergent.
  3. Since \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{\abs{a_n}} = 1,\) the Root Test is inconclusive in determining whether \(\sum a_n\) converges or diverges.
EXERCISE 7
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n^3}\]
determine whether the series converges absolutely, converges conditionally, or diverges.
SOLUTION Let \(a_n = (-1)^n/n^3.\) Our goal is to determine whether \(\sum a_n\) converges or diverges. If it converges, then we proceed to test whether \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) is convergent. For \(n \geq 1,\) \(a_n\) is alternating and decreasing in magnitude to \(0.\) Hence, \(\sum a_n\) converges by the Alternating Series Test. Moreover, \[\sum \abs{a_n} = \sum \frac{1}{n^3}\] is a \(p\)-series with \(p = 3 \gt 1,\) so \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) converges. Since \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) converges, \(\sum a_n\) \(= \sum (-1)^n/n^3\) converges absolutely.
EXERCISE 8
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n^6}\]
determine whether the series converges absolutely, converges conditionally, or diverges.
SOLUTION We let \(a_n = (-1)^n/n^6.\) Our first goal is to test whether \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) converges or diverges. If it converges, then we proceed to test whether \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \abs{a_n}\) is convergent. For \(n \geq 1,\) \(a_n\) is alternating and decreasing in magnitude to \(0.\) Thus, \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) converges by the Alternating Series Test. Now we note that \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \abs{a_n} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^6}\] is a \(p\)-series with \(p = 6 \gt 1,\) and so converges. Since \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \abs{a_n}\) converges, \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) \(= \sum_{n = 1}^\infty (-1)^n/n^6\) converges absolutely.
EXERCISE 9
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 3}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n}}\]
determine whether the series converges absolutely, converges conditionally, or diverges.
SOLUTION Let \(a_n = (-1)^n/\sqrt{n}.\) The terms of \(\{a_n\} \subsuper{3}{\infty}\) are alternating, and the terms are decreasing in magnitude to zero. Thus, \(\sum a_n\) converges by the Alternating Series Test. But \[\sum \abs{a_n} = \sum \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\] is a \(p\)-series with \(p = 1/2 \lt 1,\) so \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) diverges. Since \(\sum a_n\) converges but \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) diverges, we say \(\sum a_n\) = \(\sum (-1)^n/\sqrt{n}\) converges conditionally.
EXERCISE 10
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 0}^{\infty} (-2)^n\]
determine whether the series converges absolutely, converges conditionally, or diverges.
SOLUTION We call \(a_n = (-2)^n.\) We immediately notice that \[\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = \lim_{n \to \infty} (-2)^n\] does not exist, because the terms of \(a_n\) continually switch sign from \(-2\) and \(2.\) Hence, the Divergence Test states that \(\sum a_n\) \(= \sum (-2)^n\) diverges. A divergent series is divergent, clearly; it cannot also be absolutely convergent or conditionally convergent.
EXERCISE 11
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^3}\]
determine whether the series converges absolutely, converges conditionally, or diverges.
SOLUTION The series \(\sum (1/n^3)\) is a \(p\)-series with \(p = 3 \gt 1,\) and therefore is convergent. Because \(\sum \abs{1/n^3} = \sum 1/n^3\) converges, we conclude that \(\sum (1/n^3)\) is absolutely convergent.
EXERCISE 12
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n^{3/2}}\]
determine whether the series converges absolutely, converges conditionally, or diverges.
SOLUTION Let \(a_n = (-1)^n/n^{3/2}.\) The terms of \(\{a_n\} \subsuper{1}{\infty}\) are alternating. Also, the terms are decreasing in magnitude, and \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \par{1/n^{3/2}} = 0.\) Consequently, \(\sum a_n\) converges by the Alternating Series Test. We now consider the sum of the absolute values of \(a_n;\) that is, \[\sum \abs{a_n} = \sum \frac{1}{n^{3/2}}\] is a \(p\)-series with \(p = 3/2 \gt 1\) and therefore converges. Since \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) converges, \(\sum a_n\) = \(\sum (-1)^n/n^{3/2}\) converges absolutely.
EXERCISE 13
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty} \frac{\cos(\pi n)}{\sqrt[3]{n}}\]
determine whether the series converges absolutely, converges conditionally, or diverges.
SOLUTION We immediately recognize that the term \(\cos(\pi n)\) is equivalent to \((-1)^n.\) So we rewrite the sum as \[\sum_{n = 1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt[3]{n}} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{n^{1/3}} \pd\] Let \(a_n = (-1)^n/n^{1/3}.\) For \(n \geq 1\) the terms of \(\{a_n\} \subsuper{1}{\infty}\) are alternating; also, \(1/n^{1/3}\) is decreasing and \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \par{1/n^{1/3}} = 0.\) Therefore, \(\sum a_n\) converges by the Alternating Series Test. We now consider \[\sum \abs{a_n} = \sum \frac{1}{n^{1/3}} \cma\] which is a \(p\)-series with \(p = 1/3 \lt 1.\) Thus, \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) diverges. Because \(\sum a_n\) converges and \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) diverges, we say \(\sum a_n\) = \(\sum (-1)^n/\sqrt[3]{n}\) converges conditionally.
EXERCISE 14
Show that \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty (\cos n)/n^2\) converges.
SOLUTION It is difficult to test this series' convergence or divergence in its raw form. We cannot readily perform a comparison test with \(a_n = (\cos n)/n^2,\) since we cannot bound the sum of \(\sum a_n\) in any useful way. We can, however, bound the sum of \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) \(= \sum \abs{\cos n}/n^2\) by the convergent series \(\sum (1/n^2),\) enabling us to establish that \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) converges by Direct Comparison. Since \(0 \leq \abs{\cos n} \leq 1,\) we see \[ 0 \leq \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty} \frac{\abs{\cos n}}{n^2} \leq \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} \pd \] Therefore, \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) \(= \sum \abs{\cos n}/n^2\) converges by the Direct Comparison Test. Hence, \(\sum a_n\) \(= \sum (\cos n)/n^2\) is absolutely convergent and therefore converges.
EXERCISE 15
Show that \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty (\sin n)/n^3\) converges.
SOLUTION We cannot immediately perform a Direct Comparison with \(a_n = (\sin n)/n^3.\) But we can bound the sum of \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) \(= \sum \abs{\sin n}/n^3\) by the convergent series \(\sum (1/n^3),\) allowing us to establish that \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) converges by Direct Comparison. Since \(0 \leq \abs{\sin n} \leq 1,\) we see \[ 0 \leq \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty} \frac{\abs{\sin n}}{n^3} \leq \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^3} \pd \] Therefore, \(\sum \abs{\sin n}/n^3\) converges by the Direct Comparison Test. So \(\sum (\sin n)/n^3\) is absolutely convergent and therefore converges.
EXERCISE 16
Let \(a_n = (-1)^n/n.\) Show that \(\sum a_n\) is conditionally convergent but \(\sum a_{2n}\) is divergent.
SOLUTION The function \(1/n\) is decreasing to \(0,\) so \(\sum a_n\) converges by the Alternating Series Test. But the sum of \(\abs{a_n}\) is \(\sum (1/n),\) the divergent Harmonic series, so \(\sum a_n\) is conditionally convergent. The series \(\sum a_{2n}\) is \[\sum \frac{(-1)^{2n}}{2n} = \sum \frac{1}{2n} \cma\] which diverges. [Note that \((-1)^{2n} = 1\) for all \(n.\)]
EXERCISE 17
Prove that \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty (-1)^n/n^p\) converges conditionally if \(0 \lt p \leq 1\) and converges absolutely if \(p \gt 1.\)
SOLUTION For all \(p \gt 0,\) \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty (-1)^n/n^p\) converges by the Alternating Series Test because \(1/n^p\) is decreasing and \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \par{1/n^p} = 0.\) Now we consider the series \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \abs{\frac{(-1)^n}{n^p}} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^p} \pd\] This \(p\)-series converges if \(p \gt 1\) and diverges if \(0 \lt p \leq 1.\) Hence, \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty (-1)^n/n^p\) is absolutely convergent if \(p \gt 1\) and conditionally convergent if \(0 \lt p \leq 1.\)
EXERCISE 18
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty e^n\]
use the Ratio Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges, or state that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION If \(a_n = e^n,\) then we consider \(L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n},\) where \(a_{n + 1} = e^{n + 1} = e^n \, e.\) Thus, we have \[L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{e^n \, e}{e^n}} = e \pd\] Because \(L = e \gt 1,\) the Ratio Test asserts that this series diverges.
EXERCISE 19
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty 4^{-n}\]
use the Ratio Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges, or state that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION Let \(a_n = 4^{-n};\) then we consider \(L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n}.\) We see \[a_{n + 1} = 4^{-(n + 1)} = 4^{-n} \cdot 4^{-1}\] We have \[L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{4^{-n} \cdot 4^{-1}}{4^{-n}}} = \tfrac{1}{4} \pd\] Because \(L = 1/4 \lt 1,\) the Ratio Test asserts that this series converges.
EXERCISE 20
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty n\]
use the Ratio Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges, or state that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION If \(a_n = n,\) then we consider \(L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n},\) which is \[L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{n + 1}{n}} = 1 \pd\] Because \(L = 1,\) the Ratio Test is inconclusive. The test fails to determine whether \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty n\) converges or diverges. Of course, we know that this sum is divergent because \(\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n \ne 0.\)
EXERCISE 21
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{n}\]
use the Ratio Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges, or state that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION If \(a_n = 1/n,\) then we have \(a_{n + 1} = 1/(n + 1).\) So \(L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n}\) becomes \[L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{1}{n + 1} \cdot \frac{n}{1}} = 1 \pd\] Because \(L = 1,\) the Ratio Test is inconclusive in testing the Harmonic series for convergence or divergence. But we recognize immediately that this series is divergent.
EXERCISE 22
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{2^n}{n!}\]
use the Ratio Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges, or state that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION Let \(a_n = 2^n/n! \, ;\) then we consider \(L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n}.\) We see \[a_{n + 1} = \frac{2^{n + 1}}{(n + 1)!} = \frac{2^n \, 2}{(n + 1) \, n!} \pd\] Then we find \[ \ba L &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{2^n \, 2}{(n + 1) \, n!} \cdot \frac{n!}{2^n}} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{2}{n + 1} \nl &= 0 \pd \ea \] Because \(L \lt 1,\) the Ratio Test asserts that \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty 2^n/n!\) converges.
EXERCISE 23
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n \, 5^{2n}}{n!}\]
use the Ratio Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges, or state that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION We assert that the alternator \((-1)^n\) has no effect in absolute value bars, as \(\abs{(-1)^n}\) \(= 1.\) Thus, we ignore this factor. Let \(a_n = 5^{2n}/n! \, ;\) then we consider \(L =\) \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n}.\) Note that \[a_{n + 1} = \frac{5^{2(n + 1)}}{(n + 1)!} = \frac{5^{2n} \, 5^2}{(n + 1) \, n!} \pd\] Thus, \[ \ba L &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{5^{2n} \, 5^2}{(n + 1) \, n!} \cdot \frac{n!}{5^{2n}}} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{5^2}{n + 1} \nl &= 0 \pd \ea \] Because \(L \lt 1,\) the Ratio Test asserts that \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty 5^{2n}/n!\) converges.
EXERCISE 24
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{(-4)^n}{(2n)!}\]
use the Ratio Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges, or state that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION We first rewrite \[(-4)^n = 4^n \, (-1)^n \pd\] Since \(\abs{(-1)^n} = 1,\) we ignore the factor \((-1)^n.\) Let \(a_n = 4^n/(2n)! \, ;\) then we consider \(L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n}.\) We have \[a_{n + 1} = \frac{4^{n + 1}}{(2n + 2)!} = \frac{4^n \, 4}{(2n + 2)(2n + 1)(2n)!} \pd\] Then \[ \ba L &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{4^n \, 4}{(2n + 2)(2n + 1)(2n)!} \cdot \frac{(2n)!}{4^n}} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{4}{(2n + 2)(2n + 1)} \nl &= 0 \pd \ea \] Because \(L \lt 1,\) the Ratio Test asserts that \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty (-4)^n/(2n)!\) converges.
EXERCISE 25
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n (n + 3)}{\sqrt{n}}\]
use the Ratio Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges, or state that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION We ignore the term \((-1)^n\) because it has no effect in absolute value. So we call \(a_n = (n + 3)/\sqrt{n}.\) We see \(a_{n + 1} = (n + 4)/\sqrt{n + 1}.\) Then we find \[ \ba L &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{n + 4}{\sqrt{n + 1}} \cdot \frac{\sqrt n}{n + 3}} \nl &= 1 \pd \ea \] Because \(L = 1,\) the Ratio Test is inconclusive. We must use another series test. This series turns out to be divergent by the Divergent Test, since \[\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{(-1)^n (n + 3)}{\sqrt{n}} \ne 0 \pd\]
EXERCISE 26
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n \sqrt[3]{n}}{n + 4}\]
use the Ratio Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges, or state that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION Because \((-1)^n\) has no effect in absolute value, we ignore this term. Thus, we let \(a_n = \sqrt[3]{n}/(n + 4).\) We see \(a_{n + 1} = \sqrt[3]{n + 1}/(n + 5).\) Then \[ \ba L &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{ \frac{\sqrt[3]{n + 1}}{n + 5} \cdot \frac{n + 4}{\sqrt[3]{n}} } \nl &= 1 \pd \ea \] Because \(L = 1,\) the Ratio Test is inconclusive.
EXERCISE 27
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{n!}{n^n}\]
use the Ratio Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges, or state that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION Let \(a_n = n!/n^n.\) We have \[a_{n + 1} = \frac{(n + 1)!}{(n + 1)^{n + 1}} = \frac{(n + 1) \, n!}{(n + 1)^n \, (n + 1)} = \frac{n!}{(n + 1)^n} \pd \] Then \[ \ba L &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{ \frac{n!}{(n + 1)^n} \cdot \frac{n^n}{n!} } \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \par{\frac{n}{n + 1}}^n \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \par{1 + \tfrac{1}{n}}^{-n} \nl &= e^{-1} \pd \ea \] Since \(L \lt 1,\) the Ratio Test states that \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \par{n!/n^n}\) converges.
EXERCISE 28
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 0}^\infty 7^n\]
use the Root Test to test the series for convergence or divergence, or show that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION In using the Root Test, we consider the behavior of \(\abs{7^n}^{1/n}\) as \(n \to \infty,\) or \[\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{7^n}^{1/n} = \lim_{n \to \infty} 7 = 7 \pd\] Because this limit is greater than \(1,\) the Root Test states that \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty 7^n\) diverges, as we expect.
EXERCISE 29
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \par{\frac{2n - 2}{3n + 1}}^n\]
use the Root Test to test the series for convergence or divergence, or show that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION To use the Root Test, we raise the absolute value of the summation function to the power of \(1/n\) and take the limit as \(n \to \infty.\) We have \[ \ba \lim_{n \to \infty} \parbr{\abs{\frac{2n - 2}{3n + 1}}^n}^{1/n} &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{2n - 2}{3n + 1} \nl &= \tfrac{2}{3} \pd \ea \] Because this limit is less than \(1,\) the Root Test asserts that this series converges.
EXERCISE 30
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 2}^\infty \par{\frac{n + 4}{n + 8}}^n\]
use the Root Test to test the series for convergence or divergence, or show that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION Taking the limit of the absolute value of the summation function raised to the power of \(1/n,\) we get \[ \ba \lim_{n \to \infty} \parbr{\abs{\frac{n + 4}{n + 8}}^n}^{1/n} &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n + 4}{n + 8} \nl &= 1 \pd \ea \] This limit is \(1,\) so the Root Test is inconclusive; another test is required to determine whether this series is convergent or divergent.
EXERCISE 31
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(-3)^n}{n}\]
use the Root Test to test the series for convergence or divergence, or show that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION We consider \(\abs{(-3)^n/n}^{1/n}\) \(= \par{3^n/n}^{1/n}\) as \(n \to \infty \col\) \[ \lim_{n \to \infty} \par{\frac{3^n}{n}}^{1/n} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{3}{n^{1/n}} \pd \] We recall that if \(k\) is any positive number, then \(\lim_{n \to \infty} n^{k/n} = 1;\) here, \(k = 1.\) Hence, the limit becomes \(3/1 = 3,\) which is greater than \(1.\) So by the Root Test, \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty 3^n/n\) diverges.
EXERCISE 32
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{n^n}\]
use the Root Test to test the series for convergence or divergence, or show that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION We consider \(\abs{(-1)^n/n^n}^{1/n}\) \(= \par{1/n^n}^{1/n}\) as \(n \to \infty.\) We have \[ \lim_{n \to \infty} \par{\frac{1}{n^n}}^{1/n} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} = 0 \pd \] This limit is less than \(1,\) so the Root Test states that \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty (-1)^n/n^n\) converges.
EXERCISE 33
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{n^5}{4^n}\]
use the Root Test to test the series for convergence or divergence, or show that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION The limit of interest is \[ \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{n^5}{4^n}}^{1/n} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n^{5/n}}{4} \pd \] If \(k\) is a positive number, then \(\lim_{n \to \infty} n^{k/n} = 1.\) In the numerator we find \(k = 5,\) so our limit becomes \(1/4 \lt 1,\) and the Root Test therefore states that \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty n^5/4^n\) converges.
EXERCISE 34
For
\[\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty (-1)^n \, \frac{3^n}{n^6}\]
use the Root Test to test the series for convergence or divergence, or show that the test is inconclusive.
SOLUTION We consider \(\abs{(-1)^n \, 3^n/n^6}^{1/n}\) \(= \par{3^n/n^6}^{1/n}\) as \(n \to \infty \col\) \[ \lim_{n \to \infty} \par{\frac{3^n}{n^6}}^{1/n} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{3}{n^{6/n}} \pd \] If \(k\) is a positive number, then \(\lim_{n \to \infty} n^{k/n} = 1.\) Thus, our limit becomes \(3/1 \gt 1,\) so the Root Test states that \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty n^5/4^n\) diverges.
EXERCISE 35
A series' terms are defined recursively by the equation \[a_{n + 1} = (-1)^n \, \frac{3n + 4}{e^n} \, a_n \pd\] Determine whether \(\sum a_n\) converges or diverges.
SOLUTION We divide both sides by \(a_n\) and take the absolute value of the quotient, finding \[\abs{\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}} = \abs{(-1)^n \frac{3n + 4}{e^n}} = \frac{3n + 4}{e^n} \pd\] As \(n \to \infty,\) this ratio approaches \(0\) (by L'Hôpital's Rule). Hence, the Ratio Test asserts that \(\sum a_n\) converges.

An infinite geometric series \(\sum a_n\) satisfies the following recursive formula: \[a_{n + 1} = r \, a_n \cma\] where \(r\) is the common ratio. Find \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n}.\) Using the Ratio Test, find the values of \(r\) for which \(\sum a_n\) converges absolutely. Is this result expected?
SOLUTION Dividing both sides of the recursive formula by \(a_n\) gives \(r = a_{n + 1}/a_n.\) We then have \[\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs r = \abs r \pd\] The Ratio Test asserts that \(\sum a_n\) is absolutely convergent if \(\abs r \lt 1.\) This result is expected because we know, by heart, that a geometric series \(\sum r^n\) converges if \(\abs r \lt 1.\) So we now extend this fact by specifying that \(\sum r^n\) converges absolutely if \(\abs r \lt 1.\)
EXERCISE 37
Let \(\sum a_n\) be a series such that \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1.\) Using the Ratio Test, prove that \(\sum (a_n/n)\) is convergent.
SOLUTION To use the Ratio Test for \(\sum (a_n/n),\) we have \[ \ba L &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{a_{n + 1}}{n + 1} \cdot \frac{n}{a_n}} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}} \cdot \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{n}{n + 1}} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}} \pd \ea \] We know that \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \lt 1.\) Since \(L \lt 1,\) the Ratio Test asserts that \(\sum (a_n/n)\) is convergent.
EXERCISE 38
Suppose that the Ratio Test asserts that \(\sum a_n\) is divergent. Prove that \(\sum na_n\) is also divergent.
SOLUTION Because the Ratio Test asserts that \(\sum a_n\) diverges, we know \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \gt 1.\) To use the Ratio Test for \(\sum n a_n,\) we have \[ \ba L &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{(n + 1) a_{n + 1}}{n a_n}} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}} \cdot \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{n + 1}{n}} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{a_{n + 1}}{a_n}} \pd \ea \] Because \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{a_{n + 1}/a_n} \gt 1,\) we have \(L \gt 1.\) Thus, \(\sum na_n\) is divergent.
EXERCISE 39
Prove that \(\sum \par{n^k/n!}\) converges for all \(k.\)
SOLUTION Using the Ratio Test, we have \[ \ba L &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{(n + 1)^k}{(n + 1) \, n!} \cdot \frac{n!}{n^k}} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{(n + 1)^k}{n^k}} \cdot \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{1}{n + 1}} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{1}{(n + 1)}} \nl &= 0 \pd \ea \] We see that \(L = 0 \lt 1\) for all \(k,\) meaning \(\sum \par{n^k/n!}\) converges for all \(k.\)
EXERCISE 40
For what values of \(k\) does \(\sum \par{k^n/n}\) converge?
SOLUTION From the Ratio Test, we set up the following limit: \[ \ba L &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{k^{n + 1}}{n + 1} \cdot \frac{n}{k^n}} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{kn}{n + 1}} \nl &= \abs{k} \pd \ea \] We want \(L \lt 1,\) or \[\abs{k} \lt 1 \arrowTwoWay -1 \lt k \lt 1 \pd\] Since the Ratio Test is inconclusive when \(k = -1\) and \(k = 1\) (because \(L = 1\)), we must test these endpoints manually: If \(k = -1,\) then we have \(\sum (-1)^n/n,\) which converges by the Alternating Series Test. But if \(k = 1,\) then we have \(\sum (1/n),\) the divergent Harmonic series. So we include \(k = -1\) and exclude \(k = 1;\) the interval of \(k\) is \[\boxed{-1 \leq k \lt 1}\]
EXERCISE 41
Let \(\{a_n\}\) and \(\{b_n\}\) be sequences such that \(b_n\) is decreasing to \(0\) and \(\abs{a_n}\) \(= b_n - b_{n + 1}\) for all \(n.\) Prove that \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) converges absolutely.
SOLUTION Note that \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) converges absolutely if and only if \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \abs{a_n}\) converges. Observe that \(\sum \abs{a_n}\) is telescoping, as shown by the following partial sum: \[ \ba \sum_{i = 1}^N \abs{a_i} &= \sum_{i = 1}^N \par{b_i - b_{i + 1}} \nl &= \par{b_1 - \cancel{b_2}} + \par{\cancel{b_2} - \cancel{b_3}} + \cdots + \par{\cancel{b_N} - b_{N + 1}} \nl &= b_1 - b_{N + 1} \pd \ea \] Hence, we find \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \abs{a_n} = \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{i = 1}^N \abs{a_i} = \lim_{N \to \infty} \par{b_1 - b_{N + 1}} = b_1 \cma\] where the last step is true because \(b_N\) (and therefore \(b_{N + 1}\)) is given to approach \(0\) as \(N \to \infty.\) Because \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \abs{a_n}\) converges, \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) converges absolutely, as requested.
EXERCISE 42
Justify why the sum of a polynomial is divergent. Then show that the Ratio Test fails to prove that the sum of a polynomial is divergent.
SOLUTION Let \(P(n)\) be a polynomial as a function of \(n.\) Then \(\lim_{n \to \infty} P(n)\) must be either \(-\infty\) or \(\infty.\) Because \(\lim_{n \to \infty} P(n) \ne 0,\) the Divergence Test asserts that \(\sum P(n)\) is divergent. Now we prove that the Ratio Test fails to give this result. As \(n \to \infty,\) the ratio \(\abs{P(n + 1)/P(n)}\) is dominated by the highest-degree terms of the numerator and denominator: \(P(n + 1)\) and \(P(n),\) respectively. But both functions share the same degree and leading coefficient. Hence, \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{P(n + 1)/P(n)} = 1\) and so the Ratio Test is inconclusive.
EXERCISE 43
Let \(P(n)\) be a polynomial. Prove that \(\sum P(n) \, e^{-n}\) is absolutely convergent.
SOLUTION With the Ratio Test, we have \[ \ba L &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{P(n + 1) \cdot e^{-n} \, e^{-1}}{P(n) \cdot e^{-n}}} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \abs{\frac{P(n + 1)}{P(n) \, e}} \pd \ea \] The ratio \(P(n + 1)/P(n)\) tends to \(1\) as \(n \to \infty,\) because the limit becomes the ratio of coefficients of the highest-degree terms. Hence, we have \(L = 1/e.\) Because \(L \lt 1,\) we conclude that \(\sum P(n) \, e^{-n}\) is absolutely convergent.
