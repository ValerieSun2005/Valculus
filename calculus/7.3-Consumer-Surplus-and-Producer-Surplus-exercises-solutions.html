7.3 Exercise Solutions: Consumer Surplus and Producer Surplus

EXERCISE 1
What is consumer surplus, and how do we calculate it?
SOLUTION Consumer surplus \(s_C\) is a measure of total benefit to consumers due to a product costing \(P\) dollars. (Think of consumers who value the product at more than \(P\) dollars; these individuals save money by only paying \(P\) dollars.) If \(D(x)\) is a demand function and \(X\) units of a good are produced, then consumer surplus is given by \[s_C = \int_0^X \parbr{D(x) - P} \di x \pd\]
EXERCISE 2
What is producer surplus, and how do we calculate it?
SOLUTION Producer surplus \(s_P\) is a measure of total benefit to a company (or companies) by charging \(P\) dollars for a product. We can interpret \(s_P\) as profit since firms are allowed to sell items for higher than the production cost. If \(S(x)\) is a supply function and \(X\) units of a good are produced, then producer surplus is given by \[s_P = \int_0^X \parbr{P - S(x)} \di x \pd\]
EXERCISE 3
For
\[\ds D(x) = \frac{60}{x + 1} \cmaa P = 20\]
calculate the consumer surplus given the demand function \(D(x)\) and price \(P\).
SOLUTION We locate the quantity demanded \(X\) when the price is \(\$20.\) Equating the demand function to \(20,\) we see \[\frac{60}{X + 1} = 20 \implies X + 1 = 3 \implies X = 2 \pd\] We therefore integrate the difference \(D(x) - P\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 2\) to attain the consumer surplus, as follows: \[ \ba s_C &= \int_0^2 \par{\frac{60}{x + 1} - 20} \di x \nl &= \par{60 \ln \abs{x + 1} - 20 x} \intEval_0^2 \nl &= \boxed{60 \ln 3 - 40} \approx \$ 25.92 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 4
For
\[\ds D(x) = 120 - \tfrac{2}{3}x^2 \cmaa P = 96\]
calculate the consumer surplus given the demand function \(D(x)\) and price \(P\).
SOLUTION We locate the quantity demanded \(X\) when the price is \(\$96.\) Equating the demand function to \(96,\) we see \[120 - \tfrac{2}{3}X^2 = 96 \implies X^2 = 36 \implies X = 6 \pd\] (Disregard the solution \(X = -6\) because quantity demanded cannot be negative.) We therefore integrate the difference \(D(x) - P\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 6\) to attain the consumer surplus, as follows: \[ \ba s_C &= \int_0^6 \parbr{\par{120 - \tfrac{2}{3}x^2} - 96} \di x \nl &= \int_0^6 \par{24 - \tfrac{2}{3}x^2} \di x \nl &= \par{24x - \tfrac{2}{9}x^3} \intEval_0^6 = \boxed{\$96} \ea \]
EXERCISE 5
For
\[\ds D(x) = 70 e^{-x} \cmaa P = \frac{70}{e^3}\]
calculate the consumer surplus given the demand function \(D(x)\) and price \(P\).
SOLUTION To begin, we calculate the quantity demanded \(X\) when the price is \(70/e^3\) dollars. Equating the demand function to \(70/e^3,\) we see \[70 e^{-X} = 70e^{-3} \implies X = 3 \pd\] We therefore integrate the difference \(D(x) - P\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 3\) to attain the consumer surplus, as follows: \[ \ba s_C &= \int_0^3 \par{70 e^{-x} - 70 e^{-3}} \di x \nl &= \par{-70 e^{-x} - 70 e^{-3} x} \intEval_0^3 \nl &= \boxed{70 - \frac{280}{e^3}} \approx \$56.06 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 6
For
\[\ds D(x) = 34 - \tfrac{1}{4} x^4 \cmaa P = 30\]
calculate the consumer surplus given the demand function \(D(x)\) and price \(P\).
SOLUTION The first step is to calculate the quantity demanded \(X\) when the price is \(\$30.\) Equating the demand function to \(30,\) we see \[34 - \tfrac{1}{4} X^4 = 30 \implies X^4 = 16 \implies X = 2 \pd\] (We ignore the negative solution \(X = -2\) because quantity demanded cannot be negative.) Hence, we integrate the difference \(D(x) - P\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 2\) to attain the consumer surplus, as follows: \[ \ba s_C &= \int_0^2 \parbr{\par{34 - \tfrac{1}{4} x^4} - 30} \di x \nl &= \int_0^2 \par{4 - \tfrac{1}{4} x^4} \di x \nl &= \par{4x - \tfrac{1}{20} x^5} \intEval_0^2 \nl &= \boxed{\tfrac{32}{5}} = \$6.40 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 7
For
\[\ds D(x) = 48 - \tfrac{1}{5} x^3 \cmaa P = 23\]
calculate the consumer surplus given the demand function \(D(x)\) and price \(P\).
SOLUTION Let's begin by finding the quantity demanded \(X\) when the price is \(\$23.\) Equating the demand function to \(23,\) we see \[48 - \tfrac{1}{5} X^3 = 23 \implies X^3 = 125 \implies X = 5 \pd\] We therefore integrate the difference \(D(x) - P\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 5\) to attain the consumer surplus, as follows: \[ \ba s_C &= \int_0^5 \parbr{\par{48 - \tfrac{1}{5} x^3} - 23} \di x \nl &= \int_0^5 \par{25 - \tfrac{1}{5} x^3} \di x \nl &= \par{25x - \tfrac{1}{20} x^4} \intEval_0^5 \nl &= \boxed{\tfrac{375}{4}} = \$93.75 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 8
For
\[\ds S(x) = \sqrt x \cmaa P = 4\]
calculate the producer surplus given the supply function \(S(x)\) and price \(P\).
SOLUTION We first identify how many units \(X\) are supplied when the price is \(\$4.\) Equating the supply function to \(4,\) we see \[\sqrt X = 4 \implies X = 16 \pd\] To calculate the producer surplus, we integrate the difference \(P - S(x)\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 16,\) as follows: \[ \ba s_P &= \int_0^{16} \par{4 - \sqrt x \, } \di x \nl &= \par{4x - \tfrac{2}{3} x^{3/2}} \intEval_0^{16} \nl &= \boxed{\tfrac{64}{3}} \approx \$21.33 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 9
For
\[\ds S(x) = \ln x \cmaa P = \ln 7\]
calculate the producer surplus given the supply function \(S(x)\) and price \(P\).
SOLUTION We first identify how many units \(X\) are supplied when the price is \(\ln 7\) dollars. Equating the supply function to this value, we see \[\ln X = \ln 7 \implies X = 7 \pd\] To calculate the producer surplus, we integrate the difference \(P - S(x)\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 7,\) as follows: \[ \ba s_P &= \int_0^7 \par{\ln 7 - \ln x} \di x \nl &= \par{x \ln 7 - x \ln x + x} \intEval_0^7 \nl &= \boxed{\$7} \ea \] (To review how to antidifferentiate \(\ln x,\) see Section 6.1.)
EXERCISE 10
For
\[\ds S(x) = \tfrac{1}{2} x^2 + 5 \cmaa P = 37\]
calculate the producer surplus given the supply function \(S(x)\) and price \(P\).
SOLUTION First, let's calculate how many units \(X\) are supplied when the price is \(\$37.\) Equating the supply function to this value, we see \[\tfrac{1}{2} X^2 + 5 = 37 \implies X^2 = 64 \implies X = 8 \pd\] (We disregard the negative solution \(X = -8\) because quantity supplied cannot be negative.) To calculate the producer surplus, we integrate the difference \(P - S(x)\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 8,\) as follows: \[ \ba s_P &= \int_0^8 \parbr{37 - \par{\tfrac{1}{2} x^2 + 5}} \di x \nl &= \int_0^8 \par{32 - \tfrac{1}{2} x^2} \di x \nl &= \par{32x - \tfrac{1}{6} x^3} \intEval_0^8 \nl &= \boxed{\tfrac{512}{3}} \approx \$170.67 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 11
For
\[\ds S(x) = 2^{x/6} + 4 \cmaa P = 12\]
calculate the producer surplus given the supply function \(S(x)\) and price \(P\).
SOLUTION When the price is \(\$12,\) we calculate the number of units \(X\) produced, as follows: \[2^{X/6} + 4 = 12 \implies 2^{X/6} = 8 \implies \frac{X}{6} = 3 \implies X = 18 \pd\] To calculate the producer surplus, we therefore integrate the difference \(P - S(x)\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 18,\) as follows: \[ \ba s_P &= \int_0^{18} \parbr{12 - \par{2^{x/6} + 4}} \di x \nl &= \int_0^{18} \par{8 - 2^{x/6}} \di x \nl &= \par{8x - \frac{6}{\ln 2} \, 2^{x/6}} \intEval_0^{18} \nl &= \boxed{144 - \frac{42}{\ln 2}} \approx \$83.41 \pd \ea \] [We antidifferentiate \(2^{x/6}\) by rewriting it as \(e^{(x / 6) \ln 2}\) and substituting \(u = (x / 6) \ln 2.\) See Section 4.4.]
EXERCISE 12
In a competitive market, the price of a good is driven to the point at which quantity supplied equals quantity demanded. (Economists often call this phenomenon the invisible hand.) The intersection of the demand and supply curves is called the equilibrium point. Consider the small, competitive market for Product A, in which a supply function is \(S(x) = x^2 + 12\) and a demand function is \(D(x) = 18 - x.\)
  1. How many units of Product A are produced? (We call this number the equilibrium quantity.)
  2. Calculate the consumer surplus \(s_C.\)
  3. Calculate the producer surplus \(s_P.\)
SOLUTION
  1. Since the market is competitive, the quantity supplied equals the quantity produced. Thus, we solve the equation \(S(X) = D(X)\) to see \[X^2 + 12 = 18 - X \implies X^2 + X - 6 = 0 \implies \boxed{X = 2}\] (We ignore the negative solution \(X = -3\) because quantity produced cannot be negative.)
  2. When \(X = 2,\) the price is \(\$16.\) Integrating the difference \(D(x) - 16\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 2,\) we find the consumer surplus to be \[ \ba s_C &= \int_0^2 \parbr{(18 - x) - 16} \di x \nl &= \int_0^2 (2 - x) \di x = \par{2x - \tfrac{1}{2} x^2} \intEval_0^2 \nl &= \boxed{\$2} \ea \]
  3. The price is \(\$16,\) so the producer surplus is given by integrating the difference \(16 - S(x)\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 2,\) as follows: \[ \ba s_P &= \int_0^2 \parbr{16 - \par{x^2 + 12}} \di x \nl &= \int_0^2 \par{4 - x^2} \di x \nl &= \par{4x - \tfrac{1}{3} x^3} \intEval_0^2 \nl &= \boxed{\tfrac{16}{3}} \approx \$5.33 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 13
Total surplus is the sum of consumer surplus and producer surplus. We use total surplus to measure how beneficial a market is to a society, since high total surplus indicates a high level of satisfaction with a market. For a competitive market, the demand function is \(D(x) = 600 e^{-0.4 x}\) and the supply function is \(S(x) = x^2 + 40.\) Using a calculator, calculate the total surplus \(s_T\) to three decimal places.
SOLUTION Because the market is competitive, the quantity \(X\) produced satisfies \(D(X) = S(X),\) which occurs when \(X \approx 5.401.\) At this point, the price is \(\$69.17.\) The consumer surplus is therefore \[s_C = \int_0^{5.401} \par{600 e^{-0.4x} - 69.17} \di x \approx \$953.49 \pd\] The producer surplus is \[s_P = \int_0^{5.401} \par{69.17 - x^2 - 40} \di x \approx \$105.03 \pd\] Hence, the total surplus is \[s_T = s_C + s_P \approx \$953.49 + \$105.03 = \boxed{\$1058.52}\]
EXERCISE 14
Let \(X\) be the equilibrium quantity produced. Prove that the total surplus is maximized at the equilibrium point.
SOLUTION The total surplus \(s_T\) is the area between the curves \(D\) and \(S\)—namely, \[s_T = \int_0^x \parbr{D(t) - S(t)} \di t \pd\] (We use \(t\) simply as a dummy variable.) To maximize \(s_T,\) we find the critical numbers—the values of \(x\) for which \(\textderiv{s_T}{x} = 0.\) (See Section 3.6 to review the process of optimization.) By Part I of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, \[\deriv{s_T}{x} = D(x) - S(x) \pd\] So we see \[D(X) - S(X) = 0 \implies D(X) = S(X) \pd\] Observe that \(D(x) - S(x) \gt 0\) when \(x \lt X\) and \(D(x) - S(x) \lt 0\) when \(x \gt X.\) Hence, the First-Derivative Test asserts that \(s_T\) has a relative maximum at \(x = X.\) Note that \(s_T = 0\) when \(x = 0\) and \(s_T \to -\infty\) as \(x \to \infty.\) Hence, \(s_T\) has an absolute maximum at \(x = X,\) meaning total surplus is maximized at the equilibrium point.
