8.4 Exercise Solutions — Logistic Models

EXERCISE 1
What is a logistic growth function? Define it using a differential equation and provide the solution.
SOLUTION A logistic growth function exhibits exponential growth while \(y\) is small but slows in growth as \(y\) approaches some number—called the carrying capacity. If \(L\) is the carrying capacity and \(k\) is a positive constant, then \(y\) undergoes logistic growth with \(x\) if \[\deriv{y}{x} = ky \par{1 - \frac{y}{L}} \pd\] The solution to this differential equation is \[y = \frac{L}{1 + Ce^{-kx}} \cma\] where \(C\) is some constant.
EXERCISE 2
What is a logistic decay function? Define it using a differential equation and provide the solution.
SOLUTION A logistic decay function exhibits exponential decay while \(y\) is large (close to the carrying capacity) but slows in growth as \(y\) becomes small. If \(L\) is the carrying capacity and \(k\) is a positive constant, then \(y\) undergoes logistic decay with \(x\) if \[\deriv{y}{x} = -ky \par{1 - \frac{y}{L}} \pd\] The solution to this differential equation is \[y = \frac{L}{1 + Ce^{kx}} \cma\] where \(C\) is some constant.
EXERCISE 3
When does the graph of a logistic function have an inflection point? Where is it concave up? Where is it concave down?
SOLUTION A logistic function (either growth or decay) has an inflection point when \(y\) is halfway between its horizontal asymptotes. If a logistic graph has no vertical shift, then it has an inflection point when \(y\) reaches half of the carrying capacity, \(y = L/2.\) For \(y\) under this inflection point (that is, for \(y \lt L/2\)), the graph is concave up. But for \(y\) above this inflection point (that is, for \(y \gt L/2\)), the graph is concave down.

Figure 1 shows a slope field produced by the differential equation \[\deriv{y}{x} = \frac{1}{20} y \par{1 - \frac{y}{120}} \pd\] Consider the solution curve passing through the point \((0, 20)\) with a horizontal asymptote of \(y = 120.\)

  2. Identify the growth constant of the logistic function.
  3. At what value of \(y\) does the curve have an inflection point?

SOLUTION

EXERCISE 16
Let \(f\) be a function that undergoes logistic growth and satisfies \[\andFour {\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = 0} {f(0) = 40} {f(1) = 100} {\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = 2000} \pd \] Determine the identity of \(f.\)
SOLUTION

Because \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = 2000,\) the function's carrying capacity is \(2000\) and so we have \[f(x) = \frac{2000}{1 + Ce^{-kx}} \cma\] where \(C\) and \(k\) are constants. We determine their values by substituting the initial conditions \(f(0) = 40\) and \(f(1) = 100.\)

Solving for \(C\) and \(k\) Substituting \(f(0) = 40\) shows \[ \ba \frac{2000}{1 + Ce^0} &= 40 \nl 1 + C &= 50 \nl \implies C &= 49 \pd \ea \] Substituting \(f(1) = 100,\) we see \[ \ba \frac{2000}{1 + 49e^{k}} &= 100 \nl 1 + 49e^{k} &= 20 \nl \implies k &= \ln \tfrac{19}{49} \pd \ea \] Therefore, we have \[ \ba f(x) &= \frac{2000}{1 + 49 e^{\par{\ln \tfrac{19}{49}}x}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{2000}{1 + 49 \par{\tfrac{19}{49}}^x}} \ea \]

EXERCISE 17
A function \(g(x)\) undergoes logistic decay such that \[\andFour {\lim_{x \to -\infty} g(x) = 2500} {g(0) = 500} {g(2) = 1} {\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = 0} \pd \] Determine the identity of \(g.\)
SOLUTION

Because \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} g(x) = 2500,\) we attain the general model \[g(x) = \frac{2500}{1 + Ce^{kx}} \cma\] where \(C\) and \(k\) are constants. We determine their values by substituting the initial conditions \(g(0) = 500\) and \(g(2) = 1.\)

Solving for \(C\) and \(k\) Substituting \(g(0) = 500\) shows \[ \ba \frac{2500}{1 + Ce^0} &= 500 \nl 1 + C &= 5 \nl \implies C &= 4 \pd \ea \] Substituting \(g(2) = 1,\) we see \[ \ba \frac{2500}{1 + 4e^{2k}} &= 1 \nl 1 + 4e^{2k} &= 2500 \nl e^{2k} &= \tfrac{2499}{4} \nl \implies k &= \tfrac{1}{2} \ln \tfrac{2499}{4} \pd \ea \] Therefore, we have \[ \ba g(x) &= \frac{2500}{1 + 4 e^{\par{\tfrac{1}{2} \ln \tfrac{2499}{4}}x}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{2500}{1 + 4 \par{\tfrac{2499}{4}}^{x/2}}} \ea \]

EXERCISE 18
Students enter the school cafeteria according to the differential equation \[\deriv{y}{t} = 0.5 y \par{1 - \frac{y}{400}} \cma\] where \(t\) is time measured in minutes since the start of the lunch block. What is the maximum rate at which students enter the cafeteria?
SOLUTION Observe that the carrying capacity of the students in the cafeteria is \(400,\) since \(\textderiv{y}{t} \to 0\) as \(y \to 400.\) The number of students in the cafeteria increases the fastest when \(y\) is equal to half of the carrying capacity—that is, when \(y = 200.\) We therefore see \[\deriv{y}{t} \intEval_{y = 200} = 0.5 (200) \par{1 - \frac{200}{400}} = 50 \pd\] So the maximum rate of increase of students in the cafeteria is \(\boxed{50}\) students per minute.

EXERCISE 20
A baby goat is born with a weight of \(10\) pounds. Three months later, the goat weighs \(40\) pounds and is expected to weigh \(160\) pounds as an adult. Find a logistic growth function to model the goat's weight as a function of time.
SOLUTION

Let \(W(t)\) be the goat's weight as a function of time \(t\) in months. We expect the goat to be \(160 \un{lb}\) as an adult, so the carrying capacity of \(W\) is \(160.\) Thus, we use the model \[W(t) = \frac{160}{1 + Ce^{-kt}}\] for unknown \(C\) and \(k.\)

Solving for \(C\) and \(k\) Note the initial conditions \(W(0) = 10\) and \(W(3) = 40.\) (Since there are two initial conditions, we can solve for each of the two unknown quantities.) Substituting \(W(0) = 10\) shows \[ \ba \frac{160}{1 + C} &= 10 \nl 1 + C &= 16 \nl \implies C &= 15 \pd \ea \] Then we substitute \(W(3) = 40\) to see \[ \ba \frac{160}{1 + 15e^{-3k}} &= 40 \nl 1 + 15e^{-3k} &= 4 \nl \implies k &= -\tfrac{1}{3} \ln \tfrac{1}{5} \pd \ea \] So \[ \ba W(t) &= \frac{160}{1 + 15 e^{-\par{-\tfrac{1}{3} \ln \tfrac{1}{5}} t}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{160}{1 + 15 \par{\tfrac{1}{5}}^{t/3}}} \ea \]

A newborn wolf weighs \(1\) pound. Four months later, the wolf's weight has increased to \(30\) pounds. As an adult, the wolf is expected to weigh \(90\) pounds. Determine a logistic growth function to model the wolf's weight as a function of time.
SOLUTION

Let \(W(t)\) be the wolf's weight as a function of time \(t\) in months. We expect the wolf to be \(90 \un{lb}\) as an adult, so the carrying capacity of \(W\) is \(90.\) Thus, we use the model \[W(t) = \frac{90}{1 + Ce^{-kt}}\] for unknown \(C\) and \(k.\)

Solving for \(C\) and \(k\) Note the initial conditions \(W(0) = 1\) and \(W(4) = 30.\) (Since there are two initial conditions, we can solve for each of the two unknown quantities.) Substituting \(W(0) = 1\) shows \[ \ba \frac{90}{1 + C} &= 1 \nl 1 + C &= 90 \nl \implies C &= 89 \pd \ea \] Then we substitute \(W(4) = 30\) to see \[ \ba \frac{90}{1 + 89e^{-4k}} &= 30 \nl 1 + 89e^{-4k} &= 3 \nl \implies k &= -\tfrac{1}{4} \ln \tfrac{2}{89} \pd \ea \] Thus, our model is \[ \ba W(t) &= \frac{90}{1 + 89 e^{-\par{-\tfrac{1}{4} \ln \tfrac{2}{89}} t}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{90}{1 + 89 \par{\tfrac{2}{89}}^{t/4}}} \ea \]

EXERCISE 22
In a savanna, a pack of \(15\) cheetahs is discovered. Two years later, the population has increased to \(45\) cheetahs. After a very long time, the population remains stable at \(90\) cheetahs. Use a logistic growth function to model the population of cheetahs with time.
SOLUTION

Because the population of cheetahs remains stable at \(90,\) the carrying capacity of the ecosystem is \(L = 90.\) Thus, we attain the general model \[P(t) = \frac{90}{1 + Ce^{-kt}} \cma\] where \(t\) is time in years since the discovery of the pack. The quantities \(C\) and \(k\) are constants. Our strategy is to determine these constants by substituting the initial conditions \(P(0) = 15\) and \(P(2) = 45.\) Each initial condition allows us to solve for one constant; we therefore use two initial conditions to determine the two constants \(C\) and \(k.\)

Solving for \(C\) and \(k\) Substituting \(P(0) = 15\) shows \[ \ba \frac{90}{1 + Ce^{0}} &= 15 \nl 1 + C &= 6 \nl \implies C &= 5 \pd \ea \] Plugging in \(P(2) = 45,\) we see \[ \ba \frac{90}{1 + 5e^{-2k}} &= 45 \nl 1 + 5e^{-2k} &= 2 \nl \implies k &= -\tfrac{1}{2} \ln \tfrac{1}{5} \pd \ea \] Therefore, we have \[ \ba P(t) &= \frac{90}{1 + 5e^{-\par{-\tfrac{1}{2} \ln \tfrac{1}{5}} t}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{90}{1 + 5 \par{\tfrac{1}{5}}^{t/2}}} \ea \]

EXERCISE 23
An ecologist discovers a population of \(250\) monkeys in a jungle. Upon revisiting the biome \(4\) years later, the ecologist observes that the population has doubled. She predicts that the population will level off at \(2000\) monkeys. Find a logistic function to model the monkey population over time.
SOLUTION

The ecologist predicts that the population will remain stable at \(2000,\) so we model the carrying capacity of the jungle to be \(L = 2000.\) Thus, we use the model \[P(t) = \frac{2000}{1 + Ce^{-kt}} \cma\] where \(t\) measures the years since the ecologist's initial discovery, and \(C\) and \(k\) are constants. Our strategy is to determine these constants by substituting the initial conditions \(P(0) = 250\) and \(P(4) = 500.\) Each initial condition allows us to solve for one constant; we therefore use two initial conditions to determine the two constants \(C\) and \(k.\)

Solving for \(C\) and \(k\) Substituting \(P(0) = 250\) shows \[ \ba \frac{2000}{1 + Ce^{0}} &= 250 \nl 1 + C &= 8 \nl \implies C &= 7 \pd \ea \] Since the population doubles, we have \(P(4) = 500\) and so we see \[ \ba \frac{2000}{1 + 7e^{-4k}} &= 500 \nl 1 + 7e^{-4k} &= 4 \nl \implies k &= -\tfrac{1}{4} \ln \tfrac{3}{7} \pd \ea \] Therefore, we have \[ \ba P(t) &= \frac{2000}{1 + 7e^{-\par{-\tfrac{1}{4} \ln \tfrac{3}{7}} t}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{2000}{1 + 7 \par{\tfrac{3}{7}}^{t/4}}} \ea \]

EXERCISE 24
An introductory calculus class contains \(300\) students and is taught in a lecture hall. At the beginning of lecture, \(250\) students are present. After \(2\) minutes, \(30\) additional students have joined the lecture hall. Using a logistic growth model, determine how many students are in the lecture hall after \(5\) minutes.
SOLUTION

The carrying capacity of the students in the lecture hall is \(L = 300.\) Thus, we attain the general model \[y = \frac{300}{1 + Ce^{-kt}} \cma\] where \(t\) is minutes since the beginning of the lecture, and \(C\) and \(k\) are constants. Our strategy is to determine these constants by substituting the initial conditions \(y(0) = 250\) and \(y(2) = 280.\) Each initial condition allows us to solve for one constant; we therefore use two initial conditions to determine the two constants \(C\) and \(k.\)

Solving for \(C\) and \(k\) Substituting \(y(0) = 250\) shows \[ \ba \frac{300}{1 + Ce^{0}} &= 250 \nl 1 + C &= \tfrac{6}{5} \nl \implies C &= \tfrac{1}{5} \pd \ea \] Substituting \(y(2) = 280,\) we attain \[ \ba \frac{300}{1 + \tfrac{1}{5}e^{-2k}} &= 280 \nl \tfrac{1}{5}e^{-2k} &= \tfrac{1}{14} \nl \implies k &= -\tfrac{1}{2} \ln \tfrac{5}{14} \pd \ea \] Therefore, we have \[ \ba P(t) &= \frac{300}{1 + \tfrac{1}{5} e^{-\par{-\tfrac{1}{2} \ln \tfrac{5}{14}} t}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{300}{1 + \tfrac{1}{5} \par{\tfrac{5}{14}}^{t/2}}} \ea \]

EXERCISE 25
As a society industrializes, women tend to work technical jobs and have fewer children. This societal transition often results in a stagnant population growth, which can be modeled by a logistic growth model. Suppose that in the year \(1970,\) City X has a population of \(45000.\) Amid industrialization, the population grows to \(54000\) in \(1990\) before remaining stable at \(55000.\) Determine a logistic growth function for the population.
SOLUTION

Let \(P(t)\) denote City X's population, where \(t\) is time measured in years since \(1970.\) We therefore have the initial conditions \(P(0) = 45000\) and \(P(20) = 54000,\) with a carrying capacity of \(55000.\) Our function takes the form \[P(t) = \frac{55000}{1 + Ce^{-kt}} \cma\] where we substitute the two initial conditions to solve for \(C\) and \(k.\)

Solving for \(C\) and \(k\) Substituting the condition \(P(0) = 45000\) reveals \[ \ba \frac{55000}{1 + Ce^{0}} &= 45000 \nl 1 + C &= \tfrac{11}{9} \nl \implies C &= \tfrac{2}{9} \pd \ea \] We then substitute the condition \(P(20) = 54000,\) from which we get \[ \ba \frac{55000}{1 + \tfrac{2}{9}e^{-20k}} &= 54000 \nl 1 + \tfrac{2}{9} e^{-20k} &= \tfrac{55}{54} \nl \implies k &= -\tfrac{1}{20} \ln \tfrac{1}{12} \pd \ea \] Therefore, we have \[ \ba P(t) &= \frac{55000}{1 + \tfrac{2}{9} e^{-\par{-\tfrac{1}{20} \ln \tfrac{1}{12}} t}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{55000}{1 + \tfrac{2}{9} \par{\tfrac{1}{12}}^{t/20}}} \ea \]

EXERCISE 26
In an \(1800\)-student school, a virus initially infects \(50\) students. The following day, \(10\) more students are infected. Administration plans to close the school when the infection rate reaches its highest. Assuming a carrying capacity of \(1000\) students, after how many days from the onset of the disease should the school be closed?
SOLUTION

The disease's growth rate is highest when the number of total cases equals half of the carrying capacity, \(L = 1000.\) A logistic growth function for the number of cases is therefore given by \[y = \frac{1000}{1 + Ce^{-kt}} \cma\] where \(t\) is time in days. We want to find the time \(t\) at which \(y = L/2\) \(= 500.\)

Solving for \(C\) and \(k\) Note the initial conditions \(y(0) = 50\) and \(y(1) = 60.\) We substitute the first initial condition to see \[ \ba \frac{1000}{1 + Ce^{0}} &= 50 \nl 1 + C &= 20 \nl \implies C &= 19 \pd \ea \] Then substituting \(y(1) = 60\) reveals \[ \ba \frac{1000}{1 + 19e^{-k}} &= 60 \nl 1 + 19 e^{-k} &= \tfrac{50}{3} \nl \implies k &= -\ln \tfrac{47}{57} \pd \ea \] Accordingly, the logistic function modeling the cases of the virus is \[ \ba y &= \frac{1000}{1 + 19e^{-\par{-\ln \tfrac{47}{57}}t}} \nl &= \frac{1000}{1 + 19 \par{\tfrac{47}{57}}^t} \pd \ea \] We therefore have \[ \ba \frac{1000}{1 + 19 \par{\tfrac{47}{57}}^t} &= 500 \nl 1 + 19 \par{\tfrac{47}{57}}^t &= 2 \nl \implies t &= \log_{47/57} \par{\tfrac{1}{19}} \approx \boxed{15 \un{days}} \ea \]

EXERCISE 27
On social media, a Story is a post visible to followers for \(24\) hours. A Story is posted to a personal account with \(600\) followers, half of whom are active. The story receives \(40\) views within an hour and \(200\) views within \(12\) hours. Using a logistic growth model, estimate how many views the story will receive after \(16\) hours.
SOLUTION

Since half of the \(600\) followers are active, the carrying capacity of the Story's views is \(L = 300.\) A logistic growth model for the views on the Story as a function of time \(t\) (in hours) is given by \[y = \frac{300}{1 + Ce^{-kt}}\] for unknown \(C\) and \(k.\)

Solving for \(C\) and \(k\) Note the initial conditions \(y(1) = 40\) and \(y(12) = 200.\) Substituting the former condition, we see \begin{flalign} &&\frac{300}{1 + Ce^{-k}} &= 40 \nonum &\nl \laWord{so} && Ce^{-k} &= \tfrac{13}{2} \pd \label{eq:story-eq-1} \end{flalign} Likewise, substituting \(y(12) = 200\) shows \begin{flalign} &&\frac{300}{1 + Ce^{-12k}} &= 200 \nonum &\nl \laWord{so} && Ce^{-12k} &= \tfrac{1}{2} \pd \label{eq:story-eq-2} \end{flalign} We divide \(\eqref{eq:story-eq-1}\) by \(\eqref{eq:story-eq-2}\) to obtain \[ \ba e^{11k} &= 13 \nl \implies k &= \tfrac{1}{11} \ln 13 \approx 0.233 \pd \ea \] Hence, in \(\eqref{eq:story-eq-1}\) we see \[ \ba Ce^{-\par{\tfrac{1}{11} \ln 13}} &= \tfrac{13}{2} \nl C \par{\tfrac{1}{13}}^{1/11} &= \tfrac{13}{2} \nl \implies C &= \frac{13/2}{\par{\tfrac{1}{13}}^{1/11}} \approx 8.207 \pd \ea \] Accordingly, our logistic growth function is \[y = \frac{300}{1 + 8.207 e^{-0.233t}} \pd\] After \(16\) hours, we predict that the Story will have \[ \ba y &= \frac{300}{1 + 8.207 e^{-0.233(16)}} \nl &\approx \boxed{251 \un{views}} \ea \]

EXERCISE 28
An \(800\)-student school conducts an evacuation drill, in which all students must exit the building to a designated area. Within \(2\) minutes of the drill, \(700\) students have exited the building. After \(5\) minutes, administrators discover that \(5\) students still remain the building. Determine a logistic decay model for the number of students inside the building as a function of time after \(2\) minutes.
SOLUTION

Let \(y\) be the number of students still inside the building. Since the school has \(800\) students, we assume \(L = 800\) and so \[y = \frac{800}{1 + Ce^{kt}} \cma\] where \(t\) is the number of minutes since the drill commenced. Within \(2\) minutes, \(700\) students have exited, meaning \(100\) students still remain inside. Within \(5\) minutes, \(5\) students still remain inside. Therefore, we use the initial conditions \(y(2) = 100\) and \(y(5) = 5.\)

Solving for \(C\) and \(k\) Substituting the condition \(y(2) = 100,\) we see \begin{flalign} &&\frac{800}{1 + Ce^{2k}} &= 100 \nonum &\nl \laWord{so} && Ce^{2k} &= 7 \pd \label{eq:drill-eq-1} \end{flalign} Similarly, substituting \(y(5) = 5\) shows \begin{flalign} &&\frac{800}{1 + Ce^{5k}} &= 5 \nonum &\nl \laWord{so} && Ce^{5k} &= 159 \pd \label{eq:drill-eq-2} \end{flalign} We divide \(\eqref{eq:drill-eq-2}\) by \(\eqref{eq:drill-eq-1}\) to obtain \[ \ba e^{3k} &= \tfrac{159}{7} \nl \implies k &= \tfrac{1}{3} \ln \tfrac{159}{7} \pd \ea \] Hence, \(\eqref{eq:drill-eq-1}\) becomes \[ \ba Ce^{2\par{\tfrac{1}{3} \ln \tfrac{159}{7}}} &= 7 \nl C \par{\tfrac{159}{7}}^{2/3} &= 7 \nl \implies C &= \frac{7}{\par{\tfrac{159}{7}}^{2/3}} \pd \ea \] Accordingly, our logistic decay model is \[ \ba y &= \frac{800}{1 + \frac{7}{\par{\tfrac{159}{7}}^{2/3}} e^{\par{\tfrac{1}{3} \ln \tfrac{159}{7}}t} } \nl &= \frac{800}{1 + \frac{7}{\par{\tfrac{159}{7}}^{2/3}} \par{\tfrac{159}{7}}^{t/3}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{800}{1 + 7 \par{\tfrac{159}{7}}^{(t - 2)/3}}} \ea \]
