If you dive deeper into the water, then you feel more pressure on your body due to the increased weight of the water above you. Hydrostatics enables us to test whether a submarine will be crushed in the deep water, or whether a shark can survive at a certain depth. Let's now generalize these ideas. Suppose that an object with area \(A\) is submerged in a body of water of uniform density \(\rho\) at a distance of \(h\) below the surface (Figure 1). The hydrostatic force that acts on this body equals the weight of the water above it. The volume of the water above the object is \(Ad,\) so its mass is \(\rho A d.\) Because weight is the product of mass and the acceleration due to gravity, \(9.8 \undiv{m}{sec}^2\) or \(32 \undiv{ft}{sec}^2,\) the hydrostatic force acting on the object is \begin{equation} F = \rho A g h \pd \label{eq:h-force} \end{equation} Pressure is defined as the force exerted on an object divided by its area. (A sharp knife exerts high pressure because its edge is so thin.) So the hydrostatic pressure that acts upon the submerged object is \(F/A,\) or \begin{equation} P = \rho g h \pd \label{eq:h-pressure} \end{equation} We use \(\eqref{eq:h-force}\) and \(\eqref{eq:h-pressure}\) for a thin object whose depth is uniform.
