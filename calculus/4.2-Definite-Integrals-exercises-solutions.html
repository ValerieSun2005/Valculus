4.2 Exercise Solutions — Definite Integrals

EXERCISE 1
What does the expression \(\int_a^b f(x) \di x\) represent?
SOLUTION The magnitude of the definite integral \(\int_a^b f(x) \di x\) equals the area between the graph of \(f(x)\) and the \(x\)-axis from \(x = a\) to \(x = b.\)
EXERCISE 2
The rate at which cars enter a highway is given by the function \(h(t),\) where \(t\) measures time in minutes since noon. Interpret the following statements.
  1. \(h(2) = 12\)
  2. \(\ds \int_0^2 h(t) \di t = 50\)
  3. \(h'(2) = -3\)
SOLUTION
  1. Since \(h\) measures a rate, the rate at which cars are entering the highway \(2\) minutes after noon is \(12\) cars per minute. (Note that \(h\) does not tell us how many cars are on the highway.)
  2. In general, the amount by which a quantity—here, the number of cars on the highway—changes is found by integrating its rate function. So \(50\) cars enter the highway from \(t = 0\) to \(t = 2,\) during the first \(2\) minutes since noon.
  3. The derivative \(h'\) measures how quickly the rate \(h\) is changing. So we interpret \(h'(2) = -3\) as follows: \(2\) minutes after noon, the rate at which cars enter the highway is decreasing by \(3\) cars per minute per minute.
EXERCISE 3
Water is pumped out of a pool at a rate given by \(f(t),\) where \(f(t)\) is measured in liters per minute and \(t\) is measured in minutes. Interpret the following expressions.
  1. \(f(3) = 20\)
  2. \(\ds \int_0^3 f(t) \di t = 300\)
  3. \(f'(3) = 2\)
SOLUTION
  1. Because \(f\) measures a rate, the rate at which water is pumped out of the pool after \(3\) minutes is \(20\) liters per minute. (Note that \(f\) provides us no information about how much water is in the pool.)
  2. In general, the amount by which a quantity—in this case, volume of water pumped out—changes is found by integrating its rate function. So \(300\) liters of water are pumped out of the pool from \(t = 0\) to \(t = 3\)—that is, in the first \(3\) minutes.
  3. The derivative \(f'\) measures how quickly the rate \(f\) is changing. So we interpret \(f'(3) = 2\) as follows: after \(3\) minutes, the rate at which water is pumped out of the pool is increasing by \(2\) liters per minute per minute.
EXERCISE 4
The graph of \(y = f(x)\) consists of line segments, as shown by Figure 14. Calculate the following integrals.
  1. \(\ds \int_2^6 f(x) \di x\)
  2. \(\ds \int_{-3}^{-1} f(x) \di x\)
  3. \(\ds \int_1^2 f(x) \di x\)
  4. \(\ds \int_2^6 [2 + 3f(x)] \di x\)
SOLUTION
  1. The region between the graph of \(f\) and the \(x\)-axis between \(x = 2\) and \(x = 6\) is a rectangle of width \(4\) and height \(3.\) Its area is therefore \(12.\) Since this region lies below the \(x\)-axis, the definite integral is negative—that is, \[\int_2^6 f(x) \di x = \boxed{-12}\]
  2. From \(x = -3\) to \(x = -1,\) the region under \(f\) resembles a house—a rectangle of area \(6\) under a triangle of area \(2.\) Thus, \[\int_{-3}^{-1} f(x) \di x = 6 + 2 = \boxed 8\]
  3. The region bounded by \(f\) from \(x = 1\) to \(x = 2\) is a trapezoid of area \[\tfrac{1}{2}(1 + 3)(1) = 2 \pd\] Since this region lies under the \(x\)-axis, we have \[\int_1^2 f(x) \di x = \boxed{-2}\]
  4. By the Sum Rule for Integrals and Constant Multiple Rule for Integrals, we see \[ \ba \int_2^6 [2 + 3f(x)] \di x &= \int_2^6 2 \di x + \int_2^6 3f(x) \di x \nl &= \int_2^6 2 \di x + 3 \int_2^6 f(x) \di x \pd \ea \] Applying the Constant Rule for Integrals and substituting \(\int_2^6 f(x) \di x = -12,\) we have \[ \int_2^6 [2 + 3f(x)] \di x = 2(6 - 2) + 3(-12) = \boxed{-28} \]
EXERCISE 5
The graph of \(y = g(x)\) consists of line segments for \(-6 \leq x \leq -3\) and \(3 \leq x \leq 6.\) For \(-3 \leq x \leq 3,\) the graph consists of a semicircle of radius \(3,\) as shown by Figure 15. Calculate the following integrals.
  1. \(\ds \int_{-6}^{-3} g(x) \di x\)
  2. \(\ds \int_{-3}^3 g(x) \di x\)
  3. \(\ds \int_6^3 g(x) \di x\)
  4. \(\ds \int_{-6}^6 2g(x) \di x\)
SOLUTION
  1. From \(x = -6\) to \(x = -3,\) the region under \(y = g(x)\) is a triangle of area \[\tfrac{1}{2} (3)(4) = 6 \pd\] Thus, \[\int_{-6}^{-3} g(x) \di x = \boxed 6\]
  2. The integral \(\int_{-3}^3 g(x) \di x\) gives the area of the entire semicircle. Since a circle of radius \(3\) has area \(\pi(3)^2 = 9 \pi,\) a semicircle of radius \(3\) has half this area; thus, \[\int_{-3}^3 g(x) \di x = \tfrac{1}{2} \par{9 \pi} = \boxed{\tfrac{9}{2} \pi}\]
  3. We can reverse the integral's bounds if we attach a negative sign, meaning \[\int_6^3 g(x) \di x = - \int_3^6 g(x) \di x \pd\] From \(x = 3\) to \(x = 6,\) the region between \(g\) and the \(x\)-axis is a triangle of area \[\tfrac{1}{2}(3)(5) = \tfrac{15}{2} \pd\] Because this region lies below the \(x\)-axis, the signed area is negative—that is, \(\int_3^6 g(x) \di x = -15/2.\) Accordingly, we have \[\int_6^3 g(x) \di x = - \int_3^6 g(x) \di x = - \par{-\tfrac{15}{2}} = \boxed{\tfrac{15}{2}}\]
  4. By the Additive Interval Property, we see \[ \ba \int_{-6}^6 g(x) \di x &= \int_{-6}^{-3} g(x) \di x + \int_{-3}^3 g(x) \di x + \int_3^6 g(x) \di x \nl &= 6 + \tfrac{9}{2} \pi + \tfrac{15}{2} \nl &= \tfrac{27}{2} + \tfrac{9}{2} \pi \pd \ea \] So by the Constant Multiple Rule for Integrals, we find \[ \ba \int_{-6}^6 2g(x) \di x &= 2 \int_{-6}^6 g(x) \di x \nl &= 2 \par{\tfrac{27}{2} + \tfrac{9}{2} \pi} \nl &= \boxed{27 + 9 \pi} \ea \]
EXERCISE 6
Walkthrough of Evaluating Definite Integrals Let's compute the definite integral \(\int_0^4 2x \di x\) by evaluating the limit of a right Riemann sum of equal widths.
  1. For a right Riemann sum whose rectangles have equal widths, we use the formula \[\int_a^b f(x) \di x = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i = 1}^n f(x_i) \Delta x \cma\] where \(x_i = a + i \Delta x\) and \(\Delta x = (b - a)/n.\) Comparing this form to \(\int_0^4 2x \di x\) shows \(a = 0,\) \(b = 4,\) and \(f(x) = 2x.\) Find expressions for \(x_i\) and \(\Delta x.\)
  2. In terms of \(i\) and \(n,\) determine an expression for \(f(x_i) \Delta x.\)
  3. Simplify the limit in part (a) to show that \[\int_0^4 2x \di x = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{32}{n^2} \sum_{i = 1}^n i \pd\]
  4. Use the formula \[\sum_{i = 1}^n i = \frac{n(n + 1)}{2}\] to convert the sum in part (c) to an algebraic expression. Evaluate the corresponding limit.
SOLUTION
  1. We see \[\Delta x = \frac{b - a}{n} = \frac{4 - 0}{n} = \frac{4}{n} \pd\] Thus, \[x_i = a + i \Delta x = 0 + i \cdot \frac{4}{n} = \frac{4i}{n} \pd\]
  2. Since \(f(x) = 2x,\) we have \[f(x_i) = 2 \par{\frac{4i}{n}} = \frac{8i}{n} \pd\] Thus, \[f(x_i) \Delta x = \frac{8i}{n} \cdot \frac{4}{n} = \frac{32i}{n^2} \pd\]
  3. We have \[ \ba \int_0^4 2x \di x &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i = 1}^n f(x_i) \Delta x \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i = 1}^n \frac{32i}{n^2} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{32}{n^2} \sum_{i = 1}^n i \pd \ea \] Note that we can take the factor \(32/n^2\) out of the summation expression because it does not depend on \(i.\)
  4. Using the provided formula gives \[ \ba \int_0^4 2x \di x &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \parbr{\frac{32}{n^2} \cdot \frac{n(n + 1)}{2}} \nl &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{16(n + 1)}{n} = 16 \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n + 1}{n} \nl &= 16 \lim_{n \to \infty} \par{1 + \frac{1}{n}} = \boxed{16} \ea \]

evaluate the definite integral by using geometry and integral properties.
SOLUTION Since the upper bound \(0\) is less than the lower bound \(2,\) we choose to reverse the bounds. When we do so, we must tack on a negative sign—namely, \[ \ba \int_2^0 -\sqrt{4 - x^2} \di x &= - \int_0^2 - \sqrt{4 - x^2} \di x \nl &= - \par{-\int_0^2 \sqrt{4 - x^2} \di x} \nl &= \int_0^2 \sqrt{4 - x^2} \di x \pd \ea \] (Note that the Constant Multiple Rule enables us to pull the negative sign out of the integrand.) The graph of \(y = \sqrt{4 - x^2}\) is a semicircle of radius \(2\) lying above the \(x\)-axis. Use a right Riemann sum with \(n = 5\) equal-width rectangles to approximate \[\int_0^5 \par{x^3 - x^2 + 2} \di x \pd\]
SOLUTION With \(f(x) = x^3 - x^2 + 2,\) we split the interval \([0, 5]\) into five subintervals and evaluate \(f\) at the right endpoint of each subinterval. We see \(\Delta x = (5 - 0)/5 = 1,\) so we discover \[ \ba \int_0^5 \par{x^3 - x^2 + 2} \di x &\approx f(1) \Delta x + f(2) \Delta x + f(3) \Delta x + f(4) \Delta x + f(5) \Delta x \nl &= \par{2 + 6 + 20 + 50 + 102} \cdot 1 \nl &= \boxed{180} \ea \]

EXERCISE 21
The following table gives values of \(f(x)\) for selected \(x.\) Use a left Riemann sum with the four subintervals provided to estimate \(\int_0^8 f(x) \di x.\)
\(x\) \(1\) \(2\) \(5\) \(7\) \(8\)
\(f(x)\) \(2\) \(5\) \(-3\) \(7\) \(-4\)
SOLUTION Using the given values in the table, we construct a left Riemann sum with the subintervals \([1, 2],\) \([2, 5],\) \([5, 7],\) and \([7, 8].\) (In a Riemann sum, the subintervals do not need to be the same width.) We evaluate \(f\) at the left point of each subinterval, so we have \[ \ba \int_0^8 f(x) \di x &\approx f(1) (2 - 1) + f(2) (5 - 2) + f(5) (7 - 5) + f(7)(8 - 7) \nl &= 2(1) + 5(3) + (-3)(2) + 7(1) = \boxed{18} \ea \]
EXERCISE 22
A car travels with speed \(v(t),\) where \(v(t)\) is measured in meters per second and \(t\) is measured in seconds. A speedometer's readings are recorded every few seconds, as shown by the following table. Using a right Riemann sum, estimate the distance the car travels in the first \(10\) seconds.
\(t\) \(0\) \(1\) \(3\) \(4\) \(7\) \(10\)
\(v(t)\) \(9\) \(15\) \(18\) \(26\) \(22\) \(19\)
SOLUTION Since the car's speed is always nonnegative, the distance traveled by the car is given by \(\int_0^{10} v(t) \di t.\) [If \(v(t)\) were negative for some values of \(t,\) then the distance would be given by \(\int_0^{10} \abs{v(t)} \di t.\)] Using the given values in the table, we construct a right Riemann sum with the subintervals \([0, 1],\) \([1, 3],\) \([3, 4],\) \([4, 7],\) and \([7, 10].\) (In a Riemann sum, the subintervals do not need to be the same width.) We evaluate \(v\) at the right endpoint of each subinterval, from which we attain \[ \ba \int_0^{10} v(t) \di t &\approx [v(1) (1 - 0)] + [v(3)(3 - 1)] + [v(4)(4 - 3)] + [v(7)(7 - 4)] + [v(10)(10 - 7)] \nl &= 15(1) + 18(2) + 26(1) + 22(3) + 19(3) = \boxed{200 \un{meters}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 23
A generator outputs power with time as modeled by the function \(P(t),\) where \(P(t)\) is measured in watts per hour and \(t\) is measured in hours since \(8\) am. The power output was measured four times throughout the morning, as given by the following table. Using a left Riemann sum, estimate the energy the generator delivered from \(8\) am to \(2\) pm.
\(t\) \(1\) \(3\) \(5\) \(6\)
\(P(t)\) \(400\) \(370\) \(480\) \(440\)
SOLUTION Note that from \(8\) am to \(2\) pm, \(6\) hours have elapsed. Thus, we seek the value of \(\int_0^6 P(t) \di t.\) Using the given values in the table, we construct a left Riemann sum with the subintervals \([1, 3],\) \([3, 5],\) and \([5, 6].\) (In a Riemann sum, the subintervals do not need to be the same width.) We evaluate \(P\) at the left endpoint of each subinterval, from which we attain \[ \ba \int_0^6 P(t) \di t &\approx P(1)(3 - 1) + P(3) (5 - 3) + P(5) (6 - 5) \nl &= 400(2) + 370(2) + 480(1) = \boxed{2020 \un{watt-hours}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 24
For
\[\ds \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i = 1}^n \par{1 + \frac{2}{n} i}^3 \frac{2}{n} \cmaa 1 \leq x \leq 3\]
rewrite the limit as a definite integral over the provided interval.
SOLUTION A right Riemann sum of equal subinterval width is related to a definite integral as follows: \[\int_a^b f(x) \di x = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i = 1}^n f(x_i) \Delta x \cma\] where \[\Delta x = \frac{b - a}{n} \and x_i = a + i \Delta x \pd\] We are given \(a = 1\) and \(b = 3,\) from which we identify \[x_i = a + i \Delta x = 1 + \frac{3 - 1}{n} i = 1 + \frac{2}{n} i \pd\] Thus, \(f(x) = x^3\) and so \[\lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i = 1}^n \par{1 + \frac{2}{n} i}^3 \frac{2}{n} = \boxed{\int_1^3 x^3 \di x}\] In general, to convert a right Riemann sum of equally sized subintervals into a definite integral, we replace \(\lim \sum\) with \(\int,\) \(f(x_i)\) with \(f(x),\) and \(\Delta x\) with \(\dd x.\)
