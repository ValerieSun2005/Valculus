EXERCISE 1
State both parts of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.
SOLUTION If \(f\) is continuous over \([a, b],\) then Part I of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC I) states that \begin{equation} \deriv{}{x} \int_a^x f(t) \di t = f(x) \pd \label{eq:ftc-I} \end{equation} If \(f\) is continuous over \([a, b]\) and \(F\) is an antiderivative of \(f,\) Part II of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC II) states that \begin{equation} \int_a^b f(x) \di x = F(b) - F(a) \pd \label{eq:ftc-ii} \end{equation}
EXERCISE 2
What is the Net Change Theorem?
SOLUTION The Net Change Theorem states that the final quantity equals the initial quantity plus the accumulation of its rate. Let \(f\) be a differentiable function; then the theorem states that \[f(b) = f(a) + \int_a^b f'(x) \di x \pd\]

\[\ds \int_1^3 \frac{4y^3 + 3y^2 - 6y}{y^2} \di y\]

EXERCISE 23
For
\[\ds \int_1^{\sqrt 3} \frac{x^2}{x^2 + 1} \di x\]
evaluate the integral.
SOLUTION The hardest part of problems is to determine an antiderivative. In general, to integrate a rational expression, we want to divide the expression. Performing polynomial division enables us to rewrite the integrand as \[1 - \frac{1}{x^2 + 1} \pd\] Alternatively, the following algebraic trick exists. In the numerator, adding \(1\) and then subtracting \(1\) yields the same result: \[\frac{x^2 + 1 - 1}{x^2 + 1} = \frac{x^2 + 1}{x^2 + 1} - \frac{1}{x^2 + 1} = 1 - \frac{1}{x^2 + 1} \pd\] An antiderivative of \(1 - 1/(x^2 + 1)\) is \(x - \tan^{-1} x.\) Hence, FTC II gives \[ \par{\sqrt 3 - \tan^{-1} \sqrt 3} - \par{1 - \tan^{-1} 1} = \boxed{\sqrt 3 - 1 - \tfrac{\pi}{12}} \]

EXERCISE 34
In Figure 1, the graph of \(y = g'(x)\) is shown for \(-6 \leq x \leq 6.\) It is known that \(g(0) = 5.\) Let \(f(x) = \int_0^x g'(t) \di t.\)
  1. Calculate \(f(4).\)
  2. Find the value of \(g(4).\)
  3. Evaluate \(f'(4).\)
  4. Identify the \(x\)-coordinates of any critical points of \(f.\)
  5. If \(g''\) is continuous, then find \(\int_{-6}^4 g''(x) \di x.\)
SOLUTION In each part, be sure not to confuse \(f\) with \(f'\) and \(g\) with \(g'.\)
  1. The value of \(f(4)\) is \[\int_0^4 g'(t) \di t = \int_0^4 g'(x) \di x \pd\] Do not concern yourself with the difference between \(g'(t)\) and \(g'(x);\) the identity of \(g'\) is the same. The integral is the sum of signed areas enclosed by the graph of \(g'\) and the \(x\)-axis from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 4.\) For \(0 \leq x \leq 3\) we count the area under \(g'\) as positive, but for \(3 \leq x \leq 4\) we subtract the area because the region lies under the \(x\)-axis. The area under \(g'\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 3\) is \(8\) and contributes positively to \(\int_0^4 g'(x) \di x.\) The area from \(x = 3\) to \(x = 4\) is \(1,\) which contributes negatively. Thus, \[\int_0^4 g'(x) \di x = 8 - 1 = \boxed 7\]
  2. We are given \(g(0) = 5,\) so we need to connect this information to \(g(4).\) By the Net Change Theorem, \[g(4) = g(0) + \int_0^4 g'(x) \di x \pd\] From part (a), \(\int_0^4 g'(x) \di x = 7.\) So \[g(4) = 5 + 7 = \boxed{12}\]
  3. FTC I gives \(f'(x) = g'(x),\) so \[f'(4) = g'(4) = \boxed{-2}\]
  4. The function \(f\) has a critical point when \(f'(x) = 0\) or when \(f'(x)\) is undefined but \(f(x)\) is defined. By FTC I, \(f'(x) = g'(x).\) From the graph, we see \(g'(x) = 0\) when \(x = -5\) and \(x = 3,\) so these are the \(x\)-coordinates of critical points of \(f.\)
  5. Since \(g''\) is continuous, by FTC II \[ \ba \int_{-6}^4 g''(x) \di x &= g'(4) - g'(-6) \nl &= -2 - (-2) \nl &= \boxed 0 \ea \]
EXERCISE 35
Figure 2 shows the graph of \(y = f'(x)\) for \(-6 \leq x \leq 6.\) The graph of \(y = f(x)\) passes through the point \((1, 3).\)
  1. Let \(g(x) = \int_{-2}^x f(t) \di t.\) Find \(g(-6).\)
  2. Identify the \(x\)-coordinates of any critical points of \(f.\) Classify each critical point as corresponding to a relative minimum, relative maximum, or neither of \(f.\)
  3. Find \(f(4).\)
  4. At what \(x\)-coordinates does the graph of \(f\) have an inflection point?
SOLUTION In each part, be sure not to confuse \(f\) with \(f'\) and \(g\) with \(g'.\)
  1. We have \[g(-6) = \int_{-2}^{-6} f(t) \di t = -\int_{-6}^{-2} f(x) \di x \pd\] Do not concern yourself with the difference between \(f(t)\) and \(f(x);\) the identity of \(f\) is the same. The region under \(f'\) from \(x = -6\) to \(x = -2\) is a trapezoid of area \[\tfrac{1}{2} (4 + 2)(4) = 12 \pd\] Hence, \[g(-6) = \boxed{-12}\]
  2. From the graph, we see \(f' = 0\) at \(x = 3.\) Thus, \(f\) has a critical point at that location. Since \(f'\) changes sign from positive to negative at \(x = 3,\) \(f\) has a relative maximum at \(x = 3\) by the First-Derivative Test.
  3. We need to connect the given \(f(1) = 3\) to \(f(4).\) By the Net Change Theorem, \[f(4) = f(1) + \int_1^4 f'(x) \di x \pd\] The region under \(f'\) from \(1\) to \(2\) is a trapezoid of area \[\tfrac{1}{2} (4 + 2)(1) = 3 \pd\] The region under \(f'\) from \(3\) to \(4\) contributes positively to \(\int_1^4 f'(x) \di x,\) but the region above \(f'\) from \(3\) to \(4\) contributes negatively. Both areas are the same and so cancel each other. Hence, \(\int_1^4 f'(x) \di x\) \(= \int_1^2 f'(x) \di x = 3.\) We have \[f(4) = 3 + 3 = \boxed 6\]
  4. The graph of \(f\) has an inflection point when \(f''\) changes sign. The slope of \(f'\) (which is \(f''\)) changes from negative to positive at \(x = -2\) and switches from positive to negative at \(x = 1.\) So the graph of \(f\) has inflection points at those locations.
EXERCISE 36
Prove that the area under the parabola \(y = x^2\) from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\) is \(\tfrac{1}{3} \par{b^3 - a^3}.\)
SOLUTION We use FTC II as follows: An antiderivative of \(x^2\) is \(x^3/3\) by the Reverse Power Rule. EXERCISE 37
Explain why the following statement is false: \[\int_{-1}^1 \frac{1}{x^2} \di x = -\frac{1}{x} \intEval_{-1}^1 = -2 \pd\]
SOLUTION The function \(1/x^2\) isn't continuous over \([-1, 1],\) so we cannot use FTC II. In fact, the graph of \(y = 1/x^2\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = 0,\) so the area is unbounded. We discuss more about these integrals in Section 6.5; this integral happens to diverge.
EXERCISE 38
Find the critical points of \(g(x) = \int_1^x (1 - 1/t) \di t.\)
SOLUTION The function \(g\) has a critical point when \(g'(x) = 0\) or when \(g'(x)\) is undefined but \(g(x)\) is defined. (See Section 3.2 for a review of critical points.) EXERCISE 40
For \(0 \leq x \leq \pi,\) let \(f(x) = \int_0^x (1 - \sin t) \di t.\) Find the critical points of \(f.\)
SOLUTION The function \(f\) has a critical point when \(f'(x) = 0\) or when \(f'(x)\) is undefined but \(f(x)\) is defined. (See Section 3.2 for a review of critical points.) By FTC I, \[f'(x) = 1 - \sin x \pd\] Thus, \(f'(x) = 0\) for \(x = \pi/2,\) the only solution in the interval \([0, \pi].\) We need the critical point, so we calculate \(f(\pi/2) \col\) \[ \ba f \par{\tfrac{\pi}{2}} &= \int_0^{\pi/2} 1 - \sin t \di t \nl &= (t + \cos t) \intEval_0^{\pi/2} \nl &= \par{\tfrac{\pi}{2} + \cos \tfrac{\pi}{2}} - (0 + \cos 0) \nl &= \tfrac{\pi}{2} - 1 \pd \ea \] So the critical point of \(f\) is \[\boxed{\par{\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{2} - 1}}\]
EXERCISE 41
Let \(f(x) = \int_1^x (t^2 - 2t - 3) \di t.\) Find the values of \(x\) for which
  1. \(f\) is decreasing
  2. \(f\) is increasing
  3. the graph of \(f\) is concave up
SOLUTION See Section 3.4 if you need a review of the relationship between \(f,\) \(f',\) and \(f''.\)
  1. The function \(f\) is decreasing when \(f'(x) \lt 0.\) By FTC I, \[f'(x) = x^2 - 2x - 3 = (x - 3)(x + 1) \pd\] The solutions to \(f'(x) \lt 0\) are \(-1 \lt x \lt 3.\) So \(f\) is decreasing over the interval \([-1, 3].\)
  2. The solutions to \(f'(x) \gt 0\) are \(x \lt -1\) and \(x \gt 3.\) So \(f\) is increasing for \(x\) in \((-\infty, -1]\) \(\cup [3, \infty).\)
  3. We have \[f''(x) = 2x - 2 \pd\] The graph of \(f\) is concave up when \(f''(x) \gt 0,\) meaning when \(x \gt 1.\)
EXERCISE 42
Determine the relative maximum of \(f(x) = \int_0^x (4 - t^2) \di t.\)
