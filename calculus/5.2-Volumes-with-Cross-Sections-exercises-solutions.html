5.2 Exercise Solutions: Volumes with Cross Sections

EXERCISE 1
A solid is placed in the \(xy\)-plane between \(x = 0\) and \(x = 3.\) At any \(x,\) the solid's cross-sectional area perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is given by \[A(x) = x^2 + 2 \pd\] Calculate the solid's volume.
SOLUTION We find the solid's volume by integrating \(A(x)\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 3,\) as follows: \[ \ba V &= \int_0^3 A(x) \di x = \int_0^3 \par{x^2 + 2} \di x \nl &= \par{\tfrac{1}{3} x^3 + 2x} \intEval_0^3 = \boxed{15} \ea \]
EXERCISE 2
A solid is placed in the \(xy\)-plane between \(y = 1\) and \(y = 9.\) At any \(y,\) the solid's cross-sectional area perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis is given by \[A(y) = \sqrt y + 3 \pd\] Calculate the solid's volume.
SOLUTION The solid's volume is given by integrating \(A(y)\) from \(y = 1\) to \(y = 9,\) as follows: \[ \ba V &= \int_0^9 A(y) \di y = \int_0^9 \par{\sqrt y + 3} \di y \nl &= \par{\tfrac{2}{3} y^{3/2} + 3y} \intEval_0^9 \nl &= 18 + 27 = \boxed{45} \ea \]
EXERCISE 3
The region bounded by the parabola \(y = x^2,\) the \(x\)-axis, and the lines \(x = 1\) and \(x = 2\) is the base of a solid whose cross sections perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis are squares. Calculate the volume of the solid.
SOLUTION Let's consider a single square within \(1 \leq x \leq 2 \col\) The side of this square is \(x^2,\) meaning its cross-sectional area is given by \[A(x) = \par{x^2}^2 = x^4 \pd\] Accordingly, we find the solid's volume by integrating this function from \(x = 1\) to \(x = 2\)â€”namely, \[ \ba V &= \int_1^2 A(x) \di x = \int_1^2 x^4 \di x \nl &= \tfrac{1}{5} x^5 \intEval_1^2 \nl &= \tfrac{1}{5} \par{2^5 - 1^5} = \boxed{\tfrac{31}{5}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 4
A solid's base is the triangle bounded by the lines \(y = x + 2,\) \(x = -2,\) and \(x = 1.\) The solid's cross sections perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis are squares. Calculate the volume of this solid.
SOLUTION At each \(x\) in \([-2, 1],\) the cross-sectional area to the solid perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is \[A(x) = (x + 2)^2 \pd\] Accordingly, the solid's total volume is given by integrating this function from \(x = -2\) to \(x = 1 \col\) \[ \ba V &= \int_{-2}^1 A(x) \di x = \int_{-2}^1 (x + 2)^2 \di x \nl &= \tfrac{1}{3} (x + 2)^3 \intEval_{-2}^1 \nl &= \tfrac{1}{3} (27 - 0) = \boxed{9} \ea \]
EXERCISE 5
Let region \(R\) be bounded by the curve \(y = \sqrt x,\) the \(x\)-axis, and the lines \(x = 1\) and \(x = 4.\) Region \(R\) is the base of a solid such that at each \(x,\) its cross section perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is a rectangle of height \(2x + 3.\) Calculate the volume of the solid.
SOLUTION A cross-sectional rectangle at \(x\) has base \(\sqrt x\) (in region \(R\)) and height \(2x + 3.\) The solid's cross-sectional area perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis at each \(x\) is therefore \[A(x) = \par{\sqrt x \, } (2x + 3) = 2x^{3/2} + 3x^{1/2} \pd\] Thus, the solid's volume is given by integrating this function from \(x = 1\) to \(x = 4,\) as follows: \[ \ba V &= \int_1^4 A(x) \di x = \int_1^4 \par{2x^{3/2} + 3x^{1/2}} \di x \nl &= \par{\tfrac{4}{5} x^{5/2} + 2 x^{3/2}} \intEval_1^4 = \boxed{\tfrac{194}{5}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 6
Region \(R\) is bounded by the curve \(y = e^x,\) the \(x\)-axis, the \(y\)-axis, and the line \(x = 2.\) Suppose that region \(R\) is the base of a solid such that at each \(x,\) the cross section perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is a rectangle whose height is \(3\) times the length of its base in region \(R.\) Calculate the solid's volume.
SOLUTION The rectangle's base in region \(R\) at any \(x\) is \(e^x.\) Since the rectangle's height is \(3\) times this length, the cross-sectional area of the solid perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis at each \(x\) is given by \[A(x) = e^x \par{3e^x} = 3e^{2x} \pd\] Thus, the solid's volume is given by integrating this function from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 2\) (the boundaries of \(R\)), as follows: \[ \ba V &= \int_0^2 A(x) \di x = \int_0^2 3e^{2x} \di x \nl &= \tfrac{3}{2} e^{2x} \intEval_0^2 = \tfrac{3}{2} \par{e^4 - e^0} \nl &= \boxed{\tfrac{3}{2} \par{e^4 - 1}} \approx 80.397 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 7
The region bounded by the curve \(f(x) = x^2,\) the \(y\)-axis, and the line \(y = 4\) is the bottom face of a solid such that at each \(y,\) its cross section perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis is a rectangle of height \(3f(x).\) Calculate the solid's volume.
SOLUTION The enclosed region is in the first quadrant between the lines \(y = 0\) and \(y = 4.\) Since we consider cross sections perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis, we want to express all quantities in terms of \(y.\) Let's rewrite \(y = x^2\) as \(x = \sqrt y.\) At each \(y,\) the cross section to the solid perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis is a rectangle of dimensions \(\sqrt y\) by \(3 \sqrt y.\) Thus, the function representing the solid's cross-sectional area at any \(y\) is \[A(y) = \par{\sqrt y \, } \par{3 \sqrt y \,} = 3 y \pd\] Hence, the solid's volume is given by integrating this function from \(y = 0\) to \(y = 4\) (the boundaries of the region), as follows: \[ V = \int_0^4 3y \di y = \tfrac{3}{2} y^2 \intEval_0^4 = \boxed{24} \]
EXERCISE 8
The triangle enclosed by the line \(y = 2x,\) the \(y\)-axis, and the line \(y = 4\) is the base of a solid whose cross sections perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis are semicircles. Calculate the volume of this solid.
SOLUTION The cross sections perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis are semicircles; since we work with \(y,\) we want to express all quantities in terms of \(y.\) Let's rewrite \(y = 2x\) as \(x = y/2.\) At each \(y\) the distance from the \(y\)-axis to a point on the line \(x = y/2\) is \(y/2,\) the diameter of the cross-sectional semicircle. The radius of each semicircle is therefore \(y/4.\) Then the area of each semicircle is \[A(y) = \tfrac{1}{2} \pi \par{\frac{y}{4}}^2 = \frac{\pi y^2}{32} \pd\] We integrate this function from \(y = 0\) to \(y = 4\) to obtain the solid's volume, as follows: \[ \ba V &= \int_0^4 A(y) \di y = \int_0^4 \frac{\pi y^2}{32} \di y \nl &= \frac{\pi}{96} y^3 \intEval_0^4 = \boxed{\frac{2 \pi}{3}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 9
In the first quadrant, the triangle enclosed by the line \(y = 5 - x\) is the base of a solid. Every cross section to the solid perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is a right isosceles triangles whose hypotenuse lies in the bounded region. Calculate the volume of this solid.
SOLUTION Observe that \(y = 5 - x\) touches the \(x\)-axis when \(x = 5.\) So the region is bounded from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 5.\) At each \(x,\) the cross section to the solid \(S\) perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is a right isosceles triangle whose hypotenuse has length \(y = 5 - x.\) Thus, the triangle's legs each have length \(y/\sqrt 2.\) So at each \(x,\) the cross-sectional area of the solid is \[A(x) = \tfrac{1}{2} \par{\frac{y}{\sqrt 2}}^2 = \frac{y^2}{4} = \frac{(5 - x)^2}{4} \pd\] The volume of \(S\) is given by integrating \(A(x)\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 5,\) as follows: \[ \ba V &= \int_0^5 A(x) \di x = \tfrac{1}{4} \int_0^5 (5 - x)^2 \di x \nl &= -\tfrac{1}{12} (5 - x)^3 \intEval_0^5 = \boxed{\tfrac{125}{12}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 10
The region enclosed by the graph of \(y = \asin x\) for \(0 \leq x \leq 1\) is the base of a solid such that at each \(y,\) the cross section perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis is a rectangle of height \(2 \cos y.\) Calculate the solid's volume.
SOLUTION Because we know the identity of cross sections perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis, we express all quantities in terms of \(y.\) We rewrite \(y = \asin x\) as \(x = \sin y\) for \(0 \leq y \leq \pi/2.\) At each \(y,\) the cross section to the solid perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis is a rectangle of height \(2 \cos y.\) So at each \(y,\) the solid's cross-sectional area perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis is \[A(y) = (\sin y) (2 \cos y) \pd\] The double-angle identity for sine gives \(2 \sin y \cos y\) \(= \sin 2y,\) so \(A(y) = \sin 2y.\) We obtain the solid's volume \(V\) by integrating \(A\) from \(y = 0\) to \(y = \pi/2,\) as follows: \[ \ba V &= \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin 2y \di y \nl &= -\tfrac{1}{2} \cos 2y \intEval_0^{\pi/2} = \boxed 1 \ea \]
EXERCISE 11
In the first quadrant, the region enclosed by the curve \(y = x^3\) and the line \(y = 8\) is the base of a solid whose cross sections parallel to the \(x\)-axis are equilateral triangles. Calculate the solid's volume.
SOLUTION The phrase "parallel to the \(x\)-axis" means perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis. Accordingly, we want to express all quantities in terms of \(y.\) We rewrite \(y = x^3\) as \(x = \sqrt[3]{y}.\) At each \(y,\) the cross section to the solid \(S\) perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis is an equilateral triangle of side length \(x = \sqrt[3]{y}.\) The triangle's height is therefore \(x\sqrt{3}/2\) \(= \sqrt[3]{y} \sqrt{3}/2,\) while its length is \(x = \sqrt[3]{y}.\) So at each \(y,\) the cross-sectional area of the solid is \[A(y) = \tfrac{1}{2} \par{\sqrt[3]{y} \,} \par{\frac{\sqrt[3] y \sqrt 3}{2}} = \frac{\sqrt 3}{4} y^{2/3} \pd\] The volume of \(S\) is given by integrating \(A(y)\) from \(y = 0\) to \(y = 8,\) as follows: \[ \ba V &= \int_0^8 A(y) \di y = \frac{\sqrt 3}{4} \int_0^8 y^{2/3} \di y \nl &= \frac{\sqrt 3}{4} \frac{y^{5/3}}{5/3} \intEval_0^8 \nl &= \boxed{\frac{24 \sqrt 3 }{5}} \approx 8.314 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 12
Suppose that a solid's base is the region bounded above by the curve \(y = \sqrt x + 2,\) below by the curve \(y = 1 - 2 \sqrt x,\) and on the sides by the lines \(x = 0\) and \(x = 1.\) If cross sections perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis are semicircles, then determine the solid's volume.
SOLUTION At each \(x,\) the cross section perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is a semicircle whose diameter is \((\sqrt x + 2) - (1 - 2 \sqrt x)\) \(= 1 + 3 \sqrt x.\) Thus, its area is \[ \ba A(x) &= \tfrac{1}{2} \pi \par{\frac{1 + 3 \sqrt x}{2}}^2 \nl &= \frac{\pi}{8} \par{1 + 6 \sqrt x + 9 x} \pd \ea \] So the solid's volume is given by integrating \(A(x)\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 1\) (the boundaries of the region), as follows: \[ \ba V &= \int_0^1 A(x) \di x = \frac{\pi}{8} \int_0^1 \par{1 + 6 \sqrt x + 9x} \di x \nl &= \frac{\pi}{8} \par{x + 4 x^{3/2} + \tfrac{9}{2} x^2} \intEval_0^1 \nl &= \boxed{\frac{19 \pi}{16}} \approx 3.731 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 13
The region enclosed by \(y = x^2\) and \(y = \sqrt x\) is the base of a solid whose cross sections perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis are squares. Find the solid's volume.
SOLUTION Because cross sections perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis are a known shape (squares), we want to express all quantities in terms of \(y.\) We rewrite \(y = x^2\) and \(y = \sqrt x\) as \(x = \sqrt y\) and \(x = y^2,\) respectively. The bounded region begins at \(y = 0\) and ends at \(y = 1.\) At each \(y,\) a cross-sectional square has side length \(\sqrt y - y^2,\) so its area is \[A(y) = \par{\sqrt y - y^2}^2 = y - 2y^{5/2} + y^4 \pd\] The solid's volume is given by integrating this function using the bounds \(y = 0\) and \(y = 1.\) We have \[ \ba V &= \int_0^1 A(y) \di y = \int_0^1 \par{y - 2y^{5/2} + y^4} \di y \nl &= \par{\tfrac{1}{2} y^2 - \tfrac{4}{7} y^{7/2} + \tfrac{1}{5} y^5} \intEval_0^1 \nl &= \boxed{\tfrac{9}{70}} \approx 0.129 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 14
A solid's base is the region enclosed by the line \(y = x\) and the parabola \(y = x^2\) in the first quadrant. Every cross section to the solid perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is a right isosceles triangles whose hypotenuse lies in the bounded region. Calculate the volume of this solid.
SOLUTION Note that \(y = x\) touches the parabola when \(x = 0\) and \(x = 1.\) So the region is bounded from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 1.\) At each \(x,\) the cross section to the solid \(S\) perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is a right isosceles triangle whose hypotenuse has length \(y = x - x^2.\) Thus, the triangle's legs each have length \(y/\sqrt 2.\) So at each \(x,\) the cross-sectional area of the solid is \[A(x) = \tfrac{1}{2} \par{\frac{y}{\sqrt 2}}^2 = \frac{y^2}{4} = \frac{\par{x - x^2}^2}{4} \pd\] The volume of \(S\) is given by integrating \(A(x)\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 1,\) as follows: \[ \ba V &= \int_0^1 A(x) \di x = \tfrac{1}{4} \int_0^1 \par{x - x^2}^2 \di x \nl &= \tfrac{1}{4} \int_0^1 \par{x^2 - 2x^3 + x^4} \di x \nl &= \tfrac{1}{4} \par{\tfrac{1}{3} x^3 - \tfrac{1}{2} x^4 + \tfrac{1}{5} x^5} \intEval_0^1 \nl &= \boxed{\tfrac{1}{120}} \approx 0.008 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 15
A baker models the base of a mini-cake using the function \(y = 2 + 2 \cos(\pi x/3)\) for \(-3 \leq x \leq 3,\) where \(x\) is measured in inches. Cross sections perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis are semicircles. For each cubic inch, the baker injects \(0.1\) gram of liquid chocolate into the cake. How many grams of liquid chocolate does the baker add to the cake?
SOLUTION Because cross sections perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis are semicircles, each semicircle at \(x\) has a diameter of \(y = 2 + 2 \cos(\pi x/3).\) Its radius is therefore \(y/2,\) meaning its area is \[A(x) = \tfrac{1}{2} \pi \par{\frac{y}{2}}^2 = \frac{\pi}{8} \par{2 + 2 \cos \frac{\pi x}{3}}^2 \pd\] We calculate the cake's volume by integrating \(A(x)\) from \(x = -3\) to \(x = 3.\) Using a graphing calculator, we discover \[ \ba V &= \int_{-3}^3 A(x) \di x = \int_{-3}^3 \frac{\pi}{8} \par{2 + 2 \cos \frac{\pi x}{3}}^2 \di x \nl &\approx 14.14 \un{in}^3 \pd \ea \] The baker uses \(0.1\) gram of liquid chocolate per cubic inch of cake, so the baker adds \(14.14 \times 0.1\) \(= \boxed{1.414 \un{g}}\) of chocolate.
EXERCISE 16
The surface of a pond is the region enclosed by the curves \[f(x) = 5 \cos \frac{\pi x}{4} \and g(x) = x^2 \cma\] where \(x\) is measured in feet. At any \(x,\) the pond's depth of water is given by the function \(7 - x.\) Calculate the pond's volume.
SOLUTION The region bounded by the two curves is in the first and second quadrants. Using a graphing calculator, we find that the two curves \(f\) and \(g\) intersect when \(x = -1.449\) and \(x = 1.449.\) If we take a cross section perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis, then we attain a rectangle of base \(f(x) - g(x)\) and height \((7 - x).\) Its cross-sectional area is therefore \(A(x) = (7 - x)[f(x) - g(x)].\) So the pond's volume is given by integrating this function from \(x = -1.449\) and \(x = 1.449,\) as follows: \[ \ba V &= \int_{-1.449}^{1.449} (7 - x)[f(x) - g(x)] \di x \nl &= \int_{-1.449}^{1.449} (7 - x) \par{5 \cos \frac{\pi x}{4} - x^2} \di x \nl &\approx \boxed{66.713 \un{ft}^3} \ea \]
EXERCISE 17
The circle \(x^2 + y^2 = 4\) is the base of a solid whose cross sections perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis are equilateral triangles. Find the solid's volume.
SOLUTION The circle has vertices at \((0, -2)\) and \((0, 2).\) Because we know the identity of cross sections perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis, we express all quantities in terms of \(y.\) Solving for \(x\) in the equation of the circle, we have \[x = \pm \sqrt{4 - y^2} \pd\] At each \(y,\) the cross section to the solid perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis is an equilateral triangle of side length \(2x.\) The triangle's height is therefore \((2x)\sqrt{3}/2\) \(= x \sqrt 3,\) where \(x\) is a function of \(y.\) So at each \(y,\) the solid's cross-sectional area perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis is \[A(y) = \tfrac{1}{2} (x) (x \sqrt 3) = \frac{\sqrt 3}{2} x^2 = \frac{\sqrt 3}{2} \par{4 - y^2} \pd\] Accordingly, the solid's volume is given by integrating \(A(y)\) from \(y = -2\) to \(y = 2,\) as follows (we exploit symmetry about \(y = 0\) to simplify the calculation): \[ \ba V &= \int_{-2}^2 A(y) \di y = \frac{\sqrt 3}{2} \int_{-2}^2 \par{4 - y^2} \di y \nl &= \sqrt 3 \int_0^2 \par{4 - y^2} \di y \nl &= \sqrt 3 \par{4y - \tfrac{1}{3} y^3} \intEval_0^2 \nl &= \boxed{\frac{16 \sqrt 3}{3}} \approx 9.238 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 18
A solid's base is the region bounded by \(y = x^2 - 1\) under the \(x\)-axis. At any \(y,\) slicing the solid perpendicularly to the \(y\)-axis yields a semicircle. Find the solid's volume.
SOLUTION
Although a region may lie below the \(x\)-axis, volume is never negative. The procedure for calculating volume is irrespective of the quadrants in which the bounded region lies.
Because the shapes of cross sections perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis are known, we express all quantities in terms of \(y.\) We rewrite \(y = x^2 - 1\) as \(x = \pm \sqrt{y + 1}.\) The enclosed region is under the \(x\)-axis, meaning it's bounded above by the line \(y = 0.\) And the parabola has a vertex of \((0, -1),\) so the region is bounded below by the line \(y = -1.\) At any \(y,\) the cross section perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis is a semicircle whose diameter is \(2x.\) Its radius is therefore \(x,\) so its cross-sectional area is given by \[A(y) = \tfrac{1}{2} \pi x^2 = \tfrac{1}{2} \pi \par{\pm \sqrt{y + 1}}^2 = \frac{\pi}{2} (y + 1) \pd\] We integrate this function from \(y = -1\) to \(y = 0\) to attain the volume, as follows: \[ \ba V &= \int_{-1}^0 A(y) \di y = \frac{\pi}{2} \int_{-1}^0 (y + 1) \di y \nl &= \frac{\pi}{2} \par{\tfrac{1}{2} y^2 + y} \intEval_{-1}^0 \nl &= \boxed{\frac{\pi}{4}} \approx 0.785 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 19
Suppose that the triangle enclosed by the graph of \(y = \abs x\) and the line \(y = 5\) is the base of a solid whose cross sections parallel to the \(x\)-axis are right isosceles triangles whose hypotenuses lie in the enclosed region. Calculate the solid's volume.
SOLUTION At each \(y,\) the cross section to the solid \(S\) perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis is a right isosceles triangle whose hypotenuse has length \(2x = 2y.\) Thus, the triangle's legs each have length \(2y/\sqrt 2\) \(= y \sqrt 2.\) So at each \(y,\) the cross-sectional area of the solid is \[A(y) = \tfrac{1}{2} \par{y \sqrt 2}^2 = y^2 \pd\] The volume of \(S\) is given by integrating \(A(y)\) from \(y = 0\) to \(y = 5,\) as follows: \[ \ba V &= \int_0^5 A(y) \di y = \int_0^5 y^2 \di y \nl &= \tfrac{1}{3} y^3 \intEval_0^5 = \boxed{\tfrac{125}{3}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 20
The region bounded by the curve \(x = y^4\) and the line \(x = 1\) is the base of a solid whose cross sections perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis are squares. Find the solid's volume.
SOLUTION Because the solid's cross sections perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis are known, we want to express all quantities in terms of \(x.\) Let's rewrite \(x = y^4\) as \(y = \pm \sqrt[4]{x}.\) The enclosed region is bounded above by \(y = \sqrt[4] x,\) below by \(y = -\sqrt[4] x,\) and on the sides by the lines \(x = 0\) and \(x = 1.\) At each \(x,\) the cross section to the solid perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is a square of length \(2y = 2 \sqrt[4]{x}.\) So its cross-sectional area is \[A(x) = \par{2y}^2 = \par{2 \sqrt[4] x}^2 = 4 \sqrt x \pd\] Hence, the solid's volume is given by integrating \(A(x)\) from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 1,\) as follows: \[ \ba V &= \int_0^1 4 \sqrt x \di x \nl &= \tfrac{8}{3} x^{3/2} \intEval_0^1 = \boxed{\tfrac{8}{3}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 21
Determine the volume of a hemisphere whose base is the region enclosed by the circle \(x^2 + y^2 = 25.\)
SOLUTION A hemisphere is half of a sphere. Thus, its cross sections perpendicular to either the \(x\)-axis or \(y\)-axis are semicircles. Let's proceed with calculations such that the cross sections are perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis. (Or we could model the cross sections to be perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis; either choice gives the same volume.) We solve for \(y\) to find \(y = \pm \sqrt{25 - x^2}.\) The diameter of a semicircle is \(2y,\) from which its radius is \(y,\) so its area is \[A(x) = \tfrac{1}{2} \pi y^2 = \frac{\pi}{2} \par{25 - x^2} \pd\] The circle \(x^2 + y^2 = 25\) has vertices at \((-5, 0)\) and \((5, 0);\) hence, to attain the volume \(V\) we integrate \(A(x)\) from \(x = -5\) to \(x = 5.\) Doing soâ€”and because the integrand is even, we exploit symmetry to simplify calculationsâ€”we see \[ \ba V &= \int_{-5}^5 A(x) \di x = \frac{\pi}{2} \int_{-5}^5 \par{25 - x^2} \di x \nl &= \pi \int_0^5 \par{25 - x^2} \di x \nl &= \pi \par{25x - \tfrac{1}{3} x^3} \intEval_0^5 \nl &= \pi \par{125 - \tfrac{125}{3}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{250 \pi}{3}} \approx 261.799 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 22
The region enclosed by \(y = 1/(x^2 + 2),\) the \(x\)-axis, the \(y\)-axis, and the line \(x = 1\) is the base of a solid. At each \(x,\) the cross section to the solid perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is a rectangle of height \(3x.\) Calculate the solid's volume.
SOLUTION At any \(x,\) the cross section to the solid is a rectangle of base \(1/(x^2 + 2)\) and height \(3x.\) Its cross-sectional area is therefore \[A(x) = \frac{3x}{x^2 + 2} \pd\] We now integrate this function from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 1\) to attain the volume: \[V = \int_0^1 \frac{3x}{x^2 + 2} \di x \pd\] To evaluate this integral, we perform a substitution with \(u = x^2 + 2.\) (See Section 4.4 to review the procedure of the Substitution Rule.) Then \(\dd u = 2x \di x.\) When \(x = 0,\) \(u = 2;\) when \(x = 1,\) \(u = 3.\) So the integral becomes \[ \ba V &= \tfrac{3}{2} \int_2^3 \frac{1}{u} \di u = \tfrac{3}{2} \ln \abs u \intEval_2^3 \nl &= \boxed{\tfrac{3}{2} \ln \tfrac{3}{2}} \approx 0.608 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 23
Let \(R\) be the region bounded by the parabola \(y = x^2,\) the \(x\)-axis, the \(y\)-axis, and the line \(x = k,\) where \(k\) is a positive constant. Let \(S\) be the solid whose base is region \(R\) and whose cross sections perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis are squares. Find the value of \(k\) for which the area of \(R\) equals the volume of \(S.\)
SOLUTION The enclosed region \(R\) has area given by \[A = \int_0^k x^2 \di x \pd\] Conversely, the volume of the solid \(S\) has volume \[V = \int_0^k \par{x^2}^2 \di x = \int_0^k x^4 \di x \pd\] We equate \(A\) and \(V,\) finding \[ \ba \int_0^k x^2 \di x &= \int_0^k x^4 \di x \nl \tfrac{1}{3} k^3 &= \tfrac{1}{5} k^5 \nl \implies k &= \boxed{\sqrt{\frac{5}{3}}} \approx 1.291 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 24
Consider the family of functions \[f(x) = \frac{1}{(kx)^2 + 1} \cma\] where \(k\) is a positive constant. The region under the graph of \(y = f(x)\) and above the \(x\)-axis from \(x = 1\) to \(x = 2\) is the base of a solid whose cross sections perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis are rectangles of uniform height \(k.\) For what value of \(k\) is the solid's volume maximized?
SOLUTION At each \(x,\) the cross section perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is a rectangle of base \(f(x)\) and height \(k.\) Its cross-sectional area is therefore \[A(x) = k f(x) = \frac{k}{(kx)^2 + 1} \pd\] The solid's volume is given by integrating this function from \(x = 1\) to \(x = 2,\) as follows: \[ \ba V &= \int_1^2 \frac{k}{(kx)^2 + 1} \di x \nl &= \atan kx \intEval_1^2 = \atan 2k - \atan k \pd \ea \] (We mentally performed the substitution \(u = kx\) to attain this antiderivative.) To calculate the value of \(k\) for which \(V\) is maximized, we differentiate \(V\) with respect to \(k\) and locate its critical points. (See Section 3.6 to review the process of optimization.) Doing so, we set \(\textderiv{V}{k} = 0\) and solve for \(k.\) We find the derivative to be \[ \ba \deriv{V}{k} &= \frac{2}{(2k)^2 + 1} - \frac{1}{k^2 + 1} \nl &= \frac{2k^2 + 2 - 4k^2 - 1}{\par{4k^2 + 1} \par{k^2 + 1}} \nl &= \frac{-2k^2 + 1}{\par{4k^2 + 1} \par{k^2 + 1}} \pd \ea \] So we see \[\frac{-2k^2 + 1}{\par{4k^2 + 1} \par{k^2 + 1}} = 0 \iffArrow -2k^2 + 1 = 0 \iffArrow k = \frac{1}{\sqrt 2} \pd\] This value corresponds to a relative maximum of \(V\) because \(\textderiv{V}{k}\) changes sign from positive to negative at \(k = 1/\sqrt 2.\) Since \(V = 0\) when \(k = 0\) and \[\lim_{k \to \infty} V = \lim_{k \to \infty} \par{\atan 2k - \atan k} = \frac{\pi}{2} - \frac{\pi}{2} = 0 \cma\] the relative maximum at \(k = 1/\sqrt 2\) must be the location of the absolute maximum of \(V.\) So the volume is maximized for \(k = \boxed{1/\sqrt 2}.\)
