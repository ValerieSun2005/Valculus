3.7 Exercise Solutions: Applications of Differentiation in Economics

EXERCISE 1
Explain the Law of Demand.
SOLUTION The Law of Demand explains an inverse relationship between a good's cost and the number of people who demand that good. Simply put, a business can expect more sales if it lowers prices.
EXERCISE 2
A firm's cost function is modeled by \[C(x) = \tfrac{1}{4} x^2 + 5x + 70 \pd\] At a production level of \(10\) units, calculate the
  1. total cost
  2. marginal cost
  3. average cost per unit
SOLUTION The cost function represents the cost to produce \(x\) units.
  1. The total cost at \(x = 10\) is \[ \ba C(10) &= \tfrac{1}{4} (10)^2 + 5(10) + 70 \nl &= \boxed{\$145} \ea \]
  2. The marginal cost function is \(C'(x).\) Differentiating gives \[C'(x) = \tfrac{1}{2} x + 5 \pd\] So the marginal cost at \(x = 10\) is \[ \ba C'(10) &= \tfrac{1}{2} (10) + 5 \nl &= \boxed{\$10} \ea \] In words, we estimate that the \(11\)th unit will cost the firm \(\$10.\)
  3. The average cost per unit at \(x = 10\) is \[ \ba \overline C(10) &= \frac{C(10)}{10} \nl &= \frac{\$145}{10} \nl &= \boxed{\$14.50} \ea \]
EXERCISE 3
You are a business consultant for several firms, and you model each firm's marginal profit function. In each scenario, do you recommend that the firm expand its output level?
  1. A firm has a marginal profit of \(\$70\)
  2. A firm has a marginal profit of \(-\$40\)
SOLUTION
  1. Yes. Since the marginal profit is positive, the firm enjoys a monetary gain from producing an additional unit.
  2. No. Because marginal profit is negative, the firm takes a loss by producing an additional unit.
EXERCISE 4
For
\(\ds p(x) = 118 - x \cmaa C(x) = \tfrac{1}{2} x^2 + 6x + 14 \scoll x = 8\)
given the demand and cost functions, calculate the profit and average cost per unit at the given output level.
SOLUTION The revenue is \[ \ba R(8) &= 8 \, p(8) \nl &= 8 \par{118 - 8} \nl &= \$880 \pd \ea \] The cost is \[C(8) = \tfrac{1}{2} (8)^2 + 6(8) + 14 = \$94 \pd\] Hence, the profit is \[ \ba P(8) &= R(8) - C(8) \nl &= \$880 - \$94 = \boxed{\$786} \ea \] The average cost per unit is \[ \ba \overline C(8) &= \frac{C(8)}{8} \nl &= \frac{\$94}{8} = \boxed{\$11.75} \ea \]
EXERCISE 5
For
\(\ds p(x) = 16 - 2x^2 \cmaa C(x) = \sqrt{x^3} + x + 10 \scoll x = 4\)
given the demand and cost functions, calculate the profit and average cost per unit at the given output level.
SOLUTION The revenue is \[ \ba R(4) &= 4 \, p(4) \nl &= 4 \parbr{16 - 2(4)^2} \nl &= -\$64 \pd \ea \] The cost is \[C(4) = \sqrt{4^3} + 4 + 10 = \$22 \pd\] Hence, the profit is \[ \ba P(4) &= R(4) - C(4) \nl &= -\$64 - \$22 = \boxed{-\$86} \ea \] (The company is therefore taking a loss.) The average cost per unit is \[ \ba \overline C(4) &= \frac{C(4)}{4} \nl &= \frac{\$22}{4} = \boxed{\$5.50} \ea \]
EXERCISE 6
For
\(\ds p(x) = 49 - x^2 \cmaa C(x) = \tfrac{1}{6} x^3 + x + 5 \scoll x = 3\)
given the demand and cost functions, calculate the profit and average cost per unit at the given output level.
SOLUTION The company's revenue is \[ \ba R(3) &= 3 \, p(3) \nl &= 3 \parbr{49 - (3)^2} \nl &= \$120 \pd \ea \] The cost is \[C(3) = \tfrac{1}{6} (3)^3 + (3) + 5 = \$12.50 \pd\] Hence, the profit is \[ \ba P(3) &= R(3) - C(3) \nl &= \$120 - \$12.50 = \boxed{\$107.50} \ea \] The average cost per unit is \[ \ba \overline C(3) &= \frac{C(3)}{3} \nl &= \frac{\$12.50}{3} \nl &= \frac{\$ 25}{6} \approx \boxed{\$4.17} \ea \]
EXERCISE 7
For
\(\ds p(x) = 30e^{-x/5} \cmaa C(x) = \tfrac{3}{8} e^{x/7} + 30 \scoll x = 5\)
given the demand and cost functions, calculate the profit and average cost per unit at the given output level.
SOLUTION The company's revenue is \[ \ba R(5) &= 5 \, p(5) \nl &= 5 \par{30e^{-5/5}} \nl &= \frac{150}{e} \approx \$55.18 \pd \ea \] The cost is \[C(5) = \tfrac{3}{8} e^{5/7} + 30 \approx \$30.77 \pd\] Hence, the profit is \[ \ba P(3) &= R(5) - C(5) \nl &\approx \$55.18 - \$30.77 = \boxed{\$24.41} \ea \] The average cost per unit is \[ \ba \overline C(5) &= \frac{C(5)}{5} \nl &\approx \frac{\$30.77}{5} \nl &\approx \boxed{\$6.15} \ea \]
EXERCISE 8
A business models its demand function to be \(p(x) = 9 - x^2\) and its cost function to be \(C(x) = x^2 + x + 8.\) Find the profit function and graph it. Should the business should shut down?
SOLUTION Yes. The revenue function is \[ \ba R(x) &= x \, p(x) \nl &= x \par{9 - x^2} \nl &= - x^3 + 9x \pd \ea \] So the profit function is \[ \ba P(x) &= R(x) - C(x) \nl &= -x^3 + 9x - \par{x^2 + x + 8} \nl &= -x^3 - x^2 + 8x - 8 \pd \ea \] For \(x \geq 0,\) \(P(x) \lt 0.\) Thus, the firm is always losing money; it never attains any economic benefit from production. So the business should shut down.
EXERCISE 9
For
\[P'(x) = -5x^2 + 45x + 50\]
a firm's marginal profit is given; determine how many units the firm should produce.
SOLUTION The firm should stop production once its marginal profit is negative. We factor the quadratic function as \[P'(x) = -5(x - 10)(x + 1) \pd\] Note that \(P'(x) = 0\) when \(x = -1\) and \(x = 10.\) The domain of \(P'(x)\) in context is \(\{x \mid x \geq 0\},\) so we disregard the zero \(x = -1.\) For \(0 \leq x \lt 10,\) \(P' \gt 0;\) but for \(x \gt 10,\) \(P' \lt 0.\) Thus, the firm should produce \(\boxed{10}\) units.
EXERCISE 10
For
\[\ds P'(x) = -\tfrac{1}{4} x^3 + x^2 - 2x + 8\]
a firm's marginal profit is given; determine how many units the firm should produce.
SOLUTION The firm should stop production once its marginal profit is negative. We factor the cubic function by grouping, as follows: \[ \ba P'(x) &= -\tfrac{1}{4} x^2 \, (x - 4) - 2(x - 4) \nl &= (x - 4) \par{-\tfrac{1}{4} x^2 - 2} \pd \ea \] The domain of \(P'(x)\) in context is \(\{x \mid x \geq 0\}.\) The quadratic expression \(\par{-\tfrac{1}{4} x^2 - 2}\) has no zeros, so the only solution to \(P'(x) = 0\) is \(x = 4.\) Note that \(P' \gt 0\) for \(0 \leq x \lt 4\) and \(P' \lt 0\) for \(x \gt 4.\) Hence, the firm should produce \(\boxed 4\) units.
EXERCISE 11
Consider the cost function \(C(x) = 4x^2 + 16x/(x + 1).\) Determine the production level at which the average cost per unit is minimized.
SOLUTION The average cost function is \[ \ba \overline C(x) &= \frac{C(x)}{x} \nl &= 4x + \frac{16}{x + 1} \pd \ea \] Differentiating gives \[\overline C \, '(x) = 4 - \frac{16}{(x + 1)^2} \pd\] The function \(\overline C \, '\) has a critical point at \(x\) if \(\overline C \, '(x) = 0\) or if \(\overline C \, '(x)\) is undefined while \(\overline C(x)\) is defined. Note that \(\overline C \, '(-1)\) is undefined, but \(C(-1)\) is also undefined. So \(x = -1\) is not a critical number. Instead, we solve \(\overline C \, '(x) = 0\) as follows: \[ \ba 4 - \frac{16}{(x + 1)^2} &= 0 \nl 4(x + 1)^2 &= 16 \nl (x + 1)^2 &= 4 \nl \implies x &= -3 \cma x = 1 \pd \ea \] We disregard the solution \(x = -3\) since the domain of \(\overline C(x)\) in context is \(\{x \mid x \gt 0\}.\) Note that \(\overline C \, ' \lt 0\) for \(0 \lt x \lt 1\) and \(\overline C \, ' \gt 0\) for \(x \gt 1.\) Thus, the First-Derivative Test states that \(\overline C\) has a relative minimum at \(1.\) Note that \(\overline C(0) = 16,\) \(\overline C(1) = 12,\) and \(\lim_{x \to \infty} \overline C(x) = \infty.\) Hence, \(\overline C\) has an absolute minimum at \(1.\) The requested production level is therefore \(\boxed 1\) unit.
EXERCISE 12
A candy store delivers boxes of candy for \(\$40\) each. The cost, in dollars, of delivering \(x\) boxes of candy is \[C(x) = \tfrac{1}{3} x^3 + \tfrac{3}{2} x^2 + 20 \pd \] How many boxes should the store deliver to maximize its profit?
SOLUTION The price is a constant \(p(x) = 40,\) so the revenue function is \(R(x) = 40x.\) Then the profit function is \[ \ba P(x) &= R(x) - C(x) \nl &= 40x - \par{\tfrac{1}{3} x^3 + \tfrac{3}{2} x^2 + 20} \nl &= -\tfrac{1}{3} x^3 - \tfrac{3}{2} x^2 + 40x - 20 \pd \ea \] Differentiating \(P\) to locate its critical numbers, we find \[ \ba P'(x) = -x^2 - 3x + 40 &= 0 \nl -(x - 5)(x + 8) &= 0 \nl \implies x &= -8 \cma x = 5 \pd \ea \] The domain of \(P(x)\) in context is \(\{x \mid x \geq 0\},\) so we ignore the solution \(x = -8.\) For \(0 \leq x \lt 5,\) \(P' \gt 0;\) for \(x \gt 5,\) \(P' \lt 0.\) Thus, \(x = 5\) is the location of a relative maximum of \(P\) by the First-Derivative Test. Note that \(P(0) = -20,\) \(P(5) = 605/6,\) and \(\lim_{x \to \infty} P(x) = -\infty.\) Thus, \(P\) has an absolute maximum at \(x = 5.\) The store should therefore should deliver \(\boxed{5}\) boxes of candy to maximize its profit.
EXERCISE 13
A company sells water bottles for \(\$8\) each. The company's cost function, in dollars, of manufacturing \(x\) bottles is \[C(x) = 10 + 2x + \tfrac{1}{2} \sqrt{x^3} \pd\] How many bottles should the company sell to maximize its profit?
SOLUTION Profit is the difference between revenue and cost. The company's price is a constant \(p(x) = 8,\) so its revenue function is \(R(x) = 8x\) and its profit function is \[ \ba P(x) &= R(x) - C(x) \nl &= 8x - \par{10 + 2x + \tfrac{1}{2} \sqrt{x^3}} \nl &= 6x - 10 - \tfrac{1}{2} \sqrt{x^3} \pd \ea \] Note that \(\sqrt{x^3}\) \(= x^{3/2}.\) Differentiating gives \[P'(x) = 6 - \tfrac{3}{4} \sqrt x \pd\] We locate the critical numbers of \(P\) by solving \(P'(x) = 0 \col\) \[ \ba 6 - \tfrac{3}{4} \sqrt x &= 0 \nl \sqrt x &= 8 \nl \implies x &= 64 \pd \ea \] The domain of \(P(x)\) is \(\{x \mid x \geq 0\}.\) For \(0 \leq x \lt 64,\) \(P' \gt 0;\) for \(x \gt 64,\) \(P' \lt 0.\) Thus, \(x = 64\) corresponds to a relative maximum of \(P\) by the First-Derivative Test. Note that \(P(0) = -10,\) \(P(64) = 118,\) and \(\lim_{x \to \infty} P(x) = -\infty.\) Thus, \(P\) has an absolute maximum at \(64.\) So the company should manufacture \(\boxed{64}\) water bottles.
EXERCISE 14
A fisherman sells fish for \(\$5\) each at a local farmer's market. His cost function for supplying \(x\) fish per day is given by \[C(x) = \tfrac{1}{200} x^2 + x + 200 \pd\] How many fish should the fisherman sell per day to maximize his profit?
SOLUTION Since the price is a constant \(p(x) = 5,\) the revenue function is \(R(x) = 5x.\) Profit is the difference between revenue and cost: \[ \ba P(x) &= R(x) - C(x) \nl &= 5x - \par{\tfrac{1}{200} x^2 + x + 200} \nl &= -\tfrac{1}{200} x^2 + 4x - 200 \pd \ea \] Differentiating and finding the critical numbers, we see \[ \ba P'(x) = -\tfrac{1}{100} x + 4 &= 0 \nl \implies x &= 400 \pd \ea \] The domain of \(P(x)\) in context is \(\{x \mid x \geq 0\}.\) For \(0 \leq x \lt 400,\) \(P' \gt 0;\) for \(x \gt 400,\) \(P' \lt 0.\) So by the First-Derivative Test, \(P\) attains a relative maximum at \(x = 400.\) Note that \(P(0) = -200,\) \(P(400) = 600,\) and \(\lim_{x \to \infty} P(x) = -\infty.\) Thus, \(P\) has an absolute maximum at \(x = 400.\) To maximize his profit, the fisherman should therefore sell \(\boxed{400}\) fish per day.
EXERCISE 15
Each night, a restaurant receives \(100\) orders for pumpkin pie if they are priced at \(\$10\) each. The restaurant predicts that \(4\) fewer people will order the pie for each dollar the price is increased. At what price should the restaurant sell the pie to maximize revenue?
SOLUTION Revenue is the product of price and quantity. Let \(s\) be the number of dollars by which the restaurant raises the price of its pumpkin pie. The price is then \((10 + s),\) and the quantity is \((100 - 4s).\) We have \[ \ba R(s) &= (10 + s)(100 - 4s) \nl &= -4s^2 + 60s + 1000 \pd \ea \] Differentiating gives \[R'(s) = -8s + 60 \pd\] The solution to \(R'(s) = 0\) is \(s = 60/8\) = \(7.5.\) By the First-Derivative Test, this value corresponds to a relative maximum of \(R\) because at \(s = 7.5,\) \(R'\) changes sign from positive to negative. Note that \[\lim_{s \to -\infty} R(s) = -\infty \and \lim_{s \to \infty} R(s) = -\infty \pd\] Thus, \(s = 7.5\) must be the location of the absolute maximum of \(R.\) The restaurant therefore should raise the pie's price by \(\boxed{\$7.50}.\)
EXERCISE 16
An artist estimates that \(500\) people will attend his concert if he charges \(\$50\) per ticket. For every \($3\) increase in the ticket price, the artist estimates that \(8\) fewer people will attend. How much should the artist charge per ticket to maximize his revenue?
SOLUTION Revenue is the product of price and quantity. If \(s\) is the number of dollars by which the artist raises a ticket's price, then the price is \((50 + 3s)\) and the quantity is \((500 - 8s).\) We therefore have \[ \ba R(s) &= (50 + 3s)(500 - 8s) \nl &= -24s^2 + 1100s + 25000 \pd \ea \] Differentiating, we find \[R'(s) = -48s + 1100 \pd\] Solving \(R'(s) = 0\) gives \(s = 1100/48.\) By the First-Derivative Test, this value corresponds to a relative maximum of \(R\) because at \(s = 1100/48,\) \(R'\) changes sign from positive to negative. Note that \[\lim_{s \to -\infty} R(s) = -\infty \and \lim_{s \to \infty} R(s) = -\infty \pd\] So the absolute maximum of \(R\) occurs when \(s = 1100/48 \approx 22.92.\) Thus, the artist should charge \(\$50 + \$22.92 =\) \(\boxed{\$72.92}\) per ticket to maximize his revenue.
EXERCISE 17
An electronics store sells \(20\) computers per week if they are priced at \(\$500\) each. For every \(\$10\) increase in the computer's price, the store sells \(2\) fewer computers per week. At what price should each computer be sold to maximize revenue?
SOLUTION Revenue is the product of price and quantity. If \(s\) is the number of dollars by which the store raises each computer's price, then the price is \((500 + 10s)\) and the quantity is \((20 - 2s).\) We therefore have \[ \ba R(s) &= (500 + 10s)(20 - 2s) \nl &= -20s^2 - 800s + 10000 \pd \ea \] Differentiating gives \[R'(s) = -40s - 800 \pd\] The solution to \(R'(s) = 0\) is \(s = -20.\) By the First-Derivative Test, this value corresponds to a relative maximum of \(R\) because at \(s = -20,\) \(R'\) changes sign from positive to negative. Note that \[\lim_{s \to -\infty} R(s) = -\infty \and \lim_{s \to \infty} R(s) = -\infty \pd\] So the absolute maximum of \(R\) occurs when \(s = -20.\) Thus, the computers' prices should be lowered by \(\$20,\) for a price of \(\boxed{\$480}\) per computer.
EXERCISE 18
Prove that if the average cost is minimized, then the marginal cost equals the average cost.
SOLUTION Let \(C\) be a differentiable cost function. Then the average cost function is \(\overline C(x)\) \(= C(x)/x.\) Differentiating gives \[ \overline C \, '(x) = \frac{x C'(x) - C(x)}{x^2} \pd \] Now we locate the critical numbers of \(\overline C.\) [Note that \(x = 0\) is not a critical number, since both \(\overline C(0)\) and \(\overline C \, '(0)\) are undefined.] We solve \(\overline C \, '(x) = 0\) to see \[ \ba \frac{x C'(x) - C(x)}{x^2} &= 0 \nl x C'(x) - C(x) &= 0 \nl C'(x) &= \frac{C(x)}{x} \nl C'(x) &= \overline C(x) \cma \ea \] as requested.
EXERCISE 19
Prove that a firm's profit is maximized if its marginal revenue and marginal cost are equal.
SOLUTION Profit is the difference between revenue and cost: \[P(x) = R(x) - C(x) \pd\] Differentiating both sides gives \[P'(x) = R'(x) - C'(x) \pd\] The function \(P\) has a critical point when \(P'(x) = 0,\) that is, when \(R'(x) = C'(x).\) Because the cost function is increasing, \(C'(x)\) is positive. Since \(R(x) = x \, p(x),\) where \(p\) is the decreasing demand function, we see \[R'(x) = p(x) + x \, p'(x) \cma\] which must eventually be negative because \(p'(x) \lt 0.\) Thus, \(R(x)\) must eventually be decreasing. Accordingly, \(P(x) \to -\infty\) as \(x \to \infty.\) So \(P\) needs to have an absolute maximum, which must occur at the critical point, when \(R'(x) = C'(x).\) Hence, a firm maximizes its profit by equating its marginal revenue and marginal cost.
EXERCISE 20
Quadratic cost functions are highly applicable in economics. A manufacturing plant decides to model its cost using a quadratic function. The plant's overhead costs—the costs not associated with production, such as in purchasing a location—are \(\$500.\) If the total cost to produce \(100\) units is \(\$700,\) and the total cost to produce \(200\) units is \(\$950,\) then find the identity of the plant's cost function. Use this function to predict the total cost to produce \(300\) units.
SOLUTION The general form of a quadratic function is \[C(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \pd\] Our goal is to fit the parameters \(a,\) \(b,\) and \(c\) to satisfy \(C(0) = 500,\) \(C(100) = 700,\) and \(C(200) = 950.\)

Quadratic Fitting Substituting \(C(0) = 500\) shows \[ \ba C(0) = a(0)^2 + b(0) + c &= 500 \nl \implies c &= 500 \pd \ea \] Then substituting \(C(100) = 700\) yields \begin{align} C(100) = a(100)^2 + b(100) + 500 &= 700 \nonum \nl 10000a + 100b &= 200 \pd \label{eq:C-1} \end{align} Likewise, substituting \(C(200) = 950\) gives \begin{align} C(200) = a(200)^2 + b(200) + 500 &= 950 \nonum \nl 40000a + 200b &= 450 \pd \label{eq:C-2} \end{align} Multiplying \(\eqref{eq:C-1}\) by \(2\) and subtracting it from \(\eqref{eq:C-2},\) we get \[ \ba 40000a + 200b - 2(10000a + 100b) &= 450 - 2(200) \nl 20000a &= 50 \nl \implies a &= 0.0025 \pd \ea \] (See Section 0.3 to review solving systems of linear equations.) To solve for \(b,\) we substitute \(a = 0.0025\) back into either equation of the system—for example, into \(\eqref{eq:C-1} \col\) \[ \ba 10000(0.0025) + 100b &= 200 \nl \implies b &= 1.75 \pd \ea \] Hence, the identity of the cost function is \[C(x) = \boxed{0.0025x^2 + 1.75x + 500}\]

Predicted Cost The predicted cost of producing \(300\) units is \[C(300) = 0.0025(300)^2 + 1.75(300) + 500 = \boxed{\$1250}\]
