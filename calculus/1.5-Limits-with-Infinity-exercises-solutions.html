1.5 Exercise Solutions — Limits with Infinity

EXERCISE 1
Define a vertical asymptote and a horizontal asymptote.
SOLUTION The vertical line \(x = p\) is a vertical asymptote of a function \(f\) if the graph of \(f(x)\) approaches \(\pm \infty\) from one or both sides of the line \(x = p.\) Conversely, a horizontal asymptote is a horizontal line to which \(f(x)\) approaches as \(x \to \infty\) or \(x \to -\infty.\) A graph may have up to two horizontal asymptotes, but a graph can have infinitely many vertical asymptotes.
EXERCISE 2
Answer the following questions about the graph of \(y = f(x)\) in Figure 8.
  1. Find \(\lim_{x \to 1^-} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to 1^+} f(x).\)
  2. Where does the graph of \(f\) have a vertical asymptote?
  3. Determine the horizontal asymptotes of the graph of \(f.\)
  4. Describe the end behavior of \(f(x).\)
SOLUTION
  1. From the left of \(x = 1,\) the graph of \(f\) runs down toward negative infinity. Hence, \(\lim_{x \to 1^-} f(x) = \boxed{-\infty}.\) Conversely, from the right of \(x = 1\) the graph runs up to positive infinity. Thus, \(\lim_{x \to 1^+} f(x) = \boxed{\infty}.\)
  2. The function \(f\) has a vertical asymptote at \(\boxed{x = 1}\) because the graph approaches \(\pm \infty\) from at least one side of the line \(x = 1.\)
  3. The graph of \(f\) has a horizontal asymptote if \(f(x)\) approaches a fixed value as \(x \to \infty\) or \(x \to -\infty.\) As \(x \to \infty,\) \(f(x) \to -3;\) as \(x \to -\infty,\) \(f(x) \to 2.\) Thus, the horizontal asymptotes of \(f\) are \(\boxed{y = -3}\) and \(\boxed{y = 2}.\)
  4. The end behavior of \(f\) describes how \(f(x)\) behaves as \(x \to \infty\) and as \(x \to -\infty.\) The end behavior is as follows: \(f(x)\) approaches \(-3\) as \(x\) approaches infinity and \(2\) as \(x\) approaches negative infinity.
EXERCISE 3
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x - 1}{x + 3}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION To evaluate the limit of a rational expression as its input approaches infinity, we consider the limit of solely the highest-degree terms. Hence, we see \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x - 1}{x + 3} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x}{x} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3}{1} \nl &= \boxed 3 \ea \] Alternatively, you could divide each term by the overall highest-degree term, \(x,\) and take the limit to attain the same result.
EXERCISE 4
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{1 - x^2}{2x^2 + x}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION As \(x \to -\infty,\) only the highest-degree terms are relevant. The limit therefore becomes \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{-x^2}{2x^2} &= \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{-1}{2} \nl &= \boxed{-\frac{1}{2}} \ea \] Or you could divide each term by the overall highest-degree term, \(x^2,\) to evaluate the limit to get the same result \(-1/2.\)
EXERCISE 5
For
\[\ds \lim_{u \to \infty} \frac{4 - u^3}{8 + 2u^4}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION As \(u \to \infty,\) the value of the rational expression is driven only by the highest-degree terms; we neglect all the lower-order terms. The limit is therefore equivalent to \[ \ba \lim_{u \to \infty} \frac{-u^3}{2u^4} &= \lim_{u \to \infty} \frac{-1}{2u} \nl &= \boxed 0 \ea \] Alternatively, we could have immediately noticed that the degree of the denominator is greater than the degree of the numerator; when this is true, the rational expression approaches \(0\) as \(u \to \infty.\) You could also divide everything by the overall highest-degree term, \(u^4,\) and take the limit to attain the same result.
EXERCISE 6
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{4x^3 + 2x^2 - 3}{1 - x^2}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION This limit is equivalent to a limit in which the lower-degree terms are stripped—namely, \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{4x^3}{-x^2} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} -4x \nl &= \boxed{-\infty} \ea \] This result is logical: The degree of the numerator is greater than the degree of the denominator. Hence, the numerator grows faster than the denominator, driving the rational expression toward an infinite value.
EXERCISE 7
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x - \sqrt x}{4x + 2}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION The term \(\sqrt x\) seems unorthodox, but you can rewrite it as \(x^{1/2}.\) We remove all the lower-degree terms from the rational expression; we have \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x}{4x} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{4} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{1}{4}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 8
For
\[\ds \lim_{t \to \infty} \sqrt{\frac{t^8 - 4t^3 + t - 3}{9t^8 + 4}}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION Although the entire expression is under a square root, we still consider only the highest-degree terms. The limit is equivalent to \[\lim_{t \to \infty} \sqrt{\frac{t^8}{9t^8}} = \lim_{t \to \infty} \sqrt{\frac{1}{9}} = \boxed{\frac{1}{3}}\] You could also divide each term by \(\sqrt{t^8};\) doing so gives the same result.
EXERCISE 9
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to -\infty} \sqrt{\frac{x^8 + 3x^2 - 5}{4x^8 + x - 2}}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION Even though the entire expression is under a square root, we still consider only the highest-degree terms. The limit is equivalent to \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} \sqrt{\frac{x^8}{4x^8}} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \sqrt{\frac{1}{4}} = \boxed{\frac{1}{2}}\] You could also divide each term by \(\sqrt{x^8};\) doing so gives the same result.
EXERCISE 10
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{\sqrt{x^2 + 11x - 3}}{6x - 4}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION Despite the square root, we consider only the highest-degree terms. The limit is equivalent to \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{\sqrt{x^2}}{6x} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{\abs x}{6x} \pd\] Since \(x \to -\infty,\) we assume \(x \lt 0\) and so we rewrite \(\abs x\) as \(-x\) (because \(-x \gt 0\) if \(x\) is negative). The limit therefore becomes \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{-x}{6x} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{-1}{6} = \boxed{-\frac{1}{6}}\]
EXERCISE 11
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\sqrt{x^6 + 7x - 3}}{5x^3 - 8x}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION Taking only the highest-degree terms, the limit becomes \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\sqrt{x^6}}{5x^3} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\abs{x^3}}{5x^3} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^3}{5x^3} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{5} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{1}{5}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 12
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{\sqrt{4x^6 + 2x^2 - 3}}{7x^3 + 4}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION We consider the limit with only the highest-degree terms: \[ \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{\sqrt{4x^6}}{7x^3} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{2 \abs{x^3}}{7x^3} \pd \] Because \(x \to -\infty,\) we see \(x \lt 0.\) We rewrite \(\abs{x^3}\) as \(-x^3\) to find \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{-2x^3}{7x^3} &= \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{-2}{7} \nl &= \boxed{-\frac{2}{7}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 13
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} (x + 1) \sqrt x\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION As \(x \to \infty,\) both factors \((x + 1)\) and \(\sqrt x\) become arbitrarily large; hence, their product approaches \(\boxed{\infty}.\)
EXERCISE 14
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} (x^2 - 3x)\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION We cannot rewrite the limit as \[\lim_{x \to \infty} x^2 - \lim_{x \to \infty} 3x\] and take the difference of limits using the Difference Law of Limits (from Section 1.2), since each limit is unbounded. In fact, the form \(\infty - \infty\) is an indeterminate form, so we must convert the difference to a workable form. Instead, we rewrite the limit as \[\lim_{x \to \infty} x(x - 3) \pd\] Both factors increase without bound as \(x \to \infty,\) so this limit equals \(\boxed{\infty}.\) This result makes sense; \(x^2\) grows faster than \(-3x\) decreases and so drives \((x^2 - 3x)\) to be arbitrarily large. (Think of a "tug of war" between \(x^2\) and \(-3x.\))
EXERCISE 15
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} (x^2 - x^3)\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION It is incorrect to rewrite the limit as \[\lim_{x \to \infty} x^2 - \lim_{x \to \infty} x^3\] using the Difference Law of Limits (from Section 1.2) because neither limit exists. On the other hand, we can rewrite the limit as \[\lim_{x \to \infty} x^2(1 - x) \pd\] As \(x \to \infty,\) \(x^2 \to \infty\) and \((1 - x) \to -\infty.\) Thus, the product approaches \(\boxed{-\infty}.\) We find logic in this result: \(x^3\) is stronger than \(x^2\) and so pushes the difference \((x^3 - x^2)\) to \(-\infty.\)
EXERCISE 16
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \par{\sqrt{x^2 + 1} - x}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION It is difficult to predict this limit through inspection. Because both terms \(\sqrt{x^2 + 1}\) and \(x\) approach \(\infty\) as \(x \to \infty,\) this limit takes the indeterminate form \(\infty - \infty.\) So we need to convert this difference to a workable form; we attempt multiplying by the conjugate: \[ \ba \par{\sqrt{x^2 + 1} - x} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{x^2 + 1} + x}{\sqrt{x^2 + 1} + x} &= \frac{(x^2 + 1) - x^2}{\sqrt{x^2 + 1} + x} \nl &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2 + 1} + x} \pd \ea \] This new, equivalent form is no longer in an indeterminate form. As \(x \to \infty\) the denominator tends to \(\infty\) and so the fraction \(1/(\sqrt{x^2 + 1} + x)\) goes to \(0.\) Thus, \[\lim_{x \to \infty} \par{\sqrt{x^2 + 1} - x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2 + 1} + x} = \boxed 0\]
EXERCISE 17
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to -\infty} \par{-2x - \sqrt{x^2 + 8}}\]
evaluate the limit.
SOLUTION We cannot guess any useful result in this current form. As \(x \to -\infty,\) the term \(-2x\) pushes the difference \(\par{-2x - \sqrt{x^2 + 8}}\) toward \(\infty\) whereas the term \(- \sqrt{x^2 + 8}\) pushes the difference to \(-\infty.\) The winner of this battle cannot be easily identified. To rectify this indeterminate form, we multiply by the conjugate: \[ \ba \par{-2x - \sqrt{x^2 + 8}} \cdot \frac{-2x + \sqrt{x^2 + 8}}{-2x + \sqrt{x^2 + 8}} &= \frac{4x^2 - (x^2 + 8)}{-2x + \sqrt{x^2 + 8}} \nl &= \frac{3x^2 - 8}{-2x + \sqrt{x^2 + 8}} \pd \ea \] Therefore, the limit becomes \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{3x^2 - 8}{-2x + \sqrt{x^2 + 8}} \pd\] We could divide each term by the overall highest degree, \(x^2.\) Or we could simply consider the highest-degree terms of the numerator and denominator; doing so gives \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{3x^2}{-2x + \sqrt{x^2}} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{3x^2}{-2x + \abs x} \pd\] Because \(x \to -\infty,\) we presume \(x \lt 0.\) Accordingly, we rewrite \(\abs x\) as \(-x,\) giving us \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{3x^2}{-2x - x} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{3x^2}{-3x} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} -x = \boxed{\infty}\]
EXERCISE 18
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to 2^-} \frac{1}{x - 2}\]
determine whether the limit equals \(\infty\) or \(-\infty\).
SOLUTION We identify \(x = 2\) as a vertical asymptote of \(1/(x - 2).\) Thus, the function runs to \(\pm \infty\) on either side of the asymptote. We have two ways to tackle this problem: The first option is to substitute \(t = x - 2.\) Then \(t \to 0^-\) as \(x \to 2^-,\) so the limit becomes \[\lim_{t \to 0^-} \frac{1}{t} = \boxed{-\infty}\] The second method is to identify the sign of \(1/(x - 2)\) for \(x \lt 2 \col\) Over this interval the denominator \(x - 2\) is negative. The numerator is positive, so dividing a positive quantity by a negative quantity yields a negative quantity. So \(1/(x - 2) \lt 0\) for \(x \lt 2,\) meaning \(\lim_{x \to 2^-} 1/(x - 2)\) must be \(-\infty.\)
EXERCISE 19
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1}{\sqrt x}\]
determine whether the limit equals \(\infty\) or \(-\infty\).
SOLUTION The graph of \(y = 1/\sqrt x\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = 0\) because when \(x = 0\) the denominator is \(0\) but the numerator is nonzero. For \(x \gt 0,\) \(1/\sqrt x\) is always positive. The quantity \(\sqrt x\) shrinks to \(0\) as \(x \to 0^+,\) so \(1/\sqrt x \to \infty\) as \(x \to 0^+.\) Thus, \(\lim_{x \to 0^+} 1/\sqrt x = \boxed{\infty}.\)
EXERCISE 20
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to 6} \frac{3}{(x - 6)^2}\]
determine whether the limit equals \(\infty\) or \(-\infty\).
SOLUTION The graph of \(y = 3/(x - 6)^2\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = 6\) because when \(x = 6\) the denominator is \(0\) but the numerator is nonzero. The sign of \(3/(x - 6)^2\) is positive for both \(x \lt 6\) and \(x \gt 6.\) Thus, the graph approaches infinity from both sides of the asymptote; accordingly, \(\lim_{x \to 6} 3/(x - 6)^2 = \boxed{\infty}.\)

SOLUTION As \(x \to \infty,\) the sign of each of the factors \((2x - 3),\) \((x + 7),\) and \((1 - x)^2\) is positive. Thus, as \(x \to \infty\) the product grows arbitrarily large, to \(\boxed{\infty}.\)
EXERCISE 22

\[\ds \lim_{t \to \infty} t(3 - t)(t + 1)\]

SOLUTION As \(t \to \infty,\) the signs of \(t\) and \((t + 1)\) are both positive, but the sign of \((3 - t)\) is negative. The overall sign of the product is \[(+)(-)(+) = (-) \cma\] meaning as \(t \to \infty\) the product approaches \(\boxed{-\infty}.\)
EXERCISE 23

determine whether the limit equals \(\infty\) or \(-\infty\).
SOLUTION As \(x \to -\infty,\) the signs of \((x - 5)\) and \((x + 3)\) both become negative, but the sign of \((x^2 + 4)\) is positive. The overall sign of the product is \[(-)(+)(-) = (+) \cma\] so as \(x \to -\infty\) the product approaches \(\boxed{\infty}.\)
EXERCISE 24
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to 2^+} \frac{x}{(x - 2)(x + 4)}\]
determine whether the limit equals \(\infty\) or \(-\infty\).
SOLUTION The graph of \(y = x/(x - 2)(x + 4)\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = 2\) because when \(x = 2\) the denominator is \(0\) but the numerator is nonzero. Therefore, we consider the sign of this function as \(x \to 2^+ \col\) For \(x \gt 2\) we see \(x \gt 0,\) \((x - 2) \gt 0,\) and \((x + 4) \gt 0.\) So the overall sign of the rational function as \(x \to 2^+\) is \[\frac{(+)}{(+)(+)} = (+) \pd\] Thus, the limit is \(\boxed{\infty}.\)
EXERCISE 25
For
\[\ds \lim_{x \to -3^-} \frac{3x + 1}{(x - 7)(x + 3)}\]
determine whether the limit equals \(\infty\) or \(-\infty\).
SOLUTION The graph of \(y = (3x + 1)/(x - 7)(x + 3)\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = -3\) since when \(x = -3\) the denominator is \(0\) but the numerator is nonzero. Our goal is to find the sign of this function as \(x \to -3^- \col\) For \(x \lt -3\) we see \((3x + 1) \lt 0,\) \((x - 7) \lt 0,\) and \((x + 3) \lt 0.\) Thus, the overall sign of the rational function as \(x \to -3^-\) is \[\frac{(-)}{(-)(-)} = (-) \pd\] The limit is therefore \(\boxed{-\infty}.\)

SOLUTION At \(u = 0\) the graph of \(y = 1/(1 - e^u)\) has a vertical asymptote because \[1 - e^0 = 0 \and 1 \ne 0 \pd\] For \(u \gt 0\) the denominator is negative, so the sign of the rational expression as \(u \to 0^+\) is \[\frac{(+)}{(-)} = (-) \pd\] Thus, the limit is \(\boxed{-\infty}.\)
EXERCISE 27

EXERCISE 28
For
\[\ds \frac{x^2 - 7}{5x^2 + x}\]
find all the horizontal asymptotes of the given function.
SOLUTION Our mission is to determine the end behavior of the function, namely, its behavior as \(x \to \infty\) and \(x \to -\infty.\) We consider only the highest-degree terms when we take limits at infinity. Thus, \[ \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2 - 7}{5x^2 + x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2}{5x^2} = \frac{1}{5} \pd \] Also, \[ \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{x^2 - 7}{5x^2 + x} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{x^2}{5x^2} = \frac{1}{5} \pd \] Since both limits are the same, the function \((x^2 - 7)/(5x^2 + x)\) has only one horizontal asymptote: \(\boxed{y = 1/5}.\)
EXERCISE 29
For
\[\ds \frac{x + 4}{8 - x^2}\]
find all the horizontal asymptotes of the given function.
SOLUTION Because the degree of the denominator (two) is greater than the degree of the numerator (one), the rational function approaches \(0\) as \(x \to \infty\) or \(x \to -\infty.\) Hence, the only horizontal asymptote is \(\boxed{y = 0}.\)
EXERCISE 30
For
\[\ds \frac{2x^3 + x + 7}{\sqrt{16x^6 + 2}}\]
find all the horizontal asymptotes of the given function.
SOLUTION We seek the end behavior of the function; that is, we find its behavior as \(x \to \infty\) and \(x \to -\infty.\) Only the highest-degree terms are important when we take these limits. Thus, \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{2x^3 + x + 7}{\sqrt{16x^6 + 2}} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{2x^3}{4 \abs{x^3}} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^3}{2x^3} \nl &= \frac{1}{2} \pd \ea \] Also, \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{2x^3 + x + 7}{\sqrt{16x^6 + 2}} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{2x^3}{4 \abs{x^3}} \pd\] (When you take the square root of a term, remember to add absolute value bars.) Because \(x \to -\infty,\) \(x \lt 0\) and so \(\abs{x^3} = -x^3.\) The limit is therefore \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{2x^3}{-4x^3} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{2}{-4} = -\frac{1}{2} \pd\] Thus, the horizontal asymptotes are \(\boxed{y = -1/2}\) and \(\boxed{y = 1/2}.\)
EXERCISE 31
For
\[\ds 3 + \frac{8x^2 + 3x - 5}{7x^2 + 2}\]
find all the horizontal asymptotes of the given function.
SOLUTION Our mission is to determine the end behavior of the function, namely, its behavior as \(x \to \infty\) and \(x \to -\infty.\) We consider only the highest-degree terms when we take limits at infinity. Thus, \[ \lim_{x \to \infty} \par{3 + \frac{8x^2 + 3x - 5}{7x^2 + 2}} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \par{3 + \frac{8x^2}{7x^2}} = 3 + \frac{8}{7} = \frac{29}{7} \pd \] In addition, \[ \lim_{x \to -\infty} \par{3 + \frac{8x^2 + 3x - 5}{7x^2 + 2}} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \par{3 + \frac{8x^2}{7x^2}} = 3 + \frac{8}{7} = \frac{29}{7} \pd \] Since both limits are the same, the function has only one horizontal asymptote: \(\boxed{y = 29/7}.\)
EXERCISE 32
For
\[\ds 3 + e^{-x}\]
find all the horizontal asymptotes of the given function.
SOLUTION As \(x \to \infty,\) \(e^{-x} \to 0\) and so \(3 + e^{-x} \to 3.\) Thus, \(y = 3\) is a horizontal asymptote to the graph of \(3 + e^{-x}.\) However, \(e^{-x} \to \infty\) as \(x \to -\infty.\) Since the graph is unbounded as \(x\) goes to negative infinity, the graph has no horizontal asymptote for \(x \lt 0.\) So \(\boxed{y = 3}\) is the only horizontal asymptote.
EXERCISE 33
All rational functions have at least one vertical asymptote.
SOLUTION This statement is false. Consider the rational function \(f(x) = 1/(x^2 + 1).\) The denominator \(x^2 + 1\) is never zero, so \(f\) cannot have any vertical asymptotes.
EXERCISE 34
Polynomials do not have horizontal asymptotes.
SOLUTION This statement is true. A function \(f(x)\) needs to approach a finite value as \(x \to \infty\) or \(x \to -\infty\) to have a horizontal asymptote. But polynomials tend to \(\pm \infty\) as their input goes to \(\infty\) or \(-\infty.\)
EXERCISE 35
If \(f(x)\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = 0,\) then \(f(0)\) is undefined.
SOLUTION This statement is false; a counterexample is the function \[ f(x) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{x} &x \ne 0 \nl 0 &x = 0 \pd \end{cases} \]
EXERCISE 36
Find the vertical asymptotes of \(f(x) = 1/(x^2 - 25).\)
SOLUTION The function \(f\) has a vertical asymptote when its denominator is \(0\) but the numerator is nonzero. We equate the denominator to \(0 \col\) \[x^2 - 25 = 0 \arrowTwoWay (x + 5)(x - 5) = 0 \arrowTwoWay x = -5 \cma x = 5 \pd\] Neither of these values makes the numerator \(0.\) Thus, \(f\) has vertical asymptotes at \(\boxed{x = -5}\) and \(\boxed{x = 5}.\)
EXERCISE 37
Find the vertical asymptotes of \(g(x) = (x + 3)/(x^2 - 9).\)
SOLUTION The function \(g\) has a vertical asymptote when its denominator is \(0\) but the numerator is nonzero. We equate the denominator to \(0 \col\) \[x^2 - 9 = 0 \arrowTwoWay (x + 3)(x - 3) = 0 \arrowTwoWay x = -3 \cma x = 3 \pd\] When \(x = -3\) both the numerator and denominator equal \(0,\) which is inconclusive. We factor the denominator (recognizing that it is a difference of squares) to rewrite \(g\) as \[g(x) = \frac{x + 3}{(x + 3)(x - 3)} = \frac{1}{x - 3} \cma x = -3 \pd\] In this simplified form, when \(x = -3\) the denominator is nonzero; thus, we ignore this value. In contrast, when \(x = 3\) the denominator is \(0\) but the numerator is nonzero. Hence, \(\boxed{x = 3}\) is the only vertical asymptote of \(g.\)
EXERCISE 38
Find \(\lim_{x \to \infty} x^3\) and \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} x^3.\) Describe the end behavior of \(x^3.\)
SOLUTION If you don't immediately recognize the limits, then try substituting large values of \(x;\) for example, \[ \ba f(1) &= 1^3 = 1 \nl f(10) &= 10^3 = 1000 \nl f(100) &= 100^3 = 1000000 \pd \nl \ea \] This pattern repeats, in which \(f(x)\) grows without bound as \(x \to \infty,\) so \(\lim_{x \to \infty} x^3 = \infty.\) In contrast, \[ \ba f(-1) &= (-1)^3 = -1 \nl f(-10) &= (-10)^3 = -1000 \nl f(-100) &= (-100)^3 = -1000000 \pd \nl \ea \] Thus, \(f(x)\) decreases without bound as \(x \to -\infty,\) so \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} x^3 = -\infty.\) We therefore describe the end behavior as follows: as \(x \to \infty,\) \(x^3 \to \infty;\) as \(x \to -\infty,\) \(x^3 \to -\infty.\)
EXERCISE 39
Let \(f(x) = \sqrt{x^2 + 4x + 1}/(3x - 3).\)
  1. Find all the horizontal asymptotes to the graph of \(f.\)
  2. Locate the vertical asymptotes of \(f.\)
  3. Describe the behavior of \(f\) at each vertical asymptote.
SOLUTION
  1. We find horizontal asymptotes of \(f\) by calculating \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x).\) The behavior of \(f(x)\) as \(x \to \infty\) matches the ratio of the highest-degree terms, \(\sqrt{x^2}/3x\) \(= \abs{x}/3x,\) as \(x \to \infty.\) Thus, \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x)\) is equivalent to \[\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\abs x}{3x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x}{3x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{3}\] Also, \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x)\) becomes \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{\abs x}{3x} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{-x}{3x} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{-1}{3} = -\frac{1}{3}\] Hence, \(f\) has horizontal asymptotes of \(\boxed{y = -1/3}\) and \(\boxed{y = 1/3}.\)
  2. The graph of \(f\) has a vertical asymptote when the denominator equals \(0\) but the numerator is nonzero. Equating the denominator to \(0,\) we find \[3x - 3 = 0 \implies x = 1 \pd\] At this value, the numerator is \(\sqrt 6 \ne 0.\) Thus, the line \(\boxed{x = 1}\) is the only vertical asymptote of \(f.\)
  3. We find the sign of \(f\) on either side of the vertical asymptote. For \(x \lt 1,\) the numerator is positive and the denominator is negative. The overall sign of \(f\) is \[\frac{(+)}{(-)} = (-) \cma\] so the graph runs to \(-\infty\) to the left of the vertical asymptote \(x = 1.\) For \(x \gt 1,\) the sign of \(f\) is \[\frac{(+)}{(+)} = (+) \cma\] so the graph runs to \(\infty\) to the right of the vertical asymptote.
