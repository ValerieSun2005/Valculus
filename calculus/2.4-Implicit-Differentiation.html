2.4 â€” Implicit Differentiation

We have differentiated functions in the form \(y = f(x)\). In this section, however, we discuss how to differentiate implicit equations.

An equation of the form \(y = f(x)\) is said to be explicit; examples of explicit equations are \[y = x - 4 \and y = x^2 - 4x \period\] Conversely, an implicit equation relates \(x\) and \(y\) through an equation in which \(y\) does not stand alone. For example, \[x^2 + xy = 6 \and 9x - 4y = 0\] are examples of implicit equations.

Many curves are best expressed as explicit equations. For example, consider \begin{equation} x^2 + y^2 = 1 \comma \label{eq:unit-circle} \end{equation} the equation of a circle of radius \(1\) (the unit circle). Suppose we wish to determine the slope of this circle at any point \((x, y).\) In other words, we want to find \(\textDeriv{y}{x}.\) We apply implicit differentiation: We treat \(y\) as \(y(x),\) a function of \(x,\) so \(\eqRefer{eq:unit-circle}\) should be thought of as \[x^2 + [y(x)]^2 = 1 \period\] Now we differentiate both sides with respect to \(x.\) Applying the chain rule to differentiate \(y(x),\) we get \[2x + 2y \, \deriv{y}{x} = 0 \period\] Now we solve for \(\textDeriv{y}{x} \col\) \[\deriv{y}{x} = -\frac{x}{y} \period\]

Figure 1 shows a diagram of a unit circle with a tangent line. At \((0, -1)\) and \((0, 1),\) the tangent lines to the circle have slope \(0.\) And at \((-1, 0)\) and \((1, 0),\) the tangent lines are vertical and therefore have infinite slope. Hence, \(\textDeriv{y}{x} = -x/y\) is mathematically intuitive. Alternatively, we can solve for \(y\) in \(\eqRefer{eq:unit-circle},\) although solving explicitly isn't always possible. We obtain two functions: \[ \baat{2} y &= f(x) &&= \sqrt{1 - x^2} \nl y &= g(x) &&= - \sqrt{1 - x^2} \period \eaat \] The graph of \(y = f(x)\) is the top half of the unit circle, while the graph of \(y = g(x)\) represents the bottom. You may check that \[ \baat{2} f'(x) &= \frac{-x}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \lspace g'(x) &&= \frac{x}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \nl &= \frac{-x}{y} &&= \frac{x}{y} \period \eaat \]

EXAMPLE 1
Consider the implicit equation \(x^2 + xy - x = 2.\) Find \(\textDeriv{y}{x}.\)
We treat \(y\) as \(y(x),\) a function of \(x.\) You may substitute in this expression for clarity if you desire. We differentiate with respect to \(x\) both sides of the equation using the chain rule. This gives \[ \ba \deriv{}{x}(x^2 + xy - x) &= \deriv{}{x}(2) \nl 2x + \par{x \deriv{y}{x} + y} - 1 &= 0 \nl \deriv{y}{x} &= \boxed{\frac{1 - 2x - y}{x}} \ea \]
EXAMPLE 2
Consider the implicit equation \(2x^3y -xy^2 = 16.\) Find \(\textDeriv{y}{x}.\)
We differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\) to get \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} (2x^3y - xy^2) &= \deriv{}{x}(16) \nl 2 \left(3x^2 y + x^3 \deriv{y}{x} \right) - \left(y^2 + 2xy \deriv{y}{x} \right)&= 0 \nl \deriv{y}{x} \left(2x^3 - 2xy\right) &= y^2 - 6x^2y \nl \deriv{y}{x} &= \boxed{\frac{y^2 - 6x^2 y}{2x^3 - 2xy}} \ea \]
EXAMPLE 3
Find all the horizontal tangents to the curve \(x^3 - x^2y = 2\).

We first find \(\textDeriv{y}{x} \col\) \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \left(x^3 - x^2y \right) &= \deriv{}{x} (2) \nl 3x^2 - \left(2xy + x^2 \deriv{y}{x} \right) &= 0 \nl -x^2 \deriv{y}{x} &= 2xy - 3x^2 \nl \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{3x - 2y}{x} \period \ea \]

A horizontal tangent occurs when the slope of the tangent line is \(0.\) Thus, we let \(\textDeriv{y}{x} = 0 \col\) \[\frac{3x - 2y}{x} = 0 \implies 3x - 2y = 0\] We now have the following system of equations: \[ \ba 3x - 2y &= 0 \nl x^3 - x^2 y &= 2 \period \ea \] The first equation shows that \(y = 3x/2.\) Then \[ \ba x^3 - x^2 \left(\frac{3x}{2}\right) = 2 \implies x &= \sqrt[3]{-4} \nl y &= \frac{3\sqrt[3]{-4}}{2} \period \ea \] Thus, the curve has a horizontal tangent at \[\boxed{\left(\sqrt[3]{-4}, \frac{3\sqrt[3]{-4}}{2} \right)}\]

EXAMPLE 4
Find all the vertical tangents to the curve \(xy - y^2 = 4.\)

We differentiate both sides of the equation with respect to \(x\) to solve for \(\textDeriv{y}{x}.\) \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \left(xy - y^2 \right) &= \deriv{}{x}(4) \nl \left(y + x \deriv{y}{x} \right) - 2y \deriv{y}{x} &= 0 \nl \deriv{y}{x} \left(x - 2y\right) &= -y \nl \deriv{y}{x} &= \frac{y}{2y - x} \period \ea \] To find the vertical tangents to the curve, we set the denominator of \(\textDeriv{y}{x}\) equal to \(0.\) We get the equation \[2y - x = 0 \period\] We now have a system of equations in which we solve for \(x\) and \(y \col\) \begin{align} 2y - x &= 0 \label{eq:ex-vert-1} \nl xy - y^2 &= 4 \label{eq:ex-vert-2} \period \end{align} From \(\eqRefer{eq:ex-vert-1},\) we find \(x = 2y.\) Then we substitute this expression into \(\eqRefer{eq:ex-vert-2}\) to find \[2y^2 - y^2 = 4 \implies y = \pm 2 \period \]

Now we find the \(x\)-values that correspond to \(y = \pm 2.\) From \(\eqRefer{eq:ex-vert-1},\) letting \(y = -2\) gives \[2(-2) - x = 0 \implies x = -4 \period \] Likewise, substituting \(y = 2\) gives \[2(2) - x = 0 \implies x = 4 \period \] Therefore, the points \[\boxed{(-4, -2) \and (4, 2)}\] are the points at which the curve has vertical tangents.
