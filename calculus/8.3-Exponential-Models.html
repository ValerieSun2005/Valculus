8.3: Exponential Models

Bacteria multiplying, radioactive dating, cookies coolingâ€”our world runs on exponential models. Exponential models are fundamental to ecology, finance, and physics. In this section, we define the mathematical basis of exponential functions and showcase a variety of applications. We discuss the following topics:

  • Defining Exponential Growth and Doubling Time
  • Modeling Compound Interest with discrete and continuous growth
  • Defining Exponential Decay and Half-Life
  • Applying exponential models to First-Order Chemical Reactions, Newton's Law of Cooling, and Discharging a Capacitor

Exponential Growth

Exponential growth is a phenomenon in which a quantity undergoes very fast growth. Specifically, if \(y\) grows exponentially, then \(y\) increases at a rate proportional to itself. Mathematically, \(y\) satisfies the differential equation \begin{equation} \deriv{y}{x} = k y \comma \label{eq:exp-growth-diff-eq} \end{equation}

where \(k\) is the growth constant. For clarity, we assume \(k \gt 0.\) \(\eqRefer{eq:exp-growth-diff-eq}\) asserts small values of \(y\) grow slowly and large values grow quickly. Solving for \(y\) in \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth-diff-eq}\) using Separation of Variables, we get \[ \ba \frac{1}{y} \di y &= k \di x \nl \int \frac{1}{y} \di y &= \int k \di x \nl \ln \abs{y} &= kx + C \nl y &= e^{C} e^{kx} \pd \ea \] If \(y_0\) is the value of \(y\) when \(x = 0,\) then substituting this initial condition shows \[y_0 = e^{C} e^{k(0)} = e^C \pd\] Hence, \(e^C\) is \(y_0\) and so the solution to \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth-diff-eq}\) is \begin{equation} y = y_0 e^{kx} \pd \label{eq:exp-growth} \end{equation} In Figure 1, the graph of \(y = y_0e^{kx}\) is plotted. Since its shape resembles the letter J, the curve is often called a J-curve. We obtain different graphs by changing the value of \(y_0;\) Figure 2 shows graphs of the family of graphs \(y = y_0e^{kx}\) for selected values of \(y_0.\)

EXPONENTIAL GROWTH
Quantity \(y\) undergoes exponential growth if it satisfies the differential equation \[\deriv{y}{x} = ky \cma \tag*{\eqref{eq:exp-growth-diff-eq}}\] where \(k\) is called the growth constant. The solution is \[y = y_0e^{kx} \cma \tag*{\eqref{eq:exp-growth}}\] where \(y_0\) is the value of \(y\) when \(x = 0.\)
EXAMPLE 1
Function \(f(x)\) grows at a rate proportional to itself at any \(x.\) It is known that \(f(3) = 120\) and \(f(6) = 360.\) Find \(f(x).\)

Since the growth of \(f(x)\) is proportional to itself, \(f\) satisfies the differential equation \[f'(x) = k f(x) \cma\] where \(k\) is the growth constant. This equation matches \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth-diff-eq},\) the definition of exponential growth. Hence, for unknown \(y_0\) and \(k\) the form of \(f\) is \begin{equation} f(x) = y_0e^{kx} \pd \label{eq:exp-growth-f} \end{equation} Our goal is to solve for \(y_0\) and \(k\) that satisfy the initial conditions \(f(3) = 120\) and \(f(6) = 360.\)

In \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth-f}\) we substitute the initial conditions to solve for \(y_0\) and \(k;\) that is, we have \begin{equation} 120 = y_0e^{3k} \and 360 = y_0e^{6k} \pd \label{eq:exp-growth-f-eqs} \end{equation} It is easy to first solve for \(k\) by dividing the equations in \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth-f-eqs},\) as doing so cancels out the unknown factor \(y_0.\) Dividing the latter equation by the former equation shows \[ \ba \frac{360}{120} &= \frac{\cancel{y_0} e^{6k}}{\cancel{y_0} e^{3k}} \nl 3 &= e^{6k - 3k} = e^{3k} \nl k &= \frac{\ln 3}{3} \pd \ea \] To find \(y_0\) we substitute \(k = (\ln 3)/3\) into either equation in \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth-f-eqs};\) for example, the former equation becomes \[ \ba 120 &= y_0e^{3(\ln 3)/3} \nl 120 &= 3 y_0 \nl 40 &= y_0 \pd \ea \] We therefore have the missing values of \(y_0\) and \(k\) in \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth-f},\) so \[f(x) = 40e^{[(\ln 3)/3]x} = \boxed{40(3)^{x/3}}\]

Doubling Time

Suppose \(y\) grows exponentially with time \(t.\) The rate at which \(y\) grows is changing. But \(y\) takes the same time to multiply by the same factor; for example, \(y\) takes the same time to grow from \(100\) to \(200\) as it does to grow from \(1000\) to \(2000.\) Let \(\doublingTime\) be the time that \(y\) takes to double to \(2y.\) We call \(\doublingTime\) the doubling time. Figure 3 shows the graph of \(y = y_0e^{kt},\) where \(y_0\) doubles to \(2 y_0\) after the doubling time \(\doublingTime\) has elapsed. We solve for \(\doublingTime\) by first establishing that \(y_0e^{k\doublingTime} = 2y_0.\) Then we find \begin{align} e^{k\doublingTime} &= 2 \nonumber \nl k\doublingTime &= \ln 2 \nonumber \nl \doublingTime &= \frac{\ln 2}{k} \pd \label{eq:doubling-time} \end{align} In words, \(\eqrefer{eq:doubling-time}\) states that the doubling time for an exponential growth model is \(\ln 2\) divided by the growth constant. If \(k\) is expressed as a percentage, then \(\eqref{eq:doubling-time}\) is often called the rule of \(\mathcal{70}\) because \(\ln 2 \approx 0.7.\) We must remember that \(\eqref{eq:doubling-time}\) only applies if the base of the exponential model is \(e;\) otherwise, we must convert the base.

DOUBLING TIME
If \(y\) grows exponentially with time \(t,\) and \(k\) is the growth constant, then the doubling time of \(y\) is \[\doublingTime = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \tag*{\eqref{eq:doubling-time}}\] if the base of the exponential model is \(e.\)
EXAMPLE 2
A population of sheep is modeled by the differentiable function \(N(t),\) where \(N(t)\) is the number of sheep and \(t\) is years. The population grows continuously at a rate of \(2\%\) per year.
  1. Write an expression for \(\textDeriv{N}{t}.\)
  2. How quickly is the population changing when it has \(500\) sheep?
  3. What is the doubling time for the sheep's population?
  4. The population initially has \(300\) sheep. After how many years does the population exceed \(700\) sheep?
The sheep's population is growing continuously. So it is incorrect to state that after one year, the population is \(300 \cdot 1.02 = 306.\) This would be true if the population grew discretely once per year.
  1. The population grows continuously at a rate of \(2\%\) per year. In \(\eqrefer{eq:exp-growth-diff-eq}\) we have \(k = 0.02.\) Thus, the differential equation is \[\boxed{\deriv{N}{t} = 0.02N}\]
  2. When the population has \(500\) sheep, the population is growing at a rate of \[\deriv{N}{t} = 0.02(500) = \boxed{10 \undiv{sheep}{year}}\]
  3. The growth constant is \(k = 0.02.\) By \(\eqref{eq:doubling-time}\) the doubling time for the sheep's population is \[\doublingTime = \frac{\ln 2}{0.02} \approx \boxed{34.657 \un{years}}\]
  4. By \(\eqRefer{eq:exp-growth},\) the solution to the differential equation \(\textDeriv{N}{t} = 0.02 N\) is \begin{equation*} N(t) = 300e^{0.02t} \cma \end{equation*} where \(300\) is the initial value of \(N(t)\) at \(t = 0.\) We solve for \(t\) when \(N(t) = 700,\) as follows: \[ \ba 300e^{0.02t} &= 700 \nl 0.02t &= \ln \tfrac{700}{300} \nl t &= \tfrac{1}{0.02} \ln \tfrac{700}{300} \approx \boxed{42.364 \un{years}} \ea \]

Compound Interest

When money is lent, it earns interest. Simple interest is paid once over a time period (for example, \(1\) year). If a principal amount of \(P\) dollars earns simple interest per year at an interest rate of \(r,\) then after one year the amount becomes \(A = P(1 + r).\) Likewise, after two years we have \(A = P(1 + r)^2,\) and after \(t\) years the balance is \(A = P(1 + r)^t.\)

Compound interest is paid multiple times over a time period. Suppose a principal amount of \(P\) dollars grows at an interest rate of \(r,\) compounded biannually (twice per year). Then after one year the amount becomes \(A =\) \(P(1 + r/2)^2,\) and after \(t\) years we have \(A = P(1 + r/2)^{2t}.\) If the interest rate is compounded three times annually at the same rate \(r,\) then the amount after \(t\) years is \(A = P(1 + r/3)^{3t}.\) This pattern continues: \[ \baat{2} A &= P \par{1 + \frac{r}{4}}^{4t} \lspace &&\text{compounded 4 times annually} \nl A &= P \par{1 + \frac{r}{5}}^{5t} \lspace &&\text{compounded 5 times annually} \nl A &= P \par{1 + \frac{r}{6}}^{6t} \lspace &&\text{compounded 6 times annually} \nl & \vdots && \vdots \nl \eaat \] The general pattern is, for \(r\) compounded \(n\) times annually for \(t\) time periods, \begin{equation} A = P \par{1 + \frac{r}{n}}^{nt} \pd \label{eq:compound-interest} \end{equation} But if \(r\) is continuously compoundedâ€”that is, as \(n \to \infty\)â€”then \(\eqref{eq:compound-interest}\) becomes \begin{equation} A = \lim_{n \to \infty} P \par{1 + \frac{r}{n}}^{nt} \pd \label{eq:compound-interest-lim} \end{equation} We must notice that this form resembles the limit definition of a special number: \(e = \lim_{m \to \infty} (1 + 1/m)^m.\) Hence, we manipulate \(\eqref{eq:compound-interest-lim}\) to match this form. In \(\eqref{eq:compound-interest-lim}\) we want the quantity in parentheses to have the expression \((1 + 1/m).\) Let \(m = n/r.\) We see that \(m \to \infty\) as \(n \to \infty;\) \(\eqrefer{eq:compound-interest-lim}\) therefore becomes \[ \ba A &= \lim_{m \to \infty} P \par{1 + \frac{1}{m}}^{rmt} \nl &= P \parbr{\lim_{m \to \infty}\par{1 + \frac{1}{m}}^{m} \,}^{rt} \pd \ea \] The quantity in brackets is the limit definition of \(e,\) so we have \begin{equation} A = Pe^{rt} \pd \label{eq:compound-interest-cont} \end{equation} We use \(\eqref{eq:compound-interest}\) if the rate \(r\) is compounded a finite number of times in a time period. This growth is discrete. In contrast, we use \(\eqref{eq:compound-interest-cont}\) for an infinite number of compounding events in a time periodâ€”that is, if a quantity grows continuously.

COMPOUND INTEREST
Suppose a principal amount of \(P\) dollars grows at an interest rate of \(r,\) compounded \(n\) times in a time period. The amount of money after \(t\) time periods is \begin{equation*} A = P \par{1 + \frac{r}{n}}^{nt} \pd \tag*{\eqref{eq:compound-interest}} \end{equation*} If \(P\) dollars grows continuously at an interest rate of \(r,\) then after \(t\) time periods the amount is \begin{equation*} A = Pe^{rt} \pd \tag*{\eqref{eq:compound-interest-cont}} \end{equation*}
EXAMPLE 3
A bank lends \(\$600\) to a business at an interest rate of \(5\%\) per year. Determine the amount of money the business owes after \(4\) years if the interest rate is
  1. compounded annually
  2. compounded biannually
  3. compounded \(3\) times per year
  4. compounded continuously
In this problem, the rate \(r\) is compounded a finite number of times; the money does not grow continuously. So we use \(\eqref{eq:compound-interest}\) for all our calculations in parts (a)â€“(c). We first convert the interest rate \(r = 5\%\) to the decimal \(r = 0.05.\)
  1. In \(\eqref{eq:compound-interest}\) we have \(P = 600,\) \(r = 0.05,\) \(n = 1,\) and \(t = 4.\) So the amount the business owes after \(4\) years is \[A = 600 \par{1 + \frac{0.05}{1}}^{1(4)} = 600 \par{1 + 0.05}^4 \approx \boxed{\$729.30}\] This example shows simple interest because the interest is paid only once in the time period of a year.
  2. By \(\eqref{eq:compound-interest}\) the amount becomes \[A = 600 \par{1 + \frac{0.05}{2}}^{2(4)} \approx \boxed{\$731.04}\] Compared to (a), this part shows compound interest since interest is paid more than once in the time period of a year.
  3. Using \(\eqref{eq:compound-interest},\) we obtain \[A = 600 \par{1 + \frac{0.05}{3}}^{3(4)} \approx \boxed{\$731.63}\]
  4. We use \(\eqref{eq:compound-interest-cont}\) because the money undergoes continuous growth. We have \(A = 600,\) \(r = 0.05,\) and \(t = 4.\) Thus, the amount is \[A = 600e^{0.05(4)} \approx \boxed{\$732.84}\]
EXAMPLE 4
A bank account initially contains \(\$500.\) This amount is compounded continuously at a rate of \(3\%\) per year. The amount of money in the account is given by \(M(t),\) where \(M(t)\) is measured in dollars and \(t\) is measured in years.
  1. Write a differential equation for \(\textDeriv{M}{t}.\)
  2. Find \(M(t).\)
  3. After how many years will the \(\$500\) double?
  1. Since the money is growing continuously, it is appropriate to write a differential equation using \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth-diff-eq}.\) The growth constant is the interest rate, \(r = 0.03,\) so the differential equation is \[\boxed{\deriv{M}{t} = 0.03M}\]
  2. Initially, the balance is \(\$500.\) The solution to the differential equation in (a) is then \begin{equation*} M(t) = 500e^{0.03t} \pd \end{equation*}
  3. The doubling time is, by \(\eqrefer{eq:doubling-time},\) \[\doublingTime = \frac{\ln 2}{0.03} \approx \boxed{23.105 \un{years}}\]

Exponential Decay

In exponential growth, the rate of increase of \(y\) is directly proportional to itself. But in exponential decay, \(y\) decreases at a rate directly proportional to itself; that is, if \(y\) decays exponentially with \(x,\) then \begin{equation} \deriv{y}{x} = -ky \pd \label{eq:exp-decay-diff-eq} \end{equation} We call \(k\) the decay constant, and we assume \(k \gt 0,\) just as we do with the growth constant. Similar to steps of obtaining \(\eqref{eq:exp-growth},\) we perform Separation of Variables for \(\eqrefer{eq:exp-decay-diff-eq} \col\) \[ \ba \frac{1}{y} \di y &= -k \di x \nl \int \frac{1}{y} \di y &= \int -k \di x \nl \ln|y| &= -kx + C \nl y &= e^{C} e^{-kx} \pd \ea \] Since \(e^C\) is the value of \(y\) when \(x = 0,\) the solution to \(\eqref{eq:exp-decay-diff-eq}\) is \begin{equation} y = y_0e^{-kx} \pd \label{eq:exp-decay} \end{equation} Whereas in exponential growth \(y\) grows very quickly, in exponential decay \(y\) shrinks very quickly. Just as repeated multiplication exhibits exponential growth, repeated division shows exponential decay.

Figure 4 shows the graph of \(\eqrefer{eq:exp-decay}.\) It is a reflection of the graph of \(y = y_0e^{kx}\) (as in Figure 1) across the \(y\)-axis.

EXAMPLE 5
The mass \(m(t)\) of a chemical compound decays exponentially. Initially, \(80\) grams of the compound are present. After \(3\) days, \(40\) grams of the compound remain. Determine \(m(t),\) where \(t\) is in years.
Since \(m(t)\) undergoes exponential decay, it satisfies the differential equation \(\textDeriv{m}{t} = -km,\) where \(k\) is the decay constant. The solution takes the form \begin{equation} m(t) = 80e^{-kt} \pd \label{eq:decay-m(t)} \end{equation} Then we find \(k\) by substituting the initial condition \(m(3) = 40 \col\) \[ \ba m(3) = 80e^{-3k} &= 40 \nl \implies k &= -\tfrac{1}{3} \ln \tfrac{40}{80} = -\tfrac{1}{3} \ln \tfrac{1}{2} \pd \ea \] Thus, \(\eqrefer{eq:decay-m(t)}\) becomes \[m(t) = 80 e^{-\par{-\frac{1}{3} \ln \frac{1}{2}} t} = \boxed{80 (2)^{-t/3}}\]

Half-Life

Let's suppose \(y\) decays exponentially with time \(t.\) Similar to the case of Doubling Time, \(y\) takes the same time to shrink by the same factor. Let \(\halfLife\) be the time \(y\) takes to reduce in half; this number is called the half-life of \(y.\) In Figure 5 the graph of \(y = y_0 e^{-kt}\) is shown, in which \(y_0\) shrinks to \(y_0/2\) at \(t = \halfLife.\) To determine an expression for \(\halfLife,\) we assert that \(y_0 e^{-k\halfLife} = y_0/2.\) We then have \begin{align} e^{-k \halfLife} &= \tfrac{1}{2} \nonumber \nl -k \halfLife &= \ln \tfrac{1}{2} \nonumber \nl \halfLife &= -\tfrac{1}{k} \ln \tfrac{1}{2} \nonumber \nl \halfLife &= \frac{\ln 2}{k} \pd \label{eq:half-life} \end{align} \(\eqrefer{eq:half-life}\) is identical to the doubling time; both expressions contain \(\ln 2\) divided by the constant \(k.\) We use \(\eqref{eq:half-life}\) only if the base of the exponential model is \(e.\)

Knowing the half-life of a chemical compound is of value to scientists. It is a fundamental part of radioactive dating, a method of analyzing radioactive isotopes to determine the ages of rocks and minerals. Likewise, a population exposed to a dangerous material may want to know how long the substance takes to disintegrate. These applications of half-life cultivate the basis for scientific studyâ€”as shown by the following examples.

HALF-LIFE
If \(y\) undergoes exponential decay with time \(t,\) and \(k\) is the decay constant, then the half-life of \(y\) is \[\halfLife = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \tag*{\eqref{eq:half-life}}\] if the base of the exponential model is \(e.\)
EXAMPLE 6
Carbon-\(14\) is a radioactive isotope whose half-life is roughly \(5730\) years. Suppose that \(0.02\) gram of carbon-\(14\) is found in a historical artifact. Model the mass \(m(t)\) of the carbon-\(14\) using an exponential decay model. What mass of carbon-\(14\) was present \(4000\) years ago?

There is initially \(0.02\) gram of carbon-\(14.\) If \(k\) is the decay constant and \(t\) is time in years, then we have \[m(t) = 0.02e^{-kt} \pd\] Since the half-life of \(m(t)\) is \(5730\) years, we find \(k\) using \(\eqrefer{eq:half-life} \col\) \[5730 = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \implies k = \frac{\ln 2}{5730} \pd\] So \[m(t) = 0.02e^{-t(\ln 2)/5730} = \boxed{0.02(2)^{-t/5730}}\]

We want to look back \(4000\) years, so we consider \(t = -4000 \col\) \[m(-4000) = 0.02(2)^{-(-4000)/5730} \approx 0.032 \pd\] Thus, \(4000\) years ago the mass of carbon-\(14\) in the artifact was \(0.032\) gram.

First-Order Chemical Reactions

In a chemical reaction, reactants are converted into products. Let chemical substance \(\ce A\) be a reactant that forms a set of products: \begin{equation*} \ce{A -> products} \pd \end{equation*} Suppose that the rate of the reaction with time is directly proportional to the concentration of \(\ce A,\) which we write as \([\ce A].\) Then this reaction is then called first-order with respect to \(\ce A.\) In other words, the rate at which \(\ce A\) is used up with time is directly proportional to the concentration of \(\ce A.\) The rate law of the reaction is a differential equation that describes the concentration of \(\ce A\)â€”that is, \begin{equation} \deriv{\ce{[A]}}{t} = -k \ce{[A]} \cma \label{eq:reaction-diff-eq} \end{equation} where \(k\) is called the rate constant and \(t\) is time. The integrated rate law is the solution to \(\eqref{eq:reaction-diff-eq},\) namely, \begin{equation} [\ce A] = [\ce A]_0 e^{-kt} \cma \label{eq:reaction} \end{equation} where \([\ce A]_0\) is the initial concentration of \(\ce A.\) \(\eqrefer{eq:reaction}\) asserts that when \(\ce A\) is reacted to form products, the concentration of \(\ce A\) decays exponentially with time (Figure 6).

FIRST-ORDER CHEMICAL REACTIONS
If the reaction of chemical species \(\ce A\) is first-order, then the rate law of the reaction is \[\deriv{\ce{[A]}}{t} = -k \ce{[A]} \cma \tag*{\eqref{eq:reaction-diff-eq}}\] where \(k\) is the rate constant, \([\ce A]\) is the concentration of \(\ce A,\) and \(t\) is time. The integrated rate law of the reaction is \[[\ce A] = [\ce A]_0 e^{-kt} \tag*{\eqref{eq:reaction}} \cma\] where \([\ce{A}]_0\) is the initial concentration of \(\ce A.\)
EXAMPLE 7
Sulfuryl chloride \((\ce{SO2Cl2})\) is a compound used in the production of pesticides. At high temperatures, sulfuryl chloride decomposes to sulfur dioxide \((\ce{SO2})\) and dichlorine \((\ce{Cl2}) \col\) \[\ce{SO2Cl2 -> SO2 + Cl2} \pd\] This reaction is first-order with respect to \(\ce{SO2Cl2}.\) A scientist performs the reaction, in which the initial concentration of \(\ce{SO2Cl2}\) is \(2\) grams per liter. The scientist measures the initial rate of the reaction to be \(0.004\) gram per liter per second.
  1. Write the rate law for the reaction.
  2. Find the integrated rate law for the reaction.
  3. What is the half-life of \(\ce{SO2Cl2} \ques\)
  1. The rate of the reaction is the magnitude of \(\textDeriv{[\ce{SO2Cl2}]}{t}.\) The rate law of the reaction is the differential equation that describes the concentration of \(\ce{SO2Cl2}.\) Because this reaction is first-order, \(\eqref{eq:reaction-diff-eq}\) gives the rate law of the reaction to be of the form \begin{equation} \deriv{[\ce{SO2Cl2}]}{t} = -k [\ce{SO2Cl2}] \cma \label{eq:rxn-sulfuryl} \end{equation} where \(k\) is the rate constant. Substituting the given initial rate (\(0.004\) grams per liter per second) and initial concentration (\(2\) grams per liter) into \(\eqref{eq:rxn-sulfuryl},\) we find \[0.004 = k(2) \implies k = 0.002 \pd\] So \(\eqref{eq:rxn-sulfuryl}\) becomes \[\boxed{\deriv{[\ce{SO2Cl2}]}{t} = -0.002 [\ce{SO2Cl2}]}\]
  2. The integrated rate law expresses the concentration of \(\ce{SO2Cl2}\) as a function of time \(t.\) Since the initial concentration of \(\ce{SO2Cl2}\) is \(2\) grams per liter, \(\eqref{eq:reaction}\) gives the integrated rate law to be \[\boxed{[\ce{SO2Cl2}] = 2e^{-0.002t}}\]
  3. By \(\eqref{eq:half-life},\) the half-life of \(\ce{SO2Cl2}\) in this reaction is \(\ln 2\) divided by the rate constantâ€”that is, \[\halfLife = \frac{\ln 2}{0.002} = \boxed{346.573 \un{sec}}\]

Newton's Law of Cooling

When a hot object rests in a cool environment, Newton's Law of Cooling states that the object's temperature exhibits exponential decay with time. The temperature \(T\) decreases at a rate proportional to the difference between \(T\) and the constant surrounding temperature \(T_s.\) If \(k\) is the decay constant and \(t\) is time, then the law gives the differential equation \begin{equation} \deriv{T}{t} = -k(T - T_s) \pd \label{eq:newtons-cooling-diff-eq} \end{equation}

We analogize \(\eqref{eq:newtons-cooling-diff-eq}\) \([\textDeriv{T}{t} = -k(T - T_s)]\) to \(\eqref{eq:exp-decay-diff-eq}\) \([\textDeriv{y}{x} = -ky]\) by identifying \(x = t\) and \(y = T - T_s.\) Let \(T_0\) be the object's initial temperature, and we assume \(T_0 \gt T_s\) because the object is cooling. Then the solution to \(\eqref{eq:newtons-cooling-diff-eq}\) is, by analogy to \(\eqref{eq:exp-decay}\) \([y = y_0 e^{-kx}],\) \begin{equation*} T - T_s = (T_0 - T_s) e^{-kt} \cma \nonumber \nl \end{equation*} or \begin{equation} T = T_s + (T_0 - T_s) e^{-kt} \pd \label{eq:newtons-cooling} \end{equation} The constant \(k\) is a property of the cooling object. \(\eqrefer{eq:newtons-cooling}\) enables us to determine at any time the temperature of a cooling object if its initial temperature \(T_0\) and the surrounding temperature \(T_s\) are known, and if we can measure the object's temperature after some time has elapsed.

The graph of \(\eqrefer{eq:newtons-cooling}\) is illustrated in Figure 7: The object's temperature \(T\) begins at \(T_0\) before leveling off to \(T_s,\) the surrounding temperature. Note that \(T\) is always above \(T_s,\) which is logical because the cooling object shouldn't reach a temperature below the ambient temperature. This model assumes that the object's heat dissipation does not warm up the surrounding environment. If the object is smallâ€”for example, a cup of hot tea or a freshly baked cookieâ€”then this assumption is appropriate.

NEWTON'S LAW OF COOLING
If a hot object cools in an environment of constant temperature, then Newton's Law of Cooling asserts that the object's temperature exhibits exponential decay with time. If \(T_0\) is the object's initial temperature, \(T_s\) is the temperature of the surrounding environment \((T_s \lt T_0)\), and \(T\) is the object's temperature as a function of time \(t,\) then \begin{equation*} \deriv{T}{t} = -k(T - T_s) \pd \tag*{\eqref{eq:newtons-cooling-diff-eq}} \end{equation*} Here, \(k\) is a constant inherent to the cooling object. The solution to the differential equation is \begin{equation*} T = T_s + (T_0 - T_s) e^{-kt} \pd \tag*{\eqref{eq:newtons-cooling}} \end{equation*}
EXAMPLE 8
A cake is removed from a \(180 \celcius\) oven and placed in a \(20 \celcius\) room. After \(10\) minutes, the cake's temperature is \(60 \celcius.\) Determine the cake's temperature \(20\) minutes after it was taken out of the oven.

Newton's Law of Cooling states that the cake's temperature \(T\) undergoes exponential decay with time \(t.\) The cake's initial temperature is \(180 \celcius,\) and the surrounding temperature is \(20 \celcius.\) So by \(\eqref{eq:newtons-cooling-diff-eq}\) the cake's temperature satisfies the differential equation \(\textDeriv{T}{t} = -k(T - 20),\) for which \(\eqref{eq:newtons-cooling}\) gives the solution \begin{equation} T(t) = 20 + 160 e^{-kt} \pd \label{eq:cake-cooling-T} \end{equation}

Our objective is now to find the cooling constant \(k.\) We do so by substituting in \(\eqref{eq:cake-cooling-T}\) the initial condition \(T(10) = 60 \col\) \[ \ba 20 + 160e^{-10k} &= 60 \nl 160e^{-10k} &= 40 \nl e^{-10k} &= \tfrac{40}{160} = \tfrac{1}{4} \nl -10k &= \ln \tfrac{1}{4} \nl k &= -\tfrac{1}{10} \ln \tfrac{1}{4} = \tfrac{1}{10} \ln 4 \pd \ea \] Consequently, \(\eqrefer{eq:cake-cooling-T}\) becomes \[ \ba T(t) &= 20 + 160e^{-[(\ln 4)/10]t} \nl &= 20 + 160(4)^{-t/10} \pd \ea \] After \(20\) minutes, the cake's temperature is \[T(30) = 20 + 160(4)^{-20/10} = \boxed{30 \celcius}\]

Discharging a Capacitor

A resistanceâ€“capacitance (RC) circuit consists of a capacitor and resistor. A capacitor is a device that stores electrical energy on one of its conducting surfaces, and capacitance \(C\) (unit, farads) is the ability of a capacitor to hold a charge \(Q\) (unit, coulombs). In Figure 8, a charged capacitor is connected to a resistor of resistance \(R\) (unit, ohms). As current travels through the circuit, the capacitor is discharging and the resistance dissipates the energy in the circuit. Applying Kirchhoff's Loop Rule gives the differential equation \[\frac{Q}{C} + R \deriv{Q}{t} = 0 \pd\] Solving for \(\textDeriv{Q}{t}\) and letting \(\tau = RC\) (\(\tau\) is the greek letter tau), we attain \begin{equation} \deriv{Q}{t} = -\frac{1}{\tau} Q \pd \label{eq:rc-diff-eq} \end{equation} We compare \(\eqref{eq:rc-diff-eq}\) \([\textDeriv{Q}{t} = -(1/\tau)Q]\) to \(\eqref{eq:exp-decay-diff-eq}\) \([\textDeriv{y}{x} = -ky]\) by identifying \(k = 1/\tau.\) Through this comparison, we see that large values of \(\tau\)â€”called the time constant of an RC circuitâ€”cause \(Q\) to decay slowly because the decay constant \(1/\tau\) is small. In contrast, small values of \(\tau\) enable \(Q\) to dissipate quickly due to the large decay constant. This interpretation is logical, since resistance \(R\) impedes the flow of current and capacitance \(C\) allows the capacitor to store charge; so large values of either quantity slow the capacitor's discharge. If \(Q_0\) is the initial charge on the capacitor, then the solution to \(\eqref{eq:rc-diff-eq}\) is \begin{equation} Q = Q_0 e^{-t/\tau} \pd \label{eq:rc-Q} \end{equation}

Similar to how half-lives are a standard to measure decay, the level of charge \(Q\) on the capacitor can be gauged by how many time constants have elapsed. If \(\tau = 1,\) then \(\eqref{eq:rc-Q}\) becomes \(Q = Q_0 e^{-1} \approx 0.368 Q_0.\) Likewise, if \(\tau = 2\) then \(\eqref{eq:rc-Q}\) gives \(Q = Q_0 e^{-2} \approx 0.135Q_0.\) Thus, after one time constant has elapsed, approximately \(63.2 \%\) of the capacitor's charge has been dissipated. After two time constants have passed, approximately \(86.5\%\) of the charge has been lost. This pattern is represented by the graph in Figure 9. Calculating the time constant helps an electrician ensure that a circuit is sufficiently discharged and safe to operate on.

EXAMPLE 9
A fully charged capacitor of capacitance \(C = 5 \times 10^{-5}\) farad holds a charge of \(0.001\) coulomb. The capacitor is then connected to a resistor, whose resistance is \(R = 600\) ohms. Determine a function for the capacitor's charge as a function of time. How much charge remains on the capacitor after four time constants have elapsed?

Exponential Growth A quantity \(y\) undergoes exponential growth with \(x\) if \[\deriv{y}{x} = ky \cma \tag*{\eqref{eq:exp-growth-diff-eq}}\] where \(k\) is the growth constant. The solution is \[y = y_0e^{kx} \cma \tag*{\eqref{eq:exp-growth}}\] where \(y_0\) is the value of \(y\) when \(x = 0.\) Exponential growth has applications in population growth and compound interest.

Doubling Time If \(y\) grows exponentially with time \(t,\) then \(y\) takes the same time to increase by a constant factor. The doubling time of \(y\) is \[\doublingTime = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \pd \tag*{\eqref{eq:doubling-time}}\] This formula applies only if the base of the exponential model is \(e.\) We use the doubling time to determine how quickly a quantityâ€”such as a population or bank depositâ€”doubles.

Compound Interest Money that is lent earns interest over time. Simple interest is paid once over a time period, whereas compound interest is paid multiple times over a time period. If a principal amount of \(P\) dollars grows at an interest rate of \(r,\) compounded \(n\) times per time period, then after \(t\) time periods the amount of money becomes \begin{equation*} A = P \par{1 + \frac{r}{n}}^{nt} \pd \tag*{\eqref{eq:compound-interest}} \end{equation*} Conversely, if the principal amount of money \(P\) grows continuously at the interest rate \(r,\) then after \(t\) time periods the money becomes \begin{equation*} A = Pe^{rt} \pd \tag*{\eqref{eq:compound-interest-cont}} \end{equation*}

Exponential Decay A quantity \(y\) undergoes exponential decay with \(x\) if \[\deriv{y}{x} = -ky \cma \tag*{\eqref{eq:exp-decay-diff-eq}}\] where \(k\) is the decay constant. If \(y_0\) is the value of \(y\) when \(x = 0,\) then the solution is \[y = y_0e^{-kx} \pd \tag*{\eqref{eq:exp-decay}}\] Exponential decay has applications in science, especially in radioactive dating.

Half-Life Suppose \(y\) decays exponentially with time \(t.\) Then the half-life of \(y\) is \[\halfLife = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \cma \tag*{\eqref{eq:half-life}}\] where \(k\) is the decay constant. We use this equation only if the base of the exponential model is \(e.\) This form is identical to the expression for doubling time. The half-life is a standard we use to analyze how rapidly a quantity shrinks.

First-Order Chemical Reactions If the reaction of chemical species \(\ce A\) is first-order, then the rate law of the reaction is \[\deriv{\ce{[A]}}{t} = -k \ce{[A]} \cma \tag*{\eqref{eq:reaction-diff-eq}}\] where \(k\) is the rate constant, \([\ce A]\) is the concentration of \(\ce A,\) and \(t\) is time. The integrated rate law of the reaction is \[[\ce A] = [\ce A]_0 e^{-kt} \tag*{\eqref{eq:reaction}} \cma\] where \([\ce{A}]_0\) is the initial concentration of \(\ce A.\)

Newton's Law of Cooling If a hot object is left to cool in an environment with constant temperature, then Newton's Law of Cooling asserts that the object's temperature exhibits exponential decay with time. In this decay, the object's temperature decreases at a rate proportional to the difference between its own temperature and the ambient temperature. If \(T_0\) is the object's initial temperature, \(T_s\) is the temperature of the surrounding environment \((T_s \lt T_0)\), and \(T\) is the object's temperature as a function of time \(t,\) then \begin{equation*} \deriv{T}{t} = -k(T - T_s) \pd \tag*{\eqref{eq:newtons-cooling-diff-eq}} \end{equation*} Here, \(k\) is a constant inherent to the cooling object. The solution to the differential equation is \begin{equation*} T = T_s + (T_0 - T_s) e^{-kt} \pd \tag*{\eqref{eq:newtons-cooling}} \end{equation*}
