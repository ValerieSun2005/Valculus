EXERCISE 5
You walk \(3\) meters west and \(4\) meters north. How far are you from where you started? (This measure is your displacement.)
SOLUTION Your path is a right triangle of base \(3\) and height \(4.\) By the Pythagorean Theorem, your displacement (the hypotenuse of your path) is given by \[d^2 = 3^2 + 4^2 = 25 \implies \boxed{d = 5 \un{m}}\]
EXERCISE 6
A wind gust has a speed of \(6\) miles per hour to the east, and another wind gust has a speed of \(8\) miles per hour to the north. What is the speed of the combined gust?
SOLUTION The combined gust is the hypotenuse of a right triangle with base \(6\) and height \(8.\) By the Pythagorean Theorem, its speed is given by \[v^2 = 6^2 + 8^2 = 100 \implies \boxed{v = 10 \un{mph}}\]
EXERCISE 7
Consider two points \(R(-4, 3)\) and \(S(5, -1)\) in the \(xy\)-plane.
  1. In which quadrant does \(R\) reside, and in which quadrant does \(S\) reside?
  2. Determine the midpoint of line segment \(\overline{RS}.\)
  3. Calculate the distance between points \(R\) and \(S.\)
SOLUTION
  1. Point \(R\) resides in the second quadrant. Point \(S\) is in the fourth quadrant.
  2. The Midpoint Formula gives \[ \par{\frac{-4 + 5}{2} \cma \frac{3 + (-1)}{2}} = \boxed{\par{\tfrac{1}{2}, 1}} \]
  3. The Distance Formula gives \[ \ba \length{RS} &= \sqrt{[5 - (-4)]^2 + (-1 - 3)^2} \nl &= \sqrt{81 + 16} \nl &= \boxed{\sqrt{97}} \approx 9.849 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 8
Point \(E\) is the midpoint of line segment \(\segment{DF}.\) The coordinates of \(D\) are \((3, 5),\) and the coordinates of \(E\) are \((6, 2).\) What are the coordinates of point \(F \ques\)
SOLUTION Let \(\par{x_F, y_F}\) be the coordinates of \(F.\) Then the Midpoint Formula gives \[(6, 2) = \par{\frac{3 + x_F}{2} \cma \frac{5 + y_F}{2} } \pd\] Solving each equation, we get \[ \ba \frac{3 + x_F}{2} &= 6 \lspace &\frac{5 + y_F}{2} &= 2 \nl 3 + x_F &= 12 \lspace &5 + y_F &= 4 \nl x_F &= 9 \lspace &y_F &= -1 \pd \ea \] Hence, the coordinates of \(F\) are \(\boxed{(9, -1)}.\)
EXERCISE 9
Two triangles with areas \(40\) and \(A_2\) are attached to a rectangle with area \(A_1,\) as shown by Figure 12. Calculate the two areas \(A_1\) and \(A_2,\) as well as the three side lengths \(\ell_1,\) \(\ell_2,\) and \(\ell_3.\)
SOLUTION It is easiest to first find \(\ell_3;\) the Pythagorean Theorem gives \[ \ba 7^2 + \par{\ell_3}^2 &= 9^2 \nl \ell_3 &= \sqrt{9^2 - 7^2} = \sqrt{32} \pd \ea \] A triangle's area is given by half its base length multiplied by its height: \[A_2 = \tfrac{1}{2} (7) \par{\ell_3} = \tfrac{1}{2} (7) (\sqrt{32})= \frac{7 \sqrt{32}}{2} \pd\] The rectangle's area is \[A_1 = 10 \ell_3 = 10 \sqrt{32} \pd\] Considering the top triangle's area shows \[40 = \tfrac{1}{2} (10) \ell_1 \implies \ell_1 = 8 \pd\] Then the Pythagorean Theorem gives \[ \ba 10^2 + \underbrace{8^2}_{\par{\ell_1}^2} &= \par{\ell_2}^2 \nl \sqrt{164} &= \ell_2 \pd \ea \] In summary, the quantities are \[ \boxed{ \baat{2} \ell_1 &= 8 \nl \ell_2 &= \sqrt{164} &&\approx 12.806 \nl \ell_3 &= \sqrt{32} &&\approx 5.657 \nl A_1 &= 10 \sqrt{32} &&\approx 56.569 \nl A_2 &= \frac{7 \sqrt{32}}{2} &&\approx 19.799 \eaat } \]

EXERCISE 28
A circular sector has a central angle of \(40 \degree\) and a radius of \(8.\)
  1. Convert \(40 \degree\) to radians.
  2. Find the sector's arc length.
  3. Calculate the area of the sector.
SOLUTION Let \(r = 8.\)
  1. We convert from degrees to radians by multiplying by \(\pi/180 \col\) \[\theta = 40 \times \frac{\pi}{180} = \boxed{\frac{2 \pi}{9}}\]
  2. The arc length is \[ \ba s &= r \, \theta \nl &= 8 \par{\frac{2 \pi}{9}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{16 \pi}{9}} \approx 5.585 \pd \ea \]
  3. The area is \[ \ba A &= \tfrac{1}{2} \theta \, r^2 \nl &= \tfrac{1}{2} \par{\frac{2 \pi}{9}} (8)^2 \nl &= \boxed{\frac{64 \pi}{9}} \approx 22.340 \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 29
In Figure 13 calculate the arc length of the minor arc \(\arcHat{AB}\) and the major arc \(\arcHatLong{ACB}.\)
SOLUTION Minor arc \(\arcHat{AB}\) is intercepted by the \(100 \degree\) central angle. Major arc \(\arcHatLong{ACB}\) is the remaining perimeter of the circle. In each calculation, the radius is \(r = 3.\)

Minor Arc In radians, the central angle is \[\theta = 100 \times \frac{\pi}{180} = \frac{5 \pi}{9} \pd\] Then the length of \(\arcHat{AB}\) is \[ \ba \arc{AB} &= r \, \theta \nl &= 3 \par{\frac{5 \pi}{9}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{5 \pi}{3}} \approx 5.236 \pd \ea \]

Major Arc The major arc is intercepted by a central angle of measure \(360 \degree - 100 \degree\) \(= 260 \degree,\) which in radians is \[\theta = 260 \times \frac{\pi}{180} = \frac{13 \pi}{9} \pd\] Then the length of \(\arcHatLong{ACB}\) is \[ \ba \arc{ACB} &= r \, \theta \nl &= 3 \par{\frac{13 \pi}{9}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{13 \pi}{3}} \approx 13.614 \pd \ea \]

Observe that the entire circle's perimeter is \(2 \pi(3)\) \(\approx 18.850.\) The sum of the minor arc length and the major arc length should therefore equal this value; indeed, \[5.236 + 13.614 \equalsCheck 18.850 \pd\]

EXERCISE 30
At pizzeria A, a \(14\)-inch pizza is cut into \(8\) equal-sized slices, each sold for \(\$2.50.\) At pizzeria B, an \(18\)-inch pizza is cut into \(10\) equal-sized slices, each sold for \(\$3.\) Which pizzeria offers the better deal?
SOLUTION For each pizzeria, let's calculate the area of pizza per dollar. The pizzeria that offers more area per dollar has the better deal.

Pizzeria A The area of the entire pie is \(\pi (14)^2 = 196 \pi.\) So the area of each slice, in square inches, is \[\frac{196 \pi}{8} = 24.5 \pi \approx 76.969 \pd\] Then the area, in square inches, per dollar is \[\frac{76.969}{2.50} \approx 30.788 \pd\]

Pizzeria B The area of the entire pie is \(\pi (18)^2 = 324 \pi.\) So the area of each slice, in square inches, is \[\frac{324 \pi}{10} = 32.4 \pi \approx 101.788 \pd\] Then the area, in square inches, per dollar is \[\frac{101.788}{3} \approx 33.929 \pd\]

Because \(30.788 \lt 33.929,\) Pizzeria B has the better deal.

EXERCISE 32
A wheel's radius is \(10\) inches. Calculate the distance the wheel travels if its axle rotates \(40 \degree.\)
SOLUTION Our goal is to consider the distance traveled by a point on the wheel's rim, which is given by the arc length of the sector whose central angle is \(40 \degree.\) In radians, this angle is \[\theta = 40 \times \frac{\pi}{180} = \frac{2 \pi}{9} \pd\] Thus, the arc length is \[ \ba s &= r \, \theta \nl &= 10 \par{\frac{2 \pi}{9}} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{20 \pi}{9}} \approx 6.981 \un{in} \pd \ea \]
EXERCISE 33
Derive formulas for the arc length and area of a circular sector whose radius is \(r\) if the central angle \(\theta\) is measured in degrees.
SOLUTION The entire circle has a central angle of \(360 \degree\) and a perimeter of \(2 \pi r.\) Hence, the circular sector's arc length is a proportion \(\theta/360\) of this valueâ€”namely, \[s = \frac{\theta}{360} (2 \pi r) = \boxed{\frac{\pi \theta \, r}{180}}\] Using similar logic, the entire circle's area is \(\pi r^2;\) the circular sector's area is a proportion \(\theta/360\) of this expression: \[A = \frac{\theta}{360} \par{\pi r^2} = \boxed{\frac{\pi \theta \, r^2}{360}}\] Compare these expressions to the much more elegant formulas \(s = r \, \theta\) and \(A = \tfrac{1}{2} \theta \, r^2\) if \(\theta\) is in radians.
EXERCISE 34
If a circular sector has an area of \(32 \pi\) and an arc length of \(6 \pi,\) then calculate its radius and central angle.
SOLUTION To calculate both unknown quantities \(r\) and \(\theta,\) we establish two equations: \[ \ba 32 \pi &= \tfrac{1}{2} \theta \, r^2 \nl 6 \pi &= r \, \theta \pd \ea \] Dividing both equations, we get \[ \tfrac{32}{6} = \tfrac{1}{2} r \implies \boxed{r = \tfrac{32}{3}} \] So the central angle is given by \[6 \pi = \tfrac{32}{3} \theta \implies \boxed{\theta = \frac{9 \pi}{16}}\]
EXERCISE 35
Region \(R\) is the region bounded between a quarter-circle of radius \(5\) and a diagonal line segment that connects two of the quarter-circle's vertices, as shown by Figure 14.
  1. Calculate the area of region \(R.\)
  2. Find the perimeter of region \(R.\)
  3. What percentage of the quarter-circle is filled by region \(R \ques\)
SOLUTION
  1. Note that the central angle of the circular sector is \(90 \degree\) \(= \pi/2\) radians. The quarter-circle's area is therefore \[\frac{1}{2} \par{\frac{\pi}{2}} (5)^2 = \frac{25 \pi}{4} \pd\] The area of triangle \(\Delta OPQ\) is \(\tfrac{1}{2}(5)(5)\) \(= 25/2.\) Hence, the area of region \(R\) is \[\frac{25 \pi}{4} - \frac{25}{2} = \boxed{\frac{25}{4} (\pi - 2)} \approx 7.135 \pd\]
  2. The arc length of the sector is \[\arc{PQ} = 5 \par{\frac{\pi}{2}} = \frac{5 \pi}{2} \pd\] The length of the diagonal line is, by the Pythagorean Theorem, \[\length{PQ} = \sqrt{5^2 + 5^2} = \sqrt{50} \pd\] So the perimeter of region \(R\) is \[\boxed{\frac{5 \pi}{2} + \sqrt{50}} \approx 14.925 \pd\]
  3. From part (a), the area of the entire quarter-circle is \(25\pi/4\) \(\approx 19.635\) and the area of region \(R\) is approximately \(7.135.\) So the ratio of areas is \[\frac{7.135}{19.635} = 0.3634 = \boxed{36.34 \%}\]
