2.3 Exercise Solutions: Product Rule and Quotient Rule

EXERCISE 1
What is the Product Rule?
SOLUTION The Product Rule enables us to differentiate a product of functions. If \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable functions, then \[\par{fg}' = f'g + fg' \pd\]
EXERCISE 2
What is the Quotient Rule?
SOLUTION We use the Quotient Rule to differentiate a ratio of functions. If \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable functions such that \(g \ne 0,\) then \[\par{\frac{f}{g}}' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2} \pd\]
EXERCISE 3
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{\theta} \par{\csc \theta - 2\sec \theta}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION The derivative of \(\csc \theta\) is \(-\csc \theta \cot \theta,\) and the derivative of \(\sec \theta\) is \(\sec \theta \tan \theta.\) Hence, using the Difference and Constant Multiple Rules for Derivatives (from Section 2.1), we see \[ \ba \deriv{}{\theta} \par{\csc \theta - 2\sec \theta} &= \deriv{}{\theta} \par{\csc \theta} - 2 \deriv{}{\theta} \par{\sec \theta} \nl &= \boxed{-\csc \theta \cot \theta - 2 \sec \theta \tan \theta} \ea \]
EXERCISE 4
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{\theta} \par{3\tan \theta + \cot \theta}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION The derivative of \(\tan \theta\) is \(\sec^2 \theta,\) and the derivative of \(\cot \theta\) is \(-\csc^2 \theta.\) (The derivatives of tangent and cotangent are the only ones whose derivatives are squared.) Accordingly, using the Sum and Constant Multiple Rules for Derivatives (from Section 2.1) yields \[ \ba \deriv{}{\theta} \par{3\tan \theta + \cot \theta} &= 3 \deriv{}{\theta} \par{\tan \theta} + \deriv{}{\theta} \par{\cot \theta} \nl &= \boxed{3 \sec^2 \theta - \csc^2 \theta} \ea \]
EXERCISE 5
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{x \sin x}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION The expression \(x \sin x\) is a product of two functions, \[f(x) = x \and g(x) = \sin x \pd\] Note that \(f'(x) = 1\) and \(g'(x) = \cos x.\) Thus, the Product Rule gives the derivative of \(x \sin x \) to be \[ \ba f'g + fg' &= (1) \sin x + x(\cos x) \nl &= \boxed{\sin x + x \cos x} \ea \]
EXERCISE 6
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{4x^2 \cos x}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION The expression \(4x^2 \cos x\) is the product \(f(x) g(x),\) where \[f(x) = 4x^2 \and g(x) = \cos x \pd\] Differentiating \(f\) and \(g\) gives \[f'(x) = 8x \and g'(x) = -\sin x \pd\] Then the Product Rule shows that the derivative of \(4x^2 \cos x\) is \[ \ba f'g + fg' &= (8x)\cos x + 4x^2(-\sin x) \nl &= \boxed{8x \cos x - 4x^2 \sin x} \ea \]
EXERCISE 7
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \parbr{(\sin x) \sqrt x \,} \]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION We have a product \(f(x)g(x)\) with \[f(x) = \sin x \and g(x) = \sqrt{x} = x^{1/2} \pd\] So \[f'(x) = \cos x \and g'(x) = \tfrac{1}{2} x^{-1/2} \pd\] Thus, the derivative of \((\sin x) \sqrt{x}\) is \[ \ba f'g + fg' &= \par{\cos x} \sqrt{x} + \sin x \par{\tfrac{1}{2} x^{-1/2}} \nl &= \boxed{\sqrt{x} \cos x + \frac{\sin x}{2 \sqrt{x}}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 8
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{t} \par{2t \tan t}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION Recall that the derivative of \(\tan t\) is \(\sec^2 t.\) In this product \(f(t) g(t),\) we see \[ \baat{2} f(t) &= 2t \lspace g(t) &&= \tan t \nl f'(t) &= 2 \lspace g'(t) &&= \sec^2 t \pd \eaat \] The derivative of \(2t \tan t\) is therefore \[ \ba f'g + fg' &= (2) \tan t + 2t \par{\sec^2 t} \nl &= \boxed{2\tan t + 2t \sec^2 t} \ea \]
EXERCISE 9
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{\pi^2 e^3}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION The expression \(\pi^2 e^3\) is a constant, so its derivative is \(\boxed 0.\)
EXERCISE 10
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{u} \par{u \sqrt u}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION Instead of using the Product Rule, we write \[u \sqrt u = u \cdot u^{1/2} = u^{3/2} \pd\] The Power Rule therefore gives the derivative to be \[\tfrac{3}{2} u^{1/2} = \boxed{\tfrac{3}{2} \sqrt u}\]
EXERCISE 11
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{e^x \sec x}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION The derivative of \(e^x\) is \(e^x,\) and the derivative of \(\sec x\) is \(\sec x \tan x.\) Therefore, the Product Rule shows the derivative of \(e^x \sec x\) to be \[\par{e^x} \sec x + e^x \par{\sec x \tan x} = \boxed{e^x \sec x + e^x \sec x \tan x}\]
EXERCISE 12
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{\frac{1}{x} \, \tan x}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION
It is also valid to use the Quotient Rule by rewriting the function as \((\tan x)/x.\)
We rewrite the expression as \(x^{-1} \tan x.\) We may use the Product Rule by identifying \[ \baat{2} f(x) &= x^{-1} \quad \lspace g(x) &&= \tan x \nl f'(x) &= -x^{-2} \lspace g'(x) &&= \sec^2 x \pd \eaat \] Thus, the derivative is \[ \ba f'g + fg' &= \par{-x^{-2}} \tan x + x^{-1} \par{\sec^2 x} \nl &= \boxed{-\frac{\tan x}{x^2} + \frac{\sec^2 x}{x}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 13
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \parbr{\par{x^3 + 2} \par{x^7 - 4}}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION This expression is a product \(fg\) with \[f(x) = x^3 + 2 \and g(x) = x^7 - 4 \pd\] Observe that \[f'(x) = 3x^2 \and g'(x) = 7x^6 \pd\] So the derivative becomes \[f'g + fg' = \boxed{3x^2 \par{x^7 - 4} + 7x^6 \par{x^3 + 2}} \]
EXERCISE 14
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} e^{2x}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION Using exponential properties, we write \[e^{2x} = e^{x + x} = e^x \cdot e^x \pd\] The Product Rule therefore gives \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} e^{2x} &= \par{e^x} \par{e^x} + \par{e^x} \par{e^x} \nl &= 2 \par{e^x \, e^x} \nl &= \boxed{2e^{2x}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 15
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{\frac{\sin x}{x + 8}}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION The expression is a quotient \(f/g\) of two functions: \[f(x) = \sin x \and g(x) = x + 8 \pd\] We note that \(f'(x) = \cos x\) and \(g'(x) = 1.\) By the Quotient Rule, the derivative of \(\sin x/(x + 8)\) is \[ \ba \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2} &= \frac{(\cos x)(x + 8) - \sin x (1)}{(x + 8)^2} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{(x + 8) \cos x - \sin x}{(x + 8)^2}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 16
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{t} \par{\frac{7t^3 + t^2 - 8t + 3}{t}}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION There exists no good reason to drag out the differentiation process by using the Quotient Rule. Instead, we first simplify the expression as follows: \[\frac{7t^3}{t} + \frac{t^2}{t} - \frac{8t}{t} + \frac{3}{t} = 7t^2 + t - 8 + 3t^{-1} \pd\] Using derivative rules from Section 2.1 in conjunction with the Power Rule, we see \[ \ba \deriv{}{t} \par{7t^2 + t - 8 + 3t^{-1}} &= 14t + 1 - 0 - 3t^{-2} \nl &= \boxed{14t + 1 - \frac{3}{t^2}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 17
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{y} \par{\frac{3y^2 + 4y - \sqrt y}{y^3}}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION Instead of applying the Quotient Rule, we write \(\sqrt y\) as \(y^{1/2}\) and expand the quotient to attain \[\frac{3y^2}{y^3} + \frac{4y}{y^3} - \frac{y^{1/2}}{y^3} = \frac{3}{y} + \frac{4}{y^2} - \frac{1}{y^{5/2}} \pd\] Using derivative rules from Section 2.1 in conjunction with the Power Rule, we see \[ \ba \deriv{}{y} \par{\frac{3}{y} + \frac{4}{y^2} - \frac{1}{y^{5/2}}} &= \deriv{}{y} \par{3y^{-1} + 4y^{-2} - y^{-5/2}} \nl &= -3y^{-2} - 8y^{-3} + \tfrac{5}{2} y^{-7/2} \nl &= \boxed{-\frac{3}{y^2} - \frac{8}{y^3} + \frac{5}{2 \sqrt{y^7}}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 18
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{\frac{\cos x}{x^2 + 4}}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION We have a quotient \(f/g\) with \[f(x) = \cos x \and g(x) = x^2 + 4 \pd\] The derivatives of these functions are \(f'(x) = -\sin x\) and \(g'(x) = 2x.\) Thus, the derivative of \((\cos x)/(x^2 + 4)\) is \[ \ba \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2} &= \frac{(- \sin x)(x^2 + 4) - \cos x \,(2x)}{(x^2 + 4)^2} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{-x^2\sin x - 4 \sin x - 2x \cos x}{(x^2 + 4)^2}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 19
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{\frac{x^2}{\tan x}}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION Using the Quotient Rule with \[ \baat{2} f(x) &= x^2 \lspace g(x) &&= \tan x \nl f'(x) &= 2x \lspace g'(x) &&= \sec^2 x \cma \eaat \] we obtain \[ \ba \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2} &= \boxed{\frac{2x \tan x - x^2 \sec^2 x}{\tan^2 x}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 20
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{\frac{3\csc x}{2x - 6}}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION The derivative of the numerator is \(- 3\csc x \cot x,\) and the derivative of the denominator is \(2.\) Thus, the Quotient Rule gives the derivative of \((3\csc x)/(2x - 6)\) to be \[ \frac{(-3\csc x \cot x)(2x - 6) - 3\csc x(2)}{(2x - 6)^2} = \boxed{\frac{18 \csc x \cot x - 6x \csc x \cot x - 6 \csc x}{(2x - 6)^2}} \]
EXERCISE 21
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{t} \par{\frac{t^2}{1 - t^2}}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION Observe that differentiating the numerator gives \(2t\) and that differentiating the denominator results in \(-2t.\) Thus, the Quotient Rule gives the derivative of the fraction to be \[ \ba \frac{(2t) \par{1 - t^2} - t^2 \par{-2t}}{\par{1 - t^2}^2} &= \frac{2t - 2t^3 + 2t^3}{\par{1 - t^2}^2} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{2t}{\par{1 - t^2}^2}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 22
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{y} \par{\frac{y^3}{2e^y - 5}}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION The derivative of the numerator is \(3y^2,\) and the derivative of the denominator is \(2e^y.\) Accordingly, by the Quotient Rule the derivative of the fraction is \[ \frac{\par{3y^2} \par{2e^y - 5} - y^3 \par{2e^y}}{\par{2e^y - 5}^2} = \boxed{\frac{6y^2 e^y - 15y^2 - 2y^3 e^y}{\par{2e^y - 5}^2}} \]
EXERCISE 23
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{u} \par{\frac{u^4}{u^2 + 4}}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION Observe that the derivative of the numerator is \(4u^3\) and the derivative of the denominator is \(2u.\) Thus, the Quotient Rule gives the derivative of the fraction to be \[ \ba \frac{\par{4u^3} \par{u^2 + 4} - u^4 \par{2u}}{\par{u^2 + 4}^2} &= \frac{4u^5 + 16u^3 - 2u^5}{\par{u^2 + 4}^2} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{2u^5 + 16u^3}{\par{u^2 + 4}^2}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 24
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{\frac{\dfrac{\sin x}{x}}{e^x}}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION We could use the Quotient Rule twice—once to differentiate \((\sin x)/x\) and again to differentiate the entire fraction. But instead, it is easier to rewrite the fraction as \((\sin x)/(xe^x).\) Notice that \[\deriv{}{x} (\sin x) = \cos x \and \deriv{}{x} \par{xe^x} = e^x + xe^x \pd\] Thus, the Quotient Rule gives \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \par{\frac{\sin x}{xe^x}} &= \frac{(\cos x) \par{xe^x} - \par{e^x + xe^x} \par{\sin x}}{\par{xe^x}^2} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{x \cos x - \sin x - x \sin x}{x^2 e^x}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 25
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{\frac{\cot x}{5\sqrt{x} - 3}}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION The derivative of the numerator is \(-\csc^2 x,\) and the derivative of the denominator is \(5/(2 \sqrt x).\) Thus, the Quotient Rule gives the derivative of \(\cot x/(5\sqrt{x} - 3)\) to be \[ \ba \frac{(-\csc^2 x)(5\sqrt{x} - 3) - \cot x \par{\frac{5}{2 \sqrt x}} }{(5\sqrt{x} - 3)^2} = \boxed{\frac{(3 - 5 \sqrt{x}) \csc^2 x - \frac{5 \cot x}{2 \sqrt x}}{(5\sqrt{x} - 3)^2}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 26
For
\[\ds \derivOrder{}{y}{2} \par{2y^4 \sin y}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION Recall that \[\derivOrder{}{y}{2} \par{2y^4 \sin y} = \deriv{}{y} \parbr{\deriv{}{y} \par{2y^4 \sin y}} \pd\] The first derivative is, by the Product Rule, \[ \ba \deriv{}{y} \par{2y^4 \sin y} &= \par{8y^3} \sin y + 2y^4 \par{\cos y} \nl &= 8y^3 \sin y + 2 y^4 \cos y \pd \ea \] In obtaining the second derivative, we use the Product Rule and Quotient Rule. The Sum Rule for Derivatives asserts that the derivative of a sum is a sum of derivatives (see Section 2.1 to review properties of derivatives). We therefore see \[ \ba \derivOrder{}{y}{2} \par{2y^4 \sin y} &= \deriv{}{y} \par{8y^3 \sin y + 2 y^4 \cos y} \nl &= \orange{\deriv{}{y} \par{8y^3 \sin y}} + \teal{\deriv{}{y} \par{2y^4 \cos y}} \nl &= \orange{\par{24y^2} \sin y + 8y^3 \par{\cos y}} + \teal{\par{8y^3} \cos y + 2y^4 (-\sin y)} \nl &= \boxed{24y^2 \sin y + 16y^3 \cos y - 2y^4 \sin y} \ea \] The algebra in this answer is tricky. We suggest that you keep practicing differentiation to minimize the chance of errors.
EXERCISE 27
For
\[\ds \derivOrder{}{x}{2} \par{e^x \sqrt x}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION Remember that \[\derivOrder{}{x}{2} \par{e^x \sqrt x} = \deriv{}{x} \parbr{\deriv{}{x} \par{e^x \sqrt x}} \pd\] To find the first derivative, we apply the Product Rule to see \[ \ba \deriv{}{x} \par{e^x \sqrt x} &= \par{e^x} \sqrt x + e^x \par{\frac{1}{2 \sqrt x}} \nl &= e^x \sqrt x + \frac{e^x}{2 \sqrt x} \pd \ea \] In obtaining the second derivative, we use the Product Rule again. The Sum Rule for Derivatives asserts that the derivative of a sum is a sum of derivatives (see Section 2.1 to review properties of derivatives). Thus, we find \[ \ba \derivOrder{}{x}{2} \par{e^x \sqrt x} &= \deriv{}{x} \par{e^x \sqrt x + \frac{e^x}{2 \sqrt x}} \nl &= \orange{\deriv{}{x} \par{e^x \sqrt x}} + \teal{\deriv{}{x} \par{\frac{e^x}{2 \sqrt x}}} \nl &= \orange{e^x \sqrt x + \frac{e^x}{2 \sqrt x}} + \teal{\frac{e^x \par{2 \sqrt x} - e^x \par{2 \cdot \frac{1}{2 \sqrt x}}}{\par{2 \sqrt x}^2}} \nl &= \boxed{e^x \sqrt x + \frac{e^x}{2 \sqrt x} + \frac{2 e^x \sqrt x - \frac{e^x}{\sqrt x}}{4 x}} \nl \ea \] The algebra involved in differentiation can often weary a student; the only remedy is to continue practicing differentiation.
EXERCISE 28
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{x^2 e^x \cos x}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION We have a product of three terms: \(x^2,\) \(e^x,\) and \(\cos x.\) Let's group two of the factors and treat the group as one term. For example, let \[f(x) = x^2 e^x \and g(x) = \cos x \pd\] So \[f'(x) = 2x e^x + x^2 e^x \and g'(x) = -\sin x \pd\] The derivative of \(x^2 e^x \cos x\) is then \[ \ba f'g + fg' &= (2x e^x + x^2 e^x) \cos x + (x^2 e^x) (-\sin x) \nl &= \boxed{2xe^x + x^2 e^x \cos x - x^2 e^x \sin x} \ea \] The algebra involved in differentiation can often weary a student; the only remedy is to continue practicing differentiation.

\[\ds \deriv{}{\theta} \par{\theta^2 \sec \theta \tan \theta}\]

For example, let \[f(\theta) = \theta^2 \sec \theta \and g(\theta) = \tan \theta \pd\] Recall that the derivative of \(\sec \theta\) is \(\sec \theta \tan \theta\) and that the derivative of \(\tan \theta\) is \(\sec^2 \theta.\) Thus, \[ \baf &&f'(\theta) &= 2 \theta \sec \theta + \theta^2 \sec \theta \tan \theta &\nl \laWord{and} && g'(\theta) &= \sec^2 \theta \pd \eaf \] Thus, the derivative of \(\theta^2 \sec \theta \tan \theta\) is \[ \ba f'g + fg' &= \par{2 \theta \sec \theta + \theta^2 \sec \theta \tan \theta} \par{\tan \theta} + \par{\theta^2 \sec \theta} \par{\sec^2 \theta} \nl &= \boxed{2 \theta \sec \theta \tan \theta + \theta^2 \sec \theta \tan^2 \theta + \theta^2 \sec^3 \theta} \ea \] The algebra in this answer is tricky. EXERCISE 31
For
\[\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{x^4 \csc x \cot x}\]
determine the derivative.
SOLUTION When we identify a product of three terms, we group two of the terms and treat the group as one function. For example, we could decide to group \[f(x) = x^4 \csc x \and g(x) = \cot x \pd\] Recall that the derivative of \(\csc x\) is \(-\csc x \cot x\) and that the derivative of \(\cot x\) is \(-\csc^2 x.\) (To aid your memory, observe that any trigonometric function containing the prefix co- exhibits a negative sign in its derivative.) We therefore find \[f'(x) = 4x^3 \csc x - x^4 \csc x \cot x \and g'(x) = -\csc^2 x \pd\] Accordingly, the derivative of \(x^4 \csc x \cot x\) is \[ \ba f'g + fg' &= \par{4x^3 \csc x - x^4 \csc x \cot x} \par{\cot x} + \par{x^4 \csc x} \par{-\csc^2 x} \nl &= \boxed{4x^3 \csc x \cot x - x^4 \csc x \cot^2 x - x^4 \csc^3 x} \ea \] We recommend that you continue practicing differentiation; you will soon commit fewer algebraic errors.
EXERCISE 32
By using the double-angle identity for sine, \(\sin 2x = 2 \sin x \cos x,\) determine \[\deriv{}{x} (\sin 2x) \pd\]
SOLUTION This derivative is equivalent to \[\deriv{}{x} \par{2 \sin x \cos x} \pd\] By the Product Rule, this derivative is \[2 \parbr{(\cos x)(\cos x) + (\sin x)(-\sin x)} = 2 \cos^2 x - 2 \sin^2 x \pd\] The double-angle identity for cosine says that \(\cos 2x = \cos^2 x - \sin^2 x.\) So \[\deriv{}{x} \sin 2x = \boxed{2 \cos 2x}\]
EXERCISE 33
Prove that the derivative of \(\csc x\) is \(-\csc x \cot x.\)
SOLUTION We write \(\csc x\) as \(1/\sin x,\) whose derivative is, by the Quotient Rule, \[ \ba \frac{(\sin x)\deriv{}{x} \par{1} - 1 \deriv{}{x} (\sin x)}{\sin^2 x} &= \frac{0 - \cos x}{\sin^2 x} \nl &= -\frac{1}{\sin x} \cdot \frac{\cos x}{\sin x} \nl &= -\csc x \cot x \cma \ea \] as requested.
EXERCISE 34
Prove that the derivative of \(\cot x\) is \(-\csc^2 x.\)
SOLUTION Since \(\cot x = \cos x/\sin x,\) the Quotient Rule gives the derivative to be \[ \ba \frac{(-\sin x) (\sin x) - (\cos x) (\cos x)}{\sin^2 x} &= \frac{-\sin^2 x - \cos^2 x}{\sin^2 x} \nl &= -\frac{\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x}{\sin^2 x} \nl \ea \] By the Pythagorean identity, \(\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1\) and so the derivative becomes \[-\frac{1}{\sin^2 x} = -\csc^2 x \cma\] as given.
EXERCISE 35
If \(g\) is differentiable, then the Reciprocal Rule for Derivatives states that \[\deriv{}{x} \parbr{\frac{1}{g(x)}} = -\frac{g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2} \pd\] Using the Quotient Rule, prove this Rule.
SOLUTION Observe that \[\deriv{}{x}(1) = 0 \and \deriv{}{x} [g(x)] = g'(x) \pd\] Thus, the Quotient Rule gives the derivative of \(1/g(x)\) to be \[\frac{0[g(x)] - 1 [g'(x)]}{[g(x)]^2} = -\frac{g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2} \cma\] as requested.
EXERCISE 36
Find all the \(x\)-coordinates at which the curve \(y = x^3 e^x\) has horizontal tangents.
SOLUTION The curve has a horizontal tangent at some \(x\) when \(\textderiv{y}{x} = 0.\) The Product Rule yields \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= (3x^2) e^x + x^3 \par{e^x} \nl &= e^x \par{x^3 + 3x^2} \pd \ea \] Equating this result to \(0\) gives the solutions \(\boxed{x = -3}\) and \(\boxed{x = 0}.\)
EXERCISE 37
Determine the \(x\)-coordinates at which the curve \(y = e^x \sqrt x\) has horizontal tangents.
SOLUTION The curve has a horizontal tangent at some \(x\) when \(\textderiv{y}{x} = 0.\) The Product Rule gives the derivative to be \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= \par{e^x} \sqrt x + e^x \par{\frac{1}{2 \sqrt x}} \nl &= e^x \par{\sqrt x + \frac{1}{2 \sqrt x}} \pd \ea \] But since this expression is always positive (note that \(x \gt 0\) is the domain), \(\textderiv{y}{x}\) is never \(0.\) Hence, the curve has no horizontal tangents.
EXERCISE 38
Suppose \(f\) is a differentiable function that satisfies \(f(3) = -1\) and \(f'(3) = 2.\) If \(a(x) = x^2 \cdot f(x),\) then calculate \(a'(3).\)
SOLUTION The Product Rule gives the derivative \(a'\) to be \[a'(x) = 2x f(x) + x^2 f'(x) \pd\] Substituting \(x = 3\) shows \[ \ba a'(3) &= 2(3) f(3) + (3)^2 f'(3) \nl &= 6 f(3) + 9 f'(3) \nl &= 6(-1) + 9(2) \nl &= \boxed{12} \ea \]
EXERCISE 39
Let \(f\) be a differentiable function that satisfies \(f(3) = 3\) and \(f'(3) = 2.\) Let \(k(x) = x^2 \cdot f(x).\) Find \(k'(3).\)
SOLUTION We have \[ \ba k'(x) &= 2x f(x) + x^2 f'(x) \cma \nl k'(3) &= 6 f(3) + 9 f'(3) \period \ea \] Plugging in the given values of \(f(3)\) and \(f'(3)\) gives \[k'(3) = 6(3) + 9(2) = \boxed{36}\]
EXERCISE 40
Let \(f\) be a differentiable function that satisfies \(f(3) = 2.\) The line \(y = 2 + \frac{1}{2}(x - 3)\) is tangent to the graph of \(y = f(x)\) at \(x = 3.\) Let \(k(x) = 2x^2 \cdot f(x).\) Find \(k'(3).\)
SOLUTION We differentiate \(k\) using the Product Rule to get \[ \ba k'(x) &= 4x f(x) + 2x^2 f'(x) \cma \nl k'(3) &= 12 f(3) + 18 f'(3) \pd \ea \] We are given \(f(3) = 2.\) The tangent line to the graph of \(y = f(x)\) at \(x = 3\) has slope \(1/2,\) so \(f'(3) = 1/2.\) Plugging in these values gives \[k'(3) = 12(2) + 18 \par{\tfrac{1}{2}} = \boxed{33} \]
EXERCISE 41
Let \(f\) and \(g\) be differentiable functions such that \(f(2) = 2,\) \(f'(2) = -1,\) \(g(2) = 3,\) and \(g'(2) = -4.\) Find \(k'(2)\) for each function given.
  1. \(\ds k(x) = f(x) g(x)\)
  2. \(\ds k(x) = \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\)
  3. \(\ds k(x) = \frac{g(x)}{f(x)}\)
  4. \(\ds k(x) = \frac{f(x)}{g(x) + f(x)}\)
SOLUTION
  1. The Product Rule gives \[k'(x) = f'(x) g(x) + f(x) g'(x) \pd\] Plugging in \(x = 2,\) we obtain \[ \ba k'(2) &= f'(2) g(2) + f(2) g'(2) \nl &= (-1)(3) + (2)(-4) \nl &= \boxed{-11} \ea \]
  2. Using the Quotient Rule, we obtain \[k'(x) = \frac{f'(x) g(x) - f(x) g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2}\] When \(x = 2,\) we have \[ \ba k'(2) &= \frac{f'(2) g(2) - f(2) g'(2)}{[g(2)]^2} \nl &= \frac{(-1)(3) - (2)(-4)}{3^2} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{5}{9}} \ea \]
  3. The Quotient Rule gives \[ \ba k'(x) &= \frac{g'(x) f(x) - g(x) f'(x)}{[f(x)]^2} \cma \nl k'(2) &= \frac{g'(2) f(2) - g(2) f'(2)}{[f(2)]^2} \nl &= \frac{(-4)(2) - (3)(-1)}{2^2} \nl &= \boxed{-\frac{5}{4}} \ea \]
  4. Let's use \(f\) and \(g\) as abbreviations of \(f(x)\) and \(g(x),\) respectively. We differentiate \(k,\) getting \[ \ba k'(x) &= \frac{(g + f) \cdot f' - (g' + f') \cdot f}{(g + f)^2} \cma \nl k'(2) &= \frac{(3 + 2)(-1) - (-4 - 1)(2)}{(2 + 3)^2} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{1}{5}} \ea \]
EXERCISE 42
Let \(f(x) = 2xe^x.\) For \(n \geq 2,\) show that \[f^{(n)}(x) = 2e^x + f^{(n - 1)}(x) \pd\]
SOLUTION
The notation \(f^{(n)}(x)\) denotes the \(n\)th derivative of \(f.\)
The Product Rule gives the derivative of \(f\) to be \[ \ba f'(x) &= 2(1)e^{x} + 2x \par{e^x} \nl &= 2e^x + 2xe^{x} \nl &= 2e^x + f(x) \pd \ea \] The second derivative \(f''(x)\) is therefore given by \[f''(x) = 2e^x + f'(x) \pd\] This pattern is repeated for higher-order derivatives; for example, \[ \ba f'''(x) &= 2e^x + f''(x) \cma \nl f^{(4)}(x) &= 2e^x + f'''(x) \cma \nl f^{(5)}(x) &= 2e^x + f^{(4)}(x) \pd \ea \] Hence, for any \(n \geq 2\) we see \[f^{(n)}(x) = 2e^x + f^{(n - 1)}(x) \pd\]
EXERCISE 43
Consider the family of functions \(f(x) = 1/(x^2 + 4x + k),\) where \(k\) is a constant.
  1. Show that \[f'(x) = \frac{-2x - 4}{\par{x^2 + 4x + k}^2} \pd\]
  2. If \(k = 4,\) then find the only vertical asymptote to the graph of \(y = f(x).\)
  3. For \(k \ne 4,\) determine the \(x\)-coordinate at which the graph of \(y = f(x)\) has a horizontal tangent.
  4. Determine and interpret \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} f'(x) \and \lim_{x \to \infty} f'(x) \pd\]
  5. Show that \(f\) has no vertical asymptotes if \(k \gt 4.\)
SOLUTION
  1. The Quotient Rule gives the derivative of \(f\) to be \[ \ba f'(x) &= \frac{(0) \par{x^2 + 4x + k} - 1(2x + 4)}{\par{x^2 + 4x + k}^2} \nl &= \frac{-2x - 4}{\par{x^2 + 4x + k}^2} \cma \ea \] as requested.
  2. If \(k = 4,\) then \(f\) becomes \[f(x) = \frac{1}{x^2 + 4x + 4} \pd\] The function \(f\) has a vertical asymptote when its denominator is \(0\) but its numerator is nonzero. So we equate the denominator to \(0\) to find \[ \ba x^2 + 4x + 4 &= 0 \nl (x + 2)^2 &= 0 \nl \implies x &= -2 \pd \ea \] Thus, the only vertical asymptote to the graph of \(f\) for \(k = 4\) is \(\boxed{x = -2}.\)
  3. The graph of \(f\) has a horizontal tangent at some \(x\) if and only if \(f'(x) = 0.\) We see \[f'(x) = \frac{-2x - 4}{\par{x^2 + 4x + k}^2} = 0 \implies \boxed{x = -2}\] Thus, the family of graphs \(f(x) = 1/(x^2 + 4x + k)\) has a horizontal tangent at \(x = -2\) if \(k \ne 4,\) but has a vertical asymptote if \(k = 4.\)
  4. Observe that the denominator of \(f'\) has a higher degree than the numerator of \(f'.\) Hence, the denominator grows faster than the numerator as \(x \to \infty\) or \(x \to -\infty,\) pushing the value of \(f'\) toward \(0.\) Accordingly, \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} f'(x) = 0 \and \lim_{x \to \infty} f'(x) = 0 \pd\] These limits tell us that the slope to \(f\) approaches \(0\) as \(x \to \infty\) or \(x \to -\infty.\) The graph of \(f\) has two horizontal asymptotes, so the slope approaching \(0\) is expected as \(x\) approaches infinity or negative infinity.
  5. The graph of \(f(x) = 1/(x^2 + 4x + k)\) has no vertical asymptotes if and only if the quadratic equation \(x^2 + 4x + k = 0\) has no real solutions. The discriminant of the quadratic is \[(4)^2 - 4(1)(k) = 16 - 4k \pd\] (See Section 0.1 to review how the discriminant shows how many real zeros a quadratic has.) If the quadratic equation has no real solutions, then the discriminant must be negative: \[16 - 4k \lt 0 \implies k \gt 4 \pd\] Thus, \(f(x) = 1/(x^2 + 4x + k)\) has no vertical asymptotes for \(k \gt 4\) because its denominator is never \(0.\)
