5.2 Exercises: Volumes with Cross Sections

Click on a problem number to see its solution.

\(\hspace{0.56em}1.\)
A solid is placed in the \(xy\)-plane between \(x = 0\) and \(x = 3.\) At any \(x,\) the solid's cross-sectional area perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is given by \[A(x) = x^2 + 2 \pd\] Calculate the solid's volume.
\(\hspace{0.56em}2.\)
A solid is placed in the \(xy\)-plane between \(y = 1\) and \(y = 9.\) At any \(y,\) the solid's cross-sectional area perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis is given by \[A(y) = \sqrt y + 3 \pd\] Calculate the solid's volume.
\(\hspace{0.56em}3.\)
The region bounded by the parabola \(y = x^2,\) the \(x\)-axis, and the lines \(x = 1\) and \(x = 2\) is the base of a solid whose cross sections perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis are squares. Calculate the volume of the solid.
\(\hspace{0.56em}4.\)
A solid's base is the triangle bounded by the lines \(y = x + 2,\) \(x = -2,\) and \(x = 1.\) The solid's cross sections perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis are squares. Calculate the volume of this solid.
\(\hspace{0.56em}5.\)
Let region \(R\) be bounded by the curve \(y = \sqrt x,\) the \(x\)-axis, and the lines \(x = 1\) and \(x = 4.\) Region \(R\) is the base of a solid such that at each \(x,\) its cross section perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is a rectangle of height \(2x + 3.\) Calculate the volume of the solid.
\(\hspace{0.56em}6.\)
Region \(R\) is bounded by the curve \(y = e^x,\) the \(x\)-axis, the \(y\)-axis, and the line \(x = 2.\) Suppose that region \(R\) is the base of a solid such that at each \(x,\) the cross section perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is a rectangle whose height is \(3\) times the length of its base in region \(R.\) Calculate the solid's volume.
\(\hspace{0.56em}7.\)
The region bounded by the curve \(f(x) = x^2,\) the \(y\)-axis, and the line \(y = 4\) is the bottom face of a solid such that at each \(y,\) its cross section perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis is a rectangle of height \(3f(x).\) Calculate the solid's volume.
\(\hspace{0.56em}8.\)
The triangle enclosed by the line \(y = 2x,\) the \(y\)-axis, and the line \(y = 4\) is the base of a solid whose cross sections perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis are semicircles. Calculate the volume of this solid.
\(\hspace{0.56em}9.\)
In the first quadrant, the triangle enclosed by the line \(y = 5 - x\) is the base of a solid. Every cross section to the solid perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is a right isosceles triangles whose hypotenuse lies in the bounded region. Calculate the volume of this solid.
\(10.\)
The region enclosed by the graph of \(y = \asin x\) for \(0 \leq x \leq 1\) is the base of a solid such that at each \(y,\) the cross section perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis is a rectangle of height \(2 \cos y.\) Calculate the solid's volume.
\(11.\)
In the first quadrant, the region enclosed by the curve \(y = x^3\) and the line \(y = 8\) is the base of a solid whose cross sections parallel to the \(x\)-axis are equilateral triangles. Calculate the solid's volume.
\(12.\)
Suppose that a solid's base is the region bounded above by the curve \(y = \sqrt x + 2,\) below by the curve \(y = 1 - 2 \sqrt x,\) and on the sides by the lines \(x = 0\) and \(x = 1.\) If cross sections perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis are semicircles, then determine the solid's volume.
\(13.\)
The region enclosed by \(y = x^2\) and \(y = \sqrt x\) is the base of a solid whose cross sections perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis are squares. Find the solid's volume.
\(14.\)
A solid's base is the region enclosed by the line \(y = x\) and the parabola \(y = x^2\) in the first quadrant. Every cross section to the solid perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is a right isosceles triangles whose hypotenuse lies in the bounded region. Calculate the volume of this solid.
\(15.\)
A baker models the base of a mini-cake using the function \(y = 2 + 2 \cos(\pi x/3)\) for \(-3 \leq x \leq 3,\) where \(x\) is measured in inches. Cross sections perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis are semicircles. For each cubic inch, the baker injects \(0.1\) gram of liquid chocolate into the cake. How many grams of liquid chocolate does the baker add to the cake?
\(16.\)
The surface of a pond is the region enclosed by the curves \[f(x) = 5 \cos \frac{\pi x}{4} \and g(x) = x^2 \cma\] where \(x\) is measured in feet. At any \(x,\) the pond's depth of water is given by the function \(7 - x.\) Calculate the pond's volume.
\(17.\)
The circle \(x^2 + y^2 = 4\) is the base of a solid whose cross sections perpendicular to the \(y\)-axis are equilateral triangles. Find the solid's volume.
\(18.\)
A solid's base is the region bounded by \(y = x^2 - 1\) under the \(x\)-axis. At any \(y,\) slicing the solid perpendicularly to the \(y\)-axis yields a semicircle. Find the solid's volume.
\(19.\)
Suppose that the triangle enclosed by the graph of \(y = \abs x\) and the line \(y = 5\) is the base of a solid whose cross sections parallel to the \(x\)-axis are right isosceles triangles whose hypotenuses lie in the enclosed region. Calculate the solid's volume.
\(20.\)
The region bounded by the curve \(x = y^4\) and the line \(x = 1\) is the base of a solid whose cross sections perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis are squares. Find the solid's volume.
\(21.\)
Determine the volume of a hemisphere whose base is the region enclosed by the circle \(x^2 + y^2 = 25.\)
\(22.\)
The region enclosed by \(y = 1/(x^2 + 2),\) the \(x\)-axis, the \(y\)-axis, and the line \(x = 1\) is the base of a solid. At each \(x,\) the cross section to the solid perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis is a rectangle of height \(3x.\) Calculate the solid's volume.
\(23.\)
Let \(R\) be the region bounded by the parabola \(y = x^2,\) the \(x\)-axis, the \(y\)-axis, and the line \(x = k,\) where \(k\) is a positive constant. Let \(S\) be the solid whose base is region \(R\) and whose cross sections perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis are squares. Find the value of \(k\) for which the area of \(R\) equals the volume of \(S.\)
\(24.\)
Consider the family of functions \[f(x) = \frac{1}{(kx)^2 + 1} \cma\] where \(k\) is a positive constant. The region under the graph of \(y = f(x)\) and above the \(x\)-axis from \(x = 1\) to \(x = 2\) is the base of a solid whose cross sections perpendicular to the \(x\)-axis are rectangles of uniform height \(k.\) For what value of \(k\) is the solid's volume maximized?
